class=\"postedDate\">Posted: December 15, 2015\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMany gamebooks and interactive fictions encourage guesswork. In the best of the former, that guesswork feels more like sleuthing, with the narrative jumping in like a good improv partner to back up whatever arbitrary choices you make\u2014a shrug and a click on your part might become \u201cyou recall that bane of ivy-wort has restorative properties, you clever so-and-so.\u201d In the best of the latter a guess - even a wrong guess - has rewards outside the dubious pleasures of winning the fiction, especially if the story\u2019s been designed to weather mistakes on the player\u2019s part and has \u201cgame over\u201d scenes written with the same care as the rest of the work.<br><br>Caverns of the Snow Witch is tricky, then. This latest digital adaptation from Tin Man Games is a game of chance primarily, like most of the Fighting Fantasy line, and yet it\u2019s also a story of chance, one where coincidence and the time-tested tactic of \u201cgrab everything that\u2019s not nailed down\u201d take center stage.<br><br>Maybe that\u2019s not an entirely fair criticism to levy against a gamebook from 1984, especially one with a cheese-factor  that Pee-Wee Herman would roll his eyes at. In this tale you\u2019ll quickly go from guarding a trader\u2019s caravan to hunting a rampaging yeti to plundering the titular Snow Witch\u2019s icy fortress, with only a few peeps of internal monologue to suggest your avatar is conflicted about their course. You could imagine a gamebook that gives you a choice about the fortress raid just for flavor, with the narrative correcting to push you towards the Snow Witch regardless. That said, there is an old-timey adventure serial charm to this hyperactive hero that unthinkingly jumps headfirst into whatever quest they can find, even if it means abandoning the quest they\u2019re on.<br><br>As far as Tin Man\u2019s game is concerned, the presentation is tops. In addition to the standard difficulty, Caverns has both a hardcore more and a free-read mode where all links are available and you can heal to full stamina whenever you please. The game makes it clear what options are and aren\u2019t available at any given time, and neatly tucks away things like your character sheet and the bookmarks tab in the margins of the screen. And, naturally, there\u2019s a dice-roll animation with the sexy, chunky clunk sounds all worthy dice-roll animations have.<br><br>The one misstep is the new art, which - while passable - doesn\u2019t compare to the black-and-white original illustrations with their faux-woodcut style. Thankfully, that original art can be toggled on (from the start, and not as some silly unlockable after beating the game) in the options.<br><br>If you\u2019ve not played one of these Fighting Fantasy joints before, here\u2019s how it looks in motion. First, roll for your stats: stamina\/health, skill, and luck. Then, read along until a fork in the narrative comes up (usually as an actual fork in some path you\u2019re traveling), or until you\u2019re forced to fight (which is often). Next step is more rolling, either for combat, where you\u2019re adding your roll to your skill to best an opponent\u2019s roll added to their skill, or for the odd stat checks Caverns throws your way: roll under or above this number or that stat, pray the frost giant doesn\u2019t wind up and ping you in the noggin with a chest full of plunder.<br><br>Luck is unique in that you spend a point of it whenever you\u2019re forced to rely exclusively on good fortune. A slippery ice bridge or an encroaching avalanche will have you rolling two die; roll equal to or under your current luck and you\u2019re golden, roll over and you\u2019re liable to lose stamina or skill as the hazard hits you square in the jaw. Additionally, luck can be spent in combat following the same roll-under scheme, either to nullify a point of damage you\u2019ve absorbed or to increase the lethality of a blow you\u2019ve landed. Fail the luck check in a scrum, though, and you\u2019ll take even more damage, or see your previously dead-on blow become a glancing one.<br><br>So that\u2019s quite a bit of rolling dice. Maybe I\u2019m a few years too late on this criticism, but as someone new to the Fighting Fantasy scheme it seems like skill is far and away the most important stat, one that sees you either cruising through most straight-up fights or getting crushed in them. A bad skill roll will cut most on-the-level runs short, quick. Then again, even in default mode Caverns of the Snow Witch never asks for on-the-level play, with bookmarks meant as an easily deployed cheat.<br><br>As a narrative, Caverns has some soft failures, scenes where you\u2019ve clearly screwed up, but only enough to lose a few points off a stat. \u201cOh, the Object of Objectness was cursed, turns out,\u201d or \u201cmaybe hitting everything with my sword isn\u2019t the best stratagem.\u201d Just as numerous are the hard failures, where simply turning down the wrong corridor or failing to pick up a certain item several pages back brings the story to an anti-climactic end. In these latter situations I wondered why Caverns couldn\u2019t have some middle ground between its minor and major fail states; maybe instead of losing instantly when locked in certain rooms, you could escape through a crawl space at the risk of dropping an item.<br><br>It seems like these insta-fails were meant to increase the longevity of, say, an actual, physical gamebook, a design choice which doesn\u2019t translate well to 2015. It\u2019s not even accurate to call Caverns of the Snow Witch \u201ctough\u201d\u2013it\u2019s more\u2026 \u201cresistant.\u201d And yet, for all the quibbles I have about the \u201cgame\u201d part of this gamebook, when Caverns works, it works exactly as one would expect. Heroic battles in the snow\u2026 some light survival moments where you\u2019re struggling against the elements\u2026 fights with magical, otherworldly opponents where you need to apply knowledge and reasoning as well as your sword\u2013it\u2019s pulpy fun. Playing Caverns of the Snow Witch feels, for the most part, like stumbling around half-blind in a blizzard. direct revision (which includes an option to play using the original artwork and font type) from the Fighting Fantasy series of books written by Ian Livingstone. (This is the 9th book of the series, circa 1984.) This D&amp;D (Dungeons and Dragons) genre choose-your-own-adventure style book plays exactly as the original book form minus the physical dice. (Paper and pencil are optional.) Here the dice are built in.  Roll for stamina (health), skill (fighting ability) and that all so precarious luck.  Once the character stats are established time for some adventure which includes choosing directions (usually at the infamous 'T' junctions), making decisions (fight that creature or sneak past) and death, if one is defeated, in combat, by a poor decision, or when one's luck runs out, literally, and it always does eventually.  <br><br>The D&amp;D style books, the precursors to RPGs and their ilk, are as challenging as any AAA game in modern day and as unforgiving as any bullet-hell.<br><br>Storyline: Start as a hired sword to protect a caravan going to a snowy hamlet deep in Ice Finger Mountains.  The hamlet is attacked and the caravan owner offers 50 gold to track down and eliminate who or whatever slaughtered the inhabitants.  Off to find the monster (man or beast) with 50 gold gleaming in the eye but with no weight in the pocket, the adventure begins.  <br><br>Gameplay: This book is divided into four separate sections: 1) Tracking down the beast 2) The Caverns of the Snow Witch  3) Beyond the icy caverns and 4) Breaking the curse (of course there is a curse, a death curse to be precise.  No villian lets the &quot;hero&quot; live if they can help it, even if the villian dies first.  An auxium of Villiantry.)     <br><br>As I stated before this game is unforgiving, and the &quot;hero&quot; often dies.  However, just with a book, the player has the option to go back a page or two and try a different route or option.  There are also unlimited bookmarks (the equivalent of fingers between the pages) if the player needs or wants to try an entirely different direction or make different choices. (I suggest bookmarking often, at any place with choice and especially before battle).  Plus there is the all-important, and often can save your xxx, DICE SHAKE.  The dice shake is when the player seeing they just rolled an extremely low number like boxcars (1 and 1) on their white dice and who or what they are fighting just rolled a higher number on their red dice.  This higher number will damage the &quot;hero&quot;, if not outright kill them.  So the player has the option to quickly (like in maybe in a second) click on the dice again before they settle to get a reroll.  One reroll per dice throw, and the player has to be fast.  <br><br>There are three modes to this game. 1) Reader's mode (or cheater's mode) which allows the player unlimited health regeneration and the option to avoid all battles and poor decisions by using 'free choice' (which opens all options available to the player even ones they are not 'entitled' to have).  2) Adventure mode or normal mode, play with the stats rolled in the beginning of the game. (Remember the DICE SHAKE works here too.)  3) Hard mode play with a handicap in both stamina and skill (half for both) making battles much harder to win.  (Not recommended unless the player knows exactly where all the stamina, skill and luck power ups are.)  <br><br>An excellent example of the D&amp;D genre, the storyline is tight and moves along fast, challenging and unforgiving, where battle damage affects the player and poor decisions can have lethal consequences.  A fun read and extras include a brief history of Fighting Fantasy and the &quot;find&quot; art gallery depicting both the original and updated illustrations.   <br><br>Highly recommended to those who like old school and to anyone looking for a different type of RPG adventure.
