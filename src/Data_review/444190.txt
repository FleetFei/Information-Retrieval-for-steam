In many ways it\u2019s a bit more complex than its inspiring early 16-bit games, and in others less so. Knowing when to attack with your sword and when it might be more beneficial to attack with your limited if generous supply of magic attacks will get you through plenty of situations in which you end up surrounded by helmed thugs. Of note is that I found the final battle (the Ancient Black Dragon) far less challenging than the one before it, in which you face two of the cheapest bosses in the game at once. I\u2019m uncertain if it\u2019s a glitch or perhaps by design, but I found a strategy of dashing close to the dragon and constantly switching sides to keep backstabbing seems to make the battle nigh effortless.<br><br>In-game graphics here are best described as \u201cokay\u201d. Although we can probably chalk up the hammerspace appearance and disappearance of her sword to the premise that she\u2019s gifted in magic, I find it amusing that a barbarian warrioress with combat training to take on armies of soldiers runs into battle as if she\u2019s skipping through a field of lilies and chasing after butterflies. There\u2019s a small but serviceable variety of enemies with only a tiny amount of palette and\/or weapon swap to incorporate a few different attack styles, though as you might expect, the foes that are going to give you the hardest time are going to be the bosses themselves.
