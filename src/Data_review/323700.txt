2014\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGhost Master was the ultimate ghost game back in the year 2003 when it was first released. The ability to play as a ghost where you had to scare away people was a fun and unique experience, but somehow the idea hasn\u2019t been used in many other games after that one. The ones that did use it usually failed or were just mildly successful. So will Poltergeist A Pixelated Horror succeed where others have failed ? Let\u2019s take a closer look.<br><br>Poltergeist A Pixelated Horror (from now on referenced as Poltergeist if necessary) is at its heart a puzzle game and not a \u2018real\u2019 ghost simulator. You can\u2019t use your tricks and summons infinitely, there\u2019s a limit to how many times you can perform a certain action. But before I get to this I\u2019ll try to tell you a little bit more about the game itself.<br>After having lived in a mansion his entire life, Henry B. Knight (the main character of the game) eventually dies. Naturally, new people will start living in this mansion, but you can\u2019t and won\u2019t accept this. In fact, you won\u2019t be able to find peace until you\u2019ve scared them all away. So it\u2019s here that your quest to retake the mansion begins. <br>As you can clearly see Henry B. Knight is the star of the game. But you won\u2019t actually see him all that often. He\u2019ll sometimes appear as a \u2018special power\u2019 ghost to scare away bosses, but other than that (and for some small cutscenes) you will hardly ever see him. This is weird but also acceptable since the game is played from an isometric perspective, also known as a bird\u2019s-eye view.<br>Each level has a couple of rooms tied together. In each room there\u2019s usually at least one person or animal to be found, as well as some objects like TV\u2019s, beds and chairs. The exit can be located anywhere, it doesn\u2019t really matter, because once a person or animal is spooked he, she or it will automatically find their way towards this exit. The one thing you have to think about it is how to use the tools you\u2019re given to scare everyone away. Some of the levels will be easy and some of the later levels will absolutely challenge your mind. But fortunately you can always restart a level if things don\u2019t entirely go your way.<br>Each inhabitant has a meter that shows how easily one is spooked. A full meter (three circles) seems to be the limit but there\u2019s also the possibility that a meter is black. This means you have to scare the person first before you\u2019re able to see how full their meter actually is.<br>There are quite a few ways to scare people; you have the basic tricks like moving objects and making noise or you can sometimes summon a ghost or a ghost that can take over people and walk around.<br>I\u2019ve mentioned it before already, but since this is a puzzle game your objective is to use all of your available tools (or less, of course) to empty the rooms. Sometimes special characters (like exorcists and ghost hunters) will appear and these people have the ability to block one or more of your powers. In the beginning this is sometimes annoying, because if an action is blocked or stopped this means you usually have to restart the level. Later levels will allow you to finish it without having to use up all of your powers (if you play it strategically) making it a little bit more open and accessible. <br>There\u2019s no hint system so you have to figure things out all by yourself. This is sometimes a little harder than expected; I\u2019ve played early levels that took me more than a few tries while I could finish other, later levels in one go.<br>Poltergeist uses multiple settings like the traditional (1800\/1900\u2019s) mansion but there\u2019s also a modern setting and even an office to haunt. In total there are 60 levels; 15 for each setting with the last three levels being boss encounters. I\u2019m not sure why there are even boss encounters;  the idea sounds cool, but the execution is very poor. That\u2019s because the boss fight levels are essentially the same as all the other levels, but the deal here is that the boss can only be scared away once all other inhabitants are gone. After that you can summon the ghost of Henry to scare him off. This last part is done with a simple mouseclick and there\u2019s no real interaction. I think this could\u2019ve used a little more thought. But other than that this is good fun.<br><br>Poltergeist isn\u2019t outstanding in its graphics department. In fact, its style is a bit of a coherent mess. I understand that it\u2019s probably because of financial and\/or time constraints, but it uses both 2D sprites and 3D models. The sprites are okay but charmingly animated, but once the 3D models start to appear you get thrown out of the experience because \u2013 to me \u2013 it just doesn\u2019t look right. Or the other way around, I\u2019m not sure. The textures on the other hand are very well done.<br>The interface is basic but gets the job done. A little more polish, a bit of resizing and maybe some extra color would\u2019ve been nice but it\u2019s good enough to understand what each icon does.<br>The music is perfect with haunting, Exorcist-like tunes. It\u2019s done very well and complements the atmosphere, even though the game isn\u2019t about scary or creepy stuff (the setting, however, is). The frightened people, as well as their screams when they are running away, are realistic and believable. There\u2019s also nothing wrong with the sound effects; they might feel unnecessary at first but they\u2019re essential to let the gamers know that something is actually happening. So every object and summon you use will make a sound.<br><br>Length wise there\u2019s more than enough gameplay to keep you occupied for at least several hours. Each of the levels takes but a few minutes (if you know exactly what to do) so getting through the 60 levels will take you anywhere between 4-7 hours I think. I can\u2019t complain here, there\u2019s more than enough to do.<br><br>Poltergeist might not become a classic like Ghost Master but despite that it has become an entertaining puzzle game worthy of my time. It doesn\u2019t bring anything new to the table but anyone looking for another ghost game, or just a good puzzle game in general, will absolutely have fun with this.<br><br>[Rating: A concept that's not fully fleshed out or gameplay with some rough edges can all be forgiven if the charm is there. Poltergeist bears that charm in spades, from its blocky furnishings to its hand-drawn cartoon cutscenes, enough that you won't mind the simplistic puzzles that form its core.<br><br>You take on the role of Henry B. Knight, vengeful deceased, as he scares the stuffing out of anyone who would call his mansion home. The game plays out in 60 puzzles across four time periods, each with its own look and feel. Each puzzle has a number of folks milling around, and provides you a limited number of ghostly powers to terrify them with. The powers range from jiggling loose objects to full-on possessing people, but their mechanical functions are all similar. Puzzles are divided into rooms, and the powers do a certain amount of &quot;damage&quot; to the people in one or more rooms. When you do enough damage to a person, they vacate.<br><br>The puzzle, then, is figuring out what combination of powers in what order will get everyone gone. You might need to fling a lamp from one room into another to soften someone up for a shadowman encounter. Or you might have to send a ghost wolf to drive a lone inhabitant into a room full of people so you can explode the TV and scare them all at once. There are special characters that block certain powers, and each era of puzzles ends with three boss fights against some entertaining characters with their own ways of fouling you up. None of the puzzles are terribly challenging, since they're just sequence problems, but they're gratifying enough to overcome. My only hangup here was the fiddly attention-getting power, which works based on some unclear proximity rules that made it hard to complete a few puzzles, even when I knew exactly what to do.<br><br>What really kept me invested here was the presentation. The graphics are a peculiar mix of 2D characters against chunky, voxelly 3D levels. It works better than it sounds because the features of the levels all have physics, and your terrified tenants bang open doors and knock over vases. The adorable visuals are coupled with some equally adorable sound effects of gasps, screams, and Sims-style nonsense. You also get loads of hand-drawn cutscenes for the eras and bosses, accompanied with some charmingly not-quite-right English subtitles. The goofy endings are also worth your time to see, capping off a perfectly enjoyable package.<br><br>It isn't groundbreaking in any way, but scaring the little pixel people half to death with your goofy powers makes it more than worth your time. If you've played the classic Ghost Master, Poltergeist feels like a much lighter, more streamlined version of that.
