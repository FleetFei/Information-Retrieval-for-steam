The first game preyed upon a feeling of helplessness. The second game used panic as a vehicle for fear. This game uses the concept of struggle and strategy to out-wit your killer, as best you can in a game this limited. There's only one animatronic, so you only have one major threat to look out for. This forces you away from the cameras to your reboot menu, where you'll spend agonizing seconds not only regaining order, but losing track of where Springtrap has gone. And heaven forbid you look away from your cameras at the wrong time, linger too far away from them, or even stare at them for too long.<br><br>&quot;How do you avoid him?&quot; It's fairly simple, if perhaps not obvious at first -- your cameras are tied to the speaker system, allowing you to play sounds in various rooms. I won't spoil anything, but there's some closure to the trilogy. If you know nothing of the previous game's happenings, this game will at least give you some sort of closure by itself, but it definitely caters to players who have played all 3 games and invested themselves in it.<br><br>If you liked the first two games, you'll like this. If you didn't, you might not, though I will say it's definitely the highest production quality of all three games.
