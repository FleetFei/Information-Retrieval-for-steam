While SOCOM 1 was a sleeper hit that maintained a modest community, SOCOM2 put the series on the map in a big way. It essentially set the bar for the console online tactical shooters for years to come. The community was massive (by early 2000's standards), and it, in a way, became its own self-contained E-sport with the introduction of <a class=\"bb_link\" href=\"https:\/\/steamcommunity.com\/linkfilter\/?url=http:\/\/gamebattles.com\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\" >gamebattles.com<\/a> and other 3rd party competitive ladders. Fast-forward a few years, SOCOM 3 came and went (It was essentially the same game except with the inclusion of vehicles - yikes), SOCOM: Combined Assault was a hail mary attempt that did an admirable job of expanding the online functionality by including co-op. However, once the series transitioned to a new console generation, SOCOM as we knew was all but dead (I won't even discuss Confrontation). In the coming years Sony would disband developer Zipper Interactive, and any hope of a reboot was effectively squashed.<br><br>But then there was a glimmer of hope - a chance, if you will.<br><br>Just as 2013 was seeming to be yet another disappointing year, a Kickstarter was put on by some guy named David Sears whom claimed he was going to create a spiritual sequel to our cherished relic. The Kickstarter campaign was a huge success, and funding was secured to create this beautiful test-tube baby that would become the sequel we always wanted... Except once development started to take off David Sears abruptly left the project and was never heard from again. It was a bummer.<br><br>Here we are in 2017, and H-Hour's development has continued on despite David Sear's absence. With no completion date in sight, we are left to reconcile with the current short-comings and make the most of what we have. After all, I doubt there will ever be another spiritual successor backed with a star-power that H-Hour had.<br><br>Now that the backstory is out of the way (and if you're still reading) I can move on to what you can expect out of H-Hour's current build. Despite it being an incomplete prototype, playing as your generic operative or terrorist actually feels pretty good and can be a wholesome experience. Admittedly, the majority of that sentiment stems from nostalgia, so if you're not a former SOCOM player then you may very well disagree, but most of the core mechanics have been implemented and everything gels together rather well. Hit detection could definitely use some improvement, but I'm confident that will be addressed in the future. I'd say the biggest drawback, especially for veteran SOCOM players, is that you'll want to play this with a controller - that's how we played on the PS2 and it's what feels natural playing this sort of game. However, because this is on PC, the mouse and keyboard combo reigns supreme on the battlefield, so if you're like me and insist on using a controller like a stubborn grumpy grandpa, expect to be at a disadvantage from the get-go.<br><br>The community is great, albeit VERY small. At any given time you might find a couple of lobby's inhabited or you might find that nobody is online, which is a real drag. In addition to the player-base, the map-creating community deserves many many kudos as they've done an excellent job of recreating or at least drawing inspiration from classic SOCOM maps. It has been an absolute thrill to once again battle for the streets of Crossroads and die senselessly in the tombs of Requiem. What a treat.<br><br><br>All-in-all, H-Hour is not only the closest we'll ever get to a true SOCOM Swan Song, but it is also an opportunity for the old guard to rekindle some of the magic of a game style that has long since lost its place in the mainstream.
