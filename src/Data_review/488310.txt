killer-app and true &quot;eSports&quot;<\/div><br>Disclaimer:<br><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li>Played with Oculus Rift + Touch, 2-front-facing camera setup<br><\/li><li>I like table tennis in RL. If you don't, this game may not be for you.<\/li><\/ul><br>Before buying this game, I tried VR Sports Challenge from the Touch pre-order bonus. This was fun for 1-2 hours, but never felt like real sports. My expectation when buying Eleven Table Tennis: just another tech demo with unintuitive controls and assists that make every shot magically hit the target. <br><br>But, what a pleasant surprise: The controller is just a paddle! The buttons have no function. <b>The only control is to swing your paddle against the ball<\/b>. There are no assists. This is pure physics, the real thing. To make such a direct, raw control scheme is brave because it requires perfect tracking and perfect physics. And to my tremendous delight, both are spot-on. The tracking shows how marvelous the Oculus Touch tracking works (judging from reports of other players, the Vive tracking works equally well). The physics can only be credited to the fantastic work of the developers. Ball and paddle physics are nearly perfect. All kind of spins work as expected. If there are differences to the real world, they are subtle enough to only be noticed by very experienced players.<br><br>As a sport, <b>Table tennis is a perfect fit<\/b> to the current generation VR headsets. It requires little room and is played front-facing with minimal movement on the spot. The paddle has comparable size and weight to the motion controller. The ball is small and light enough to make the controller haptics feel realistic. While the player moves his whole body, all movement culminates in the force applied to the paddle and therefore it is adequate to only track the hands.<br><br>As a result, <b> this game skips the uncanny valley and goes directly to holodeck.<\/b> After finding the right settings (which is a bit troublesome, see below), the game feels just like playing real table tennis. As someone who likes playing in real life, I can immediately use the play style I already know. And I notice making the same errors.<br><br><u>Single Player mode<\/u><br><br>The setting is a stylish apartment with the ping pong table in the middle. In the left hand you see the Oculus Touch model, in the right hand the paddle. You have no body or arms. The opponent is shown the same way, plus a (somewhat scary) mask for the head that conveys which way he is looking. Each game is basically one set (11 points). The basic rules are enforced by the game.<br><br>In Singleplayer mode, you play against a surprisingly good and natural AI of several difficuly levels. On the higher levels, the AI starts to apply spin and play faster balls.<br><br>Additionally there are a few Practice Minigames. For example, in one you must return balls to specific corridors. This game mode as a lot of potential to turn it into a more useful training tool, but feels quite limited at the moment.<br><br><u>Multi Player mode<\/u><br><br>In Multiplayer mode, you can play against a (Steam) friend or random player.<br><br>A critical point here is the connection quality, especially the ping. In most cases, this worked surprisingly well for me. If the connection is good, there is no noticable difference to the Singleplayer mode. If it is bad, the ball &quot;jumps&quot; a bit after the opponent hits, making it harder to react. While the game does everything it can to smooth that out, above a certain lag threshold, the game becomes unplayable.<br><br>Unfortunately, there is no ranking system yet. Still, to be able to play table tennis against players from all over the world as if in the same room is magical and tremendously fun. The game has voice chat activated by default. Laughing together on funny balls or praising nice shots makes the experience even better (of course not all player use their microphone or speak the same language).<br><br><u>Settings<\/u><br><br>The most important setting I had to change was the paddle position. For Oculus Touch, the default position is unfortunately tilted. I adjusted it so that when keeping my hand vertically the paddle is also vertically, like a continuation of my hand. The Rift's nose gap is very useful here to AR-like see your own hand and the paddle at the same time.<br><br>Also there are some settings related to the paddle. I imagine it to be like the choice of the physical rubber properties of the paddle. You can experminent and adjust those to your liking.<br><br><u>Wish list for the developers<\/u><br><br>The raw gameplay is in place and extremly fun. But I think there is a lot of potential to make this game even better. If the developers are reading this, here are my top three wishes:<br><br><ol><li> Better Match making: Introduce rankings (visible or hidden). It is no fun for either side if the gap in play strength is big. Also check the connection quality and do not match players if it is too bad.<br><\/li><li> Simplify the settings and provide better default values. Oculus Touch paddle position is tilted, use the correct rotation as default. Instead of sliders give some beginner friendly choices for paddles. Hide the sliders in advanced settings.<br><\/li><li> Bring the game to Oculus Home to reach more Oculus players (Cross-play with Steam!).<\/li><\/ol><br><u>Conclusion<\/u><br><br>Table tennis is a uniquely suited sports for VR. Eleven Table Tennis provides an incredibly realistic simulation of an olympic sports making it arguably the first true &quot;eSports&quot;.
