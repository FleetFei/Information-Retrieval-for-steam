class=\"bb_h1\">Intro<\/div>DashBored is a game of personality. You play as Nicolas, a young man who finds himself in a surreal world and only wants one simple thing: to get himself home. Along the way the player will explore and compact world and engage in turn-based battles.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Personality<\/div>The element of this game that most players are going to resonate with is its crass charm. DashBored is a sleazy game where you buy things from a Jason Voorhees impersonator and fight monsters that throw children at you. The characters have diverse personalities but are frequently profane. The dialogue in DB is juvenile but not to an extent where it crosses a line. No, it's pretty perfect.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Presentation<\/div>Graphically DashBored is amateurish but it's cartoony style goes good with its snarky, vulgar cast and with its offbeat absurd plot. The music is a highlight. It reminded me a lot of LISA and Earthbound. Some songs work in sound effects into their melodies which is very evocative. One of my favourite tracks was a town theme that incorporated police sirens with its instrumentation. Very cool.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Gameplay<\/div>Although DashBored gets by on its personality, players looking for great turn-based battles are going to be disappointed. The battles in this game at their best are pushovers. You are given about 30 abilities among all your party members and I didn't use more of the half of them on regular. DB suffers that classic RPG problem where you are given buffs and debuffs but since most battles are over in a couple rounds, there's no point wasting MP on status ailments that are very circumstantial when that character could just be attacking to make the battle end quicker. This is averted in boss battles, of course.<br><br>I also found myself having trouble getting around. Finding shops, a save point, or even an inn can be frustrating and the last thing you want when you're low on health and items is to be searching for a sanctuary. Maybe that was the intention of the developer but in this game it seems unnecessary. You could give DB the benefit of the doubt though and just assume it was this reviewer's inability to navigate.<br><br>Interestingly enough, even though I felt on edge a lot and felt like I was only barely overcoming the game's obstacles, there was never a time where I was out of resources and needed to grind. Even when I was low on items and cash, I always got through without grinding so that might be some expert balancing there.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Final Word<\/div>I'm ultimately going to recommend this game because of it's length. DashBored is no longer than 4 hours and very little of that time is wasted. You're always seeing new and interesting things in this world and the plot moves at a good pace. It's not a great plot but its a plot that allows the developer to tell a lot of jokes and have the personalities of the story's characters bounce off each other. That's what DashBored is. It a game as a vehicle for a lot of weirdness and comedy and if you can tolerate some boring battles then you'd do well to pick it up.<br><br>NOTE: Right now DashBored has a few bugs and is a little janky.
