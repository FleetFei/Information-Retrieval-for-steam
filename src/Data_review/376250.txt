please be aware that I am writing this Review while Ground Breakers is still in Early Access, so it is not completely polished yet and things might change before the official release.<br>With that being said, most of what I have to say about Ground Breakers is very positive. Let's get to the most important part for a game like the first, the Gameplay:<br><br>Ground Breakers is a turn-based strategy game where you fight with Robots on a randomly generated Grid of varying size. While this sounds simplistic, it gives the game a surprising amount of depth and I'll do my best to elaborate why. The grid itself is nothing new of course and they way United Indie implemented this cornerstone of turn-based gaming is very good. It gives you a good battleground to fight on without feeling too small and restricting, but also not so big that there is much downtime.<br>Where Ground Breakers shines is the implementation of height differences and the individual robots. Being on High Ground gives you and advante in combat, but it is more costly to move to a higher position. So moving one of you Robots onto a good high ground position is a big commitment, but also with a good reward attached to it, if you can pull it off!<br>Now let's talk about the Robots, which is of course what this game is all about. I'll get to the artstyle in a moment, let's focus on the mechanics for now. There is a surprising number of different Robots available, that you unlock gradually and randomly (the order in which you get them) as far as I can tell. Each Robot comes with 3 Skills. This in combination with the general Attributes (like Movement, Damage, Health or AttackRange) make for very unique Robots and they all have a very distinct feel to them. There is also a big reward to unlocking a new Robot and being able to use him for the first time in combat.<br><br>All in all, Ground Breakers gets a lot of the Gameplay bascis right, which makes for a very solid foundation to build upon. Let's move on the the Graphics:<br><br>Art Style and wether or not you like it is a very subjective matter. I personally really like the style they are going for. It is simplistic and very fitting with the Genre. The Robots themselves are designed with a lot of care and once again they managed to created something that not only handles uniquely, but also looks the part. United Indie did an amazing job of creating a package that at least for me works very well. From the choice of skills to the design of the individual Robots it comes together in a very fitting way.<br>The Main Menus background is a bit bland at the moment, but I assume they will change that before the game comes out of early access. Aside from that there I not much I could criticize.<br><br>Let's move on to another aspect of the game that could make or break a game like this, and that is the A.I.:<br>From what I have seen of the A.I. it is very good. They make smart choices and use their skills. They attack weakened units and make good choices in their priority. I am playing on normal most of the time (as long as you can choose the difficulty), so it should get harder on a higher difficulty. That being said, it never really feels unfair. If you have a good grasp on the game mechanics, you can appreciate the fair fight that the A.I. is putting up. I don't know too much about the mechanics behind A.I. in general, but in my opinion it looks like solid work that will be challenging, but not entirely unfair.<br><br>Now that I have covered the cornerstones, let's talk a little bit about the game modes you have available. First up, there is the Campaign mode. It's not really a sort of Story mode but a way to take what the game does, so the individual battles and put it in a more complex setting. In the campaign you choose the difficulty, the amount of opponents and the size of the map. You then try to conquer areas (so squares on a gridded world map) and try to eliminate your opponents, who are doing the same. This is rounded out by a round system. So you have only limited actions per turn and once you have used them up, your opponents make their move. Then the whole thing starts over again. It's a nice way to give your battles a bit more meaning then just the short-lived battles.<br>There is also a layer of economy in the campaign that is absent from the rest of the game, which adds in another interesting layer to work with.<br><br>Next up you have the Scenarios. Here you get thrown into specific settings you have to win. For example you have to defend a Crystal from the enemy until you either destroy them all or the time runs out. The enemy Robots will be respawning though, so it is harder than it sounds. Admittedly I haven't gotten too far into the Scenarios yet, but what I have seen so far looks good. It is more about working with your ressources to win a single battle, than an overall campaign.<br><br>The next game mode is the Infinity mine. At this point there are 100 levels of the Infinity Mine and they get gradually harder. Something I haven't mentioned so far is the option to customize and upgrade your Robots. With that you can modify them or just make them overall better. You will have to do that to progress in the mine, as the enemy does not match you strength, but works of a set difficulty. You can upgrade you Robots a lot and if you want to make progress you will have to do that. Even though this means grinding out items to upgrade your Robots, I like the mine. It's an awesome feeling to be really challenged, because you might be a bit too weak for the mine and still win. They also use the Mine to implement a simplisitc Quest system that allows them to teach you a bit about the story and history of the world they have created. Although personally I wouldn't necessarily need that, it is a nice added bonus.<br><br>So before I come to and end here, let me explain the customization of the Robots a little bit more. What you need to know first is that even though there are several game modes, the Robots you unlock and their upgrades stay in every mode. So you don't need to start over in every mode, but can share your progress throughout them all!<br>There are two ways of upgrading your Robots. The first one is the improvement of the quality of materials in the Robot. You start on the Iron level and if you have the correct Silver parts, you can upgrade your Robot to a higher level. Next will be Gold (I think) and so on and so forth until you reach the end. It can be a bit frustrating if you can't find the right parts to upgrade a Robot, but once you do it feels very rewarding and the increase in power is noticeable enough to make a difference.<br>The second way is to insert Chips into the Robot. You once again have a Grid where you can insert Chips to get varying effects, for example a small healthregain on every normal attack. So even though two Robots might be same Level, they could play differently, depending on the Chips used.<br><br>The last game mode is Multiplayer, but as I did not get a chance to try that one out yet, I can't say anything about that.<br><br>With that said, let me come to a conclusion. This review did run a bit longer than I initially anticipated. Even though Ground Breakers is an Early Access game, it feels and plays very good already. In my opinion the found the right mix between game modes and progression to keep you motivated and invested in the game. It is a lot of fun to play and once you grasp the concept and figure out how to use the individual Robots and how their skills can synergize with each other, it gets even better.<br>If you are into turn-based strategy games you can't do anything wrong with Ground Breakers.
