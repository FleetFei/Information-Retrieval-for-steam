review purposes. This did not influence my review in any way.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n---------------------------------------OVERVIEW-------------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Note: The Superfluous is currently in Early Access. The version reviewed was current as of the date of this review (20161203).<br><br>The Superfluous is a two-dimensional action platformer with some rogue-like elements from new indie developer Voided Pixels.<br><br>The background story is amusing and sets the scene well for your task. You see, there's a large asteroid on a collision course with Earth, but it's not due to hit for a thousand years. You're part of a die-hard group that wants to destroy the asteroid to ensure that it won't cause destruction of the Earth, no matter how far in the future, and the group eventually receives enough funding to warrant a deep space voyage to the asteroid to destroy it. The game even keeps track of how long you have until the Earth is destroyed; it starts at a little over 365 237 days.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n----------------------------------------QUALITY-------------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Graphics - The Superfluous uses 2D large-pixel graphics exclusively, adorned with coloured dynamic lighting, and it looks pretty darn good. It won't make you swoon or marvel at the graphics while you play it, but the characters are somewhat likeable and the enemies, though rather derivative, are mostly recognisable.<br><br>Weapons and explosions look a little boring, and there could definitely be a bit more variety in the scenery, but the game is set on a large asteroid cruising through deep space, so the atmosphere is appropriate.<br><br>Graphics settings are limited to windowed or full screen: no detail levels, special effects, or even resolution settings. But that's okay. The game achieves the look for which I assume the developer was striving, and it all works pretty well.<br><br>Sound - Sound effects are okay, if nothing special. There are no voiceovers. Music is nice, but fairly limited. I've been playing for a couple of hours and haven't turned it off, so that has to say something.<br><br>Gameplay - The game plays like a cross between a twin-stick shooter and a typical action platformer. It has full controller support, or you can use mouse and keyboard, but in either mode you move about with the left hand and control the aiming direction of your weapon with the right.<br><br>The stated full controller support is present, but it's awkward and hard to use. For some unexplained reason the developer has chosen some very odd controls that aren't all in keeping with conventions, and they just don't work very well. To begin with, in the menus you have to select items by pressing the right stick. This is only a minor annoyance, but navigation would be so much easier using the 'A' button for selection and 'B' for cancellation, like pretty much every other game with controller support. But worse, the in-game controls then use the 'AXYB' buttons for controlling gun direction\/aiming, when it would be so much better to use the right stick, again like pretty much every other game with independent weapon controls. Keyboard controls are a bit better, but I'd prefer to use the mouse for aiming and left and right mouse buttons for shooting my weapons, instead of everything using the keyboard. And the game doesn't seem to allow setting up your own control configurations, either.<br><br>Once you manage to overcome the control difficulties, though, the game is quite fun, if rather challenging. You start each level in a cave within the asteroid and need to reach the exit at the bottom, killing aggressive creatures and collecting loot as you go.<br><br>Enemies are mostly a bit derivative and some seem a bit out of place. For example, I can't help but wonder why there are crabs on an asteroid in interstellar space, but there they are, regardless. But they do their jobs well enough, most often causing considerable pain frustration on your journey.<br><br>Before each level you choose up to two squad members to go on each mission and you can swap between them at will with a key or button press during the game. To begin with you only have three squad members from which to choose, but there are more that can be unlocked, if you can manage to play well enough to beat a level or two. Each character has predefined stats and primary and secondary weapons or abilities. Some primary weapons and explosion-related secondary weapons (e.g. grenades) actually damage the level geometry, allowing you to reach otherwise unreachable places. And never mind about getting stuck: the unlimited jetpack equipped by each character can be used to escape from even the deepest hole.<br><br>The touted rogue-like elements include procedural level generation, which works quite well, though enemy types seem very limited; permadeath, requiring you to start again from the beginning when you die (albeit with clones of the same characters available); and unlockables that last across deaths and restarts, such as new characters and character upgrades. There's a notable lack of any cool loot, though, and the game itself tells you that character upgrades aren't effective enough to make a big difference. A bestiary would be nice, though, especially if it lasted across multiple play throughs, and would fit in with the story well enough.<br><br>The game includes three worlds ('biomes'), but at this stage only the first one is near completion. I'm not sure how they tie in with the backgound story, but given the rest of the background story, I'd expect there to be at least a tongue-in-cheek reason to be provided for their existence before release.<br><br>As far as Early Access issues are concerned, I found The Superfluous to be very stable, with no crashing issues or serious bugs. But the grammar is atrocious, to the point where it really lets the game down. The story is quite clever and could be amusing, but it falls flat with grammar and spelling errors, and a writer who doesn't know the difference between 'then' and 'than' or 'effect' and 'affect'. Hopefully the developer will have the game text professionally edited before release. Oh, and there seems to be no way to quit from in game or the main menu; I had to ALT-TAB and kill the process.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n-------------------------------------PROS &amp; CONS------------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Pros:<br>+ Good background story with a sense of humour<br>+ Rogue-like elements are relatively well done<br>+ Trading Cards<br><br>Cons:<br>- Bad controls, on both keyboard and controller, with no reconfiguration screen.<br>- Very hard to get into.<br>- Terrible grammar.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n--------------------------------------CONCLUSION-----------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Ultimately, The Superfluous doesn't really offer anything you haven't already seen before, but it does what it does reasonably well. It's overly difficult and a bit clunky in places, and it could definitely do with a rework of the control systems and some editing of the in-game text, but it is still in Early Access, so there's a chance these might happen before release.<br><br>If you're into challenging action platformers and enjoy some tongue-in-cheek humour, pick it up and give it a try.<br><br><div
