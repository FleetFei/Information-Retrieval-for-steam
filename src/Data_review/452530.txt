little hellscape had to become available. I say that fondly. Sunless Sea on its own is an undeniably creepy (sorta Lovecraftian, sorta abstract, very Victorian, but nonetheless creepy) game that plays on its main strength of incomparable writing to excel. You've got an underground lake, monstrous space bats, a creepy unblinking eye that follows your ship, and a few amicable zombies that really just want to do some sightseeing. <br><br>Now you're going under that sea. Everything you found creepy about the abovewater game? Dial that up to eleven, and expect the writing to match it. Zubmariner takes you down to a place where the shadows are ever-encroaching, and what few sources of light you do encounter frequently try to kill you. Some are merciful; they just want to eat you. Frankly, those are the nicer ones. The people you encounter there are weirder, from crystalline beings with a taste for debate, to people idly swimming naked inside an endless, hungering maw. The mobile, tortured, on-the-back-of-a-giant-crab city is actually one of the nicer ones to visit, and that takes doing. The writing is amazing, the adventure is nail-biting, and the exploration might have taken a few years off my life. Oops. And I won't even talk about the Constant Companion. Whoever put that THING in the game, I love you and I hate you. Also I died. A lot.<br><br>As far as gameplay goes, I honestly think Zubmariner does better than the original game. Disclaimer; I like Sunless Sea on its own already, so this isn't the review for you if you're looking for someone who came back just for the DLC. Regardless, I think one of the main problems Sunless Sea had was the endless repeat of early adventure each time a captain died and you needed to start over. The islands would scramble locations, but the stories wouldn't. Zubmariner doesn't depart from this, per se, but it does add a new skein of adventure to help break things up. Under the water, things are constantly changing. Monsters - and treasures - are more frequent, and more dangerous. A trip round your favorite trade cycle becomes that much more interesting when you can dive, and suddenly going in a straight line isn't a guarantee anymore, and new, valuable treasures may be just within your grasp.<br><br>In conclusion, excellent writing, deliciously terrifying, does a good job of breaking routine, and is definitely worth the price.
