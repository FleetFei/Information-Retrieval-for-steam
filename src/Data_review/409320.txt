published by Camel 101. The dev team had delved previously into sci-fi with their first Steam project, Mechs &amp; Mercs: Black Talons. That one wasn\u2019t as well received as Syndrome but at least we\u2019re talking about experienced developers.<br> <br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Gameplay<\/div>It\u2019s becoming sort of a trend nowadays in video games, to focus a lot on style over substance. No one can deny that Syndorme doesn\u2019t look the part and yet if you spend a few hours with it, the cracks do emerge along with several other minor issues. But we shall focus on the gameplay for now. I never make a habit out of spoiling any details regarding the stories in the games I review and thus I shall not make an exception even in this case. Suffice to say, it\u2019s wholesomely unoriginal. You got game mechanics and tropes from almost every other space horror title that I can think of. From Dead Space, Doom and all the way to Alien Isolation. Don\u2019t get me wrong. I know it\u2019s rather hard to put a new spin on a tale that\u2019s been copied dozens of times even in the film industry (Event Horizon, comes to mind) but I was expecting at least one original aspect within Syndrome. Something to set it apart, at least in gameplay terms from the titles mentioned above. You do have the option to fight back, which I really appreciated. Survival horror does not does not equate to being a helpless victim. The enemies have enough variety and even the weaponry, on the later levels. <br><br>The one big problem however, the elephant in the room if you wish, has to be the artificial intelligence. Precisely, the lack thereof. For a game which revolves around avoiding threats rather than facing them head-on, Syndrome\u2019s foes are near oblivious to sounds unless you are in close proximity and I found them to be blind as bats, when it comes to sneaking up in front of them and switching the flashlight on and off. I wasn\u2019t expecting the kind of AI you have in Alien Isolation, for example. But when you fight and kill an enemy at a few feet\/metres from another one which never reacts, that experience was just game breaking. And even if it was a pathfinding glitch, it went on a bit too much. The basic weapon of choice, a wrench, was more potent than the various firearms which proved the enemies are bullet sponges. And with ammo being quite scarce, you\u2019re far better off avoiding many encounters and simply sneak by. And backtrack. A lot and quite annoyingly. The game\u2019s map isn\u2019t even that small not to accommodate a more logical liniar succession of objectives. But you still have to travel far too much between the ship\u2019s several decks. Back and forth, rinse and repeat.<br> <br>In truth, Syndrome\u2019s saving grace comes in the form of its beautiful use of Unity assets. Running the game maxed out, you will become more forgiving of its shortcomings and appreciate the atmosphere it creates through the careful use of audio and visual cues, mimicking hallucinations. Witnessing more graphic scenes or being chased by your foes, does trigger some nice effects which I personally found welcoming even if Syndrome has no jump scares. It creates tension nonetheless. It could have been a far better game but even as it stands now, you won\u2019t be disappointed by Syndrome. In that sense, this is arguably closer to a classic survival horror a la early Silent Hill games (i.e. minimal ammo and weak melee weapons) than the more recent, super-stealthy likes of Outlast and Amnesia (though there are still bits where stealth is of the essence). <br><br>   Whether this approach is a good or bad thing is up to the individual: myself, I like both angles on the genre, though I've definitely been favouring the stealthier end of things in recent years (if only due to it being a slightly fresher take on an obviously tired genre of gaming). So think of this game as more of a flawed but evocative trip down Memory Lane than anything terribly &quot;new&quot; or ground-breaking, with just the rare moment of modernity thrown in for good measure.<br><br>   To be frank, this game appeared at a perfect time for Yours Truly, given the heart-breaking news that Outlast 2 WOULDN'T be released in time for this Halloween. I consider this a perfectly acceptable consolation for that delay, and if you're anything like me, chances are you will too. It's a super-solid, suitably immersive first-person horror game. Don't expect another SOMA, and you may well be pleased. But unless you simply can't wait - as I couldn't! - it might not hurt to wait for a decent sale. horror\/sci-fi\/stealth\/combat game is both ambitious and flawed. On balance, there\u2019s very little new or unique here, but there\u2019s also plenty to like.<br><br>What the game unquestionably nails is ATMOSPHERE\u2014especially the lighting (flickering shadows, ink black corners, smoke hanging directly at eye level, epileptic florescent lighting kicking on and off) and the sound design (minimalist synth music, the low hum of engines, hissing of steam pipes, unknown items falling off some faraway shelf and clanking to the metal floor, the jump-scary-loud whooshing of automatic doors opening and closing as you walk past). These elements blend very well to maintain a downright anxious vibe throughout.<br><br>In the big picture, there\u2019s something else equally noteworthy: Syndrome does an admirable job of balancing resources in a way that keeps the player from getting too comfy--while also avoiding frustration. A surplus of ammo or medkits rarely happens; simultaneously though, most encounters are still winnable and death is avoidable (as long as you don't attempt blasting through the maps like an invincible 14-year-old, all of whom deserve to die...oh, did I say that out loud?). Enemies (former crewmembers who have been transformed by an alien artifact while you blissfully slept in a cryo-tube) can (and should) be avoided in some circumstances; however, the game also forces the player into combat on some occasions\u2026and that\u2019s exactly how it should be. And if you\u2019ve blown through all your ammo, you can always hide in a locker or closet (in \u201cAlien: Isolation\u201d style)\u2014a stealth tactic which always worked as it should in \u201cSyndrome\u201d (though, of course, the AI here is not nearly as evolved as in \u201cIsolation\u201d). For me, the game never stalled due to becoming too difficult, but venturing into every new area of the ship was decidedly tense and required caution.<br><br>Even more impressive is Bruno, one of the devs. After I complained on the community hub about not being able to disable the native controller support in the game so I could build my own controller layout with a third-party program (Xpadder), he personally set up several beta versions of the game where he \u201cmangled the code\u201d on purpose to try and turn it off. Ultimately, it didn\u2019t quite work, and I ended up finishing the game with the default layout anyway (which is serviceable, although the button config didn\u2019t make sense to my FPS brain.) Anyway, that kind of personal attention from devs, as you know, can be rare on Steam. I appreciated it greatly.<br><br>Of course, there\u2019s a flip side to all this positivity: Take heed, if you don\u2019t like backtracking in games, player beware! The game consists of eight (or so) floors of the spaceship Valkenburg, and you use an elevator (and then later, ductwork) to traverse them...again and again and again. Your quests take you from floor 1, to floor 5, then to floor 4, then back to 1, then back to 5, then to floor 6, then back to 4\u2026on and on, visiting the same areas many, many times. You get the picture. Each map is large enough to provide lurking dangers and each is generally distinct from the next, but by the time you finish the game, you can practically walk blindfolded through the entire ship\u2014to me, that\u2019s not a good thing. I have to be honest and say that, while it took me over 20 hours to finish the game, so much of it consists of backtracking that the game seems like it really should be half the length. Some serious editing would probably strengthen this title considerably.<br><br>Next, you can play for the story\u2014but that won\u2019t get you too far. Though the voice-overs by the handful of actors is competent, the narrative is shallow and the backstories and personalities of these doomed folks are surface-level only. You won\u2019t get emotionally attached to them or care much about their plight, and there are no thought-provoking twists here, really. If you are familiar with any other survival-games-in-space, you\u2019ll see the ending coming a light-year away. Personally, these weaker elements don\u2019t bother me, as long as the tension in the game is palpable\u2014and in this case, it truly is.<br><br>Last, I\u2019ve come to the decision that believable, smooth animation in games must be one of the most difficult elements to achieve for indie devs. If all you play is AAA games, you probably know you\u2019re spoiled by top-notch, realistic movement of NPCs (usually developed through mo-cap or whatever billion-dollar tech). Indie games don\u2019t have those resources\u2026and in Syndrome, it shows. Unfortunately, that means that every monster who chases or assaults you does so in the most mechanical, stiff way possible\u2014and usually, it\u2019s giggle-worthy. While the game builds admirable tension within the dark environments of the creaking Valkenburg, as soon as the first transformed crewmember appears, that tension deflates like an old birthday balloon. On my first encounter with the mannequin-like monsters, I personally had to grin and just keep on playing. Eventually you shrug off the goofy, budget-strapped animations and deal with the enemies in whatever way necessary, since some of them are pretty darn fast and can kill you with a single hit. Ouch. They may wobble and move like refrigerators on wheels, but they are quite dangerous.<br><br>Anyway, I feel the game is a strong buy (not a must-buy, but a strong-buy, especially on sale) for genre
