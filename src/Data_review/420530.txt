think it's finally time to put in my thoughts on this quant little game. For those of you wanting a brief summary of the game, I won't bore you with the details and summarize right away.<br><br>OneShot is among the best games I've ever played. The gameplay was entertaining and rarely left me confused on where to go, the puzzles were entertaining and didn't overstay their welcome,  the writing was among the best I've even seen, and the story itself left a satisfying conclusion that achieved its purposes wonderfully. If you were a fan of games like Undertale or Yumi Nikki, you'll love OneShot. My biggest complaint is the game's fairly short length, and there are other games that will keep you coming back longer for the same price or less if you're looking for a long-term game to play. However, this game's strengths more than make up for its weaknesses. If you have the money and a couple hours to kill, pick it up. You won't be sorry.<br><br>Alright, now that that extensive summary is over, time to go into a bit more detail for those who want a bit more information.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> History <\/div><br>OneShot was originally an RPGMaker freeware game a few years back. OneShot resurfaced in December 2016 with a full remake on Steam. The free version still exists if you want to try it, but I wouldn't reccomend, especially if you plan on getting this remake. This update reworked practically every area of the game, added plenty of new puzzles and characters, and heavily streamlined the menu system. On March 19th 2017 was the beginning of the Solstice update that expanded the game dramatically by unlocking a certain timer door, though the actual update wasn't rolled out until the 26th due to development issues. This has so far completed the game, though much more minor updates may come in the future.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Gameplay <\/div><br>OneShot has an interesting take on the RPG formula, relying mostly on small puzzles rather than any form of battle system. I'd liken it most to Grim Fandango (or another classic point-and-click adventure game) gameplay in the world of Undertale. Throughout the game, Niko will encounter various items that can be used to progress by combining and\/or using them in specific areas. Most of these are fairly straightforeward, but a few might require a bit of experimentation. Movement and interaction is where most of the RPG elements take place. Each map is a near top-down perspective that Niko can move through like a grid. Items, environmental pieces, and other characters can all be interacted with, leading usually to Niko making a remark or talking with someone. Like most point-and-click games, OneShot is deceptively linear. Each item has only one or two major uses outside of interesting dialogue segments with some characters, and they tend to automatically disappear once they've outlived their usefulness.<br><br>That's most of the gameplay, but it gets a bit crazier from there. I'm not going to spoil anything because these events are best experienced firsthand. That said, OneShot has some interesting usage of the medium in creating puzzles. This will become quickly apparent once you see it.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Graphics <\/div><br>The art for OneShot is all pixel art, but it actually has that &quot;art&quot; aspect. All the environments and character designs are unique and memorable. The art execution is beautiful and really captures the emotions. Just take a look at the pictures above. Not much else to say here.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Music <\/div><br>It's good. Not entirely the most catchy songs like you'd hear in Mario, Sonic, or Undertale, but it doesn't feel like its trying to be. I'd liken the music to something like Portal. Nice songs that really fit the mood and complete the experience, but nothing too incredibly noteworthy. Best part of buying soundtrack is supporting the devs.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Story and Writing <\/div><br>Time for one of my favorite parts of this game. Lets start with a spoiler-free synopsis of the story.<br><br>Niko, a young catlike person, winds up in a mysterious new land and tasked with a mission: deliver the sun to the top of the Tower to save the dying world from a mysterious degeneration. Niko can't do this without your help, so it's your job to help ensure everything goes smoothly.<br><br>Of course, things get <b> much <\/b> more interesting from there. The execution of this is phenominal. Along the way, you and Niko meet new and interesting characters that feel really nicely fleshed out, even when many only appear for a short time. I only wish I had more time to get to know them. Niko actually has some of the best characterization in any form of media I've seen. Niko's childlike wonder, homesickness, joy, fear, sadness\u2014all of them feel like that of a real person, and in many cases complimented my own emotions. It's truly difficult to encapsulate how fantastic OneShot's writing is. You just have to play it for yourself. And no, watching a video won't cut it.<br><br>The Solstace update (which was ironically on the Equinox) added a whole bunch of new content, but it's all spoilers. My main complaint of the short length of the game was fairly well remedied and I left with a satisfying, though somewhat bittersweet conclusion. <br><br>Alula is best girl and if you don't agree you are objectively wrong. I'll also accept Rue I guess.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Conclusion <\/div><br>Wow, have I rambled long enough. I've already said most of what I can think to share. OneShot is a fantastic game and I highly reccomend picking it up. For 10$, there are other games that can keep you entertained for longer, but OneShot provides a wonderfully crafted experience that is well worth the money.
