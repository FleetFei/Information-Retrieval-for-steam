What is it with mankind anyway? It seems like every alien race in the universe wants to see us enslaved, obliterated, or pushed into the mud. Once again you're the lone hero who must push back, and you can't stop until the extraterrestial menace is well and truly wiped out. Shoot 1UP starts with the most basic concept imaginable: You shoot, and you collect 1UPs. Since I grew up on arcade 2D shooters, I'm surprised to see any game where more than two or three extra lives can be attained per play-through. In Shoot 1UP, you get a 1UP every two or three seconds. Wow, not only is it Christmas, but I got the cyborg unicorn that I always wanted.<br><br>Now hang on a second. What's with this generosity? In this game, you don't control one ship, you control up to 30. That's right, all of those extra lives are under your control at the same time. So let's just say you're in charge of a squadron. The triggers on your Xbox 360 controller expand and contract your squadron. When expanded, the ships can cover more ground, and this increases the multiplier. This also results in a wicked sweet laser. When you need to conserve ships, that's when you contract for that extra space. Note that contracting is a concious decision to place survival over score, which means your multiplier drops. Once you get a handle on the controls, it isn't hard to figure out when and where to spread your squadron.<br><br>Now if you want the best of both worlds (survival and scoring) you have to master the shield. By letting go of the fire button, you start building energy for a shield. Hit the fire button again, and all bullets and most enemies are destroyed. Shield-kills result in a HUGE multiplier bonus, so you'll want to go for them whenever possible. Granted, not every situation will allow you to easily shield-kill everything, so you should use your shot\/laser weapons to weaken larger enemies before attempting the finishing blow. Shield-kills are risky because they can result in lost ships due to collisions or stray bullets. When your ships are destroyed, they create small explosions that eliminates nearby bullets and enemies. Unfortunately, this means you don't get a fat multiplier bonus. It's like poetry. <br><br>Each of the six stages revolves around applying what you've just learned. Enemies will come from multiple angles; all with their own tactics. It's up to you to figure out how to destroy everything, get the highest possible score, but with minimal losses. This aspect of the game can lead to some very inventive play. Since you're controlling multiple ships at once, you have to monitor their positions, making slight movements to avoid dangers. It's very important that you don't spend too much time playing defensively. The harder difficulties make it seem like you're supposed to play it safe. However, if you don't take the initiative, everything becomes increasingly claustrophobic. Since you have to destroy enemies to obtain 1ups, it can mean sacrificing one ship, in order to obtain two ships. Above all, don't forget about the shield. It's actually a very powerful weapon, and can make short work of most enemies when properly timed.<br><br>The Score Trek mode takes Shoot 1UP into a different direction. You're given one ship that becomes stronger with each power-up collected. However, since you're limited to a single solitary ship, it's game over if you take a hit. To balance this out, the game saves after the completion of each stage. Once you defeat the last boss, you start again on a harder loop. This continues for I don't know how long. There's an achievement for completing at least five successive loops. The key to scoring in this mode is simple: abuse the shield. With each loop, the shield becomes more effective. Before long, it's faster to destroy bosses with shields than anything else. Conceptually, this is a neat extra, but it's a little too one-dimensional compared to the main game.<br><br>Aside from the long-term appeal found in all shmups, which is playing for the hi-score, Shoot 1UP's original ideas and abstract art-style are what give it staying power. Controlling multiple ships at once is a cool idea, and it's thoroughly explored, as opposed to having an idea but never bothering to capitalize on it. The standard level of difficulty isn't too high. This gives players an incentive to really learn how everything works, so they can maximize their score. Mommy's Best Games always has an eye for choosing colors that I never knew existed. While the environments are repetitive, the creature design is well-done, and the art-direction reminds me something found on the Amiga.
