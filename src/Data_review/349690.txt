Astroport. Wait, really? It's another one of these games... Pshaw. Why can't these guys develop a visual novel about a catgirl witch school or something? Instead, this is yet another tale about giant robots and explosions. The world is doomed, only one pilot can save the day, blah blah blah. When is Astroport going to make a game that speaks to me? I can't relate to mechas, but I understand catgirl witches.<br><br>Seriously though, this is something that I appreciate about Astroport, they make games that play to their strengths. ARMED SEVEN is a 16 minute game. You start it up, choose your weapons, and attempt to save the world. Aside from being easily-digestible, this shooter is very entertaining. <br><br>What I appreciate the most about this game is that it's so accessible. Upon starting the game, you have a choice of weapons. The capabilities of the main gun, secondary, and charge weapon are clearly explained. While you're liable to have better results with certain weapon combinations, the best measure of your survival is being able to dodge bullets. Each of the four difficulties changes things up nicely, with enemies implementing new and more difficult attacks.<br><br>Since this is a 16 minute game, you probably won't fret as much when you lose. There aren't any continues, but the lives and shields are fairly common. However, mastering this game is a very difficult endeavor. Most of the stages in this game only have three power-ups. <br><br>The scoring system also revolves around destroying enemies as quickly as possible. Whenever a large battle-cruiser or exceptionally tough mech appears, you'll want to focus on them to get a larger multiplier bonus. Depending on your weapon set-up, this can lead to a multitude of situations. You might even have to put yourself in a disadvantageous position by leaving weaker enemies alive. However, you can't allow yourself to take damage because of the max-shield bonus. These conundrums, alongside the unique weapon-system, give ARMED SEVEN a lot of replay-value.<br><br>And...well...that's it. Once again, this is a 16 minute game, there isn't much to talk about. ARMED SEVEN is a very good shootemup, and is sure to entertain fans of the genre. about Armed Seven, not least the concept. Armed Seven is a side on, old-school, doujin shoot-em-up, where you play as a mech defending Earth from the alien hordes laying waste, before leading Earth's armies on the counter attack. It's all set to some bombastic Metal Slug esque music, it's a very assured game. <br><br>While Armed Seven isn't a great looking game what's there is well done. The backgrounds remind me of old Mega Drive games like Streets of Rage, all blurred skyscrapers and distant fires. There's touches of flourish happening in the distance, companions being shot down, an upcoming boss wiping out a few city blocks. Said bosses are creative and themed, the way bosses used to be, they split and change, again harking back to the 16bit era without feeling like yet another indie retro game. <br><br>Gameplay wise Armed Seven isn't quite a bullet hell shooter, nor is it particularly gimmick laden, it's something in between. Your hit box is tiny, particularly in comparison to the size of your mech, and there's certainly something approaching a swarm of bullets on screen at any one time, but it's not that frenetic. The gimmicks come on the form of selectable weapons, before the game starts you get to kit out your ship as you see fit (to a point). I never found much to suit me beyond the defaults, but I do like the homing missiles as a special. The reason to switch and try new things is because you can alter your aim, not unlike Zero Gunner. You can tilt, lock yourself in to shooting upwards or downwards, and if you don't want to deal with that pick a build that does it for you. <br><br>While we might not be talking Dodonpachi levels of bullets there's certainly enough that you'll be pleased your hit box is so small. That said I still took hits from bullets I couldn't see, lost in the background, my outgoing fire and exploding war ships. The health system is interesting. There's pickups to increase your weapon damage and shield, lose all your shield and you'll lose a life, which drops your weapon damage down. It means that your first life can last quite a long time, but staying alive after that requires all the concentration you can muster <br><br>On my first at easy mode I completed Armed Seven on a single credit. It\u2019s not insultingly easy, but it\u2019s certainly eminently doable, especially if you play shooters on a regular basis. The step up to normal is certainly more of a challenge, all of a sudden you\u2019ll be put on the back foot, using everything you\u2019ve got to wrestle back control. It\u2019s where my lack of proficiency at the genre makes me doubt things. I can\u2019t go in to any great detail about the scoring because I simply don\u2019t understand the minutia of it, I can\u2019t comprehend it. It makes me wonder about the game\u2019s difficulty too, is the step up in difficulty evidence of a flaw, something not quite right in the high end of the game, something that is nigh on impossible to get right. Or maybe that I hit a wall with the game on the harder settings less about any tightness of control and refinement and more my lack of ability.<br><br>Whatever the answer I do know that I like Armed Seven, Without being overbearing it reminds me of my 16bit childhood in the Sega camp. It\u2019s a fun game, with good ideas, some interesting enemy patterns, and memorable bosses. It lacks something though, a (vague) tightness, something to really mark it out.
