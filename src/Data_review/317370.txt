class=\"postedDate\">Posted: March 21\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Keep could flippantly be described as Legend of Grimrock Lite. It's a first-person, grid-based dungeon crawler with a single character and real-time combat. If you're looking for Might &amp; Magic-style turn-based combat, you have the wrong game. The DNA on this one goes back to Dungeon Master.<br><br>The Keep is a decent length, you could likely finish it in under 10 hours unless you're looking for all the secrets. You can add another hour or two to that by forgetting to hit Quicksave (F5, by the way) and having to replay the whole freaking level over again if you get killed, because there's no autosave. That's not recommended unless you know some really creative swear words. Anyway, the game has a predictable story about crystals corrupting stuff, which would be unremarkable were it not for the scenery-chewing, Shakespearean voice actor reading it out. The in-game voice acting comes from the pizza delivery guy and is less impressive, but fortunately there's not much of it beyond reading signs and the occasional non-interactive exchanges with other characters. The art, which probably looked great on the 3DS, is a bit dated here. There's also not much texture variety between levels, so I hope you like rock walls with minimal decoration. At least it makes secrets easy to spot. Beware, at least two secrets are essential to progression, although most of them involve spotting barely-concealed buttons on walls. Still, I've done my share of searching for the more elusive ones.<br><br>You only play a single character, and although there are RPG elements in terms of leveling up and finding loot, it's pretty basic stuff. The first half of the game is essentially linear, with every new piece of loot obviously replacing something older. Near the end you start to get more difficult choices about which things to keep, depending on how you want to play your character. Realistically though, you are going to end up with a combined fighter \/ mage. Those are the only two options, and there's not much incentive to specialise.<br><br>The combat system is a bit more interesting. Melee involves swiping across a 3x3 grid to attack different zones of your enemy, which are supposed to have varying defences. Every few successful hits you'll get a highlighted combo for bonus damage, this is further influenced by what weapon you're using. The timing takes a bit of getting used to, it feels sluggish at first especially if you naively swipe the enemy instead of using the combat grid, but it works quite well if you don't rush it. The magic system is better; you will find a number of runes and have to arrange them on a grid, casting the spell by dragging through them in the correct order. You find scrolls which reveal spells, although it's possible to guess a few too. At first this is straightforward, but later on as the options stack up you have to put some thought into how you arrange the runes to give yourself easy access to your favourite spells, and near the end you'll have to make difficult choices because there's no optimal arrangement of runes. I quite enjoyed rejiggering my spellbook every so often.<br><br>Make sure you read the manual (right click on the game in your Steam list). The in-game tutorial is pretty basic, and doesn't tell you any of the shortcut keys which are - frankly - pretty much essential. There are no customisation options in-game, but you can set some basic detail levels and set key bindings in the launcher. My favourite key, F, switches between melee and magic combat grids, good to know if you want to survive later combat encounters.<br><br>I have to say I enjoyed it quite a bit, except those times when I lost my progress. Be warned, you only get a single save slot. Do not, for example, mistakenly click on &quot;Restart Level&quot; instead of &quot;Load game&quot;, thinking you can say &quot;Oh deary me, what a clumsy oaf&quot; before hitting &quot;Load game&quot; instead, because you'll find it has gone. Then you won't say &quot;Oh deary me&quot;, trust me. You'll say --CENSORED-- instead, which tends to frighten the dog.<br><br>Overall?
