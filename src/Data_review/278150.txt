While it can do fancy gymnastics over a song like pitch bending it up or down throughout the entire course of a song which i think most people won't do unless they plan on making a derivative work, simply changing pitch from 440 Hz to 432 Hz for example is completely obscure. It doesn't give a reference as to what note you're changing. Instead it does an overall change of pitch in terms of semitones or part of it by allowing you to drag several nodes up or down (i.e. if you want to make more than one pitch change throughout the length of the track) or a single straight line if you want to alter pitch only once for the entire track.<br><br>In other words, you have no idea what note is changed and by how much in terms of frequency. You'll have to do your own calculations if you want to make the right amount of adjustment. For example if you want to change 440 - 432 Hz you need to know that G4 is 392 Hz which is 2 semitones lower i.e 48 Hz difference. So 432 Hz is 8 Hz difference which is -0.166666 (recurring decimal places) less of a tone. <br><br>As mentioned before, in this software, the way you make the adjustment is by moving the line or points on the line. The range you can adjust is between +\/- 2 semitones to between +\/- 24 semitones. The left slider adjust the scale or precision level. At the most precise level (+\/- 2 range) you can only move the line at 0.1 increments so to make a -0.16666 adjustment, the closest value you can make is -0.2 so it's not very accurate; you can't input the precise values you need.<br><br>Based on the above observation and my understanding of how this works, it's not a mathematically intensive software and lacks precision where pitch adjustment is concerned. One thing positive i can say about this function is that it has the option of maintaining the song duration so the song doesn't lengthen or shorten after the pitch change.<br><br>As this is just a partial review, i can only assume that as a whole it does its job quite well.
