The Doom 3 engine is put to great use here; the visuals (mostly) hold up pretty well even by today's standards. Doom 3 was going for relentless claustrophobic dread pretty much the entire game, but this game has a lot more variety in color, tone, and emotion that it's attempting.<br><br>The overall level design philosophy is very close to Quake 2 both in the Strogg architectural motifs as well as just the design of the combat areas and layouts.  Except for some reason the sky isn't orange - maybe they thought that would make it look too much like Doom 3's Mars? The stroggification scene was cool but they didn't really do anything with that concept afterwards and it was spoiled in the trailers.  The story that is there is functional in that it builds the tone and feel they're going for.  This is an area where the game excels.  Fighting alongisde other marines in trenches while explosions go off around you creates a strong, early Call of Duty-esq war feeling to the game, but more Starship Troopers than WW2.  There's also a strong element of body horror as you work your way through the game, see the stroyent creation, the stroggification facilities, see biomechanically crucified marines merged with computers, etc.  The  soundtrack is more cinematic than Quake 2 and less memorable (outside the main theme), but builds this atmosphere better.  Just overall, Quake 4 feels less bland and brown than Quake 2 did at times.<br><br>This game picked up some bad habits of the games in between the old school shooters and the more modern ones; exposition-powered doors, NPC's standing in your way until you let them finish talking, forced turret and vehicle sections with bad looking outdoor areas and clunky controls to &quot;break up the monotony&quot; - there's gameplay variety from strong central mechanics that give the player interesting choices, like Dishonored or Doom 2016, and there's insecurity that your players will get bored so you force them to do some other type of gameplay for a while.  There's weapon upgrades, but unlike Doom 2016 or Rage they're just gated based on your progress through the campaign and given to you at specified times so that feels kind of pointless to not just make the weapons have all the features to start with.  Unlike Doom 3, this game is more straight action, without any real puzzles or environmental interaction, but that means the handful of times the game expects you to do anything but just &quot;shoot the thing until it dies&quot; it's frustrating not because it expects you to do anything hard to figure out, but because you're not in that mindset.  There's some frustration from having a handful of old school big arena fights but now you have to contend with drastically reduced movement speed and reload times and no sprint mechanic.<br><br>Most of the weapons are very satisfying to use, but some of the weapons feel nerfed, like the grenade launcher, the railgun, and the BFG.  Weapon reloading was fine early game, for the assault rifle and shotgun, but when you have 10 weapons it's annoying to constantly cycle through all of them after each fight to make sure they're ready to shoot.  The railgun is already a slow firing weapon so adding a long reload animation after 3 shots was excruciating.  Keeping the reloading for the human weapons, and maybe the hyperblaster would have added to that more realistic war feeling the game was going for without hurting the gameplay for the rest of the large arsenal.<br><br>So there's some flaws and frustrating \/ dated bits, but it holds up well as a game from that last generation of shooters on the evolutionary tree of games like Doom and Duke
