owning as a child... and am proud to have beaten as an adult.<\/b><br><br><br>I was invited to playtest the late pre-release builds and was immediately hooked. Admittedly, I spent more time <i>playing<\/i> than <i>testing<\/i>, but it was often too fun to stay on task. Honestly, the game feels like it was made just for me but I think it can still be appreciated by the young whippersnappers to whom the 4-color limited palettes will seem alien. <br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Pros<\/div><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li>Nostalgia-driven visuals<br><\/li><li>Incredible soundtrack<br><\/li><li>Refreshing, engaging game mechanics<br><\/li><li>Poignant satire with still-relevant lessons<br><\/li><li>Varied stages<br><\/li><li>Charming character &amp; enemy designs<br><\/li><li>Clean, rhythmic controls<br><\/li><li>Passionate creators<br><\/li><li>Difficulty controlled by player<br><\/li><li>Countless pop culture references &amp; video game homages<\/li><\/ul><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Cons<\/div><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li>Nostalgia-driven visuals<br><\/li><li>Potentially slow progression<br><\/li><li>Inconsistent enemy density<br><\/li><li>Repetitive cinematics<\/li><\/ul><br>There will be many first impression reviews here, so instead I'll provide an in-depth review of the game's core mechanics. After beating the story a couple dozen times, earning all of the achievements (including Naked Raygun several times--and even once without backlight or arcshot) and sinking countless late nights, early mornings, and lunch breaks into the Time Attack mode, I can say only this: <b>I would do it all again<\/b> &amp; here's why: <br><br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Energy System<\/div>I love the tactical design of Rad's energy mechanic because it forces me to be a better player. Limited resources are often unsuccessful in platformers because they're either too punishing or just overlooked in normal gameplay. Rad's energy system is fully integrated and it powers everything from double jumps to weapon blasts. Energy will only begin to regenerate once a specific amount of time has passed since the last used ability (there is a chart in the room above Dr. Y in Cape Canaveral which details the exact energy requirements and recharge rates per battery). <br><br><i>Why is limited energy a good thing?<\/i> Because it means you need to be aware of the abilities you're using. <br><i>But why is THAT a good thing?<\/i> Because it means you're not flying through the game on autopilot: you need to be accurate with your attacks and precise with your movements. There are very few instant-death pitfalls and there are zero situations with unavoidable damage. Every boss can be defeated without receiving damage, every stage beaten without so much as chipping Rad's plastic... the challenge isn't always from risk of dying--as is the case in many platformers--the true challenge of Super Rad Raygun is in managing your energy. If you know a boss is about to lunge at you then you'll need to consciously stop attacking to reserve enough energy to double jump or dash around them. <br><br>More power isn't always the best solution. Higher stages of weapons require exponentially more energy per use, and there are many situations where it's better to have continuous damage instead of burst damage. A level 6 arcshot deals 45 damage and requires 100% of your energy while a level 4 arcshot deals 22 damage but requires just 36% energy. You won't receive any bonus damage for releasing an arcshot below its energy requirement: at level 6, even if you're at 99% energy, the attack will essentially fizzle and deal 2 damage but will still drain your entire energy reserve. If you plan to hit a boss with a devastating arcshot then you need to give our titular hero enough time to charge. <br><br>Instead, you could step down to a lower weapon tier to attack more frequently and with more flexibility, but with substantially lower damage per hit. This strategy has its own drawback: You can only ever have one arcshot on-screen at a time, so you should make it count. Similarly, you can only have three blaster projectiles on screen at a time. These limitations are by intentional design and are accurate to the era of gaming which has inspired Super Rad Raygun and they heavily contribute to the combat system's balance. You could keep your weapons at level zero and still beat the game (trust me)... but you can be much more efficient by playing around with the energy mechanics to find the perfect amount of energy you can expend before backing yourself into a corner. <br><br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Battery Allocation<\/div>Inseparable from Rad's energy system is the player's ability to freely change Rad's active upgrades. While it is possible to acquire every spare battery without replaying a single stage, it's much more likely that you'll only discover a handful on your first play through the story. Some are quite well hidden. The brightside is that you don't need all of them... or even most of them! At any time you can open Rad's powerup menu and shift around your batteries to suit the current situation. <br><br>Doing some recreational swimming? You won't need any Agility batteries.<br>Frolicking in the sunlight? No need for Backlighting!<br>Cosplaying a pin cushion? Move batteries from Max Health to Protective Case.<br>Not in the mood for boss hugs? Increase your Agility and air backdash to safety. <br><br>There's no downside to moving your batteries around--the menu even pauses the rest of the game. Some players may find this procedure a little tedious, but a little exploration is the perfect remedy. Stages are marked with an X once all of their batteries have been found, otherwise they're a blank circle. You're free to go through previous stages to claim undiscovered batteries. This is also a fantastic way to earn bits for upgrades because the batteries that you have already found are replaced by bit crates. Washington DC is especially lucrative for so-called <i>farming<\/i> because it has two normal crates, three battery-replacing crates, and a boss for at least 1400 bits for each full clear. <br><br>You can greatly improve Rad's potential with a little farming at any point in the story. Nothing is stopping you from purchasing all of the upgrades before even moving on to NYC. Sure, you won't have enough batteries to actually fill the slots until much later, but you could shift specializations on a whim! This would create a relatively tame, relaxed atmosphere while you cruise through the rest of the game. Other players may find more satisfaction in depriving themselves of upgrades as an additional challenge. There's an achievement called <i>Naked Raygun<\/i> which involves completely abstaining from the purchase of Dr. Y's upgrades and it's actually very fun (and hard). It completely changes your pacing and tactics and provides the brutal, unforgiving experience that is secretly craved by us closet masochists. <br><br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Endgame<\/div>There are a lot of fancy maneuvers that are only possible once Rad has been fully upgraded, and luckily TRU FUN has developed an outlet to explore your full capabilities. Playing a stage a second time allows you to activate a special mode in which you are timed through the entire stage. After finishing the stage, your final time is sent to a leaderboard and you can see how you stack up to everyone else. <span class=\"bb_spoiler\"><span>There is even a timed Boss Rush for the truly radical. You get one life and a preset battery loadout so it's fair to everyone. It's a really great way to learn the boss patterns to improve your campaign Time Attack scores.<\/span><\/span><br><br>There is very little &quot;luck&quot; or &quot;RNG&quot; involved in this game. Enemies appear in fixed locations and behave in predictable patterns. Bosses, too, behave in patterns and can be manipulated with careful, deliberate movements. Aside from a few minor bugs, there's little to catch an experienced player off guard. If you get hit or die it's almost always because you messed up; not because the game is unfair. <br><br><br><br><b>I cannot recommend this game enough. <br>If you enjoy retro games, 80s culture, political satire, or just platformers in general then there's no question: Super Rad Raygun was made for you.<\/b>\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"gradient\"><\/div>\r\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div
