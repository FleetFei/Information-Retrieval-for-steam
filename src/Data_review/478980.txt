only person on the planet to have been very excited by the IDEA of the first edition of Mansions of Madness, but a little disappointed by the results. I'm glad to say that the app-powered second edition finally delivers the goods, and despite my initial misgivings about the idea of a board game NEEDING an app in the first place, I'm happy to say it's the digital enhancement in question which finally makes this highly ambitious idea &quot;work&quot;.<br><br>   Yes, that's right, I did say &quot;board game&quot;. Not &quot;board game adaptation&quot;...board game! So if you're not willing to: a. move miniatures around a bunch of cardboard tiles, and b. spend quite the bucketload of cash in order to attain said board game elements, you may want to skip this review and Store page altogether.<br><br>   Oh, and did I say &quot;bucketload of cash&quot;? Now, now, I know that's anathema to Steam customers worldwide, since you're such a bunch of whining pansies who post no end of negative reviews reading &quot;I spent five dollars to get this game on sale, and only got ten hours worth of gameplay out of it. RIPPED OFF. \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 YOU, GABEN.&quot; Yeah, sorry, but you people can just kindly \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 off at this point. Modern board games are for people with MONEY. Or at least a willingness to spend some money on entertainment that perhaps offers quality over quantity (me, I'm mostly a pauper, but I'm still willing to shell out upon occasion).<br><br>   Not that this game doesn't offer potentially tens of hours of moderately varied entertainment - possibly more, once they  start releasing more expansions and whatnot - but you probably won't be getting thousands of hours of amusement out of your hundred-dollar-plus investment. To me, that kind of math is more than acceptable; if you're not that kind of person, then it's back to your Left 4 Dead 2 or Call of Duty, and no awesome tabletop fetishism for you. Some of us still enjoy a bit of TANGIBLE materialism every once in a while, evil capitalist pigs that we are.<br><br>   This does bring us to the inevitable question, though: why, in Cthulhu's name, do we need a video game app in order to play a board game? Why would anyone think it's a good idea for these two rather disparate mediums to meet? Well, I was pretty skeptical too, but I gotta say, after this - my first foray into such territory - I'm mostly sold. MOSTLY. Erm...let me explain some more...<br><br>   Yes, this could have been done purely-and-utterly as a video game. As far as I can deduce, there's no good reason why that couldn't have happened. EXCEPT: Fantasy Flight Games are a board game company, this is a second edition\/complete rethinking of an earlier board game of theirs, and besides all this, sometimes tiles and miniatures and cards and dice are JUST \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665ING COOL. Simple as that.<br><br>   The first Mansions of Madness tried hard to be the closest thing possible to the classic tabletop role-playing game The Call of Cthulhu transmogrified into somewhat simplified &quot;board game&quot; form. It didn't quite succeed, not least of all because it required one of the players to be the Keeper (also known in other tabletop RPGs as the Dungeon Master or Game Master).<br><br>   This was its first mistake: a competitive board game, in which it was basically God versus the human investigators, and more often than not, God had the upper hand (what a \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665ing shocker, eh?)! I mean, it was an interesting experiment, trying to transfer such traditional RPG-ish elements into a board game, but as a competitive game it didn't fully work because the investigators and the Keeper weren't really playing the same game with the same rules. It wasn't a level playing field, in other words, and while the designers tried their damndest to make it one, it was all a bit &quot;square peg in round hole&quot;-ish by the end of the day.<br><br>   Enter the app.<br><br>   In this new version, the app plays the Keeper, leaving all the players (or just yourself, if you wanna Nigel it up and play alone) free to be the investigators and get on with, well, investigating! And this is where Mansions of Madness 2nd Edition succeeds admirably in being perhaps the best compromise between the worlds of board gaming and tabletop RPG-ing that I, at least, am thus far aware of (though I'll obviously have to check out Descent: Road to Legend in good time). The amount of &quot;agency&quot; granted to the players in this game is quite impressive, and there is a genuine feeling of &quot;adventure&quot; as you explore spooky mansions and other mysterious locales. It's certainly the closest you're liable to get to a single-player tabletop role-playing game this side of your wildest, wackiest dreams.<br><br>   Sure, you don't get to do crazy \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 like &quot;I'm going to amass my own army, butcher innocents, and crown myself ruler of the whole kingdom&quot;, but then this wouldn't be a board game any more; it'd be a fully-fledged RPG. Given this game's somewhat more narrow, yet still admirably high ambitions, I think they've done a sterling job. I'm still not quite sure how far this app-driven board game thing can be taken - nor am I sure how far it SHOULD be taken - but I can safely opine that it works quite a treat in this instance. The gaming world truly is our communal oyster, and I for one am keen to see what bold new treasures await us in the future. To the cosmos and beyond!<br><br>Verdict: 9\/10.<br><br>(PS Love the sometimes shamelessly Resident Evil-style music!
