free<\/div>\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWadjet Eye Games asked me to provide feedback about Resonance late in its development process -- because our game Primordia was being published by WEG at the same time.  Ultimately, a game-blocking bug prevented me from finishing the game, and I haven't had the time or inclination to return to it.  So there are at least three reasons to take my review with a grain of salt: (1) I received the game for free; (2) I have a professional relationship with the publisher; and (3) I didn't finish the game.<br><br>For reasons not clear to me, Resonance went from being a game that got a lot of attention -- much more than Primordia -- to a game that has basically fallen off the map.  That is unfortunate.  In my opinion, it is the only WEG title to seriously innovate when it comes to gameplay, specifically through the &quot;short-term memory&quot; dialogue mechanic.  STM operates as a kind of dialogue inventory, allowing you to &quot;take&quot; any hotspot in the game (up to five at a time) and use it as a topic of conversation with any NPC.<br><br>This innovation was meant to solve a recurrent design challenge in adventure games (and, actually, RPGs), in which the dialogue options sometimes mistakenly presuppose that the player has noticed something (often a potential puzzle avenue) and realized that the NPC can help with it.  For example, the game might give the player the option of asking a maintenance man to help with a leaking faucet, but the player may not have made the (fairly obvious) connection between the maintenance man and the maintenance problem, such that the dialogue option solves the puzzle prematurely.  By requiring the player to &quot;take&quot; the leak into his STM and then &quot;give&quot; the leak memory to the maintenance man, the game ensures that it never gets ahead of the player.<br><br>Ultimately, this innovation is great in theory but basically frustrating in execution -- the game ends up BEHIND the player many times, requiring tedious backtracking to gather the necessary and obvious memory &quot;items&quot; to advance conversations.  When this is coupled with having to individually control four characters and pass items among their inventories, the bread-and-butter aspects of adventure games (moving, collecting and using items, and talking) become extremely tedious. Ironically, the game's more elaborate puzzles are usually extremely well designed, such that the basic actions are grindingly slow but the complex ones are elegantly designed.<br><br>Despite these drawbacks, I think Resonance's innovation makes it required playing as an example of a way in which &quot;retro&quot; adventure games are in fact advancing the art.  And the game has many other nice qualities, including the use of color and sprite design in the graphics, the high degree of polish, and a few genuinely clever puzzles. <br><br>I think the game would've been much stronger if the management of memory were thematic -- as best I can tell, this major gameplay mechanic is almost entirely detached from the game's narrative, and in fact the scrupulous effort to make sure the character and player have identical knowledge is contrary to a major plot twist.  As it stands, the game's interface tells a story that is unrelated to the game's actual story: that people are oblivious to almost everything going on around them even when those things are unusual and important and that a small group of people is incapable of moving in tandem or cooperating in very basic tasks.  As best I could tell, the story was about science and evil conspiracies, and perhaps even about how people are very good at remembering things and working together.
