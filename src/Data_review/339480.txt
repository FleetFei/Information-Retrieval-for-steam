<i>Payday 2<\/i> starts off strong with two excellent maps, a new contractor, a weapon, masks and other such criminal paraphernalia in the new DLC release, <i>The Bomb Heists<\/i>. What struck me most about <i>The Bomb Heists<\/i> is its excellence in map layout. Unlike the previous DLC, <i>The Diamond Heist<\/i>, which featured a buggy and rather lacklustre stealth mission until long after release, Lion Game Lion's contribution to the ever-growing gallery of <i>Payday 2<\/i> DLC releases manages to surpass a large number of missions that have been released by OVERKILL and Starbreeze Studio.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">The Levels: &quot;The Forest Heist&quot;<\/div><br>Instead of merely one new map where usually the only fun and enticing option is to go loud (good luck convincing your team mates to suffer through stealth with you just because it tickles your lonely pickle), this new DLC offers two maps that are both very entertaining and rewarding. Despite being far from unique in terms of its location and setting--i.e. the forest from &quot;The White Xmas Heist&quot; and the train-themed objective with the tedious and punishing &quot;The Train Heist&quot;--Lion Game Lion manages to avoid the pitfalls of its predecessors and offer a rich and varied map that is genuinely enjoyable to play through. If this is OVERKILL apologising for the migraine-inducing chore that is &quot;The Train Heist&quot; and offering us an enjoyable train-themed heist, then it certainly was a success.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">The Levels: &quot;The Dockyards Heist&quot;<\/div><br>Then there is &quot;The Dockyards Heist&quot;, a heist where you can either sneak your way through or go loud and proud. Uncovering the minutiae of a map, memorising guard patrols and wandering civilians, discovering safe spots, calculating openings in spaces throughout the map and, finally, combining all these variables into an almost fail-proof system that was born from a myriad of failures was a process of devising theory and practice in gameplay that I thoroughly enjoyed in this game. Sadly, the many flaws and issues with <i>Payday 2<\/i>'s stealth system became more and more apparent each day and ultimately caught up with any enjoyment that might be garnered from this style of play. The stationary guard that blocks your progress to the camera guard in &quot;Shadow Raid&quot; or the immobile guard that obstructs all access to the vault in &quot;Framing Frame&quot;, to name a few scenarios, simply ruined what fun there was left in the game. Then there are other issues such as guards spotting you through walls, guards with eyes in their backs, guards or bank managers that walk through walls, etc. It didn't necessarily make stealth impossible, but it simply wasn't fun any more and simply made it downright unfair at points.<br><br>Lion Game Lion, however, managed to revitalise a stagnant and inefficient stealth system through the level design of &quot;The Dockyards Heist.&quot; Throughout my many playthroughs I have only once encountered a stationary guard who was camping at a particularly inconvenient spot (the path to the berthed ship). Similarly, the issue of guards with the X-ray vision that spot you through walls, guards that momentarily transcend the physical plane and walk straight to you through solid matter, guards that have achieved a 360-degree field of vision, all of these were absent from the game due to tighter guard patrols and other clever choices in level design that tackles the inherent issues with <i>Payday 2<\/i>'s stealth system. Moreover, the new addition of two camera guards instead of one was certainly a nice touch.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">The Rest<\/div><br>The achievements highlight the new implementations offered in <i>The Bomb Heists<\/i> DLC and the masks are pretty damn cool (personal favourite being the Tech Lion mask).
