wouldn't look out of place in <i>Final Fantasy 4<\/i>'s Dwarven Kingdom. I mean, just look at that magnificent beard. Anyway, you've been summoned by Odin to rid Asgard of Loki's minions. The story is a standard Norse tale, but the final boss being determined by choices made at pivotal moments in the plot is a nice touch. Oh what am I saying? Not even one paragraph into the review and I'm already talking about the end. So let's step back for just a minute.<br><br>Your progress in <i>Trial by Viking<\/i> is determined not just by being able to reach the end of the 100+ levels. You also have to find sun-crystals. These shiny gems are used to purchase abilities, tools, and weapon-upgrades. This is one of the interesting aspects of this game. Eventually you'll need certain items in order to progress, but there's nothing stopping you from getting them early, in order to make certain levels easier. However, you also have to be considerate of the fact that there are only so many crystals in each level. You can't expect to repeat levels in order to farm them. <br><br>Before I start discussing the controls, I want to briefly touch on Super Metroid. As fantastic as this game is, its controls could be considered &quot;floaty&quot; by some people. The jump physics, while extremely flexible, might even frustrate a handful of gamers. I think that this is a unique quality, and the identity it helps create is part of the reason why Super Metroid is so revered. <i>Trial of Viking<\/i> has controls that are quite different as well, which also gives it an identity of its own. Now if you're thinking that I might be giving this game a little too much credit, by comparing it to a genre-stalwart, then you're probably right. <br><br>The Viking in this game moves in a very loose manner. Moving around will take time to get used to, and for some players, the pure-platforming levels are going to be especially difficult. However, this can work to your advantage. If you get some momentum going, you can quickly move past a lot of danger. Also, unlike some other games, taking damage typically won't knock you into the nearest pit. You'll get pushed around, but you have ample time to recover. You might even purposely get hurt just to give yourself a little boost. Oh and even though there are a wealth of spikes in most levels, they don't cause instant-death.<br><br>All that said, if you're playing with an Xbox 360 controller, I highly recommend that you change the dead-zone settings.<br><br>For the most part, the level-design in this game is great. Each area is well-paced, and offers a good mixture of puzzles, traps, and monsters to contend with. The tools are all very effective, and it's not too much trouble to switch between mid-level. You can expect to use bombs to destroy walls, daggers to hit far-away switches, and so on. The one trap I don't like is the dark hole. These appear very late in the game, and you're liable to struggle for awhile until you figure it out. It's odd to learn a new mechanic far into the game, especially since it only makes a few appearances. Aside from those annoying holes, I don't have any complaints about the level-design. <br><br>Unfortunately, the melee combat isn't particularly good. There are numerous ranged options, including throwing your axe, that help mitigate the problem. Until you get used to how the game operates, you'll clumsily bump into the monsters. Basically, there's not a lot of finesse to fighting. For most of the bosses, you'll learn patterns and figure out strategies. Still, that doesn't mean you won't run into a few bruisers that fall to relentless attack-button mashing. Anothing thing to keep in mind is that your survival rate is liable to go up, if you ignore certain monsters. A key aspect to keep in mind is that there aren't any ambushes. Monsters won't appear out of thin air nor will they respawn, unless they're summoned by others. It might be worth clearing the area of danger, so you can explore at your leisure. <br><br>Altogether, <i>Trial by Viking<\/i> is not only a pretty good game; it also offers a fresh spin on the &quot;Metroidvania&quot; genre. You play as a viking who looks like a cousin to Erik the Swift from The Lost Vikings (1992), taking on the jotun Loki and his minions across a set of nine realms, created by Loki and seeming quite unlike the original ones.<br> <br> <br>Story<br><br>Loki has, we learn, destroyed the sunstone, and scattered it across his new realms, and our fearless viking is chosen by the Alf\u00f6\u00f0r (aka Odin) to collect these and restore order.<br>Oddly enough, you can use these sun-stones to trade for upgraded equipment; You were meant to get them for Odin, but end up bartering them away for faster shoes, better axes, and grappling hooks. Whether this is in trade with Odin, or if you\u2019re handing them off to a 3rd party despite your initial deal with the Allfather, is left unsaid.<br> <br> <br>The game does manage several nods towards various elements of Norse Mythology, though with a few liberties, that may or may not irk some people. An early example in the game has N\u00ed\u00f0h\u00f6ggr the dragon portrayed as a winged, fire-spewing lizard, when dragons in the Eddas are closer to ground-based, or subterranean, serpents with poison or acid, though one reference does state that N\u00ed\u00f0h\u00f6ggr can fly.<br>The wolf Fenrir is naturally also included in the game, being it is a son of Loki, but visually it seems closer to later revisions of the Greek hell-hound of Cerberus with its 3 heads.<br>Of course deviations from old mythology is nothing to get too worked up about, or I\u2019d be busy writing about Marvel\u2019s \u201cinterpretations\u201d.<br> <br> <br>Controls<br><br>Controls are separated into their own section here as they can be make or break in a game like this and in this one they feel a bit slippery and awkward.<br>The first thing you\u2019ll notice is it uses a slightly odd combination of WASD and arrow-keys, but you can remap these easily to e.g. a controller. Yes, I recommend you find an old controller and use that instead of keyboard, even if you\u2019re used to the latter.<br>The initial introduction level seems simple enough, and controlling the main character seems easy, but soon it feels as if some ice has been added to the floors, and you skid a little bit after jumps, making landing on ledges slightly very frustrating.<br>There is also a spirit-rolling-maneuver, which is initiated by pushing forward twice \u2013 this is an ingenious setup, as it means you\u2019ll drop off edges if you try inching forward step-by-step.<br>Puzzles<br>The game presents all the usual puzzles and mechanics (rotating big gears and stacking boxes), and a few novel ones (Polar bear!), and while they are usually trivial enough, there are cases where the controls seemingly freak out and the box you were pushing starts flying through the air.<br>Some of the puzzles seem designed to be revisited once you have bought the required gear, while others are very in-your-face about how to solve them immediately.<br> <br> <br>There are 130 levels in the game, across 13 areas and with 27 boss battles, so some repetition should be expected, but the game still manages keep things varied through most of this. Level design is generally good, but there are places bound to frustrate you simply because the ways it can be failed.<br> <br> <br>Overall<br><br>Trail By Viking is definitely a little gem, with a decent amount of playtime and value for money at the usual 15 Euro, especially if you\u2019re into single-player 2D platformers and puzzle games. The save-game mechanics let you stop and restart on any level already visited, so even taking a longer break is not a problem; just go back a few levels to get into the groove again.<br>The graphics are clear, though music is a bit underdeveloped. Sound effects are simple and to the point, but the game could perhaps do with some Voice Over for the story-parts.<br> <br>Bammsters\u2019 Rating: Design 7, Challenge 8, Fun 7, Replay 7, Price 8, for an overall rating of 7.4 out of 10.
