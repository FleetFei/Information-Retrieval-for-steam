2017<br><b>Type:<\/b>  Single-player<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">General Impression<\/div>What we have here is another clear example of an indie dev who\u2019s sticking to the formula he already tried and tested. It\u2019s the second 2D game in their case and I\u2019d say that those 4 years of development hiatus between Sweezy Gunner and Geo have been time well spent. Luckly instead of another arcade shooter (of which Steam most certainly has its fair share already), in Geo we have a very interesting hybrid of multiple genres. It features elements from economic sims along with a sprinkle of casual arcades thrown in for good measure.<br><br>A rare case in which the actual screenshots don\u2019t do a game justice. We\u2019re more used to \u201cdoctored\u201d footage and images on the Steam Store pages, which trick potential buyers nearly as efficient as those juicy burger ads which thoroughly disappoint, after you\u2019ve unwrapped your next meal. Geo may not look any better or worse than the screenshots but its hidden complexity, surely cannot be described in words or pictures. Don\u2019t let the drilling part with all those different tile shades convince you that the game is reduced to that singular and repetitive activity. Managing your interstellar drilling company is the better half of the game and that is most fortunate.<br><br>At a brief and superficial first look, I honestly thought that I\u2019m being handed over for review another lazy clone of Candy Crush or Zuma (I\u2019m far from being a fan of casual games in general) but I couldn\u2019t have been more wrong. You don\u2019t even start the actual drilling bit until you\u2019ve set up shop, with a shiny and initially small HQ plus a couple of crazily cheap for hire (but mostly, just crazy) employees. The headquarters serve as both a base of operations and a research facility which will allow future upgrades to be implemented and thus, making your business ever more profitable. You will continuously expand both the building and the skilled team of workers under your command.  A balance between income and expenses serves as a natural restraint from becoming \u201coverpowered\u201d too fast. At least in the early stages of the game, grinding (or drilling, if you may) isn\u2019t much of an issue.<br><br>You do have to keep an eye out for mining opportunities since that \u201cO\u201d from G.E.O. does stand for \u201cObserve\u201d. \u201cGather\u201d and \u201cExplore\u201d do require a keen eye as well, not just quick reflexes and intuition. I\u2019m also glad that there aren\u2019t many obstacles to steer you from your path. The randomly generated exploration part most certainly guarantees a new perspective every time you play. No time limit either but your drill does have hitpoints which simulate the wear and tear level and the inevitable damage the machines sustain when piercing the many types of soil and materials found underground. Other than that, gameplay is pretty straightforward: aim for the trasure chests filled with goodies (coins and upgrade points). Luck will only get you so far. Familiarity with the aforementioned soil types will make the job easier and the shortest path towards the buried treasure may not also be ideal, if your drill explodes from too many pierced tiles made of metal. The main objective of any mining location, is to reach the core and collect its essential resources which ultimately fuel your business and subsequent HQ improvements via Geo\u2019s upgrade tree, which is referred to as the \u201cCoretrium\u201d.<br><br>The graphics while not impressive by a long shot, are still pleasant enough but the sounds are a tad too repetitive for my tastes. The presence of a proper settings tab (along with options for resolution or effects) would be a welcome feature in a future update. Also a price drop won\u2019t seem out of place. aren't that many games like it that I'm aware of, and it's quite addictive after you get into it and learn the game (which can take a while). There's also quite a lot of content, I'm 17 hours in and feel like I'm barely half-way through the game. The music is minimalist but pretty nice.<br><br>On the other hand, the game does suffer from a clunky and often non-intuitive GUI, a lack of notifications where needed, an unnecessarily wordy tutorial, and often poor English with an amazing number of typos. It also has fairly primitive graphics (not just in terms of being 2D and simple, but also in terms of poor color harmonies). There are no hotkeys -- none. Not even escape. Everything is done with the mouse, which can get very tedious, particularly when you are exploring a large map and clicking a square, clicking 'explore' (half the screen away), then going back and clicking the next square, then going back and clicking 'explore' again, repeating 400 times, when it could easily have just had a hotkey for explore ('E' or something) and been ten times as fast. Speaking of speed, the speed at which the text scrolls in is glacial; maybe I'm just a fast reader, but the tutorial text always felt like it was appearing at least half as slow as I wanted to read it. There are also almost no mouseovers, which is annoying when you want to know what type of collectable or what type of tile you are hovering over. Instead you need to memorize what it looks like, go to HQ, and then look it up in a big list. Similarly it'd be nice if there were an easy way to get a list of the levels that you've been in that have a particular tile or collectable, it'd be useful for finishing collections for medals or research. The whole GUI just screams 'argh!@#$%' to me whenever I have to interact with it.<br><br>To me those downsides did actually distract from the experience quite a bit, it's hard to get into a game where every other sentence contains a typo (I've been messaging the developer about these typos and eventually many of them may be fixed, but it really needs a professional editor; thankfully most of the text is flavor text and not essential to understand, and the game has no real story and typos aren't quite as bad if it's just flavor text rather than dialogue or story text). But it's still addictive enough that despite those problems I'm probably going to get to the end game.<br><br>Though speaking of getting futher into the game, it does feel like the game eventually gets more and more repetitive. For instance, I was able to notice patterns of block and crate placement, it seems to use not a truely randomized level design but something more akin to Spelunky HD's level system where large blocks of content are randomly mixed together. And while that made sense in that game, it makes less sense here because you can dig, so there's no reason that I can see to have the same exact configurations of enemies and crates over and over again (e.g. the big blowfish with the left\/right create right above it, or the enemy that turns tiles purple and dangerous is almost always next to a grenade create 1 tile to the right and above it; maybe most people won't notice these patterns, but to me they were distracting and took away from the immersion, because I could sort of see the algorithms by which the levels were put together).<br><br>What I'd really like to see is a sequel to this game which fixes all the GUI and other problems I mentioned, upgrades the graphics, and adds a greater variety of enemies, crates, maybe gives the drill some more options to fight off enemies, etc. -- overall this sort of felt like an amazing but flawed beta for a game rather than a finished game, I think a few more months testing and ironing out the rough spots would have done wonders (and I know it's difficult to find playtesters prior to release, but if all else fails they can be hired on Fiverr, I have a Fiverr
