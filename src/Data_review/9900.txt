universe, much like the original Star Wars online game experience before it got ruined by patches and so on. A true sandbox for Star Trek-ness. However, it was taken over by another developer, one known for making comic-like games and generally actionning and arcade-ing things up to the max. My heart sank, because my favourite Star Trek games before then were games that simulated life on board your starship of choice.<br><br>Bridge Commander for PC was one of those games, I'd go back and download all the old Star Trek games now and then and play them just to see if any were good, and some were excellent. Another was the Interactive Technical Manual\/Captain's Chair set of games, which made it so that you could walk through little 360-degree photograph simulations of the ships and learn all sorts of things about everyone. Also one game that gets little to no cred is the Elite Force expansion for PC also, which took the Star Trek Voyager game and made it so that you could explore the ship, mess with the systems, play on the holodecks, and so forth. It took a good 3 hours to push every switch, which isn't long, but it's a good slice of life if you want to experience a walking simulator about ST:VOY. Also the new VR game looks to have some of that spirit - maybe.<br><br>Star Trek Online is the antithesis to all of that. There's no classic roaming about and taking in the scenery, no smelling the roses, and for some reason the corridors are ridiculously huge - all the bridges and so on are blown up to the size of small soccer fields, even the one for the shuttlecraft looks like a basketball court you could do laps in. It's inulting to me as a veteran gamer, since I'm stuck wondering - &quot;what, are they expecting so many casual players who have never tried to play an online game before, that they had to make it so they wouldn't be frustrated walking into walls all the time? is it literally made for mentally challenged people?!&quot; I've never seen a game put so much freaking walking space everywhere. The transporter pads which are supposed to be somewhat uncomfortable to stand on with 5-6 people are suddenly these huge affairs the size of a moderately-priced living room. O_o; smh<br><br>No, you could say that STO tries to bring back the feeling of Original Series Star Trek, with Kirk facing a new escapade each episode and generally the ships and life and so forth falling into the background. I used to despise the Original Series for being too fast and loose with ... well, logic itself, frankly. Sometimes, most of the time in fact, the things the crew encounters and their reactions to it make a lot of sense. But now and then there was just no sense to what they were doing at all, and what my mom calls &quot;60s values&quot; were everywhere - people getting angry for absolutely no reason, being &quot;chivalrous&quot; (almost mocking) towards female crew members, and so on. I've recently watched TOS in its completeness and I have a much greater appreciation for it now, I found the fun in it as it were. But even Roddenberry seemed to back off from the feeling of the original, considering the way that the early Trek movies became almost pedantic affairs about Shakespeare and philosophy and politics. I like those best I think, out of all the Trek stuff that's been created, but this game seems to mostly if not entirely abandon those principles in favor of BANG! BANG! Space cowboys to the rescue!<br><br>The thing is, it works. The Next Generation, Deep Space Nine, and Voyager all seemed to want to be saying something more fun and action-oriented than they actually did, esepcially in the episodes involving scary new species or little special events featuring warzones. It's like there was this anxious little boy hidden away inside the hearts of the producers, and every now and then he'd come out and for several episodes we'd just have a general romp around the galaxy, with torpedoes blasting around and so on. That's where Star Trek Online shines. It takes the time period from those series and tries to make it work as a series of TV-style episodes, which are crystallized into about a dozen quest chains (yes, that's all there is in the game for my faction, but they are relatively long - several hours at minimum each, more like 12-20 hours for some). Want to see Denise Crosby finally getting some serious attention in any Trek franchise? Well, here her performance as Empress Sela is charming and impeccable.<br><br>The difference between STO and the actual TV shows is that while some of the best story arcs in the entire Star Trek universe - all the 29th century nonsense, the Year of Hell characters, the DS9 take on the Mirror Universe, and so on - were mostly ignored after their individual episodes and two-parters, the creators of Star Trek Online took the most interesting and audacious ideas and expanded them into significant things which affect everyone on a daily basis almost. More fun, more action, more romping around, more crazy conspiracies and interesting intrigue than the shows were usually willing to do. If the original shows were vanilla-flavored, this is artisinal chocolate with jalapeno sauce. The difference between fan theories and the actual material, especially when it comes to Star Trek fans, is that the fans come up with intellectual extensions that make it cool for nerds but are impossible to justify when you're marketing to a general audience who are just expecting to turn on their television sets and get a decent story. This does the fun nerdy &quot;footnotes section at the Memory Alpha wiki&quot; stuff that hopefully you've wanted to see (if you're a Trekkie).<br><br>I tend to love and play (and review!) games which are kinda poopy when taken as a chance to fulfill one's hopes and dreams about what a person can do in a virtual world, but are immensely enjoyable when played on their own terms. This is a game which is best played on its own terms. It tries to take the pre-reboot storyline and tie it together with almost everything that's ever been created in Star Trek.. happening all at once, in a coherent storyline. For the most part, the writers (who are these gods?) manage to tie a whole bunch of ridiculously contrived continuities from the creators into something much more coherent than it originally was. It might slip past you if you don't care about the plot or aren't paying enough attention, but seriously, this game turns BAD (with a capital B!) episodes from the TV shows into something that makes sense and can actually form a long story that ties in with others. Play it for the episodes and very little else, and you'll find a fanfiction-style work that ties things together with amazing detail.<br><br>My only real complaint is that literally nobody plays the PVP anymore, as far as I can tell. Want to use that amazing new ship you bought for more than your own personal masturbation in the latest quest line to be released? Well, tough. Apparently enough people ruined it early in the lifecycle of the game that you just can't find anyone to play with anymore. The PVE Queues the game has, where everyone defends against the Borg or whatnot, are full 24\/7, and an excellent way to level (you can get 90% to the level cap within 12 hours if you just keep running them!). However, the PVP queues are at zero, zero, zero, zero, zero for every match type, all day and all night, with little to no flukes where there are suddenly more people in them. There's just nothing. I really wanted to try PVP even if it sucked, but I guess it's back to living through some pretty interesting Star Trek episodes for me. :) Which is both a blessing and a curse, as is most of this game.<br><br>Accept STO as the flawed piece of semi-trash that it is, and you'll find plenty of treasures within, it just might be best to wait for enjoying them until you're in the mood to appreciate
