Terra Nova also pioneered and perfected squad commanding in an fps environment, with competent, realistic bots. The character models were based off of a dynamically animated skeleton with bipedal physics simulations that outclass current fps\u2019 movement systems. Similarly to System Shock, Greg LoPiccolo composed and programmed the soundtrack to dynamically react to the player\u2019s status. It was Paul Neurath\u2019s dream, the director of Looking Glass, the team of programmers, artists, and designers responsible for Deus Ex, System Shock 2, Terra Nova, System Shock, Thief, and Ultima UnderWorld. Unfortunately, like their previous titles, Terra Nova received critical acclaim and no popularity. The company went bankrupt and was summarily purchased by Ion Storm. The highly experienced team proceeded to develop the highly regarded Deus Ex and System Shock 2 games. After Ion Storm\u2019s breakup, the Looking Glass team was divided amongst many current developers, with some now returning under Paul Neurath\u2019s new company, OtherSide Entertainment. So there are no nostalgia goggles going on here.<br><br><br><b>Graphics &amp; Sound<\/b><br>Terra Nova was released in 1996, and you can tell that it's an old game by just looking at it. The graphics look pixelated, the FMVs look poorly compressed and are in many cases shown in a smaller window. Many objects are also 2D sprites despite the game being a 3D game. <br>  With that being said, the developers did a really good job working with what they had. The environments feel big, and despite how pixelated things are, it's never hard to tell what things are supposed to be. They also made good use of simple effects in order to convey different conditions, far more so than any contemporary game I can think of. While the game looks old, it does not feel old, if that makes any sense.<br>  The sound is a bit of a mixed bag. Some of the tunes that play are good, but some clearly needed better hardware. For an example, during a thunderstorm, there's a delay between you seeing the flash, and hearing the sound. It's a tiny detail, but it does help build immersion.<br><br><br><b>Story<\/b><br>In the future humanity has managed to colonize the solar system. Now earth is once again showing interest in your land, and you're forced to fight the invading earth forces.<br>  There are hints of a really interesting universe in this game, but sadly the setting feels underdeveloped. Had they spent more time fleshing out the game world, the story &amp; setting might actually have been something memorable. It's still better than that of most action games, in particular of the era, but there's so much room for more.<br>  Also, there's one huge inconsistency. How large are your mech suits (or Power Battle Armour (PBA) as they're known in game). In the cutscenes they look like they're about 2-2.5m, but in game you tower over trees and when standing next to a house, they look to be at least 2 stories tall.<br><br><br><b>Gameplay<\/b><br>For a game released in 1996, Terra Nova feels surprisingly modern. The game uses a form of proto-WASD controls, which are easy to get used to if you've played any modern shooter. There's no mouse look, but that's in part due to you having to use your mouse to manipulate different systems in your suit, and there's at least mouse aiming.<br>  The suits are a versatile and agile lot. There are 3 kinds of suits, light, medium and heavy, and they do what you would expect. The light suits can only have 2 weapons equipped at once (or 1 big weapon), and has far less armour, but  moves faster. The medium &amp; heavy suit can both equip 4 weapons (big weapons take 2 slots), and there are weapons that only the heavy suit can equip. The medium suit is of course faster but less well armoured. All suits are equipped with jumpjets, allowing you to fly several times your height up into the air. There are also plenty of other equipment that you get to use through the game, giving both replay value and variety between the missions.<br>  You also have a squad of up to 3 more members that you sometimes need for different tasks, but often just acts as a bunch of extra guns that can help you out in combat. You can give orders to the squad, but most of the time they behave in a semi-intelligent way. They're also not very good at self-preservation, but for most missions, losing squad members is not a big issue, they'll be back for the next one with no harm done to them.<br><br><br><b>Final thoughts<\/b><br>Just how good Terra Nova: Strike Force Centauri came as a bit of a surprise to me. There's a good amount of variety between the missions, and the fact that you can bring your own loadout for most missions also makes for some great replay value. <br>  There are also plenty of details in here that makes the game very immersive. Sometimes the lack of self preservation among squadmates can force you to restart missions, and the game is both short (in game time after my first playthrough said 2:41:38, but that does not count cutscenes), and relatively easy.
