yourself to pick up this game. For $2 it's an absolute steal.<br><br>And now, here's why!<br><br>Put simply, Helen's Mysterious Castle avoids most of the pitfalls common in RPGs throughout the ages- it even avoids the ones that Undertale falls into across repeated playthroughs. This isn't to say that it's a perfect game or that it avoids every pitfall, because it doesn't. But what it does do is avert a lot of expectations about the genre in very cleverly done ways.<br><br>Don't like random encounters? Good! Like Chrono Trigger, enemies wander around the various dungeons and battles only trigger on running into them. However, the enemies also move around in different patterns depending on the enemy. Cerberus will lunge at you at a high speed, Skeletons won't move, some enemies will move much faster if they notice you, and so on. This results in a very streamlined experience where you fight the battles you want to fight, or fail to avoid. Throughout the game there are very few forced non-boss encounters and these alone give you enough XP to beat the game, if you're careful.<br><br>Grinding is greatly alleviated and Helen has a very incremental rate of progression. Rather than leveling up on obtaining enough XP, you decide which weapons to level up with your experience. This can be done at any time, and poses some interesting strategic decisions. Do you level everything equally to be prepared for everything, or do you specialize in one or two attacks and hope that it's enough to take down everything in your path? The balance here could stand to be a little better, as the Blacksmith you encounter about a third of the way through the game can refine equipment 1 level beyond what Helen can, and the LongswordSP shatters game balance to the point where I highly suggest avoiding it. Other SP weapons are perfectly fine for balance, though.<br><br>As for the weapons throughout the game, there's a very solid mix. All attacks have three stats, and optionally a bonus effect such as piercing (ignores defense) or draining HP. These stats are Effect, Defense, and Wait. This results in a very simple system similar to Paper Mario where every attack has a predictable damage output, while also adding a huge layer of tactical planning that turns most boss encounters into puzzles. How can you exploit their moveset to create an opening to attack without leaving yourself open? This gameplay was very engaging to me and I played through the entire game in one sitting, taking about 5 hours.<br><br>Damage dealt is Effect of attacking move - Defense of defender's move. The tactical aspect comes in with the Wait stat- everything has one, and attacks only happen when the counter hits 0. If you cue up a Thunder spell, you'll be waiting a lot of ticks and any enemy with a fast attack will be free to wail on you during the cast time. This can sometimes feel a little bit like trial and error early on when you don't know how enemy attacks work, or what attacks a given foe has at their disposal, but Helen's Mysterious Castle allows you to save anywhere, at any time. Some battles do feel a little like there's One Right Choice for your loadout, but the sheer size of Helen's potential armory can make replays fresh, and with grinding being entirely optional, the battles don't get old even when they start playing out the same way every time. This simple system adds a ton of replay value by making the best choice at any given time dependent on what you're holding, and what your opponent is doing.<br><br>Many battles have creative gimmicks to them that can force you to change your entire strategy; one removes your current attack as an option for the rest of the battle, several poison you at the start which makes you take 5 damage after your every action, several can lower your max HP within the battle, some will make your next attack take longer to execute, one notable case will reflect all damage blocked... the list of creative gimmicks goes on and on. Every boss feels unique and fresh, which is a marvel for how overall simple the systems involved are. Helen's Mysterious Castle is definitely a case of real complexity through fewer meaningful choices. This is compounded by the fact that Helen can only carry 8 pieces of equipment at a time- anything dropped can be picked back up at a specific area opened fairly early on in the game.<br><br>On the topic of replay value: the longest cutscene in the game is about a minute long, barring the final boss gauntlet of the good ending. Again, this neatly dodges the common issues of RPGs having more dialogue and cutscene than gameplay these days. What gameplay there is, as mentioned, is only as tedious as you want it to be. The final boss gauntlet is even very nice in that it autosaves between each of the battles, instead of making you sit through the cutscene and refight any bosses you've already beaten. So many RPGs make you do this, it's kind of absurd! It doesn't hurt that the soundtrack throughout the game is always a good listen and always an amazing fit. Everything from the hanging gardens boss onwards is amazing, and while testing speedrun strategies for the last bosses of both the good and bad ending, the soundtrack never got old. And I was testing strategies for over an hour on the good ending gauntlet!<br><br>Let's see... I suppose I should mention the story. There really isn't much of one, and the characters don't stick around too long or develop much. But this is an otherwise small blemish on an otherwise fantastic game: especially since the small story manages to throw in a couple surprising twists even to longtime fans of the genre. Even I fell for the fake ending, and I can usually see through those a mile away.<br><br>Oh and I mentioned speedruns of this earlier- everything it averts above makes it a very fun RPG run with very little downtime. It fit everything I'd been looking for in an RPG speedrun, and I played it because I was looking for a short RPG to play through. I didn't expect to enjoy it as much as I did, but I'm very glad I gave it a play soon after picking it up.<br><br>In conclusion: Helen's Mysterious Castle is a fantastic experience for any RPG fan, new or old.
