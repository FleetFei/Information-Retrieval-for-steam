history and listen anew to the ancestors' wisdom.&quot;  - Maya Angelou<\/b><br><br>Remember that great aunt of yours that you are supposed to look exactly the same? Or the father of your grandfather, smiling the same peculiar way that you do according to your mother? Looking back beyond our own lives and limited lifespans, we cannot help but wonder about our own families - the roots that we came from. Men and women before us, and many others before them consist of the roots of a tree or veins in a whole body, connected to one another to serve a magnificent whole. Metaphors aside, we get to wonder about the notion of a bloodline. A bloodline - aside being a definitive biological function, serves as roots for people. Tracing them back to ages before, we get the fundamental feeling that a part of us has always been travelling in between times through our ancestors before us and will keep travelling after us through our own offspring. Considering the poetry of lineage, one does also wonder whether the same souls would be travelling in the same bloodline, waking into new lives in new generations...<br><br><i>The Sad Story of Emmeline Burns<\/i> is a story about ancestry and the third visual novel coming from Ebi-Hime, after <i>The Way we All Go<\/i> and <i>Asphyxia<\/i>. It is also the first of Ebi-Hime's games that I've come to play yet. Aside being a bit short, I must confess that it is incredibly well-done for a freeware, both with the narrative content and art work. As a side notice, this is a Yuri title, containing female\/female romance with exceedingly beautiful and elegant depiction.<br><br>Our heroine is Toma Andrews, a young girl of age 15 living in Barrowby, England. Toma, growing up with her mother and her peculiar obsession about family history has come to develop a certain affinity for graveyards. Considering her shy and introverted personality, this wouldn't be the weirdest thing about Toma though. She is a sensitive young girl with a vast inner world - always an outsider to most of her coevals. One day, she decides to skip the school and take a walk in the nearby cemetery, only to meet a strange young girl named Emily. Exchanging stories with her, Emily comes to tell her the sad story of Emmeline Burns - full of vibrant beauty and a shocking tragedy.<br><br>The art in the game comes from SillySelly - who also illustrated various other games for Ebi-Hime - and her style is incredibly beautiful with pastel colors, fleeting images and a rich environment. Characters look like as if they've been taken out of a Renaissance poem; with flowing hair, shiny eyes and blushing cheeks. This ideal beauty - Donna Angelicata - serves to be a striking contrast with the dark tone of the story. This kind of Petrarchan idealization in visuals coming together with the melancholic narrative creates a sense of eeriness, wrapping the setting in a Byronesque air with strong impulses and an impending darkness hidden behind the vibrant colors.<br><br>The narrative uses countless literary references - especially centering on English poetry and some nuances from Gothic literature. Descriptions are always elegant, yet point on with the intended feeling, engulfing you before you'd be aware of its effect. The Yuri part is more of an undertone rather than the aim, arranging a fittingly immaterial nature to the storyline and all its characters, depicted almost spiritual.<br><br>Overall, I loved <i>The Sad Story of Emmeline Burns<\/i>. Even though short lived, it presented a beautiful impact and a lesson that anyone can benefit from in the end: life is fragile and very valuable. The notion especially becomes wondrously sacred and meaningful if it were to be thought as a whole, rather than a single life. A lineage is a being, and a whole with the rest, throughout history.
