While, structurally, this could be considered similar to many other adventure games, the details and world were very different than the usual fare. For this reason, I was willing to forgive a great deal.<br><br>The puzzles are a mixed bag for someone who doesn't really remember pixel hunting with any fondness. There are two pixel-hunt puzzles, and I would have been much happier with something else, but they serve their purpose well. Most of the others are more about brain power and less about stubbornly looking for a hotspot. I was mildly annoyed that the characters implied I couldn't have known the answer to one (very simple) puzzle without the walkthrough, but that could be considered a nod to the fact that in their world, ours is basically a myth, and thus what is common or easily acquired knowledge here could be esoteric there.<br><br>The dialogue... left me in a weird place. I found some of Reader's lines of conversation annoying, but at the same time, they drew me further into the world, in some cases even as I speed-clicked through topics I had seen before. The reverb didn't really bother me, but I know some people find it very annoying. Definitely find a Let's Play video or something and watch a tiny bit of the game to see which camp you fall into. For me, it just corresponded to the pulsing of the scenery- of course the sound is going to be odd. <br><br>The ending... everything important gets dealt with, it's just that there's no epic ending cutscene to help you feel accomplished. There is a mildly entertaining non-epilogue, which served just fine for me, but this is the money brick wall many indie games run into, so I am a bit used to it, I suppose.<br><br>All in all, I am pleased that I played it, and pleased to own it, but I definitely do think this is a niche piece, for those of us who like their surreality without a strong horror overlay.
