The players controls only one character at the time, the AI controls the others, and it's possible to change the controllable character with the press of a button, even in the heat of battle. Technically this means it's possible to attack the enemy with the melee character, switch to the fire-throwing mage and burn them until they're dead, and finally switch to the healer and bring everyone's HP back up.<br><br>In practice, it's more likely that the player sticks to the melee character (the main character of the story) and just lets the AI handle the others. The AI does a good job and doesn't require handholding, and it's even possible to tweak the AI settings per character.<br><br>As the story progresses, the party visits several towns to heal up, stock up on items and purchase new equipment, while also exploring dungeons searching for whatever they need to find there, as mandated by the narrative. Naturally, there's enemies abound, which leads to the game's most problematic part: the combat.<br><br>At first the combat feels great. As the sword-wielding main character, the player has several different attacks at their disposal: Down + Attack for a quick forward thrust, Up + Attack for an upreaching swipe with which to hit flying enemies, and so on. More sword techniques are learned as the character levels up, requiring slightly more complex button combinations. At this point the combat feels nice and methodical: block the enemy's attack, thrust, slash, block again, roll behind him, slash some more, and so on. It's fun.<br><br>Unfortunately, at some point the enemies become numerous (though never more than 5-6 at a time) and the combat often becomes a confusing mess. This isn't helped by the fact that some enemies can trap the player into a loop: they pick the character up, toss them down, then come back for another go or three before the character can even get up. The enemies can also dodge attacks proficiently, and it feels if the player and an enemy attack each other at the same time, the enemy's attack always has priority. This can be quite frustrating. <br><br>The game's dungeons can be large, and there are no maps of any kinds available, so a good sense of direction (or making your own maps) is recommended.
