class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFreestyle football is good, simple fun that captures the feel of a game of soccer.  Despite several very obvious failings, it's fairly easy to recommend thanks to the combination of the free pricetag and a not especially crowded field of similar games to compete with on Steam.<br><br>  The main failings of the game are controls and matchmaking.<br><br>  On the control front, the characters don't move that well on the field and the controls in general feel kind of clunky and awkward.  Character movement isn't responsive.  This sort of game begs for a gamepad, but gamepad support is poor because you're locked to moving in cardinal directions and diagonals and because skills that require pressing multiple buttons don't work unless you bind your gamepad buttons to their matching keyboard buttons using a third-party app.  Additionally, the tutorial and practice mode do a poor job of teaching you and letting you practice the controls.<br><br>  Matchmaking wise, wait times can be long on the order of several minutes to find a game.  I typically ALT-TAB away and browse the web while listening for the game starting while searching.  Occasionally when I'm already in a lobby with a team or partial team, it seems to break and won't find a game after I've waited thrice as long as I would normally expect to find a game or longer, provoking me to quit and start a new search.<br><br>  Also as a negative, but one that is to be expected from all but best F2P games, it is an F2P game where you can buy mechanical advantages for yourself.  With your free currency you can buy small, time-limited stat boosts, a chance at tiny permanent stat boosts via &quot;training&quot; that are priced to be a near-bottomless hole for points at the rate you earn them once you get to level 20, and a variety of skills to help you manuever the ball on the field better.  When you reach level 40, the game pulls it's meanest trick of promoting you to an advanced class with a whole bunch of new skills that cost much more than the default skills.  You can equip a lot of them- 5 by default and more if you buy extra skill slots- and playing for free I wouldn't be surprised if it took you more than 30 hours of play per skill unlock to earn.<br><br>As far as the paid currency, for non-cosmetics you mostly get access to more of the same, although for $3 for 12 days you can get a double-effectiveness stat boosting clothing item and for $39 you can permanently unlock a new character (with a unique model) that has some small stat boosts built into it as well.  The one paid currency item of note is extra skill slots beyond the 5 you start with, which is something you'll want eventually and costs $6 per slot and can be bought up to nine times.<br><br>  This is not great, but I recognize that F2P games need to make their monies somehow and this is one of the less obnoxious P2W implementations I have encountered.  It's relatively straightforward, doesn't pull some of the nastier tricks like tempting you to spend real currency in-game or holding loot hostage, and the stat increases don't have much of an obvious effect on gameplay.  Still, it is what it is so be aware that it is a factor.<br><br>  For all that I have dedicated the most of the text of this review to negatives, however, I would still recommend Freestyle Football.  It's free.  It's easy to get in to.  It's fun and addictive.  Playing it feels like you're playing a small-team game of soccer or basketball, with a focus on postioning and coverage.  There's not a ton else like it on Steam, so it both feels fresh to me and doesn't have a lot of competitors.<br><br>  If the idea of playing some internet sportsball has any appeal to you, I'd recommend at least giving Freestyle Football a chance.
