adventure, but I would define it more as a horror-comedy adventure. Actually even a b-horror comedy in many ways due to the main character being full of one liners, puns, and juvenile humor at times. The game also reminds me a bit of the POSTAL series in the way it looks as well as the over the top gore in certain situations, not to mention the vulgarity in humor. SOLD.<br><br>Blackbay Asylum is a point and click adventure game at its heart, but most of my enjoyment came from the absurd humor. You are a psychopath and a serial killer that has been incarcerated in an asylum for the criminally insane. At the outset of the game you awaken in your cell with your only true friend, Teddy. He is your stuffed teddy bear and you think he is alive. You talk to him as if he were once in a while and care for him. He doesn't play much of a role past this other than some odd hallucinations you have later, but it gives you some insight into your childlike mind right from the start. You soon realize that your cell door is open and as you leave you find that there are no other inhabitants in the asylum besides you. Well...alive anywyas, or human. Your quest to escape the asylum is now underway. <br><br>You play as the pschopathic killer with his only true goal being one of escape. As you roam around the halls of the asylum you begin to uncover an occult element within the asylum that for some reason you decide you must solve and find out where it hails from! This is some of the silly in the game as the story combined with who you are as a protagonist doesn't really mesh. Why would this guy care, or why would he bother to look into this and try to stop it? There isn't much in the way of reason here, but that doesn't stop it from being fun! <br><br>Gore, vulgarity, and goofball humor follow you all the way through this tale. Most objects in the game world can be interacted with in some way. Well...they can be looked at and give you a bit of dialogue from the main character as he looks at them. Paintings will show his complete disdain for art in general, he will tell you how he doesn't believe in washing his hands in the sink you look at, and you will find out quickly that wooden boxes are useless! Our hero isn't a smart man, but he definitely enjoys the one liner. He also has a very dark sense of humor and leans heavily towards violence within it. He has a very childlike mind, but he can be quite clever in his quips at times as well. This dialogue from the protagonist is really the highlight of the game for me as it keeps you laughing throughout, but of course he makes you groan just as often. <br><br>This game has some interesting concepts and gameplay mechanics for an adventure title. It is designed like a point and click adventure game with the puzzles and the story elements where you play most of the game from an overhead view as you walk around the Blackbay Asylum, but certain chapters mix it up nicely and put you into first person mode! This isn't off putting at all. It is done very well and feels completely natural to go from the overhead to the first person views in certain levels. The first person levels actually lend to the horror elements and atmosphere better than the normal overhead view as well. While the graphics remind you some of the POSTAL series in the style, there are sections that have some creepy atomospheres. Sound helps to make these areas feel scary, and there are a couple sections that are very creepy with the use of drarkness, sound, and gore. The gore and violence is cartoonish for the most part with your character looking like a roided out weirdo covered in blood when you do get to see him most times, but the style fits fine and the graphics are fitting. <br><br>Like any point and click adventure sort you will have to solve an array of puzzles. Blackbay Asylum excels in the puzzle realm. I played the game without guides and found that I was writing things down on paper and referring to them later for puzzles. This is always something I enjoy about adventure games when I must &quot;work out&quot; the puzzles in this way more than just pulling a lever here and there with no thought at all. The puzzles here are a good mix of using symbols, poetry, excerpts from books, some math, and many others. While most seem fairly logical, there are a few that are illogical such as the use of safe combinations. So just an FYI when it comes to using a combo safe lock...forget what you know about a traditional lock combo because this game doesn't abide by those. Turning a combo lock is MUCH more straightforward in game than it would be in real life. ;)<br><br>Combat isn't a factor in the game really. You have a couple of scenes where you need to run away, but other than that you don't have too much in the way of &quot;fighting&quot;. There are some boss &quot;fights&quot;, but theese are actually done as puzzles rather than actual combat scenarios. The final boss is quite a unique battle I must say with several puzzle elements involved to win  he final battle. While I wouldn't say it was genius by any stretch, or even logical at all steps, it does offer a refreshing change to a boss fight that you won't see elsewhere. <br><br>The only major negative in the game is the lack of save features. The game saves when you complete a chapter. There are 10 chapters, but they all have varying lengths to them. Within these chapters are &quot;checkpoints&quot;, BUT these checkpoints only matter for the current play session in case you happen to die. If you quit in the midst of a chapter you must start that chapter over the next time you play. Now...don't let this turn you off too much, however. This is a point and click adventure game with puzzles after all so levels can be VERY short once you know what you are doing. As in any adventure game the time it takes to get through an area depends mostly on your ability to solve the puzzles. So even if you must quit in the middle of a chapter, if you have solved most of the puzzles already it isn't a huge step back when you continue as you can breeze throught he puzzles you already know solutions to. <br><br>Overall, Blackbay Asylum is quite a fun and entertaining adventure game. Full of gore, vulgar humor (I'm talking poop jokes even), and top notch puzzles. All combined with a strange occult tale as backdrop. Most of all I found this game to be incredibly FUN. I laughed throughout at the goofy lines, and was intrigued by an array of interesting puzzles. While I wouldn't recommend this as something to scare your pants off, I would recommend it for anyone looking to have a good time and enjoy a solid adventure game with childish humor and a lot of gore. :D<br><br>If you go through this without cheating it should probably take you around 5-10 hours to get through depending on your ability to solve puzzles. Blackbay Asylum is like having both of those things at once.&quot;<\/div><br>Blackbay Asylum is a top-down\/first person puzzle\/horror game set at the eponymous Blackbay Asylum, which is located off the coast of Innsmouth, Massachusetts. For those who are unfamiliar, Innsmouth is a fictional city known for it\u2019s relation to the HP Lovecraft mythos. In here, you play as Doug, a large and heavily muscled man who recently incarcerated at Blackbay for being a mass murdering sociopath.<br><br>Gameplay in Blackbay Asylum generally consists of moving Doug through the level, collecting the equipment and knowledge to solve each puzzle as it pops up. Speaking of the game as a whole, the puzzler aspect is much stronger than the horror one. Although the first few chapters left me unimpressed, (if you can make sense of it then you can get through the first level within 10 minutes) it was probably designed that way to bring people into the game a lot more. The puzzles in the first level aren\u2019t too difficult and they get you really immersed in the game and also makes you feel more intelligent than when you started. And it doesn\u2019t take long for them to get genuinely tricky. Some of them have fairly simple gimmicks to work out before you can progress, but for the most part they\u2019re cerebral enough to keep the old noggin\u2019 working. Just like any good puzzler, the challenges you\u2019re faced by start off fairly simple but ramp up the difficult as you go on.<br><br>Characters in the game are rare to come by; a lot of the other people that you see in the game are dead, dying or will soon be dead (either by monsters hands or by you) but the people you do come across aren\u2019t overly well written. For instance you come across a man who is hiding in a dumpster in the first level who sounds insane, ironically, and some of the things he says just seem a bit too wild even for an insane asylum. Also a receptionist at the front desk who is working and on her cell phone the whole time doesn\u2019t seem to care when a giant man covered in blood walks from Isolation into the lobby which is a bit odd. That being said, it\u2019s worth reminding you that <i>Blackbay<\/i> is indeed a horror game. While the dialogue and descriptions are exceedingly silly, every aspect of the game is eerie.<br><br>So, I\u2019d played through the first two chapters of the game and then I landed in Chapter 3. The third Chapter of BBA is almost flawless. It is genuine horror, perfectly executed. The camera moves from being a top down puzzle adventure game to a first person perspective and it\u2019s at this point where you start to notice the graphics of the game, allowing you to see more of the surrounding environment more than before. From the top down perspective the graphics don\u2019t really seem to great however once you start looking through Doug\u2019s eyes you notice some smaller things in the game. And this was a great way to use the shift in perspective because there are things that you can only see through Doug\u2019s eyes at this point such as items on the middle shelf or a piece of information written on a notice board. While in this first-person section, there were times when I had to stop playing for a few minutes, and just breath. I don\u2019t mind admitting that I got genuinely spooked by the atmosphere it managed to create.<br><br>Whether you should play this game is determined by your tastes as a gamer. If you\u2019re on the older side and looking to play as many adventure games as you can or don\u2019t like puzzles, horror games, or any horror games that don\u2019t take themselves seriously, I can\u2019t say I\u2019d recommend BlackBay Asylum. As for me, I think that Blackbay Asylum is a welcome addition to my Steam library and hearkens back to the days of top down action adventure, with a touch of horror, and some first person puzzle games
