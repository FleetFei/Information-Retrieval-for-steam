class=\"bb_h1\">TL;DR:<\/div><br>Howler is a great program for digital painting, but it has some design decisions that might annoy you if you are used to other art programs.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">The Long Version:<\/div><br>First off let me say that I've owned Howler for a few months, but just recently got it on Steam, so my hours listed here don't reflect my time with the program.<br><br>Howler is an interesting beast - on the one hand you have a tool which seems to have an almost endless list of really useful features. These are tools that can save you lots of hours of work if used properly. The breadth and depth of the functionality is impressive, there are nifty things here for just about everyone.<br><br>On the other hand you have lots of little design decisions in the software that will drive you completely insane if you are coming from other programs. The program seems to be almost intentionally deviating from the standards that seem to be in place in just about every other art package, especially in regard to keyboard shortcuts.<br><br>Let me list here the things that I love about Howler, then I'll list a few of my annoyances:<br><br>What I love:<br><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li>The number of brushes available. You have your standard brushes, which use an impressive brush engine that allows a LOT of customization. Just about everything you can possibly imagine doing with a brush can be done here. Making new brushes is extremely simple as well. There are also a lot of useful brushes you won't find anywhere else, like the orbicles (I love these for concepting out design shapes, just a perfect &quot;random&quot; brush), as well as the foliage, particle, and bristle brushes. When I first got the program I spent hours just fiddling with the brushes to see what I could come up with, pretty cool stuff.<br><\/li><li>The 3D designer. This tool is awesome - I mostly just paint for fun, and like to do mountain scenes. This tool makes generating  backgrounds of a mountain scene extremely easy. In about 5 minutes I can get a really nice mountain range with snow, grass, rock, whatever I want.<br><\/li><li>The Color Selection tools. There are a lot of useful ways to select colors - including a bunch of palletes that come built in that are pretty nice, as well as a color mixer, where you can live mix paint, and of course your standard triangle and sliders selection. Seems like this was very well thought out.<br><\/li><li>An impressive set of filters. Photoshop probably has more (I didn't count), but Howler has a very extensive set of functionality there. You can do lots of adjustments through these as well, like values, levels, etc.<br><\/li><li>The ability to do animation. I haven't really done this yet, but I know Howler has extensive functionality for onion skinning and various other animation tools.<br><\/li><li>The &quot;documentation&quot; and support team. Philip and Dan (the devs) have done an incredible job with the youtube channel as well as the various communities, including the one here on Steam. If you have a question it is answered within a day, without exception in my case. The youtube channel has a ton of information for all of the features in Howler.<\/li><\/ul><br>What drives me crazy:<br><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li>Keyboard shortcuts are very &quot;non-standard.&quot; This is my #1 complaint with Howler. Let me give you a few examples. In just about every art program out there that I've used, the eye-dropper tool uses the &quot;alt&quot; keyboard shortcut. Sometimes the &quot;Ctrl&quot; shortcut, but that's rare. Howler? The comma key. Another one - every program I've ever used uses the space bar to pan around the image. Howler - the space bar brings up the color selector. I could go on, but in general the keyboard shortcuts are in my opinion extremely non-intuitive. I realize Howler doesn't want to be like everyone else, but they have to realize that this isn't the only tool in a user's toolbox, and people want consistency. If I had the ability to change these keyboard shortcuts I'd be fine with that, but currently that's not possible. <br><\/li><li>The &quot;Layers Thing.&quot; This unfortunately is going to be a deal breaker for a lot of people. Howler does not have opaque layers. This means that when you paint, your paint doesn't obscure the layer beneath it (it uses a &quot;multiply&quot; layer instead). It also means you can't do things like draw a mountain first, then drop in a layer behind the mountain layer and draw a sky. In fact there are a LOT of things you can't do because of this limitation. If I was Dan and looking at the number of forum requests that centered around the lack of this functionality, that feature would be #1 on the list of improvements that I would make. Now, that being said, if you just change your workflow it's not too bad. Just paint like people did in the old days, back to front, and you should be good. <\/li><\/ul>In conclusion, I do want to say that I heartily recommend Howler. The things that it does well far outweigh the bad. The brush customization is incredible, and for me, the brushes are really the most important thing. If you're looking for a fun and powerful tool to add to your art toolbox, look no further than Howler.\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"gradient\"><\/div>\r\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div Howler is a very capable digital painting software that as you learn the workflow, you may wonder if it's more of a video compositing software without nodes.  There are 2 tutorial playlists that you should walkthrough, starting with Tunnels.   <b>Workflow<\/b>, <b>Floatinng Transparency<\/b>, and <b>RGB morality<\/b> are what confuse most people coming to Howler. Howler's workflow is different because it is more like a compositor workflow. Howler is also different as it uses <b>floating transparency<\/b> instead of layer transparency.  <b>Workflow<\/b> is shown in the older Tunnels series.  Working with floating transparency is shown in the <b>Transparency<\/b> series.  <b>RGB morality<\/b> is more of a joke on my end, but basically I mean that the RGB value will always display regardless of whether transparency is on or off.  For those who want to do 2D animation, there are no bones, so while Howler has exceptional potential for quality background work, you're going to want to import those backgrounds into software that has bones(rigging).<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">The Floating Alpha Tools Menu<\/div>Found in this menu - View &gt; Other &gt; Alpha Panel... <br>Floating transparency is transparency you call whenever you need transparency, whereas layer transparency is always there, you just turn it on whenever you need it.  Neither is necessarily better, as they both have advantages, it's only that every other program in the digital painting category does not use floating transparency so even Howler in the future is planning to change to layer transparency too.  So, if you try to bring a photoshop file with transparency included on every layer whether it's used or not into a program that relies on only the efficiency of floating transparency, well, the result makes people scratch their head.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">1. Tunnels<\/div>Tunnels is THE series to watch to understand PD Howler.  That's all there is to it.  Anything else might assume you're already familiar with the Tunnels series.
