writing this review, as I'm currently at university doing a minor in Creative Writing, and I've recently had to &quot;critique&quot; the work of a few young female students as part of our assessment and in-class exercises. I've also been messing about with a certain piece of interactive novel-writing software called Twine, which is frankly a great place to start if you know as little (i.e. absolutely nothing) about video game programming as I do. <br> <br>   So what do you get when you combine the style of the aforementioned female writing students with a newbie to Twine? Something called Ping Ping, apparently. That's certainly how this Pinky person's writing reads to me; and that's not necessarily a criticism either, just an observation. She actually writes quite well - as long as you like your writing to be more &quot;evocative&quot; than easy-to-understand - so that's really not the issue here. The real question is: just how much of a &quot;game&quot; are you getting for your four dollars? Hmmm. Well, first of all, let's take a closer look at this &quot;Twine&quot; thing... <br><br>   One of the things which Twine offers is a vast variety of font types, colours and sizes. The developer has definitely chosen to be unambitious in this regard, choosing a simple greyish-black background with tiny white writing, with the linking words (which you click on to make choices, or at least move to the next page\/passage) being a shade of blue only a couple of tones away from the program's default colour. Again, not necessarily a &quot;bad&quot; thing, but it is tempting to say the writer's choice may have been a little lazy in this regard. Buy hey - it works, it's fine, and really isn't a basis for heavy criticism of her work. It's certainly not as bad as seeing that exact same asset for the umpteenth time in yet, yet another Unity or GameGuru &quot;product&quot;, and it's sufficiently aesthetically pleasing to allow the narrative itself to do most of the talking.<br> <br>   Okay, conspiracy theory time, boys and girls. There's a certain claim the dev is making on her Store page, one which I can't authoritatively speak the truth on. But as other reviewers\/spiteful former friends with a grudge (seriously, just read the reviews in question) have pointed out, it's a pretty big claim that the story has 777 &quot;choices&quot; which the player can make. Having toyed with Twine a tiny bit myself, I can vouch that to give a player that many choices would be an ASTRONOMICAL effort. Like, many hundreds of hours of work, at the very least. What she more likely means is that there are 777 PASSAGES, i.e. pages of text, a lot of which are linked by a single &quot;choice&quot; of word or words to click on in order to progress to the next page. I may be wrong, but I'm fairly sure a &quot;choice&quot; generally involves having more than one option on display. <br> <br>   Furthermore, a quick glance at the statistics of my own largest Twine piece to date - which I can safely vouch has more frequent &quot;choices&quot; on offer than Ping Ping does - tells me that I currently have 517 passages, and just under 20,000 words. The dev here is claiming that she has written over 80,000 words with 777 &quot;choices&quot;, but given the math I've just done here, I do indeed think she may mean 777 passages, otherwise we'd be getting a choice of two or more options pretty much every page, and this interactive novel sure-as-\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 doesn't offer THAT many decisions for the reader to make. (At least not judging by the two short playthroughs I've managed so far. And I just did a couple of rapid-fire spam-throughs to test this theory, and the longer of the two attempts came out at just under 60 bona fide &quot;choices&quot;, while the other didn't even make 30. So in the words of Billy Joel, &quot;You may be wrong, for all I know, but you may be right&quot;).<br> <br>   Now, unlike others, I'm not necessarily calling the dev\/author up on being a &quot;liar&quot;, but I do suspect there may be some crucial inaccuracies on her Store page. Also, in her defence, there's no denying the fact that just one simple choice can take the reader on extremely divergent tangents - indeed, entirely different story paths - so there's certainly plenty of replayability here for those who find it all compelling enough to bother. Just don't come in expecting to make a gazillion &quot;choices&quot; in the course of a single playthrough, as such assumptions are only liable to lead to disappointment.<br> <br>   On the whole, though, if you're in the mood for a rather, ahem, &quot;poetically&quot;-written, reasonably original, and somewhat surreal piece of dark\/&quot;cute&quot; interactive fiction, with extremely minimal visual gimmickry and absolutely no sound to speak of, you could presumably do much worse than this (though the four dollar price tag really is taking the \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665, all things considered; 99c is a more than adequate asking price, given what's on offer).
