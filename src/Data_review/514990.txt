singleplayer for the various scenarios and modes made available. Players choose from a pool of characters each with their own unique abilities, and which game mode they would like to play. The default game controls are a bit unintuitive, I had to go in and change the keybindings to be able to play effectively, though there are six different control schemes to choose from which range from the Classic Tank Controls, to the more modern keyboard and mouse action style and the game also supports controllers.<br><br>Before starting a scenario, players are given the option to equip their character with extra weapons and supplies they've earned through playing the game. Players are also able to scavenge around the map for weapons, ammo, and healing items that are randomly generated each time. With continously spawning undead hordes, and limited inventory space, players are hard pressed to make it through the level before they're left with nothing to fight with.<br><br>Onslaught mode is a wave survival mode that most people will atuomatically identify with Call of Duty's Zombies, to which I'll tell those people to turn that game off and go play Dead Frontier or SAS Zombie Assault. Players in Onslaught are trapped on their map of choice and must hold out against waves of increasingly dangerous undead, earning points to purchase more gear spread around the level that gets more expensive as you buy them. Available for purchase around some of the maps are keycards that open up more of the map and access to new items.<br><br>Nightmares mode focuses on specific character-oriented scenarios, presenting the player with a more story-driven narrative than the semi-random generated style of the main campaign. Loot is scarce and strategically placed around the levels while enemies are pre-determined and event spawned. No more random, endlessly spawning monsters to trap and sandwhich you in those tight corridors. Even though this mode's scenarios are shorter in length compared to the campaign,  Nightmares mode is the genuine survival-horror experience that really hits back to the genre's roots Outbreak is trying to adhere to.<br><br>With a price point of 17 dollars Canadian, or roughly 13 american, the game art and sound assets do leave a bit to desired, however the amount of content available is decent, with each level taking around an hour to two hours to beat depending on how many players are playing and there's a fair variety of weapons and levels to play with. Overall Outbreak is a step in the right direction for revitilizing the co-op survival horror, so if you've been looking for your fix in this genre, Outbreak just might be a game to keep on your The Perspective.<br><br>In Resident Evil, the cinematic camera angles limited your view and added a lot of tension to the gameplay. In Outbreak, the top down viewpoint allows you to see every threat coming at you from every angle, and the tension suffers as a result. Also, I feel less attached to my character and the characters of my teammates because I can only see the tops of thier heads. Characters make survival games interesting, they should be customizable, have back stories, voice lines, and be viewable in game. Otherwise the player is less driven to see them succeed. Also, when you were damaged in RE, you could see your character limp, which is great visual feedback for the player and is not possible with this perspective.<br><br>2. Level Design.<br><br>The levels are built around exploration and key collection. Thats not bad, but it really feels like it's missing puzzles and set pieces. Scenarios in RE had scripted events and felt like more than just walking around, shooting all the enemies, and collecting all the items. I'd suggest adding map creation tools to the game to allow players to craft thier own adventures, and include the ability to make thier own puzzles and solutions.<br><br>3. Sound Design.<br><br>Sounds could use some work. Voice acting, and more unique sound effects for certain enemies could add so much to the game. The music in this game is great, however.<br><br>4. Monster Design.<br><br>Pink pigs? Come on. Also, enemies should be placed logically, with spiders in sewers, creatures in labs, and zombies everywhere. Right now those three enemy types come seemingly at random. The game needs boss type characters, and perhaps even a tyrant type character which is slow yet unkillable. Right now players just kill everything on &quot;normal&quot; mode, they should be required to run sometimes.<br><br>5. Graphics.<br><br>They get the job done. Sprites can be good if done correctly.<br><br>6. Interaction.<br><br>Players should be able to push objects, carry wounded team members, open and close doors.<br><br>That's all that I can think of right now. Right now, it's hard for me to convince my friends to invest the small amount of money to play the game.
