gameplay involving moving and jumping your way to the exit. This familiar and direct-to-the point platformer feeling quickly and gradually transforms itself into something deeper, more complex and thought provoking as you are given the ability to slingshot yourself through solid walls to reach the previously unreachable orbs needed to exit the stage.<br><br>The action and the speed with which you break through each surface leading to your next goal are set to a pace that is addictive and constant, keeping you moving and diving through each cleverly placed obstacle at increasingly speedy times. It lulls you into a false sense of safety, starting out in the most basic of ways with a challenge that is easy and all-too obvious. The music <b>at first<\/b> bears a strikingly repetitive yet charming 8-bit tone and the map seems to be nothing more than a series of boxes connected by a line. The music from the always incredible Dubmood grows and evolves as more adventurous compositions are layered into the mix and draw you into the expanding challenge. The map twists and conforms into complex shapes as it progresses, branching off into multiple paths of varying difficulty.<br><br>With loads of alternate stages locked behind hidden pathways on a huge almost metroidvania-like map, insane par times to challenge yourself with for each stage, and leaderboards to check your skills against the rest of the world the content here is enormous with 150 stages and plenty of replayability keeping you coming back to each one long after you've completed a basic run.<br><br>Rising from its roots as a Ludum Dare 29 gamejam under the fitting theme &quot;Beneath the Surface&quot;, The Sun and Moon has come a long way and developer Daniel Linssen has turned what was a fantastic and simple concept with suprisingly deep mechanics into a content-rich package that is much sleeker, shinier, and polished than before. Ideas thought up in the heat of a gamejam can even make it to a full retail product, and join the likes of Surgeon simulator or Receiver. For Ludum Dare 29 all competitors created games to fit the theme \u201cbeneath the surface\u201d, and now its winning game, The Sun and Moon, has graduated to a full Steam release.<br><br>First impressions are key, so you could be forgiven for thinking that The Sun and Moon\u2018s visual simplicity belies an even simpler game. You play as a small worm creature with the ability to dig underground, but in order to dig deep down you\u2019ll need to leverage the power of gravity and your own weight. Whilst the game is mechanically simple, it\u2019s the ways in which you\u2019ll utilise them, to overcome the game\u2019s puzzles, that provides variety.<br><br>Each quick-fire level has you collecting a number of gems, and you can make your way to the exit once you\u2019ve picked them all up. A three-tier grading system is included to encourage\/taunt you, and you\u2019ll face a real challenge to even gain the lowest grade in some levels.<br><br>The game drops you in the shallow end to begin with, tasking you with only picking up gems, but the difficulty ramps up very quickly. Pretty soon you\u2019ll be throwing yourself off platforms and stairs, trying to gain enough momentum to fire yourself to other, higher platforms. Each level has an ideal solution, and its up to you to figure it out. But even if you do manage to decode a level, you\u2019ll still need to seamlessly weave every movment, dive and dodge together to get to the exit quickly enough for that top ranking.<br><br>The control scheme is, at least, on your side, and you\u2019ll only need to use the directional keys alongside the shift button if you want to dive underground. Some levels may prove too difficult or demand some time to fully understand, luckily the overworld map has a branching structure that allows you to visit multiple different areas at once. Each new area brings its own challenges, but also a refreshing change of scenery, as well as an extra layer for the game\u2019s chiptune soundtrack.<br><br>And about that soundtrack, it\u2019s pleasant enough to keep your head bopping and feet tapping, whilst not breaking your concentration as you fail for the 20th time. It\u2019ll even extend itself as you unlock new areas, adding new layers to the beat, making it more complex and diverse.<br><br>You probably already know if you\u2019ll enjoy The Sun and Moon. Its excruciating difficulty and intense gameplay make it an acquired taste, only fit for those who can pick themselves up after repeated failure, only to continue playing.<br><br>It\u2019s the very epitome of videogame masochism and punishes far more than it rewards \u2013 unless you can give it the ample time required to master its 150+ levels. It\u2019s one of those games that really makes you work for your gratification, even if  only a fleeting sense of achievement. As much as we wish we were any good at The Sun and Moon, we just aren\u2019t \u2013 as our ground teeth, pounded keyboard, and flipped desk will attest to.
