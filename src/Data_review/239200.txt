unimaginable<\/b><br><br>Compared to the first game - <i>Amnesia: The Dark Descent<\/i> the second &quot;Amnesia&quot; game hasn't gotten such an extremely positive reception. This is probably more due to people constantly comparing it to the first game than to it being the lesser of the two. Comparisons with the original Amnesia game are of course to be expected - after all, that game has obtained a cult-like status as one of the scariest horror games EVER - but are not really fair in my opinion.<br><br>Of course, the two games share part of their title and there is also a fairly strong connection between both games' storyline (although more in the background, subtle referred to in the documents that are to be found everywhere). But the developers clearly had a different goal in mind with <i>Amnesia: A Machine for Pigs<\/i>, and the deserves to be rated on its own merits. These are very strong and pronounced indeed: the all important story is told in a brilliant way, with lots of documents being interlaced with revealing diary fragments and mysterious phonecalls. Although the story is certainly not straightforward and demands some thinking and re-reading to become completely clear, The Chinese Room has done an awesome job in this department.<br><br>Equally well done is the setting of the game: there is a very pervasive sense of darkness, gloom and horror during the game, helped by excellent use of the (fairly limited) graphical engine and, especially, by anothr gorgeous soundscape and musical score. Especially the music - freshly composed for the game by Jessica Cury - shines: it jumps between styles with astonishing ease, sometimes sounding as classical as Bach, and then all of a sudden going very dissonant and most unsettling. In fact &quot;unsettling&quot; in perhaps the single word that defines this game best: the unfolding story, the entire setting, the slow realisation of the unimaginable horrors that wait to be discovered, turned this game in a true horrific experience.<br><br>But that is not the same as a truly frightening experience: I didn't feel the same sense of utter fear as when playing the original game, when I often had to stop after some time in order to recompose myself. The horror in <i>Amnesia: A Machine for Pigs<\/i> is of a different sort: it is the horror of discovering the extreme, unhuman cruelty which lies beneath it all, combined with the slow but dreadful realisation of one's own part in this. Quite often I found myself reluctant to progress as I didn't want to discover more of the story, however intriguing it was. I simply refused to believe this was actually happening - which testifies to the complete feeling of immersion this game manages to achieve.<br><br>As with all true horror games, it is of course very highly recommended to play this one in a very dark environment, preferably using headphones in order to absord the amazing sound world of this game. Recently some sort of &quot;sound mod&quot; for the game was released, offering players using stereo headphones the opportunity to experience the sounds and music in a true threedimensional, positional fashion. I didn't try this out myself yet, but I can imagine that it even adds to the atmosphere. Please refer to the guides in the community section for the game.<br><br>Not everything is perfect though: the amount of actual gameplay has been reduced, not by a lot, but at least in one very important way. In the first game, you had to collect tinderboxes to be able to light candles, and lamp oil for the lantern you could carry with you (and which proved to be utterly necessary in the many pitch dark passages). Collecting tinderboxes and oil, adn keeping an eye on your oil level and the amount of tinderboxes left, made me play that game in a cautious, very slow manner, trying to keep at least something in reserve. In this game, however, the entire inventory has disappeared, and so have the light sources which needed to be managed. The lantern you are carrying with you stays &quot;on&quot; all the time, and the only thing you have to worry about, is not to attract the attention of &quot;the enemy&quot;. It seems like a minor quibble, but it is not: the game changed from an interactive horror game into an almost exclusively story- and atmosphere-driven experience, although an extremely strong one.<br><br>But in the end, both the story and superb soundtrack manage to overcome any shortcomings, and make this game an essential one for anybody not being deterred by the horrors to be found within.<br><br><b>Very highly recommended, but don't expect a sequel comparable to the original one.
