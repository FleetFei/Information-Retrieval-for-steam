class=\"postedDate\">Posted: November 10, 2016\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCursed Castilla is intended to be a retro action platformer very similar to Ghosts n Goblins, albeit a bit updated. The base weapon consists of two thrown swords, meaning that it is not possible to throw new swords as long as the previous two have not hit a target or left the screen. In consequence, every attack has to be deliberate otherwise an enemy can slip through and touch the player. Other weapons can be found in chests, and the game kindly leaves the choice of which to pick (just wait for the icon to change).<br><br>The quality of life improvements end here. For the rest, the game goes really deep in its retro roots. Jumps are stiff with too little air control. While this was often the case in the 80s, since then platformers are much more fluid in the way the character should handle. There are lives and continues, but thankfully they are infinite. However, after losing four continues the good ending seems disabled. Cursed Castilla is brutally hard, so not many people will be able to achieve that feat. To reach the last levels it is also mandatory to find a few items scattered through the game, a daunting task considering that it is impossible to revisit a previously visited level without starting over. Additionally, although there are frequent enough checkpoints, leaving the game and coming back to play forces to restart from the very beginning of a chapter. All these annoying issues are probably there because the game aimed too extremely for a retro feel. Retro can be good, but in healthy moderation.<br><br>Controls can be remapped for the keyboard and controllers, however only Xbox type of controllers are recognized by the game. A lovely CRT effect ban be turned ON or OFF. The screen aspect ratio is 4:3, it can be stretched (don\u2019t!) or game art\/black bands can be added to fill the sides. The graphics and animations are very good. Simple yet full of character. Monsters and bosses have a very inspired design and behavior, and there are so many of them, it is a joy just to see what the game comes up with next. Variety is a huge virtue of Cursed Castilla. The monsters seem like a mix between classic catacomb dwellers, Spanish and medieval folklore, and the manga Berserk with its demonic denizens. While there is little dialog, the overall feel of the story and the denizens of Castilla is evocatively dark.<br><br>Some levels are infuriating. Having to jump atop turtles while being attacked by bees and having to avoid spikes hanging in the air. Climbing a tower using an elevator while being drowned in murderous cogs. Swinging from chain to chain over deadly spikes while being chased after by poisonous bubbles. Jumping on moving platforms while spiders crawl about anywhere on the screen. And every time the player dies, they lose their weapon! I found that sometimes the difficulty was way too high, stretching the border towards unfair. And yet when I replayed those levels, I almost went through them in a breeze. Practice makes perfect. <br><br>Despite the flaws and brutal difficulty, I loved the game. It is very earnest in being a hardcore retro-platformer, so it is hard to blame the game for being exactly what it set itself to be. Taken as an old-school platformer that forgot the passing of time, it is brilliant. Released in the 80s or early 90s, it would have been a true classic, still beloved today.<br>Cursed Castilla is clearly a labor of love, from the game mechanics to the graphics and music. I would heartily recommend it to any speedrunner aficionado, any tough platformer enthusiast or someone hugely passionate about that era of gaming, but it is a harder sell for more mainstream audiences. Curious people that do not intend to go far could just play the freeware, although I am glad that there exists a paid version of the game to reward Locomalito for his excellent work. As to myself, I had a good time with the game; charmed by its graphics and the depth of its adventure, pumped by its precise action, left cold by some very difficult sections and repelled by two last levels that seem almost impossible to reach within a decent time frame. Hard mode separate from a new normal mode would have been nice!<br><br>Want to see it in action?
