class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCar Mechanic Simulator (henceforth CMS) 2015 is a distinct improvement over what was offered with CMS 2014. The fact that you can choose what jobs to accept or decline is very nice, especially as you are starting out and may not know where a particular problem might lie. However, where I think this game really shines is being able to buy a heap for cheap, gut it, repair what parts you are able, replace the rest, and sell it for a tidy profit. You may also elect to keep it for yourself, but there is little to do with it if you do, other than to simply look at it. My recommendation to any new players of the game is to do enough jobs to save up a bit of money, then start buying and fixing up cars from the auction. You can bet that the price of the car is going to double or triple in the auction house, and I would say to save up enough money to possibly double THAT amount before you start fixing things up. Until you really level up your repair skill (something I would definitely focus on fairly early on), you'll probably need to buy parts to replace all of the ones that you fail to repair. All of that aside, here is how I would evaluate the game:<br><br>The Good:<br>- Fixing up old cars and selling them is fantastic. This is where this game shines, and the enjoyment of this outweighs all of the negative points of this game put together. At the time of this review, I have clocked a little over 30 hours on the game, and I periodically return to it simply to do this. Although a little unrealistic, it's very satisfying to take something that's absolute garbage and get it into like-new condition, and there is a hefty bonus for selling a car that is in 100% condition.<br>- Leveling up your skills as a mechanic is now separate from money, which you will be needing for the purchase of cars when you get tired of doing work orders.<br>- Havng the garage means that you can hang onto a car that you really like, and it can be expanded with plenty of space for you to collect them all.<br>- Fixing parts is free, but it has the possibility of breaking them, as well. This means that many jobs can be done quite cheaply, and this further increases the potential for profit in selling cars.<br>- The game looks very nice and runs smoothly, at least on my computer.<br>- The core gameplay of removing and replacing parts is executed well, and most things are pretty efficient, although it would be nice to have a side-by-side for your inventory and the shop.<br><br>The Bad:<br>- There is very little to do with cars once they are fixed up, other than sell them or send them to the garage. However, I don't really like driving or racing games, so this has little bearing on my opinion of the game.<br>- Accessibility of parts is kind of an issue. Even if you are doing things in the most efficient way possible, you have to use the lift multiple times to get at every single part. The worst culprit by far is the crankshaft (and, by extension, the engine block). You have to use the lift once to remove the oil pan and the pieces keeping the crankshaft in place, another time to remove the pistons (and any other engine parts required to get at them), and once more to get the crankshaft out. Then, if you need to get at the engine block itself, it's a FOURTH go on the lift to get it out once the crankshaft has been removed. This is all a pretty minor gripe at the end of the day, and it may be fairly realistic, but it's still kind of annoying.<br>- Even with the convenience of the PDA, which makes it so that you can buy parts without having to run back to your desk, the shop menu is separate from your inventory screen. This means that it can be cumbersome to buy a lot of parts to replace those that you have broken when fixing up an old junker.<br>- Collecting oil requires you to be a sniper. Don't bother with it. Just pay the $100 cleanup fee.<br>- Parts that you are repairing break quite often. When fully leveled, you have a 90% chance to repair a part, but you do not FULLY repair it. This means that you roll those dice multiple times per part. They really should've had a decrease in condition by a random amount if that 10% chance comes up, but instead, the thing is totally borked. So if you have a part that is in poor condition so that you need to hit &quot;repair&quot; 4 times to get it fully to 100%, then you actually have a chance that is 90%*90%*90%*90%, or about 66% chance of getting it fully repaired and 34% chance of breaking it entirely and paying to replace it. That may not sound bad, but that is what it is like when you're at max level. <br>- DLC. Gross. Well, it's not AWFUL, but here's the thing: the &quot;Gold&quot; version of this game only includes some of the DLC. Most of the rest of it adds actual brand name vehicles to the lineup. So, since they have to pay to use those brands, it makes some sense. Even so, it leaves a bitter taste in my mouth to buy the Gold version and not even get the majority of the DLC.<br><br>The Ugly:<br>- The game actually looks very nice on the whole, but the textures for new parts sometimes doesn't say &quot;shiny chrome&quot; so much as it does &quot;plastic thing painted to look like shiny chrome.&quot;<br>- Some of the paint colors are pretty awful.... well, just like with real-life cars, truthfully. Many of them are quite nice, but it seems like you're more likely to wind up with something pretty ugly.
