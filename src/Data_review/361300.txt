initially seems, sound\/art design is fantastic, and it actually gets support from devs<br><br><br>One of the most brutal, rewarding beatemups out there. And I'm not only talking about the aesthetic, which is brutally beautiful in its own right. <br><br>First, to get it out of the way: Mother Russia Bleeds is not a particularly well-balanced game. It's a hard game on its normal difficulty, sometimes approaching the point of cheapness with the amount of enemies they can throw at you. This is especially hard when you're playing by yourself - the game can often turn into a game of managing space so you don't get annihilated - but this issue is mostly alleviated if you have a co-op partner. If you're new or casual to beatemups, go for Easy for your first run.<br><br>But make no mistake: co-op partner or not, if you put the time into this game you'll get the systems and it's incredibly satisfying to play. This is a beatemup with eight functional buttons, not including the d-pad\/stick movement and taunt button. Each player character shares the same hit\/grapple combos, but character stats, hitboxes sizes, and unique moves vary for each of the four characters. Enemy goons may require to be hit to death like you would think, but almost every enemy type will require a specific strategy to dispatch them without getting punished by their special ability. Some mooks prefer opening with slide kicks, others will automatically block punch combos, some will dash-grab into you, and some will divekick you. These enemy abilities may seem really annoying and cheap at first, but as you put time in the game and the challenge arenas, you learn how to deal with every single type with your tools of violence.<br><br>Let's talk about one of these tools, the dodge. Unlike a game like Double Dragon Neon, which has a dodge roll\/duck maneuever that has invulnerability frames (i-frames), Mother Russia Bleeds has a dash-dodge that works vertically and horizontally. This dash-dodge has no i-frames, which means you can't use it to cheese through most attacks thrown at you, with the exception of grabs. However, it can be used to escape enemy punch-combos, vertically avoid projectiles, create\/close horizontal space instantly as opposed to initiating a double-tap run command. Jumps, kicks, punches, and grapples can all be done off a run, and the dash adds a run-property to any move you do during it, essentially allowing you, say, slide-kick on command.<br><br>To the game's fault, they don't teach you these sorts of systems from the get-go. The tutorial honestly needs a lot of work and without a guide, you don't learn the pretty neat systems this game has to offer unless you pound your head and thumbs into pulp repeatedly like I did. But once you do...you keep coming back.<br><br>Gameplay aside, the other thing that this game really excels with is its presentation and sound. The game is almost entirely pixel-art from top to bottom, with evocative environments setting the stage and grotesque characters punching, taunting, and bloodying up as fights rage on. There is such a sheer quantity of different pixel art for mook types that it's pretty clear why this was in development for so long. The animations, however, are about Capcom beatemup standard at best, and don't expect anything like an SNK fighting game.<br><br>The sound design is another real treat here, which was all done by a single composer, Fixions. Hits are meaty and responsive, and audio cues for danger and opportunities are distinctive so they help serve the gameplay. While gameplay sound is relatively crisp and staccato, the music feels usually feels like a low-fi take on Pertubator, which fits in nicely with the grimy and brutal artistic design. The music also tends to use samples such as hearbeats, sirens, and chants to improve the atmosphere. <br><br>For $15 you get a campaign that lasts about 2-4 hours depending on the difficulty and your skill. It's a varied romp between different stages including a sewer, city streets, nightclub, and a really fun fight inside and on top of a moving train. Each level has a final boss that is never copy-pasted from previous bosses, and have their own unique moves, stage hazards, and openings to take advantage of. The campaign has a suprising emphasis on the writing for character dialogue between fights, even if the plot\/character development is pretty shallow and serves to set up fight after fight. The arena mode is probably where you'll spend a lot of the time if you enjoyed the campaign, serving up 10 stages with their own sets of mooks and stage hazards you'll have to deal with. It's honestly in the arena modes where you can learn the meat and potatoes of the game and you can also unlock alternate types of drugs that have different properties. Some drugs enhance your rage mode, others heal more for less charges, and some cause your enemies to explode like a grenade. Good times.<br><br>There's two more things I want to say about this game. First, it lacks online co-op, which can be a problem. Friends in the flesh are hard to come by these days. However, I will say this is a beatemup styled after games of yore, where it was a couch or arcade machine affair with an emphasis on difficulty and score; so, I'm more willing to forgive them for it. Second, this game actually gets support from the devs. One of the huge problems of ported beatemups like Double Dragon Neon or Shadows over Mystara is that post-launch support is poop. DDN is a great game but still really unstable to this day, and Shadows over Mystara only supports x-input, period. Le Cartel have actually taken the time to patch their game for bugs, controller support, and some minor balance improvements and I really appreciate this.<br><br>Congrats for making it to the end of my stupid long review of a $15 game. It isn't perfect, but it is a well-made game and I really enjoyed it for the reasons above.
