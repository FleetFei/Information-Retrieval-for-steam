than 4 hours beat, and I enjoyed every minute. I do think it is telling that nobody who reviewed this game seems to have more than 20 hours. Overall the game reminded me of the excellent game \u201cUndying\u201d <br><br>The Story<br><br>There is not much to the story but it is there. It starts you off with a storyboard, which makes little sense. You can find books, letters and plaques, with a few hints to the story. Nothing is really clear, other than that the stonehenge can weaken the barriers to other worlds -don\u2019t say it's magic-. I don't think it is very fitting, however. <br><br>The Graphics<br>People complain about the graphics in this, and they are not very realistic. I think they are nice and stylised and reminded me much of \u201cundying\u201d. There is fog, and it\u2019s all not very colourful, but I think the graphics well suit the game. There was graphical errors, but these had little effect and I think it is because it is randomly generated. You can make out things well, the robots and houses and such look appealing, the ground and the rocks could use some work. There is fog, and I can see some people being bothered by that. <br><br>The Gameplay<br>There are a few weapons(pistol, shotgun, rifle, blunderbuss, axe) which is a good thing, as inventory management is a big thing, and there is little space for many weapons and an effective load of ammo. Each has a defined purpose, and there is nothing else fitting that could be added and not feel unnecessary. It took me awhile to get used to combat, as the sensitivity was strange at all levels. New items don\u2019t spawn in, which mean you can\u2019t play around in one place too long. Some enemies are unkillable, and over all it feels like you are meant to avoid all enemies. Despite being open world and randomly generated, it feels limiting. These limits I would welcome should it not be the only mode <br> \t-Stealth<br>               Not like most games, there is no sneaking up behind people and stabbing them in the neck or what not (there is no one hit melee). Rather you are more like a powerless convict running from police. Areas are randomly patrolled, and there are a few options of distracting enemies. This is hard to do, and sometimes it feels just easier to kill them. The game does not pause when you loot, so distractions are even worse. There is bunch of guarded items you need to collect, I think only 2 of which I used a distraction. Some other reviewer said this game was more like guerrilla warfare, you hide, shoot a few shots, pick something up then run away. <br><br>\t-Combat <br>\t  Once you get used to it, it feels good. The health system is very forgiving, but once you run out of rags(which do not respawn) to bandage, you are dead. Eating food frequently will allow to regain health. The guns are powerful, but lack range, even for the sniper. Ammo and guns are rare, yet almost necessary. There is an ADS function that greatly improves damage and accuracy. Going in guns blazing never really works even on small groups later in the game, and if you chose a real class, you will only have a gun by then. If there is something you really want, you can try to sneak up to it, distract the guards or pick one off, hide, pick another one off, hide\u2026. It is repetitive, but also fun.<br><br> \t-Inventory<br>You can keep thing in homes without them despawning, so you are encouraged to keep caches of food and ammo in easy to reach spots. Nothing knew spawns in, yet you can discard things, so watch out. There are 3 types of loot: junk, dubious and useable. Junk does nothing, dubious hurts your hunger when used, and useable is what you want. I at first wondered why there was useless items, but I figure it is to keep you looting and exposed longer. People complain about not being able to go in homes, yet being able to enter them would ruin the game. It is never worth it to kill every one guarding lootables, so you need to sneak up on them, and loot what you can quickly before you are seen, to be able to enter the homes would break this. Judging whether it is worth the risk is one of the biggest aspects about this game. <br>Horror<br>The game was unconventionally scary just like its stealth. You hide behind a stone wall as a clunky robot flies past, wonder to yourself if it will see you kinda scary, no jump scares or anything. I know the point of the game is to avoid enemies, but the ones that i felt would have been scary i just avoided.  I think more horror would have improved this game. <br><br>Other Notes<br> Over all it is a beautiful game, I just love to see a line of hunters march in a line thru the long grass. However it is kind of niche, and the novelty of it wears off. There is just not enough game for it to be worth 20$. I encountered no bugs, and it felt finished.  It took me 4 hours to beat it, and I was not rushing. The Devs claim the game has replayability do to its randomness, however when the game is so straightforward do to its one goal and limited supplies, that replayability is questionable. The is no other modes. There is starting classes, and that's all to make each game different, other than the map not being the same. This game is dying for more modes. <br><br>It's a great game that will take up an afternoon, but for 20 bucks? random or procedural elements, but it never occurred to me to apply those principles to a stealth game. I always thought stealth games needed to be tightly tuned, with carefully-designed levels and sharp AIs on patrol routes and such. Sir is very much a stealth game that benefits from randomness, but not without some significant drawbacks.<br><br>Sir drops you into the middle of five randomly-generated islands, each populated by murderous metallic monsieurs out for your human hide. A mysterious device brought you here, and is now scattered into 16 pieces across the archipelago. Each piece must be returned to the standing stones at the center of the center isle if you are to return home. To aid you in your task, there's a whole host of items to scavenge up, some useful, some not. As you find more pieces, the robots step up their efforts, including robo-dogs, scarecrow sentries, hot air balloons, and more monstrous machines to hunt you down.<br><br>The robots are the heart of this game, and they are at times terrifying. Sure, they make conspicuous beep-boop noises as you draw near, but once they spot you with that heart-stopping bleep, you're in deep trouble. The 'bots are almost as fast as you, but they have guns and are pretty good shots with them. They're also intelligent about chasing you, following as far as they saw you go and then making educated guesses about where to search from there. Most robot patrol the islands in groups, paying close attention to structures that can be scavenged but following and suspicious leads they find. Other robots mill around the artifacts you need to recover, making sure you have to deal with some of them to get home. Getting caught can spell disaster if you can't get away fast enough, or if they plug you with a lucky shot that leaves you bleeding out.<br><br>You have two important numbers to keep track of, Health and Vitality. Either one bottoms out, you're done. Health regens slowly unless you've been shot, in which case you need to bandage yourself up using an item. Vitality drains over time with activity, and must be restored with food. And THAT means risking patrols to sneak into abandoned villages and churches to loot. You can't enter any structure in the game; the doors to buildings serve as loot containers. It's a bit of a blow to immersion and options for hiding, but other than that the loot system is solid. You have limited space to store weapons and traps, food and bandages, and any other interesting odds and ends you find. It's up to you to figure out what use some items have, which adds an element of mystery and experimentation to the game.<br><br>You can only save at the standing stones or the boats to other islands, so you really have to think twice about risky moves. Stealth in this game is tricky... it seems more based on proximity than action, and the robots are good about looking over their shoulders. Proper sneaking takes a long time given the distances, and waiting for patrols to move on is the safest but most time-consuming route. I'm still learning how to deal with them, but honestly I've been able to run by and grab a few artifacts right in front of them without being murdered. Getting caught can be frustrating though, given how hard the robots are to lose. You can fight back if you find weapons, but combat is not a focus and can be pretty fiddly. And in terms of navigation, you have a compass that marks the boats, but if you want to get any additional use out of it you have to find a map and mark it up yourself with points of interest. <br><br>Is it a good game? Yes, but one you have to put time into. Finding 16 fragments across 5 islands is going to take time, even moreso if you play the stealth game right, scavenge diligently, and use your map to the fullest. The randomization keeps the game fresh but can lead to situations where you're trapped between patrols for extended periods or find artifacts in nigh-impossible locations. It's not a particularly attractive game either, with some low-poly models and muddy textures. Your goal -- to travel to each island (five in all) and acquire pieces of a machine to transport you to safety. All the while, you have to mind your hunger and scavenge for items like food, bullets, weapons, and alarm clocks. The islands are procedurally generated with each game with a choice of one of five biome types. I really like the procedural islands; each biome is different and they feel natural and well thought out. On the default setting, the game starts with a few robot  hunters and, as you collect more pieces, ramps up the difficulty until finding those last pieces becomes seriously difficult.<br><br>The good and the bad (caveat: I haven't finished the game yet because of the serious difficulty mentioned above):<br><br>Good:<br>Procedural generation: I'm a sucker for it, my favourite game genre of all time is roguelikes, and this is roguelike-y in random generation of the islands, the buildings and the enemies.<br><br>Stealth: this game is at heart very stealth oriented, inasmuch as even if you do find weapons going in with guns blazing, even on the lower levels, is not a winning strategy. It's entirely possible to play the game without shooting any weapon at all, which I find refreshing.<br><br>Graphics: I game on Linux, which okay, I'm already at a disadvantage, and I game with a low-endish business model Thinkpad, which again, problematic, but the game is smooth as anything on my machine and still is graphically nice to look at. Don't try to play without having your sound on; you'll get clobbered. You'll need to hear the robots approaching -- each type has a distinctive sound -- in order to avoid them. There's an option in the game to turn on an indicator, which puts a little Metal Gear Solid-ish &quot;!&quot;, &quot;?&quot; above the robots' heads to gauge how aware they are of you, and this is *invaluable* - at least if you're playing the game without headphones -- but even so you'll have to listen hard to survive.<br><br>Repeatability: There are *lots* of options to tune the robots appearances and numbers. I haven't messed with this yet as I want to finish the game on the default, but it's great to have something beyond &quot;Easy, Medium, Hard&quot; for difficulty.<br><br>Gender: It's a small thing, but the fact that you can pick your gender is a good one. More games should do this.<br><br>Bad:<br>Stealth: hey wait, didn't I just put stealth in the &quot;good&quot;? Yeah. But in this game you spend a *lot* of time crouched in reeds or ducking behind buildings while all the while your vitality trickles to zero. This does present a nice amount of tension to the game, but the islands are *big* muthas and finding the pieces without the piece detector is hard enough; trying to find them blind is *really* hard. And when you've spent hours combing an island to find that last piece, while all the while the *hordes* of robots that populate the endgame are shooting you, trapping you in bear traps, running you over with rocket horses, and biting your foot it gets real frustrating real fast.<br><br>Sound: I have a mild hearing disability and I don't particularly like games that are super dependent on sound, so I sort of resent the fact that I *have* to have sound turned on in this one. Not sure how it would work without it though.<br><br>Items: The primary way one gets ahold of things is by searching buildings for them, and there are a *lot* of junk items you can't actually do anything with. I would love an option to turn off junk items because it's annoying to go to a door and just find bones. You can't do anything with junk -- not even throw it as a distraction -- so I'm not sure why the designers included the junk items in the game at all. As far as I can see, they serve no purpose beyond annoying window dressing.<br><br>Buildings: The buildings look great, but you can't go inside them. They exist solely as a cache for (mostly) junk items. The game would be improved greatly if you could enter a building and peek out a window, or have, say, fixed items to heal damage, like beds or something. Instead they're basically giant crates.<br><br>I'd say, eh, three and half stars out of five to four stars out of five depending on your tolerance for frustration in the difficult
