The game has to have a decent balance of both platforming elements and controls as well as puzzle elements to keep fans of both genres entertained and satisfied. Aero\u2019s Quest attempts to achieve just that. From screenshots and videos it seems more platforming than puzzle at first glance, but let\u2019s take a deeper look to see if Aero\u2019s Quest can bring out the best of both genres.<br><br>Aero\u2019s Quest revolves around Aero who is attempting to save his girlfriend Ariella who has been taken captive by a fearsome android named Andraus. After Andraus captured Ariella, he also captured Aero and partially turned him into a machine before dumping him back into the woods.  Now Aero must prove his love to Ariella before Andraus can rip it apart. In other words, it\u2019s your basic story of \u201cboy likes girl, villain also likes girl, villain takes the girl, and you have to rescue her\u201d.<br><br>The game is comprised one hundred stages which is spread across five areas, each separated into two parts with ten stages in each. In each stage you\u2019ll find anywhere from one to several red switches on the ground. To complete each stage you have to turn on each switch by stepping on it which opens a cage at the end that contains Ariella, then reach the cage only to have Andraus take her away. Kind of Meat Boy-esque in a way. Unsurprisingly it\u2019s not typically as straight forward as that as you\u2019ll have to occasionally find keys to open locks to some of the switches, dodge enemies, and basically find the best way to accomplish all of this without getting hit even once.<br><br>The early stages are simple enough; hit the switches and get out. As the game progresses you\u2019ll be introduced to different enemies and obstacles you have to avoid such as cannons and rockets that shoot out from the walls, as well as a few different power-ups to collect such as temporarily being able to jump higher or the ability to slow down time.  These add some interesting mechanics to the game, but at times can seem a bit misplaced. I\u2019ve seen a couple of times that I\u2019ve had to backtrack through some obstacles and the game gives you a stealth power-up to pass right through them, as it would be very difficult otherwise, but then a stage or two later you have a very similar scenario and the game gives you a Time Warp power-up instead. Granted that slows down time but with the fire rates and speeds of some of the bombs and rockets, it\u2019s still difficult to find the right moment to proceed without getting blown up, even with time slowed down.<br><br>The controls aren\u2019t too bad but they feel a bit tight and loose at the same time. The controls are very responsive and you can even change directions in the air on a whim, but the jumping itself is kind of floaty. What I mean by that is when you jump you kind of float back down a bit as opposed to realistically descending at a normal rate. It\u2019s hard to put into words but think of various classic games such as Mario and Mega Man, or even more recent games like Axiom Verge.  When you jump up you come down at a reasonable rate.  Here it\u2019s a bit more slow and can throw off your timing or make various obstacles hard to avoid.  I swear I had one stage early on in which I died close to two dozen times at a set of steps that launched out rockets because I kept mistiming my landing speed and end up landing right on a rocket.<br><br>Graphically the game looks okay.  I wouldn\u2019t say it\u2019s anything to write home about, but it\u2019s not bad either.  It fits the game pretty nicely, but there is one thing that I have to complain about, and that\u2019s the foreground.  The game has around one hundred stages taking place across several different environments, but sometimes the foreground and area effects can actually hinder things, such as in the volcanic area.  I appreciate the art as well as the heat effects they put in, but on numerous occasions they\u2019ve actually hindered my progress and I\u2019ve lost several lives because the foreground blocked my vision of what was ahead.<br><br>In terms of replay value there\u2019s quite a bit if you can manage to clear the stages themselves first. However, if you fulfill any of these requirements when you first play through the stage while going through the story, you\u2019ll already have that star completed when you go back to it later.<br><br>Finally there\u2019s a speedrun mode which lets you basically rush through the game in two different ways: either the entire game as a whole or each set of ten stages (keep in mind that each of the five areas has two parts of ten stages each).  I like this as outside of the full game run you only have to worry about ten stages, but there are two things I thought would have been done differently.  First, I\u2019m a little curious as to why you can do each individual part, but not the area as whole. Second, you can even do the areas that you haven\u2019t even been to yet.  I figured that like when going for the achievement stars you\u2019d have to have an area available in the story to speed run it, but apparently not.  I was able to go straight to the last area in Speed Run mode before I was anywhere near it in Story mode\u2026not that I recommend it, I died a lot.<br><br>In the end, Aero\u2019s Quest is a fun and challenging game if you like puzzle-platformers, but it\u2019s not without its flaws and a couple of them can frustrate some players quickly.The foreground elements can on a few occasions get in your way, but not to the point that it\u2019s a major hindrance for the entire game.The jumping is probably the worst area for me and, again, it\u2019s not game breaking or anything, but I felt like a lot of the deaths weren\u2019t necessarily because of me, but because of how the game handles the jump physics.
