kitsunebi,<br>Cooler than square bass.<br><br>One of the most telling signs, in my opinion at least, that a game designer is doing something really right is when I can spot his or her fingerprints immediately on a game. Eric Chahi's fingerprints are all over Another World and Heart of Darkness, for example, but one can feel his presence in Flashback's DNA, too, even if it's clear that the game wasn't his own work. Jordan Mechner's cinematic eye is similarly unmistakable, and elements of it can be felt most powerfully on another game from this author...but Larry Stover (aka Blaze Epic) rightly holds his place as a game visionary of his own, and his titles, though influenced by Mechner, have a distinct flavor of their own, and can be recognized by his fingerprints as readily as Chahi's and Mechner's titles can be recognized by theirs. <br><br>If Mechner is Cheb Khaled, then Stover is Rachid Taha, a little wilder, a little earthier, a little more raw and visceral, and a little less constrained by form. The ponderousness of the flesh evident in the inertia of Mechner's unnamed Karateka and Prince is remembered but not heeded. Blaze Epic's games feature protagonists that soar, float, defy gravity, and whose true capabilities grow apparent only when you silence the empirical &quot;traffic cop&quot; chasing after them, demanding they stop making a mockery of physics. These games have flow, have a tight, bespoke timing and range of motion that would remind one of a slightly less punishing Ninja Gaiden (the NES version in particular), and a large part of their reward sets in once you enter their &quot;zone,&quot; and flow with the almost-flying protagonist. The controls of the game feel strange and alien until you learn to move with them, and then you're Zatoichi for a glorious moment.<br><br>Blaze Epic's games share a few common qualities; they're all finesse-driven platformers with a decidedly retro graphics style. (I taste SMS influence in particular, as the Jump\/Boxer cover art suggests.) Significant, small differences tune the physics and flow of each title, but there will be much jumping, and the games' magic comes from learning what one can do with jumps. There will be many frustrating deaths. There will be many &quot;missed it by that much&quot; moments. Seeing the next screen is a palpable reward. For all that the simple combat controls seem, at first glance, to be button-spammingly simple, success in the game is much more about finding rhythms than rapidly hammering triggers. The controls are frustratingly literal in the way anyone fond of third-generation consoles will immediately recognize and love-while-hating. <br><br>Having set the stage, let me tell you why Shin Samurai Jazz is my favorite of these titles.<br><br>The world of Shin Samurai Jazz is a noir pulp novel steeped in Jazz Age chic, with a rather Moby-esque low-key electrojazz soundtrack that anchors one in tones of rainy streets at night, distant music and laughter, secrets and mysteries. The atmosphere drips, and the mixture of goons and yokai encountered, with their markedly different combat styles, keep the fights interesting and particularly varied. Yes, you can try to power through by hammering the attack button, but learning your deceptively varied move set and employing it wisely has proven to be a far more useful strategy, in my experience. Once you really get the hang of dashing back and forth, dropping in and out of stance, and hurling enemies aloft for airborne slicing and dicing while taking advantage of the chuunori-inspired jump-and-dash physics, you'll appreciate just how much variety there can be in a one-attack-button game, and how much of a difference learning your moves makes.<br><br>The jump-puzzle elements work much the same way; learning your moveset, and slipping &quot;into the zone&quot; with the game's physics, is instrumental. You'll practice double-jumps, jump-and-dash, wall rebounding, wall climbing and sliding, and folks who just rush in and spam the jump button will find some of the trickier levels quite daunting. Understanding one's range of moves, and their relative advantages, is key. That's true, to a degree, in every game, but Shin Samurai Jazz elevates this to a real brightly-polished art. For all that you have a limited number of buttons and moves to worry about, knowing them, and knowing when and how to use them effectively, plays a large role in the game. So many &quot;retro&quot; action-jump-and-run titles focus so heavily on timing and pixel-perfect placement that they neglect to reward good mastery of one's range of options, and good planning, aside from pitch-perfect execution. Whether Shin Samurai Jazz is maddening or simply satisfyingly tricky is, in my experience at least, more a question of how well you speak its language.<br><br>Jumping, wall-rebounding, and clever timing play a role in many Blaze Epic games. If you find you'd like simpler combat and more of those deathtrap jumping levels, the Ninjahtic series will probably make you quite happy, and if you'd like even more of a nod to Mechner, The Way Of The Pixelated Fist is his most recognizable love letter to Karateka and Prince of Persia. Shin Samurai Jazz, however, remains my favorite of the titles, for its compelling atmosphere and flavor, and for its surprisingly versatile and mood-reinforcing moveset.<br><br>I enjoyed the NES Ninja Gaiden back in the day, quite a bit. Shin Samurai Jazz brings back much of what I loved about it, while fixing a few of the things I really didn't, like the very trouble-prone wallgrab and climb.<br><br>Finally, I don't normally discuss price as a factor, but the dollar-or-two pricing scheme of Blaze Epic's games really bumps them to must-owns, if the retro action-platformer with third-gen-console-style difficulty appeals to you. This one or Ninjahtic would both make excellent introductions to the Blaze Epic game family, and as fond of I am of Ninjahtic, I'd personally give Shin Samurai Jazz a recommendation over it, as I think it expresses not just the game mechanics, but the thematic and atmospheric je-ne-sais-quoi, that makes Blaze Epic titles so lovely.<br><br>You make a name for yourself as a creator by sticking your neck out, owning a &quot;flavor&quot; and saying, &quot;This is what I'm about.&quot; Blaze Epic's games have quite a recognizable tone and character, and of course, tastes will differ, and this little storm of third-gen-consoles and remixed-Japan will not appeal to everyone. They'll surely appeal to some, though, myself emphatically counted among their numbers, and if you're even remotely curious as to whether they're for you, this is a splendid straight-into-the-deep-end countless deaths I completed Shin Samurai Jazz, a mash-up of retro sensibilites and the more modern trend of masochistic platforming. At the low price of a dollar this makes for an easy recommendation but the game is not for everyone. If you are frustrated easily or have no affection for classic game design you should probably avoid this title. On the flip side, if you like a challenge and long for the days of gaming on an NES or SMS than I think this is a true gem of a game and an absolute steal at a dollar. Chances are if you watched the preview video here on Steam and thought &quot;That looks amazing&quot; than you will love this game once you adjust to the controls.<br><br>A lot of indie games these days go the &quot;Retro&quot; route because it is the &quot;In thing&quot; or  considered &quot;easy&quot; but I don't find that to be the case here. This game was clearly crafted by someone with a great admiration for classic game design and personally I think it feels right. Authentic, if you will. If you appreciate that than by all means give the game a shot. <br><br>At first play the controls will feel a bit stiff but give it some time. Once you get a handle on things you will be flying around the screen deftly avoiding spikes as you make your way through the architectural death traps and easily take out groups of foes. I had no issues at all with the controls once I grasped how everything worked. Learning when to jump and when to air dash is all part of the fun and when it clicks you will be begging for more. <br><br>Combat is somewhat simple but offers enough to keep it fresh I think. You have one attack button and pressing that in succession performs a basic combo that can knock some enemies back. I loved that enemy bodies will damage anyone in their path. It was especially fun to knock an enemy into another and send them flying off the platform into spikes. You can also perform a dash attack by simply dashing and attacking and this too will knock some foes back. Finally you can hold up and press attack to send an enemy into the air for a nice juggle combo and yes, his falling body can smack into other enemies. And that is about it for the most part. Like many games of the 8bit era, a lot of the combat comes down to learning a foe's pattern and then putting that knowledge to use. Many times enemies will be placed in ways to make you rethink your typical strategies and keep you on your toes. While we aren't talking about a super in-depth combat system I feel that it never wears out its welcome and is far better than it may seem at first glance.<br><br>That being said, the bulk of the gameplay is  definitely the spike-filled platforming. You will be using every trick up your sleave to make it through some of these death traps and later stages can be quite brutal. You will die and a few may frustrate you but I never once felt the game was unfair. When I died it was because I wasn't good enough to complete that section not because of something designed to fool you. These platforming segments are well designed and there are no surprises. It may ask a bit of your skill but you can leave your luck at home. The one complaint I'd levy at the game is found within these platforming sections but since it is consistant I can't really dock the game for it. Still, I felt I should mention it. Should your feet dip below the bottom of the screen you will die, regardless if a well timed double jump or dash could have saved you. This accounted for most of my deaths but again, it is designed this way and is consistant so once I got used to it it wasn't as big of an issue. <br><br>A smaller porttion of the gameplay is the adventuring segments. This generally involves entering buildings and recieving cryptic hints on where to head next. Get a hamburger bun so the restaurant can make a burger for you to give a guy who will trade you his key. It is really a minor diversion meant to break up the flow of the game and keep it feeling fresh. I love that nothing is spelled out for you but there is nothing really all that taxing here with few opportunities to actually get lost. It is within these sections that you can save your game. Simply enter the market and talk to the worker. You have three slots.<br><br>A quick snippet on the difficulty. As I said, it is tough but fair. I've heard complaints of checkpoints being too far apart but I don't find that to be the case. Each room is designed to be completed in one go and dying at any point in a room puts you at the start of that room. That there are infinite retries and it starts instantly upon death with no waiting period cuts back on any feelings of anger or tedium. Look at it like a run in one of the Trials games.  If you are the type to save state your way through emulated NES games though, the game may seem cruel. If you were used to gaming on the actual hardware back when it was current, well, you might find the infinite retries a bit easy. Either way, I stand by my assessment that the difficutly is high but fair.<br><br>The music is great and very relaxing. It suits the game well and compliments the action by offering a nice mellow atmosphere when you are risking death constantly. The look of the game is either amazing or putrid depending on your outlook. I love it. It really captures that 8bit feel  and the design is right up my alley. My only issue is that the spikes can somethimes blend into the background due to color choices.<br><br>Just want to touch on the controls for a second. I highly recommend using a 360 pad as I can't imagine some of these areas on a keyboard. The main menu strangely is controlled by the number keys on the keyboard. Once you get in-game you can perform everything else via the controller save for...saving... which is handled via the numbers as well. If you do not have a 360 controller you may want to avoid the game. I can't in good faith recommend it on a keyboard.<br><br>Overall, I had a great time with this title and will be buying the dev's other games right away. There is plenty of game here for an absurdly low price. If you have even an inkling that you would like this than I urge you to give it a shot.
