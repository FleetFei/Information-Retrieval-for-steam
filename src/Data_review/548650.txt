class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"bb_h1\">Basic Information<\/div><b>Title:<\/b>  Lightspeed Frontier<br><b>Status:<\/b>  Early Access<br><b>Developer:<\/b>  Crowdwork Studios<br><b>Publisher:<\/b>  Riveted Games<br><b>Genre:<\/b>  3D Space Simulation<br><b>Release Date:<\/b>  7th of March, 2017<br><b>Type:<\/b>  Singleplayer <br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Introduction<\/div>This is going to be my first (out of many, I hope) preview for a Steam title, instead of a review. What does it mean? Well, that \u201cP\u201d is an important distinction. You wouldn\u2019t mistake Prussia for Russia and in the same manner, my previews shall differentiate from reviews, by not containing a final rating. The games in question have been provided by the developers for a more informative role than my usual constructive criticism. Anyway, I\u2019d like to think that I was never too harsh in my writings and that you will enjoy reading even my occasional previews. I shall not skimp on the details, no matter how often I have to adapt my style to the needs of my sponsors, so you, as my readers can always expect an in-depth analysis from me.<br><br>Lightspeed Frontier is a 3D Space Sim that wishes to offer an open-ended experience for its players, within a singleplayer mode designed to accept both randomly generated assets and complex customization options for the spacecraft itself. An ambitious project from a debutante dev team, but so far they haven\u2019t given me any reason to doubt their potential to actually come through with their first Steam game. If you know me by now, you probably noticed that I rarely care for the multiplayer component and even if there are many space sims on Steam or otherwise, few of them come close to the liberty and exploration found in 2003\u2019s Freelancer. That was singleplayer experience that combined trade, combat, intuitive control scheme and an intriguing cast of character &amp; storyline, with the sheer vastness of space. And no, Freelancer is not available on Steam or any other digital distribution platform.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Story <\/div>It\u2019s an obvious Early Access stage in which the developer wanted to focus on the core gameplay mechanics, before adding a proper story. Lightspeed Frontier has some mission elements implemented already, but they aren\u2019t yet consolidated enough to form narrative coherence. Disparate assignments that have you warp jumping from one solar system to another and reaching some coordinates that frequently prove to be elaborate traps involving space pirates. It gets old fast but I\u2019m certain the fully released version of the game shall offer a campaign worthy of Lightspeed Frontier\u2019s potential.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Graphics<\/div>Judging from the title\u2019s launcher, I presume it\u2019s running on Unity Engine 5 assets so eye candy is more or less present along with enough customization options to satisfy even the most creative digital spacecraft designer\/player. The ship components act like near-literal building blocks that can be manipulated to such an extent, to which you can recreate famous space ships found in Sci-Fi films or TV series. And there are already a handful of skillfully crafted examples, ready to be downloaded from Lightspeed Frontier\u2019 Workshop page.<br><br>Performance-wise, the game is running maxed out and at 4K resolution without so much as a frame-rate dip if you use anything other than a huge ship design (which would still require copious amounts of in-game currency). I can imagine that running the game at only 1080p or 2K resolutions, ensures buttery smooth performance no matter what gets shown or blown to bits, on your monitor. So the lack of glitches or frame rate instability proves that Lightspeed Frontier is not your typical Early Access indie game on Steam. This dev team isn\u2019t beta testing its game on your own expense and spare time.<br><br> <div class=\"bb_h1\">Audio<\/div>Sounds might need an overhaul and perhaps even the soundtrack would benefit from a few added songs. The ones already present tend to repeat themselves way too often. Also there\u2019s no voice acting whatsoever. Not for the tutorial or even the chat of sorts, being simulated. It\u2019s all a much too silent playthrough and so far, a missed opportunity for some really epic tracks. Space sims deserve them almost as much as games belonging to the fantasy genre.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Gameplay<\/div>As soon as you start the game, you can pick beween Campaign and Endless modes. True, no unified story is present yet and the collection of slightly repetitive missions may not seem like much, but exploration is encouraged regardless. Space stations represent crucial hubs for any activities you undertake in Lightspeed Frontier. These massive orbiting structures feature everything you need. A marketplace is the perfect spot to unload the minerals you mine and components you capture after a space battle. It is here, where you can buy new \u201cmodules\u201d which act as spare parts that help upgrade your ship by your heart\u2019s desire. When you\u2019re not trading in the classical term, you can try your \u201cluck\u201d with the in-game stock market, which features a simplified version of what real life brokers have to deal with. It\u2019s still a nice addition and shows that the developer wants to add personality to the game with the inclusion of stock prices for their faux brands (that are also highlighted through billboards on the space stations themselves).<br><br>Stations feature refueling docks and resupplying your ship, costs Wex (the game\u2019s currency) but repairing the vessel seems to be free of charge. I\u2019m not complaining since earning enough cash in Lightspeed Frontier isn\u2019t an easy task, when you constantly have to replace the destroyed bits and pieces you lose after most battles. For all their extra armor you may install, the ships still seem fragile and the weaponry present already is divided in three categories from lowest damage output to the highest: gatling gun (bullets), laser beam and plasma shot. Three is apparently the magic number for current ship style\/template, with themes such as rusty brown metal plates with yellow lights, modern white\/aluminum look with shades of blue and my favorite, the darker design which features lots of red to go with those black plates. Nothing spells \u201cfriendly trader\u201d more than recreating Star Wars' Slave I or a Star Trek Borg Cube (if only green lights were available).<br><br>As I mentioned above, when you\u2019re not blasting randomly spawned foes (there aren\u2019t any neutral or allied vessels yet), you can earn Wex by mining the asteroid belts around certain planets you find in most of the solar systems you visit. The game\u2019s map is huge to say the least so I do hope it may get filled with activities and special locations and characters eventually. As with most other controls, travelling from one system to another is as easy as opening the map and clicking on the warp command. Going from one planet to another is also as easy as engaging the warp mode and pointing your mouse cursor towards your intended destination. Even players that aren\u2019t accustomed to space simulations, can simply pick this title up and Lightspeed Frontier\u2019s tutorial shall prepare them for any challenge ahead.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Verdict<\/div>Final rating or not, I still recommend you purchase Lightspeed Frontier even in its current Early Access stage. Its lack of a storyline or campaign is vehemently offset by the presence of a stable and diversified gameplay which won\u2019t bore you. On the contrary, should you wish to craft a space ship worthy of your dreams, you can even do so with the safety and unlimited budget present in the Endless mode. As you will find out, nothing\u2019s gonna stop you from exploring the ever shifting universe of Lightspeed Frontier and no, the sky was never the limit!<br><br><blockquote
