originality and genuinely chilling content. For a low-budget horror game, it was basically flawless.<br><br>Its sequel, Underworld, threw away every ounce of ingenuity and decided to copy all the other horror games - we had a bunch of Slenderman-esque chaser sequences and that nightmare 'maze' which might go down as some of the worst game design I've ever suffered.<br><br>Unfortunately Saibot just ignored any critical comments from those who'd been disappointed by the sequel, and provided no support or answers.<br><br>Ergo I was wary about going anywhere near them for Holy Mountains of Flesh, especially after the early access version had been merely 'ok' and had failed to actually follow on from the previous game at all.<br><br>I needn't have worried - I took the risk on this, needing something new to play, and so far it has erased the memory of Underworld, and while not quite reaching the heights of the original, comes pretty close.<br><br>Once again you are private investigator Thomas Foster (brilliantly voiced I have to say) of the psychic detective agency Doorways, and once again you are in the mind of serial killers to find out what really happened. Everything Underworld got wrong is erased here, and there are some really good moments in this, with the feel of the mountainous village of hell really working well - you feel like you're in a hell, and it's intense.<br><br>I have not finished the whole game, but thus far I'm pretty impressed by what I'm seeing here. <br><br>Saibot's customer care could be a lot better, and their consistency is lacking, but when they get it right, it really is worth your time.<br><br>EDIT (13\/08\/16):<br><br>I have now fully completed the final retail version, and have to admit Saibot rectified everything they got wrong with both customer care re: Underworld and the flaws with the game itself, and really did produce a fine third chapter in Mountains of Flesh, a fitting end to the series.<br><br>They really listened to us during Early Access and incorporated a great deal of the constructively critical feedback to make the final game a polished and thoroughly enjoyable game, and I cannot speak more highly of their hands-on approach this time around.<br><br>Holy Mountains of Flesh is a psychological horror, and lacks contrived jump scares, instead going for foreboding doom. It works brilliantly as it did with Prelude, and they even added some cinematics to give us even more story. <br><br>This is one of those times where promise in Early Access is truly delivered on with the final retail game.<br><br>If you like good horror, and accept it's not marquee budget, you could do far worse than both this game, and its whole franchise.
