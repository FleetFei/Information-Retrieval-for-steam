version of a fast playing card game. Imagine a 3x3 grid, with an obstacle in the middle. Each turn, you play two cards into that grid. No, it's not Tic Tac Toe, bear with me. Each card represents a supply route, an attack range, and a support fire range. The supply route dictates where you can play, you must keep a supply chain from your side of the base to where you seek to play. If the card has an attack range that overlaps an enemy card when you play, and you have another card's support range overlapping that card, you can attack with that placement, destroying an enemy card. Only two card plays per turn, yes, but these are two incredibly stressful plays where you and your opponent are engaged in a proverbial knife fight in a phone booth, trying to wreck each other's placements and advance as best you can with each play, trying to squeeze every ounce of firepower you can out of these two simple plays. Because, really, you're only three cards from the opponent's base at all times, and it is incredibly easy to forget that if you try to advance up one side and leave the opponent with one card on the table.<br><br>The game itself runs fine. Only played AI but the AI is solid. Online play is on offer, in the mobile sense of you make a move and wait for the opponent to sign back in and make a move, which absolutely murders the pace of the game on PC but works just fine for mobile. The rules link just goes to a PDF at the official site. The digital implementation feels a little bare bones, but there is not much to complain about beyond that; you have music, decent thematic animations...<br><br>All in all, this is an incredibly smart Lines<\/div>The Battle for Sector 219 is a strategical card game where two players vie for control of a capital city. Utilizing various troop cards, you must maintain supply lines alongside attacking enemy units, all in an effort to advance towards the enemy base while defending your own. The push &amp; pull style of gameplay where any single turn can mean defeat keeps these games short; however, complexity is still very much present. Although The Battle for Sector 219 is really fun and unique, highly desirable game features that are currently absent, along with a lack of depth due to a weak variety of cards keep Sector 219 from being something truly great. <br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Gameplay<\/div><u><b>Rules<\/b><\/u><br>The complete rules for The Battle for Sector 219 can be found <a class=\"bb_link\" href=\"https:\/\/steamcommunity.com\/linkfilter\/?url=http:\/\/sector219.com\/rules.pdf\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\" >[here]<\/a> on the developer\u2019s official website. When you start up The Battle for Sector 219 in Steam, the \u201cHow to Play\u201d (tutorial) will just present you with a slideshow of that website. It is a very weak introduction to the game, akin to throwing you into the deep end of a pool, but automatic rule enforcement along with highlighted spaces\/enemies alleviate the burden. After playing a couple times, everything will make sense and things will start to click. For those that just want a basic idea of the game, I\u2019ll present the bare bones edition below.<br><br>A single card sits in the middle of a virtual, dynamic board (no definite boundaries, expands as you place cards). This single card represents the world\u2019s capital. Your base is permanently positioned immediately below that card, and your opponent\u2019s base is mirrored accordingly. Cards are placed on an imaginary grid, adjacent to each other. The goal of the game is to place one of your troop cards onto your opponent\u2019s base spot. <br><br>Each troop card will have 3 different stats representing it: supply direction, attack direction, and support direction. The supply direction dictates where that troop can be placed (adjacent to a previously placed card, diagonally, away from other troops, etc.). The attack direction dictates where that troop can attack once placed onto the board. The support direction can be thought of as spaces of continuous suppressing fire provided by that placed unit.<br><br>An enemy will be destroyed if a unit attacks (which it can do only when initially placed on the board) while that particular enemy is also under support fire. There are some cards that deviate from this, such as the Artillery, which can automatically destroy an enemy one or two spaces in front of it. 2 Airstrike cards are also provided to each player that can be used to destroy any enemy on any space. Exceptions to all of the above rules and additional complications are presented in the above linked website.<br><br><u><b>Multiplayer<\/b><\/u><br>Online multiplayer is supported with a matchmaking system; however, local coop is not. The matchmaking system is automatic (no lobbies or private matches) and asynchronous play can be utilized \u2013 make a move, leave, come back and continue. Also, online\/solo play has no option to create a custom game: modify time limit, number of rounds, card limits, etc. <br><br>For those that are wondering, there also doesn't seem to be much of an online community to play against. To be fair, The Battle for Sector 219 is fairly new so that is to be expected, but due to the lack of depth (explained in the next section), it would have had a hard time maintaining a solid player base anyways, especially without continuous support (additional troop cards, game features, etc.).<br><br><u><b>Lack of Features<\/b><\/u><br>The Battle for Sector 219 definitely feels like an Early Access game, or one that is still in its beta build, even though the game is <b>fully released<\/b>. I understand that this is the virtualization of a physical tabletop game so there is only so much the developer can do I suppose; however, it doesn't excuse the lack of very basic\/QOL features that The Battle for Sector 219 desperately needs. Some of the missing features include:<br><br><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li><u>No local pass &amp; play coop<\/u>.<br><\/li><li><u>No deck customization<\/u>: Each player has a deck of 26 cards. There are only 5 different cards, not including airstrikes. The lack of variance is disconcerting.<br><\/li><li><u>Only 1 AI difficulty level<\/u>: After acclimating to the rules, the game becomes a little too easy. There isn\u2019t much of a challenge, or even any surprises for that matter, as you continue to pummel the AI enemy. To be fair, the AI doesn\u2019t make any stupid moves\/mistakes, but a couple common strategies will always do the trick.<br><\/li><li><u>Inability to host\/play custom matches<\/u>.<\/li><\/ul><br><u><b>Video Gameplay<\/b><\/u><br><a class=\"bb_link\" href=\"https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=SceHdmqDGws\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noreferrer\"  id=\"dynamiclink_0\">https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=SceHdmqDGws<\/a> <br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Verdict<\/div>I can easily see the tabletop version being a blast to play. The game mechanics themselves are very interesting and unique; however, the PC version offers essentially no online play (due to having such a small community - I don't fault them for this), no local coop play, and a very weak solo\/AI play. At least with the physical cards, it's possible to modify the decks and play with a buddy. Even so, I still recommend The Battle for Sector 219 to fans of tabletop games, <i>barely<\/i>, because it seems to be a huge pain to purchase the physical copy itself (not available on major online retailers), and the premise behind the gameplay is still enjoyable and worthwhile to try out.
