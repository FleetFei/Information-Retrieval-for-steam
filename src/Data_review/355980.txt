this, it is one of the best out there. Way more towers than most games, lots of levels, good difficulty level, great game. <br><br>I think this might be the best Tower Defense game I've played. I played a lot starting with Warcraft 3 mods, and then Desktop Tower Defense the flash game. Since then I've played dozens of TD games, on iPhone, Android, and PC. They all have pros and cons, PVZ is unique and fun but not all that interesting or deep, Gemcraft is the same and also grindy, Defence Grid is great and has really nice graphics but the game maybe lacks a little of something, Bloonz is great but a bit repetitive, Creeper World is smart yet shallow and quite sedate, etc..etc.. I could easily list 20 more.<br><br>But there is something about this game that just ticks all the right boxes. It is challenging without being a pain in the \u2665\u2665\u2665, you sometimes have to do a level a few times until you can finally nail it, but it is always doable without relying on luck or something. Also some games don't let you shape your path which annoys me, this game has plenty of path shaping so you get to pick different routes through the map. There are actually at least several ways to win each map, not just with the paths you make the creeps take, but with tower selection too. There are a LOT of towers in this game, unlike the lame TD games that only give you several, this one has something like 20 ish towers, each one with a unique effect, and each one upgradeable which sometimes gives an extra effect too. The maps are also made so that you use the environment a lot, there are bottomless pits, pools of lava, spikes on the wall, etc. You can sometimes ignore all of that and just win using towers, but sometimes you really need to use traps that fling enemies off the ledge into a pit or slam them into the spikes. There are also railroads running through many of the maps and you can again ignore these if you want, but it is a lot more efficient to make the creeps run along the path of the railway and get injured or killed by the mine carts that race along them. <br><br>The graphics are retro pixelly style but they look nice enough to be able to make out what everything is clearly, and everything has its own style and feel, each creep type works in a unique way and looks unique, the towers are easy to spot by looking at them etc. The game is also very satisfying when killing stuff. Creeps don't just die like some TD games, in this game they die violently based on what killed them, so if you throw them into a pit they shout AAAAAAAAAAHH!! If you smack them into spikes they will splat with a cloud of blood. If you electrocute them they ZAP! with a satisfying sizzle. In a game that is all about carnage, I think this is important, and the devs did a good job of it.<br><br>Also there is some longevity with the gameplay as well, each map has modifiers so you can play in normal mode which is fine for below average players. They might have to replay some missions while they are figuring the game out, but mostly I think they will win each map first time. But if you click the Blood modifier for example, it means you have only 1 life. I can win each map with that enabled and it gives more XP for each map. And there are many more that increase that further but make it much harder. And there are even ones that lower the health of the creeps to make it easier. So you can easily tweak the game to be a bit easier or harder depending on how good you are at the game. Overall I think this only changes the amount of grind in the game. If you are good and can win each map with the 1 life modifier like I do, it means there is no grind at all in the game. You don't need to re-play any levels, you can just keep going on to the next new map and you will already have enough gear to beat that. If you struggle with maps and are maybe getting less XP from not using any modifiers, and therefore getting fewer upgrades, you might need to re-play a few maps to level up. But in terms of grind this is nothing like some games. Overall this game just seems to be about giving you a fun time. <br><br>There are a decent number of levels, good for the price, and there are many more you can download on Steam Workshop.  Most TD games I just play to completion and then don't go back, there are only a few that I have completed multiple times, and this one of those games.<br><br>p.s. kill or you die.<br><br>To more accurately describe the game, Dungeon Warfare is a tower-defense game focusing on installing traps in pre-defined dungeons in order to kill waves of enemies. The game starts you out with access to only two basic traps: The floor-mounted spike trap, which periodically juts up deadly spikes to damage the enemies above it, and the the wall-mounted dart trap, a device that continuously fires darts at whatever direction it is pointed at. As you progress through more of the levels, more traps types become available. Want to push enemies into a bottomless abyss? The pusher trap's got you covered. Want to burn them? The flamer will take care of that. Want to summon demons to fight for you? You can do that too. Each trap type is unique and varied, with over 13 different types as of this writing and more on the way. The fact that you can only take a limited number of trap types with you into a level means that you will need to plan out your defenses ahead of time. Often I've found myself having to decide just how I was going to deal with that massive wave of peasants in wave 5, for example.<br><br>Speaking of enemies, the enemies that you are fighting are more &quot;good&quot; than most things usually found in dungeons. In Dungeon Warfare YOU are the big bad evil guy, defending your portal from the legions of good. From the humble peasant, slow and weak but deadly in large numbers; the rough and tough warriors, able to take a beating; the theives, quick enough to bypass most of your traps; the mighty knights, that push your damage skills to the test; to the haughty nobles, that have more gold than they know what to do with; with over 12 different enemy types, every enemy in Dungeon Warfare is unique and presents a different challenge to the player.<br><br>The game deals with progression well, allowing the player to learn how to deal with each new enemy type over the course of levels. The game utilizes an experience system to reward the player with bonus starting gold for all levels, as well as &quot;gems&quot;. In this game, gems are the currency used to upgrade the trap mastery of each trap, with increasing cost per level. For example, raising your spike trap mastery to level 5 takes 10 gems, as 1 (Level 2) + 2 (Level 3) + 3 (Level 4) + 4 (Level 5). The effects of trap mastery are twofold. Immediately, each increase in level affects the stats of a particular aspect of each trap type: For spike traps, it is a percentage increase in damage, for dart traps, the cooldown time between shots is decreased by a percentage, etc. There is no need it be worried about spending your gems on the wrong upgrades; at any time between levels the player can reset their gems at no cost, allowing them to mix up their play style for the next level. The second effect is that for every 5 levels in a trap mastery the player gains, the player unlocks a new tier of upgrades for that trap. These are straight upgrades that the player can spend gold on during a level to upgrade a single trap, and the upgrades often cost less than it does to place a new trap, although sometimes more can be better; again, depends on the playing field of the level.<br><br>On the topic of levels, for that matter, the game boasts a count of 29 unique maps as of this writing, with more levels being added each week. Also, while not currently implemented, the game will include a level editor that will allow the player to make their own custom levels and share them through steam workshop. Level layout will determine your strategy, forcing you to adapt. Some levels have natural defenses in them, such as bottomless abysses, thin corridors, and minecarts running through the map; figuring out how to best use what you're given is up to you.<br><br>In Dungeon Warfare, your experience total is the sum of all of your best scores across all levels, meaning that it pays to go back and revisit a level to boost your score. What seperates this from most games is the Modifier system that the game uses. In each level, you can choose from nine different modifiers that make the game more (or less!) challenging. Each modifier changes different aspects of the game: The &quot;Nil&quot; modifier decreases the bounty you get from killing enemies by 10%, forcing you to be frugal; the &quot;Anchor&quot; modifier increases enemy health by 5%, and enemy weight by 100%, making shoving them into the abyss much harder; the &quot;Breeze&quot; modifier is for those that are having difficulty, increasing the spawn times between waves, lowering enemy speed and health, but also cutting your score by 40%. All the modifiers affect your final score with multipliers, and up to 8 modifiers can be stacked (with the exception of the breeze modifier) to provide a massive challenge that, should you emerge victorious, gives you a outrageous multipler of x5.23 for your final score for the level. Be warned, this challenge is not for the weak; you will need all the skills you can muster for this monster difficulty.<br><br>All in all, Dungeon Warfare is a promising Early Access game. With more content being continuously added, massive replayability value, and a creator that is vocal and very visible to the public, this is one game you don't want to miss. and addictive tower defense game--albeit, more accurately, dungeon defense.  Pesky heroes are assailing your various lairs en masse and it's down to you to devise elaborate combinations of booby traps and minions to keep them at bay.  Unlike most tower defense games where you're given a bevy of said towers to line some paths and periodically make some mazes, Dungeon Warfare's arsenal plays much more heavily into utilizing the surroundings and managing the different sorts of heroic intruders battering at your dungeon doors.<br><br>One of the most ready examples of this are the pits and chasms that you'll find in many of the locales; there are a number of physics and movement related traps that can push, catapult or pull intruders off ledges and send them tumbling into the abyss (the harpoon trap being particularly brutal and fun--firing a big tethered javelin into enemies and 'reeling them in'.)  <br><br>Different enemies have different properties, so a heavily armored knight is a bit harder to push around than their lighter fare.  Horsemen try to rush through your arsenal to batter down doors and bee-line for your inner sanctum, dwarves seek out your barricades to sap and destroy, adventurers try to duke it out with your minions and are generally nimble, etc.  Similarly, stages can include tracks with mine carts circulating them to plow enemies around in dangerous intersections, spiked walls ready for you to shove lines of troops against them, etc.  You'll also find giant boulders you can set tumbling to crush everyone in its path.<br><br>There are quite a few different traps and setups available at your disposal which can compliment one another fairly well.  I'm pretty fond of a lair that brings about a little necromancer who will go about raising slain enemies as a tiny army of skeletons.  Many of the traps bring Orcs Must Die to mind--but they're solid to take inspiration from (such as the springboards that launch enemies into pits, for instance.)  <br><br>Accompanying the traps are a bevy of cooldown-based powers you can utilize ranging from giant bear traps to bombs to eggs which hatch minions when enemies draw near or power-ups for your traps.  There is also an excellent 'trap mastery' system wherein you can invest gems to unlocking upgrades for your traps, reducing cooldowns \/ costs, making them do more damage, etc. (gems being earned by completing stages well and leveling up.)  You can also put gems into basic upgrades such as being able to sustain more enemies in your sanctum before losing, or starting the stages with more gold to work with--and leveling up also gives you more gold to start with as well.<br><br>Additionally, there are 'runes' which can be activated for stages to make them more challenging (faster enemies, fewer resources, limited load-out of traps, etc.) in exchange for more experience bonuses.  Completing a stage with four runes active unlocks the ability to play that stage in 'endless mode' for additional challenge.  Between the runes, large variety of traps and tools, I feel like there's plenty of mileage to be had--and that's before taking into consideration that the game includes a pretty easy to use map editor and Steam Workshop support.  Cool!<br><br>It's hard to fully do the full aesthetic experience of Dungeon Warfare justice in text and I feel like the screenshots don't necessarily do the game justice--but when you've got an elaborate gauntlet of death traps going full-force and a hoard of invaders trying to barge their way through it, it really gets quite rad.  It may seem a bit low-fi at first glance, but when grinders and darts and spring-loaded spikes are firing away while lightning arcs are chaining between knights and necromancers and demons are keeping footmen mired in boiling oil, it's all to fun effect.<br><br>If you're the sort that typically gives tower defense style games a pass, I'd urge a bit of a longer look at this one--I was very surprised
