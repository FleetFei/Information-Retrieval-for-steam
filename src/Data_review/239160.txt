One thing I'm pretty sick of in stealth games are all of cheap gimmicky abilities like Dishonored's warp that take a lot of the tension out of sneaking around. In Thourf, the most you have to work with is the Thief Vision that highlights collectables, important objects, and enemies. It's also got limited use in the form of a bar, so it's not as powerful as say the Detective Visor in the Batman Arkham series. No, the rest of the time, you have gadget arrows, a blackjack, and some smoke bombs to get you into trouble, out of trouble, or past it, and none of them seem particularly game-breaking for any playstyle.<br><br>One particular feature I really like that seems to be divisive is Garrett's full range of animations, especially in regards to picking up loot. To me, it feels satisfying to snag individual pieces or cut a painting out of a frame, but also adds an element of danger when you have to do it in places where you can get spotted, as opposed to games that just let you mash E to instantly pick up anything you see.<br><br>You get money for all loot you steal which can then be spent on gadgets, items, and upgrades for Garrett. The economy is set up much in a way that you can't really get everything you want, and start having to make choices later in the game on how much you want to spend on stealth-friendly or combat-friendly items. I appreciate that over games that let you get everything and play any way you want.<br><br>Non open world levels are set out really well. There are lots of alternate paths and lots of loot to pick up, but I never felt like there were too many paths that broke the game. You are still forced to have to sneak around enemies if you want to make progress or get extra money.<br><br>The open world is a mixed bag. The big positive comes in the form of being able to sneak into peoples' houses and rooms to nab valuables, and there are plenty of places to do so. It gives the feeling of being a real thief, especially when going into the many, many side mission and mini-mission buildings.<br><br>Outside the actual fun gameplay and stunning visuals, though, there are serious rough spots. The biggest problem I have is with actually navigating the open world. It's fine in concept, but there are some serious problems I can't gloss over. They are:<br><br>1) Transitions between subsections of the world are not marked 90% of the time. I've had moments where I'm just running around for pretty inexcusable amounts of time before I found out that to get to the next zone, I had to find a well-hidden beam to shoot a rope arrow into to climb up into a hidden window to get to the next zone. That, or having to walk up to some crates that don't seem all that important until you rub your face against them so you can hit E to have Garrett contextually squeeze through them. Keep in mind, this is with mission trackers with waypoints on, but the waypoints only tell you where in the city the objective is without marking transition zones.<br><br>2) The world is confusingly laid out, you are constantly forced to sneak around patrols, and the map is often unhelpful. This results in probably the majority of your time spent slowly skulking around in transit through the world.<br><br>3) There's no sort of fast travel. I wasn't joking when I said there were tons of side missions in the game. And it's a huge pain in the \u2665\u2665\u2665 to have to navigate back and forth past guards every time you go to and from these quests.<br><br>The plot is wholly unentertaining with characters that are either uninteresting or incredibly forgettable (literally, too. People kept saying the names of very important characters whose faces, voices, and importance I could not remember). The plot is pretty lame, too, mostly involving Garrett feeling guilt over the disappearance of a woman who regularly admonishes in the opening parts of the game.<br><br>There's also a pretty lame throwaway horror mission in an asylum. I'm guessing it's supposed to emulate the Shalebridge Cradle mission from Thief 3, but it mostly comes off as an overly-long pop-scare museum. After spending an eternity, I got tired of it and just plowed through it, killing everyone I could.<br><br>Beyond the negatives, though, this game is a surprisingly good gem that fans of stealth The atmosphere created is amazing. Thief1 and 2 were all about a rotten environment and so is Thief4. Dirty parts of town... Sewers. Rats. Spiders. Thief's solitude. The sound of the guards' footsteps and the constant worry never to be seen. Always stick to the shadow. Avoid the light. The clinking sound of looting treasure, so sweet for the ears. You could simply stand still and analyze the guards patrol patterns, spend long minutes before making your move.<br><br>The storyline is a good one, the cutscenes are flashy. The story is a little dark, but it's the nature of the series. If that is your cup of tea. Constantine and Victoria in Thief1 and 2 were so exceptional... yet in the new game we were presented with old friends like Basso. And last but not least we have Garrett, the main protagonist. The same Garrett. The voice of the master thief was excellent in Thief1 and 2 and so is in Thief4.<br><br>To sum it up, Thief4 is a must play, especially for Thief-series fans.
