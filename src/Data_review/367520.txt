What I can say with certainty is that two years later, Hollow Knight does not disappoint. <br><br>Hollow Knight styles itself after the traditional metroidvania format of interconnected levels, backtracking and equipment-based mobility upgrades, but throws a few interesting curveballs into the formula. <br><br>The art direction (environments, musical score, character sprites, animation) starts strong and only ramps up as the game progresses toward its climax. The controls are similarly polished and very responsive allowing for the precision platforming and carefully-executed combat maneuvers that certain late-game challenges demand (looking at you, White Palace).<br><br>The story and setting are melancholy and may seem familiar to veterans of FromSoftware's titles. However, the cutesy hand-drawn character design affords Hollow Knight a little more humor and levity than what's present in those ubiquitously depressing Souls titles. The character cast is varied and endearing, and interacting with the locals is a welcome break from dodging falling stalactites and eviscerating hapless wildlife. <br><br>On top of the traditional exploration and platforming, along with Souls-lite checkpointing system, the game offers several novel modifiers.<br><br>The SOUL system governs your mana reserve. You earn mana to spend on spells by hitting enemies (<i>not<\/i> smashing candelabra or furniture), which is somewhat reminiscent of Hyper Light Drifter. Moves that will require spending mana include projectile attacks as well as your healing, so at times you may have to choose between spending it on health recovery or on damaging ranged attacks. <br><br>Maps will not fill out automatically as you explore rooms. You are required to purchase an incomplete map from a character within the zone before you get the ability to add to the map yourself. Furthermore, the map will not update until the knight sits at a checkpoint and adds to it. Those aquainted with the metroidvania formula may find this unnecessarily roundabout, but ultimately I think it encourages the player to pay attention to environmental cues and to explore more carefully than they might otherwise. <br><br>The charm system takes the place of equipment loadouts in this title. As you explore and complete challenges you may discover charms that grant passive buffs or abilities. The knight has a finite number of slots to equip those charms, and certain charms may take up multiple slots depending on their function. You will usually have to make compromises about your loadout, so the knight will not be overpowered even after you've unlocked every charm and slot. But the system does afford a degree of flexibility in customizing a 'build' for a challenge or a boss that you may be struggling with.<br><br>The dream nail is a tool that allows the knight to read characters' minds, interact with spirits of the fallen and unlock optional challenges and bonus bosses. 'Optional' in a sense; if you wish to face the true final boss and earn your golden ending, you're required to make liberal use of the dream nail. <br><br>All in all, very strong title and candidate for my favorite metroidvania to date, which isn't a recommendation made lightly. However it isn't without its flaws; there's reports of the game not running well among certain GPUs, specifically inconsistent frame rate and input delays. I had little trouble personally, though I still experienced the occasional frame skip, likely due to map load and particle effects. <br><br>Most of the upgrades were quite useful, except for the Cyclone Nail Art, which felt underpowered considering the fact that it's a charge attack with substantial startup. Given the input to execute, you may find yourself occasionally using it accidentally instead of your more useful charge attacks. <br><br>Backtracking becomes more prevalent in the endgame, owing to the tremendous size of the world map, and relatively sparse quick-travel stations. I didn't mind this personally, because the environments are all extremely intricate and interesting, but less patient players might get frustrated.<br><br>Lastly, although the bosses are all unique and visually distinct, there's a pool of common attacks that seem to pervade many of their movesets; dash attacks, for instance, jumps, tracking projectiles.  <br><br><div
