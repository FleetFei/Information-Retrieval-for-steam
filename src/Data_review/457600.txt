class=\"postedDate\">Posted: April 27, 2016\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<b>Emerge: CotA<\/b> is a fun mix of turn-based strategy and realtime defense combat. You aim to capture a certain amount of sectors on the map (and perhaps complete some additional objectives) before the roaming zombie horde becomes too strong to hold off.<br><br>Each turn, if a pack of zombies moves into a sector controlled by you, you will be shown how any automated defenses you may have had fared at fending them off, and should there be undead survivors, you can attempt to prevail in realtime combat. Using the mouse cursor, you aim and shoot your weapon, while the keyboard serves to expend combat consumables like grenades or limited-ammunition turrets. Up to four AI-controlled companions can lend their aid in these encounters, while there's also the option to delegate the main character's gunfire to the very same AI logic and solely utilize items yourself. Combat lasts until the player's barricade is destroyed, in which case there's a chance for characters to die fleeing, or until all attackers have been fended off.<br><br>All characters partaking in combat gain experience points based on how they perform, and this is also the only regular source of experience points, somewhat necessitating use of the realtime combat to grow in strength (while stronger enemies appear after a certain amount of turns, giving the game a bit of a 'race against the clock' vibe). On levelup, characters can be given another point in one of their traits, which for example enable the use of higher-level weapons or give resource bonuses for the turn-based side of things. Manual actions by the user there, such as attempting to conquer or scout a neighbouring sector, cost action points, which replenish each turn. As far as resources go, there are production points used for the construction of defenses (for the simulated combat phase) and research points that let you progress along a <i>Civ<\/i>-style tech tree, as well as economy points that give you an influx of credits every turn, and two monetary resources \u2014 credits and crystals.<br><br>As far as content goes, there are ample unlocks, although the vast bulk of weapons are already available from the get-go. As the game progresses, you gain many more character classes to use, get starting bonuses or new scenarios, bigger maps, and a lot of different enemies to encounter. Unfortunately there is no character persistence between campaign levels (&quot;areas&quot;), and thus you start anew in the next one, only able to bring one of your items. I typically carry with me one that boosts research per turn, to get a faster start the next time. Gameplay fundamentals are virtually unchanged throughout the campaign stages, and this is a title that prides itself on replayability much more than on diversified visual design.<br><br><br>The game definitely aims at a more old-school audience who embrace a lack of hand-holding, who relish having to find out how to unlock things on their own instead of being spoon-fed the unlock conditions. In that spirit, your power grows at a slow <i>Dark Souls<\/i> pace, and the power difference between a level 1 and a level 2 weapon feels truly minuscule.<br>Weapons are divided into 'hitscan' ones that only hit when your recoil-affected cursor is on the enemy's model, while projectile weapons fly towards opponents. Of the former type, I only somewhat enjoyed the sniper rifle, while the bullet-fed automatic weapons were too inaccurate for my tastes. I ended up strongly preferring weapons capable of harming multiple opponents per shot, namely the jolly crossbow and the grenade launcher. One character trait increases the amount of targets a crossbow bolt can penetrate and immediately became my favourite.<br>The music fits the scenario's mood very well and reminded me strongly of an older videogame gem I only recently discovered.<br><br><br>Bug reports and feature requests I churned out while playing were responded to quickly and diligently, and from what I could observe, this game is definitely a labour of love crafted over the course of several years by the solo developer.<br><br><br>Due to technical limitations, it only runs at 1024x768 or 1280x720, fullscreen or windowed, and thus might not be very fun on a 4K screen. On my 1080p screen, I played the game in a 720p window and had no engine-related issues. Pressing <i>F4<\/i> will make the windowed game stretch to fill the screen, but everything \u2014 including text \u2014 becomes notably more pixelated in the process.<br>As far as Steam Achievements go, this isn't a <i>quick 100%<\/i> \u2014 and it doesn't want to be. Dozens of hours are needed to see all <b>Emerge<\/b> has to offer, and the cheevos mirror this.<br><br><u>A review copy of this game was provided by the developer. the Apocalypse<\/b> mixes elements of the <i>Rebuild<\/i> and <i>The Last Stand<\/i> flash game series into a compelling, satisfying and addictive format. I never really played much of <i>Rebuild<\/i> but I did play a lot of <i>The Last Stand<\/i> back in 2007, and the sequel on the next year. If enjoy both of these series or at least one of them (like myself), I think you\u2019d enjoy this game. That said, brace yourselves for I have a feeling this is going to be a rather long one\u2026 <u><b>TL;DR at the bottom<\/b><\/u><br><br>First of all, I\u2019m going to adress some caveats which are the lack of support for resolutions above 720p and any other kind of video options (the game doesn\u2019t look that great but still\u2026) and no mouse sensitivity options. As far as I\u2019m aware the game seems to be locked at 60 fps. That said I find that one of the weakest points of the game are the visuals and the sounds. The sound effects get the job done but they\u2019re pretty \u2018meh\u2019 and they, along with the visuals, remind a lot of typical flash games. If you can ignore these superficial details, you\u2019re in for quite the treat.<br><br>The game is divided into 2 different sections, a real-time section and a turn-based section, the latter being the one you\u2019ll spend most of your time on. You\u2019ll be responsible for handling a sector of human resistance in a post-apocalyptic world filled with zombies and, at the same time, your goal will be to explore and expand. In each turn you\u2019ll have a certain amount of action points which you can spend on capturing or scouting sectors, boosting your weapon damage or your research for the next turn, looking for survivors to join your squad or simply just exploring which can trigger certain random events and they can be both good and bad for you. There are also various types of resources which you need, production for building defenses, research in order to shorten the amount of turns it takes to research new technologies, economy which allows you to produce more credits each turn, crystals which are a special type of currency and infrastructure which allows you to capture more sectors.<br><br>In order to complete your mission you\u2019ll have to capture sectors by moving your squad over a sector and wiping out the enemies inside, assuming there are any. By doing so you\u2019ll gain credits and crystals, and you\u2019ll also have a chance to get some random loot (weapons, mods, grenades, defenses, etc). Still, having a lot of sectors means your defenses will be spread thin but it also means you can build more improvements which will grant you more resources per turn. Scouting also plays a huge role in this part of the game, in the sense that allows you to know what kind of resources a sector produces and how many enemies are there. At the end of each turn zombies will move, so if you\u2019ve scouted the sector to which they moved to, you\u2019ll be able to spot them. Zombies can also try to capture one of your sectors and, depending on your defenses they might succeed or not. If they do succeed you can attempt to reclaim that sector with your squad, or you can just abandon it. <br><br>It is when you directly engage in combat, by either capturing a sector or reclaiming one, that the real-time section of the game begins. This is where the game resembles <i>The Last Stand<\/i>. Your squad is placed on the right side of the screen behind a barricade and you need to keep the zombies, coming from the left side, from destroying that barricade. Here you\u2019ll face different types of enemies, your standard zombies, crippled zombies, crawlers, zombies that attack from a certain range, burning zombies, you name it. Of course it may sound simple but in truth you there is more depth to it, each member of your squad has different attribute which you can improve upon leveling up and you can also equip them with various different weapons (pistols, plasma rifles, crossbows, grenade launchers, etc) which play a vital role in whether you\u2019re victorious or not. You can also configure the AI of your survivors so that they target either far or closer enemies, weaker, faster, ranged and so on.<br><br>That about pretty much sums up the overall gameplay which is explained pretty well in the game tutorial. Now, <u><b>there are some things that bother me about the game<\/b><\/u> and the first one is the fact that you can\u2019t compare the weapon you currently have equiped with the one you\u2019ve selected on your storage\/merchant. If this was an option, it would be much easier to compare the loot you just got with the weapon you\u2019re using but alas. Second and last, the game offers a campaign and skirmish mode but the campaign is pretty much just a set of scenarios and there isn\u2019t really any progression. It would be great if you could carry your team to the next mission and maybe even your research, while increasing the difficulty of the game just enough to keep things from becoming too easy but sadly that is not the case. <br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"><b>TL;DR<\/b><\/div><b>Emerge: Cities of the Apocalypse<\/b> might not be the looker or the game to feature the greatest and latest in sound design but it combines 2 elements I really enjoy, explore and expand, and the sort of barricade wave defense that I greatly enjoyed from <i>The Last Stand<\/i>.<br><br>The game sure is repetitive after a few hours but, for me at least, it has that \u201cone more turn\u201d feel to it, which makes it rather addictive. If you enjoy turn-based games of expansion, conquest and resource management but you also like facing your foes head on in real-time combat this might be something you\u2019re interested in.
