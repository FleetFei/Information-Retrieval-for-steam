I'm excited for the future of the product and for Dovetail's next flight simulator, and I think it's worth the money.<br><br>Full Version: DGT Flight School is, first and foremost, a new approach to flight simulation, based on the Microsoft Flight Simulator X engine, though heavily modified (64-bit support being the main modification.)  So, first things first - I'm coming from a background of a few thousand hours of flight simming that goes all the way back to subLOGIC's FS2, then on to Flight Assignment: ATP, Microsoft Flight Simulator 4, 5, FSFW95, FS98, FS2000, FS2002, FS2004, FSX, and for fun, Terminal Reality's Fly!, plus a host of combat flight simulators.  I've flown a LOT, run a few virtual airlines, and more.  So, I've been in this hobby more or less for 25 years, and that's my perspective as I write this.<br><br>On to the review.  The first thing you need to know if you're reading the forums about this product is to understand what a lot of the chatter is about.  Flight School is based on FSX, but that's no different that saying that Windows 10 is based on Windows 8 (and all the prior versions of Windows.)  Just because it's based on a prior product doesn't mean that Flight School is the next version of that product.  It's not, and that was never the intention.  Instead, it's a product designed to introduce people new to the flight simulation genre the &quot;proper&quot; way - that is, instead of just dumping them into the pilot's seat and letting them have at it, it strives to introduce the new virtual pilot to flying in the same way that a real pilot would be introduced to flying.  And that's the first reason I'm giving this product a positive review: with the exception of ATP, no other product has really done that.  I've wanted to see something like this for a long time - and while FSX (and versions of Microsoft Flight Simulator before that) had lessons that were available, the aim of the product was to let you do whatever you want, and if you wanted to skip the lessons and just learn by trial and error, good for you.<br><br>Many people were looking to Flight School to be the &quot;next version&quot; of FSX since it was &quot;based on &quot;FSX&quot;.  Dovetail is working on a full-fledged flight simulator that will likely be close to that &quot;next version&quot; of FSX those people were looking for, but that's not the intention of Flight School.  Again, it's based on the FSX engine, but it's not supposed to be the &quot;new&quot; FSX.<br><br>So, what is it?  It's a simulator that includes two (at launch) beautifully rendered aircraft: the Piper Super Cub, and the Piper Cherokee, along with flight lessons for both a Light Aircraft Pilot's License (LAPL) and a Private Pilot's License (PPL).  There is a structure to getting these licenses - first, a series of lessons, followed by a requirement to build hours in Free Flight or Mission modes, then a skills test to wrap it all up.  This is representative of the steps a real trainee pilot would go through, and does a good job of giving you a taste of the real thing.<br><br>If you're already an experienced pilot, however, this content will not last you very long, and you'll likely move straight into Free Flight mode.  Flight School, because it is based on FSX, includes 24,000 airports covering the entire globe, and you can fly into any of them.  Here, we see an evolution of the FSX engine.  The move to 64 bit has improved performance of the sim significantly compared to FSX on the same machine.  The old default terrain mesh and auto-generated scenery has been vastly improved, incorporating textures from ORBX, a company that consistently produces very high quality scenery packages for FSX and it's variations.  That alone, as a side note, is worth the price.  I never invested in ORBX scenery for FSX due to the fact that on my machine, FSX would likely have run very poorly with it.  The improvement in the default texturing between the two is phenomenal.<br><br>For Free Flight mode there is a flight planning tool that operates quite nicely, but is a bit more limited than it might appear at first glance.  You can basically point and click to generate a flight plan, but what happens is that you're in essence just giving the GPS coordinates to fly to - if you click on a navigation aid, for instance, you don't get a flight plan that includes that navaid's radio frequency.  You just turn and fly to those coordinates.<br><br>Another area of deviation from FSX is that you are truely flying VFR - no autopilots on these planes, which again is fine with me.  The intent of the product is to teach you how to fly, not how to program the autopilot.  Yes, airliners fly mainly on the autopilot, but not by pilots with no flight time in their logbooks.  When the gizmos go haywire, you still need stick and rudder skills, and that's what this sim is all about.<br><br>Potentially the most exciting feature - though currently not utilized to the fullest in my opinion - is the pilot profile, which keeps track of your progress as  you earn ratings and build time in both aircraft.  Oddly, there's not a &quot;logbook&quot; per se (that I can find) that lists your individual flights, but you do see your endorsements and your overall progression.  I would like to see Dovetail expand the profile to include an actual logbook so you can review the details of your flights down the road.  Or, if you really want to go all out, hop onto Amazon and buy a real pilot's logbook and keep track of it by hand.  That'd actually be more realistic!<br><br>Back to the profile - I hope Dovetail continues the same profile into the future.  Soon, Flight School will be expanded to include the DA42, a twin-engine aircraft, and new lessons likely aimed at instrument flight rules. I've been highly anticipating this game. I downloaded it onto my computer last night, and I was excited to play! My PC is about 5 years old and not a gaming powerhouse, but it ran FSX fine so I thought it could surely handle Flight School. I booted the game up, and then I got a message telling me &quot;Your PC does not have a graphics card that can support directx11. You can't play this game on your PC. I don't know about you, but I think it's a good game, and a solid effort by Dovetail Games. But you need to look at it from a different perspective. This game is NOT Flight Simulator 2016. This is Flight SCHOOL. That means you aren't going to get all the features that a full fledged flight simulator has. And that is where I see all the complaints. If you are a hardcore and experienced flight simmer, this game is not for you.<br><br>First of all, DON'T ASK ME ABOUT THE GRAPHICS. My brother's PC is not a gaming PC at all, and I had to turn the graphics settings way down. Luckily, on low settings, the game runs at a manageable 15-25 fps. I don't care about the scenery enough that not having it period will ruin my experience. Remember, this is a FLIGHT SIMULATOR, not a SCENERY SIMULATOR. Nice scenery is nice, but it shouldn't be the whole purpose of the game. Other than that, the aircraft models look exceptional even on low settings!<br><br>For gameplay, it feels a lot like FSX. And that is a good thing. The planes fly highly realisticly, and the game works perfectly with my joystick, with most of the mapping exactly the same. I do wish I could map my own buttons though, since I had certain buttons manually assigned for the flaps, but I can't assign those buttons in Flight School. But that's not a big deal. I like the structure of the game a lot. I think it's cool how the game simulates being in an actual flight school, where racking up flight hours will earn you new liscenses. It's much more engaging than having just a series of tutorials!<br><br>Finally, I like the new user interface. It's modern, works well, and it's easy to navigate. One little feature I like is how I can adjust the graphics settings during a flight so I can maximize framerate or increase the quality of the scenery based on what my location is. The new flight planning works very well and is very intuitive to the new user. I like how you can fly in and out of any airport in the entire world - a feature that's neat considering this is a $15 game and it's not the full fledged flight simulator. Intuitive elements such as the ATC and GPS have been retained from FSX, and I like having the familiarity of both of those.<br><br>Seeing all the negative reviews, this game deserves more than just that. It's not meant to be the next Flight Simulator, that's coming out later this summer. This is meant to be a stepping stone for new pilots who have never played a Flight Simulator before or want to improve their skills. If you want to improve your skills or you want to get into the flight simming genre, then this is absolutely the game for you. Dovetail Games did a great job reviving the flight simming genre.
