Still, the game\u2019s clear defining feature is the atmosphere, which is clearly inspired by Salvador Dali\u2019s famous \u201cDream Sequence\u201d in the 1945 movie <i>Spellbound<\/i>.<br><br>The main gameplay takes the form of puzzles in which you move your character from an isometric viewpoint to safely guide the sleepwalking NPC, Bob, to his bed.  The puzzle aspect of the game revolves around the methods you must use to manipulate the rules of the gameworld to alter Bob\u2019s path without letting him die.  The puzzles are mostly easy; the NPC won\u2019t die unless he collides with specific objects\u2014 in which case the level restarts.  Fortunately if he sleepwalks right off the edge of the platform, he will simply respawn at the start; so you can let him loop around while you figure out how to solve the level.  <br><br>Once you beat the game you will unlock \u201cNightmare Mode,\u201d which is more challenging, but still relatively simple.  The \u201cNightmare Mode\u201d felt to me like what I would have personally preferred as the baseline difficulty. There are only 30 levels, most of which can be solved in a minute or less.  In \u201cNightmare Mode\u201d you will replay the same 30 levels, only you must hit several points on each map instead of just the end one.  This will take a little longer to play through.  Expect two to four hours if you play through both modes.  There is also an achievement for speed running the game in less than 45 minutes.<br><br>As I alluded to earlier, what makes the game stand out from other short and mediocre puzzler\/platform-lite games is: the well-crafted, surreal atmosphere.  As the game is related to sleep, sleepwalking, and the \u201csubconscious,\u201d the art style and soundscape are purposefully very abstract and dreamlike.  The whole dreamlike ambiance in the game is undeniably a direct homage to Dali\u2019s work and is definitely very well put together.<br><br><b>Back to Bed<\/b> is certainly an enjoyable affair, albeit a short one.  This game is great for any fan of the genre that might be looking for something casual and light that still offers a quaint experience.  It is the kind of game you can just zone into and beat in one sitting. The game's aesthetics are firmly rooted in iconic imagery from classic surrealist paintings: Ren\u00e9 Magritte's bowler hats, fishes, trains, odd mirrors and the prominently featured green apple from &quot;The Son of Man&quot; meet Salvador Dali's floating &quot;Eye&quot; and the dripping clocks from &quot;The Persistence of Memory&quot;. Even the little creature you control seems plucked straight out of a Hieronymus Bosch nightmare. The result is a bright, clean, colourful and charming comic style that actually represents its impressive bucket of inspirations brilliantly well.<br><br><i>The unfortunate thing is that these inspirations remain the backdrop and wrapping of the game, they never really leap onto the gameplay.<\/i><br><br>I can count one or maybe, if I'm charitable, two puzzles that somewhat utilize odd perspectives and optical illusions; the rest of the game is a conventional, uninspired puzzler of rather low difficulty, with some real time elements. You are asked to escort the clockwise-only sleepwalking Bob back to bed, by placing apples as blocks and fishes as platforms, while avoiding leaps into the void, angry wolves and killer whale-trains. Despite the timing elements, the game rarely punishes you for failing (Bob mostly just respawns), and therefore there is no real sense of urgency - which will be a positive thing for some folks and a negative for others. There are 30 something levels to the game, some of which are very very short, and after finishing them (in about an hour) &quot;nightmare mode&quot; is unlocked: The same levels, with the extra objective of collecting a number of keys, an addition that forces you to rethink the level's pathfinding and makes the game more challenging. It is a pity that the developers would choose to tackle such a unique theme, only to then ignore it completely when designing the actual gameplay. It makes the whole experience feel sadly shallow.<br><br>Thankfully the soundtrack and the minimal yet interesting and well placed audio cues from the creepy narrator (&quot;The apple is a hat...&quot;) enhance the game's dream-like atmosphere and reinforce the feeling of floating within a surreal dream.
