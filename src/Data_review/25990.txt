paper RPG in your life, and would like to know what it feels like to be a game master, then Majesty: The fantasy Kingdom sim will give you a taste of how it can feel.<br><br>Majesty: The Fantasy Kingdom Sim is an old RTS (originally released in 2001) where you don't have direct control over your units. Instead you need to treat your units like you were the quest giver from an RPG, in other words, you need to bribe them into doing whatever you want them to do.<br><br><b>Graphics &amp; sound<\/b><br>The graphics in Majesty: The Fantasy Kingdom Sim was considered to be sub-par back in 2001. The HD edition does not do much for the graphics, other than zoom out a bit further, and it's still not exactly a looker. That said, unlike many 3D games from 2001, the graphics in Majesty has not aged much. The detailed 2D art looks relatively appealing, far more so than the crude 3D graphics in many of its contemporaries.<br>   <br>In terms of sound, Majesty won't set your world on fire. A single song loops in the background, and after a while you won't even notice it. The sound of units attack each other or peasants building things is not anything wrong write home about either. It works, but it's not great.<br>   On the plus side, the voice acting is entertaining. A Sean Connery impersonator reads the mission briefings with enough conviction for it to work, and he also acts as the announcer during the missions. The game would not be the same without this voice actor, and it helps giving it a lot of personality. All units have their own voice lines as well, and they do a good job at conveying the personality of the units.<br><br><b>Story<\/b><br>Majesty does not have much of a story. You're the king of Ardania, and it's your job to make it prosper. Each individual mission does have its own backstory though, and while they are not &quot;good&quot; in the traditional sense, the mission briefings can often be quite entertaining to listen to.<br><br><b>Gameplay<\/b><br>In Majesty it's your job to build a typical fantasy village\/town. You start with a castle, and then you build the kind of buildings you often see in fantasy RPGs, like market places, warrior's guilds, rogue's guilds, temples to different gods, dwarven workshops and so on. Most buildings are used to recruit heroes, while some offer services that the heroes can use, and at the same time generate some much needed money that you then can use to build more buildings, or recruit more heroes.<br>   The heroes have wills of their own, which often aligns with their classes. A rogue is a greedy coward, so he's easily attracted to bounties, but if the going gets tough, the rogue will run away. A paladin on the other hand is brave and selfless, and while try to protect the weak, so she will go out and slay monsters on her own. A wizard is bookish, and prefer to read books in the library rather than fighting, and thus might need a really big bounty for him to take notice. There are plenty of different heroes, all with their own strengths &amp; weaknesses, and some are mutually exclusive (sun worshipers won't work with moon worshipers, and paladins won't work with necromancers), so deciding what heroes to use is a strategic choice you need to make for each level.<br>   The heroes also gain experience and level up. Fighting enemies will always give them experience, but certain heroes also have their own activities that will make them level up. A ranger that is exploring the map will for an example gain experience that way. This makes keeping your heroes alive really important. <br>   Sadly the game is not perfectly balanced. Some heroes are better than others, and it's really easy to fall back on the same strategy for most levels due to this. Paladins for an example, while expensive, tends to be really powerful and thus are a better investment than necromancers. <br>   Most of the game comes down to managing your economy. As long as money keeps rolling in, you can recruit more heroes, build more buildings, and cast certain spells. These spells are the only thing you can use to directly influence a fight, and they tend to cost a lot of money. But money spent on keeping your high level heroes alive tend to be worth it.<br>   There's no linear level progression, instead you get to chose from almost any level right from the get-go, with only some levels requiring you to have beaten some other levels first. All the levels are randomly generated, based on certain parameters for each level, so there's plenty of replay value, even if the level layout can often feel a bit generic, due to its randomly generated nature. Majesty is also not a very hard game. Between the core game and the expansion, there are really only 2 levels that can be considered &quot;hard&quot;. Many levels give the illusion of difficulty, but as long as you don't panic or give up when things look bad, victory won't be far off.<br><br><b>Final thoughts<\/b><br>I love Majesty. Yes, it's a game with its share of flaws, but it's really fun to watch the heroes try to accomplish things. As someone who used to play pen &amp; paper RPGs, I recognize the behavior of my old gaming group, they used to be just as bad at noticing the obvious as the heroes in Majesty are. ;) Fun is actually how I would describe the entire game, because while it might not be the deepest strategy game on Steam, it's one of the most entertaining ones.<br>  <br>So if you like strategy games or RPGs (be it However, it does stray from RTS convention in one major aspect and that was enough for its developer, Cyberlore Studios, to have considerable difficulty finding a publisher even during the great RTS glut of the 90s.<br><br>To fully appreciate Majesty, you need to talk about RTSes in general. Now, the term Real-Time Strategy itself is a little generic -- Crusader Kings II, for instance, is real-time and it is a strategy game but no one would confuse it for an RTS. When gamers use the term, they're referring to very specific types of games -- games like Blizzard's Warcraft II and Westwood's Command &amp; Conquer, the two titles released in 1995 that inspired the RTS glut.<br><br>These games required both speed (measured in actions per minute) and efficiency (through optimised build orders) and you needed tight control over proceedings for both. Control was important to such an extent in those 90s RTSes that your army would sit idly while the enemy was marching close by simply because you hadn't given your troops the attack order.<br><br>Majesty's major deviation from RTS convention was to give its Dungeons &amp; Dragons-inspired units autonomy and personality. Your units weren't manufactured goods and you didn't have total control over them. You barely had any. Instead of commanding and conquering in this game, you cajoled and hoped for the best. To entice your &quot;heroes&quot; to do what was required of them, you provided them with financial incentives attached to flags. You'd plant a Explore Flag with 200 gold to encourage exploration of that distant area of the map or plant an Attack Flag with 400 gold on the head of the Troll assailing the marketplace. This indirect approach could both be delightful (when the &quot;heroes&quot; performed unexpectedly heroic actions) and exasperating (when the &quot;heroes&quot; demonstrated precisely why sneer quotes were necessary).<br><br>Majesty charmed enough gamers with its unusual approach in 2000 to warrant an expansion pack the following year and Majesty Gold bundled both together in 2002. By 2007, the game had sold an impressive 500,000 copies. While there was enough demand for more Majesty, Cyberlore once again had trouble finding a publisher. The developers sold the rights to Paradox which then tasked Russian developer Ino-Co to work on the sequel.<br><br>Released in 2009, Majesty 2 was an honest attempt to improve the design but the designers erred in giving the player greater control over the heroes. In doing so, the designers brought the game closer to conventional RTSes rather than taking the design further down the sim path.<br><br>It's perhaps telling that Paradox felt compelled to release an updated version of the original three years after the sequel was released. Something about Majesty does seem to inspire loyalty and affection from everyone who's encountered it.<br><br>The key question, however, is whether anyone who's unfamiliar with the game could be tempted to play it today. All things considered, Majesty Gold HD's appeal today would mainly be to players of the original looking to revisit an old favourite.<br><br>Yet there could still be a place for a Majesty 3 or a spiritual successor to the series.
