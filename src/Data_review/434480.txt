seems like only one other reviewer actually bothered to play this game all the way through.<br><br>Mathoria: It All Adds Up is a RPG-Maker game inspired by Pokemon with a combat system where the player must answer simple math questions in order to defeat opponents\/obtain items.<br><br>The math aspect isn't particularly interesting (though it probably helps that my mental math skills are terrible), but anyone who rates this game negatively on the basis of 'it's teaching me math and too simple for me' likely isn't the intended demographic. This game is for parents who want to spend time together while teaching their kids. In that respect, this game does well. After playing through it, I'd say that if I had a kid I probably would do just that. <br><br>That being said, a few aspects of it didn't quite feel right for a children's game. The plot was much too deep for a children's story, especially the ending. Not only this, but I feel like a child 10 or under would have trouble with some of the words in the game as well (such as Vegabond). The story didn't even need to be as intricate as it was - most children beneath the age of 10 are usually willing to forego most plots in exchange for gameplay, so having a compelling story isn't too big a requirement.<br><br>There were also a few moments, such as someone saying '\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 off', that didn't sit well with me for a children's game. The rest of the game is good, innocent fun and there were actually a few sections I found myself genuinely enjoying, such as the minotaur puzzles and some of the villager interactions.<br><br>The controls are definitely clunky to begin with but you get used to them in time. As another reviewer mentioned, having to press Enter to enable the answer prompt and removing the question from the screen, as well as having a timer on the question itself (which the game claims makes you answer when it runs out, but in reality fails you if you don't), feels like a bad design choice. Especially when learning math on an easy difficulty, timing the player feels too difficult and forcing them to remember the question can easily be too complicated.  <br><br>The game also focuses solely on addition and not any of the other modifiers like subtraction, division or multiplication, even on higher difficulties. I suppose it makes sense for younger children, but adding this as higher-level option would extend your demographic in a fitting enough way.<br><br>The delay between the answer box popping up and when you can type feels too short as well, causing me to mistype whenever I typed too fast, but somehow I don't see that being a problem for children who may type more slowly. <br><br>An options screen would be nice, however I find I didn't have much of a problem playing at a fixed resolution, and something tells me a child wouldn't mind it all that much either and possibly wouldn't even notice.<br><br>Visually the game uses a lot of RPG maker assets, which is fine since I feel like most children won't mind much unless they've played an RPG Maker game before. What looked like custom assets, however, stuck out like a sore thumb, such as a certain type of house and the ship at the end. Music felt very RPG maker-y and I hardly noticed it (which is a good thing) except for the credits\/title music that felt far too hard rock.<br><br>As a game designer myself, here's my two cents: Dumb it down more. Even if you're targeting it towards parents and children, parents won't mind playing a more child-like game if their child is playing it with them. Your Pokemon references were enough to appeal to the older side of me, but most kids would be left behind by much of the game's design. This applies to controls as well - I know kids aren't stupid when it comes to controls, but an unintuitive interface could interfere with a child's enjoyment who might get bored easier than an adult. It's doubly bad if gamers are complaining about it! <br><br>All that being said, this is a student project targeted to children, so coming out of it those are my only notes. I got much more into it than I expected to, it just needs some tweaking to actually feel like a final product. It's up to him to save her, by visiting the island, by defeating obstacles and by helping others. His skills maths will be useful, especially his addition knowledge.<br><br>The game is simple: you lead a character thanks to the arrows or ZQSD\/WASD (forget the mouse), you speak to other persones who will ask you to answer a problem, like &quot;My brother ate 20 apples yesterday, I ate 35 today, how many apples in total have we eaten?&quot;. The answer field consists in 4 cases for each number (the first one is for the thousand, the second one for the hundred, the third for the dizain, the last one for an unity - for the answer of the problem above, it should look like 0055).<br><br>You have also some fights to handle: you have the choice between a simple attack (with adapted calculs, generally, the easiest you can find during a battle) and powers (with calculs that are more complicated to answer like 465+354). The equation is shown on your screen, you have a timer (ranging from 5 seconds to 30 depending of the difficulty). Hit &quot;Enter&quot; so that the answer field is appearing, write the answer and hit &quot;Enter&quot; again. However, it's not the only items in the game: you can also put a set of clothes that can improve or decrease your HP or your damage. It means that you have to be careful for answers as a set can inflict 60 damages but leave you with 10 HP only. A single wrong answer and you're dead - though in the game, dying isn't accepted: goons are putting themselves on the side when defeated, much still alive.<br><br>You have three difficulties: I've played the Easy one, as I didn't want to be confronted to harder calculs, even if in the end, I was because of the powers.<br><br>There are several problems that can be improved if there is any sequel.<br><br>For me, the most important is probably the one that will never be taken in account: the game is in English, which means that any non-English speaker kid can't answer the problems unless an adult can translate. However, as the game is only about addition, you know that you need to add this number to this other one. But still, for sequels, if there is a mix of calculs with substraction or multiplication or division, there will be a problem.<br><br>The second one is related to the fact that I've explained the controls above: you don't have really any tutorial in real time: you have to read what is explained (again in English), if you don't miss the characters that will tell it (for the fight, it's included naturally, not for the problems). You're speaking to kids, it's way more useful to show them in real time than rely on the fact that they've understood the mechanics just like that.<br><br>The third problem is that it's short. In more or less 1h30, I was already at the end. Not that I don't complain, because for kids, it's not too short. However, it would have been much better to have a little more of exploring. However, given the end, it seems clear that the idea of a sequel was in their heads and that substraction could be the next focus.<br><br>The fourth problem is the difficulty: an easy difficulty for me means calculs like 1+9, 10+10 but not 345+114. For any young kid that can't handle these, they can't use the powers and are just stuck to use simple attacks, which means that some of the enemies will take long to defeat. The developers should revise the difficulty at least to stick more to it.<br><br>I don't mind the fact that the game was made with RPG Maker - a blog post explains the beginning of the conception where Unity wasn't easy to handle for Mathoria. But it's not a bad move as the game can still attract children.<br><br>Mathoria isn't as bad as the reviews seemed to say. Many of the players missed the full point of the game, which is drilling in mental calcul and only saw the fact that they didn't get a RPG. Anyway, while Mathoria isn't perhaps the best educationnal game out there, it's still a nice try. It's free, so, your wallet will not be hurt by this one.
