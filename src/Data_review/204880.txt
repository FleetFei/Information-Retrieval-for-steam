hours, not only the steam-version but also an earlier version.<br><br>What is very unique about it, is it's great epic battles. You expand your empire. You build your fleet(s). And when the decisive face-off with your opponent finally has come, &quot;Sins of Solar Empire: Rebellion&quot; gives you the most brilliant epic battles you will ever have seen in RTS.<br>Don't be mislead by my words. There will be lots of matches, where victory or defeat is inevitable and unspectacular. You won't see much of the &quot;magic&quot; then. <br>Of course that all depends on your game settings, strategy and the quality of your opponents. You can set the game to go faster. Then you will experience 30 minute matches in PvP or PvE, that leave little time for micromanagement and decisions. Or you can really go epic and slow, where you carefully make your choices and feel the electricity in the air ... uhm ... void I mean ... , as your fleets slowly close the distance and hell brakes loose. Then you will see how every choice you make affects the battle and finally turns the tide. Also, &quot;rock paper scissors&quot; still applies. With huge fleets and many different ship types, finding the right line-up is not trivial.<br>You can choose from a wide variety of settings, to get the game to be more of a minimalistic skirmish or almost turn-based. I once left a slow singleplayer match for a few seconds and it turned out to take me 15 minutes. I had forgotten to pause. When I returned, my fleet had fought off some space pirates but nothing else happened. In other games I might have returned just in time to see the final building of my base collapse. <br>Whatever floats your capital ship. If you have played the traditional SOASE, the capital ships there were really big and important for your micromanagement. But now you get the Titan, sort of a huge hero unit unique to every faction. Nothing can survive long in the presence of a Titan and as it &quot;learns&quot; it becomes a fleet in being, crushing entire enemy fleets.<br><br>Establishing your empire gets a bit of the old &quot;Master of Orion&quot; feeling. You conquer planets, build up stuff on them and in the orbit.<br>Orbit is a nice key word. The battles don't take place in space but in the orbit around contested planets. The planets are connected through space lanes. That may narrow down your options and spoil the open-world idea of an RTS game fought in space. On the other hand it creates nice tactical elements like bottle necks or flanking maneuvers.<br><br>Another great aspect is diplomacy leading to alliances, which even grant advantages in combat.<br><br>Unfortunately the game still has some balancing issues in PvP which is not helped by the fact, that the player community has shrunk and you will encounter lots of veteran players. You might get frustrated. But the game also has a great PvE mode and the AI is not bad at all. Sometimes the AI is rushing into a futile battle. But then again they seem to calculate their odds on-the-fly and retreat even before you know you won, only to be back before you expected them to. For SP I would recommend downloading some maps from the community, cause the PvP maps are all about balance and symmetry and PvE should be more about atmosphere and exploration. There are maps out there with huge star systems, where a game will last for days, mind this is still RTS, not &quot;Civilization&quot;. <br>With the option to get AI on your side or more on the enemy side, with - I think up to 8 - teams and difficulty settings, everyone can get a challenging yet enjoyable game experience.<br><br>You will need to play hundreds of hours to get all of the tactical depth of this title and still be surprised. And if you really think you have seen it all, you can benefit from the community friendliness of SOASE. The community has been very active and there are not only lots of maps and units and &quot;improved balancing&quot; tweaks but also great mods.<br>I cannot tell which one you will like best as that depends on your nerdiness. However there are two very impressive mods based on the universes of Star Wars and Star Trek and even a Battlestar Galactica Mod, that i have not tried yet.<br>Other game developers make you pay for every piece of DLC, cutting off fans from implementing their own ideas. Ironclad Games and Stardock stick with another philosophy here. To try your own ideas, the game comes with two tools, a quick map designer and an editor. Thanks for that, by the way, really appreciated. Ironically that makes the game still attractive today and people are still buying it.<br><br>Any negative points? Well, the game is from 2012 and graphics might not be thrilling anymore plus you will play zoomed out most of the time and not see much of it anyway. I have it run smoothly on medium settings even on my old Core2 notebook. Also, if you prefer fast paced RTS with a focus on gut feeling and micro managing small troops, then you might be more comfortable with &quot;Company of Heroes&quot; or something similar. Personally, I like both, but they are very different. I would never start a game of SOASE if i knew, I only have got an hour of time. In Singleplayer you can pause and even accelerate but it will never be a casual play.<br>Another flaw is that units may take the initiative if not carefully set. Sometimes a ship will come back to the fight although you ordered it away and be destroyed because of some older stance. You can merge ships to different fleets or release them, but every now and then they do what they want. When you command hundreds of ships, that is not what you need. Also sometimes you will have trouble noticing a minor push or incident because the interface is not that well arranged.<br><br>So all in all I would highly recommend SOASE:R to anyone looking for a unique RTS experience in a scifi world. The game, at present, is still worth its 75% discount, largely due to the open design and mods. I would not pay more, since it's an older title.
