probably my favorite game on Steam! :)<br><br>In a nutshell, Hyperrogue can be described as a minimalist tactical roguelike in the hyperbolic plane. So, what does that mean, specifically?<br><br>First of all, it's a minimalist roguelike, and there is no equipment, and no items except for Orbs (basically spells) which are often activated upon pick up. Hyperrogue is turn- and grid-based, and combat rules are simple: Every attack is guaranteed to hit, and is usually deadly - for monsters as well as for the player. As a roguelike, it has permadeath. To avoid accidental player deaths, the game prevents you from performing actions that immediately result in your death, though there's a hardcore mode for the more confident players.<br>Basically, the goal is to collect as much treasure as possible in different game modes, either in total, or for a specific type of treasure. In addition, there are some very challenging quests (most of which would be easy in a Euclidean setting), and a lot of achievements and leaderboard categories.<br><br>The game is easy to pick up, but difficult to master, and is suitable for anyone from casual players to science nerds or hardcore players. However, be warned that there are no difficulty modes, so it can be frustrating, and you won't be able to unlock certain game modes (chaos mode, Orb of Yendor challenge mode) unless you're very ambitious. Also, you have to be a top player to legally get 100% achievements.<br><br>About the more unusual aspect:<br>Hyperrogue is the only elaborate game that I know which features hyperbolic geometry, a type of non-Euclidean geometry! To give a short explanation for this weird kind of geometry, I start with the other (more intuitive) type of non-Euclidean geometry, elliptic geometry: 2-dimensional elliptic geometry is simply the surface of a sphere. So if inhabitants of Earth couldn't move up or down, they'd be living in an elliptic plane. As a grid, we could combine regular hexagons and pentagons in a way that 2 hexagons and 1 pentagon meet in a vertex, which gives us a &quot;football&quot;.<br>Euclidean geometry is the &quot;normal&quot; geometry we're used to, and the Euclidean plane is simply a flat surface. For a grid we can use regular hexagons.<br>Now hyperbolic geometry is kind of special: While we can combine regular hexagons and pentagons to get a (positively curved) plane, a possible grid for hyperbolic geometry consists of regular hexagons and heptagons, making it &quot;negatively curved&quot;. If we try to do that in the Euclidean world we are living in, we get a wrinkled surface resembling the structure of corals, and it gets more extreme the larger the structure becomes, to the point where our Euclidean universe can't handle the non-Euclidean madness.<br>However, Hyperrogue uses another method of representation (see Poincar\u00e9 disk model), the same which you see in M. C. Escher's <i>Circle Limit<\/i> patterns: In this kind of representation, cells appear smaller the further away they are from the center, and quickly become too small to display on the screen. But if we travel to those cells, we'll see they're the same size as other cells. :)<br><br>An interesting property of hyperbolic geometry is that space extends more quickly, and you could fit the surface area of Earth in a circle with a comparably small radius. As a consequence, walking a few steps, and then walking back in the approximately (but not exactly) same direction will likely cause you to end up in an entirely different place, so it's very easy to get lost! Another consequence is that escaping pursuers is easier: Head towards a seemingly small gap between two pursuers, and that gap will turn out to be larger than expected! Also, there is no exact equivalent to &quot;parallels&quot; in a Euclidean geometry, and if your pursuers try to walk in parallel, the outer ones have to travel a larger distance, and will fall back, conveniently lining up for you to defeat them one-by-one. On the other hand, running into unexplored territory means a lot of cells are quickly revealed, potentially containing lots of monsters.<br><br>Another interesting aspect is that you can have a hub land with quick access to many infinitely large lands without using teleporters, or a waypoint system. :)<br>The world of Hyperrogue consists of such hub lands, and lots of other different lands with their own kind of treasure. Each land has some unique mechanics, and plays very differently, often requiring an entirely different approach. For example, the starting land - Icy land - has heat-based mechanics, there's a land where the ground vanishes beneath your feet, and there's another (cellular automaton-based) land where the walls have a life of their own, and you have to be careful not to get trapped.<br><br>The game is being actively developed, and new lands and game modes are released with major updates: Currently, there are 37 regular and 4 hub lands, which is a lot more than the first Steam release had! Also, the (small) community is very active, and there's probably always a friendly person who will help you if you are stuck, have questions, or just want to discuss several aspects of the game. Also, the developer has been very open to suggestions, and is eager to fix reported bugs, and keep the leaderboards &quot;clean&quot; in case there are obvious cheaters. A drawback is that major updates often introduce a considerable number of bugs, but with the excellent bug fix support they usually don't persist long after being reported.<br><br>The game currently has the following modes:<br><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li>The standard mode where you can collect treasure to unlock more lands, and attempt several quests<br><\/li><li>Collecting a certain amount of treasure unlocks the respective land in the pure tactics mode, where you can try to get highscores in individual lands w\/o using items or monsters from other lands<br><\/li><li>There's a cheat mode that also gives you access to map editor tools, and lets you experiment with hyperbolic patterns. <br><\/li><li>In the random pattern mode, lands appear with unusual floor\/wall patterns. This mode is not well-balanced, but it can lead to some interesting and fun situations<br><\/li><li>The Euclidean mode features Euclidean versions of the basic lands, which is interesting if you want to find out which impact the unusual geometry has on the game<br><\/li><li>You can print a sheet to create a warped paper model of a scene from the game, and there's a mode where the game simulates such a paper model (remember what I mentioned about corals, and trying to embed the hyperbolic plane in our Euclidean world?)<br><\/li><li>The shmup mode removes grid restrictions and is real-time, allowing for local coop with up to 4 players (I didn't try this mode much)<br><\/li><li>The heptagonal mode allows you to explore the world of Hyperrogue using a different grid that is a bit simpler (only heptagons), but where the hyperbolic aspect is much stronger<br><\/li><li>Collecting a lot of treasure unlocks the chaos mode, where lands are more dense and w\/o walls \/ hub lands, leading to some interesting interactions<br><\/li><li>For very ambitious players who manage to clear the Orb of Yendor or Prince(ss) quest, there are related quest modes. Especially the OoY challenge mode is noteworthy!<\/li><\/ul><br>The soundtrack is released under a Creative Commons license, and the game comes with the source code, in case you care about that.<br><br>If you're unsure about buying:<br>You can download a slightly older, free version of the game (along with the source code) from the developer's homepage, which should give you a good impression what the game has to offer. However, don't forget that it neither has leaderboards, nor Steam achievements, which are IMhO important aspects of the playing experience. The price is IMO far below the game's worth, but that's a very subjective point; still, I think the price has always been very fair given how unique the game is, and how well it is
