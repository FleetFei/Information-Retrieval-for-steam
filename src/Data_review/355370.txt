It may be wrapped in the graphics of a JRPG from 1994, but <i>Vidar<\/i> isn't a game from your childhood.<br><br>There is no combat, but death is a core mechanic. The secluded town is prayed upon by &quot;the Beast,&quot; a mysterious creature around which a shroud of vague lore has taken shape. At the dawn of each day of gameplay, the identity of its latest victim is revealed. The order in which the townsfolk are slain is entirely random. Therein lies the charm of <i>Vidar<\/i>.<br><br>The cast is comprised of two dozen townsfolk, each written with an often startling degree of depth. They have hopes and fears, friends and family, and business to which they must attend. But most impressively, they are consistently human in their reactions to your choices, successes, and failures. Very rarely do the NPCs offer throwaway lines appropriate for any occasion. Quite the opposite; the dialog is strongly driven by current affairs. Each death that is announced sends ripples through the tiny community. Family members are inconsolable, friends are beside themselves with grief. Quests that were issued by or depended upon the deceased become impossible to complete. In many cases, however, the death will also cause new quests to become available from the survivors.<br><br>The randomized order of deaths paired with the realistic responses from villagers combine to make the town feel truly alive. On your first playthrough, you might see a parent lose a child and struggle to cope. The next, the child may lose the parent and be forced to live with one of the dwindling number of villagers who remain. In this way, <i>Vidar<\/i> delivers where other games like <i>Heavy Rain<\/i> fall short. It does not offer you the illusion of a forking story line only to reconcile divergent paths shortly thereafter. In <i>Vidar,<\/i> consequences are permanent and impactful.<br><br>In order to learn more about the Beast, the protagonist must venture into the caverns beneath the town each day. The caves are filled with classic puzzles to navigate, reminiscent of <i>Chip's Challenge<\/i> or <i>Sokoban<\/i> and ranging from trivial to genuinely challenging. Your time in the caves is limited--you are automatically returned to the village when night falls. This forces you to budget your time wisely. Quest objectives often require backtracking to complete and so must be weighed against making overall progress in your explorations. Like the order of villager deaths, which rooms are accessible and the puzzles in those rooms are randomly generated at the outset of the game.<br><br>The combination of randomized deaths, rooms, puzzles, player decisions and failures make for a near-infinite number of possible game experiences. Some cutscenes and events require a mindbogglingly improbable combination of circumstances to occur. In each case, however, the thoughtful and comprehensive NPC dialog creates a deeply compelling experience.<br><br>For better or worse, the game does not hold your hand. Villagers issuing quests are often ambiguous in their descriptions. You carry a quest log but the UI offers no reminders of your current objectives. You will lose an occasional day in the cave because you were not clear on a mission's prerequisites. The game's unforgiving nature can remind you of 90s JRPGs as much as its art can.<br><br><i>Vidar<\/i> artfully captures the complex web of interpersonal dependencies present in any community. It is a dark exploration of the humanity of grief. As death follows death and despair sets in, the game inescapably parallels life. Alongside the beautifully sorrowful music and tense soundtrack, the tone is the only foreshadowing you'll get besides grief, death, sadness, frustration and sometimes strength.
