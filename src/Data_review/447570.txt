It's fairly simple, and while it has a few small twists, the player ought to figure it out reasonably easily. If, for whatever reason they cannot, the game basically pokes you with the obvious answer over and over. This is not a problem as it's the tutorial case and you're not really supposed to fail. It's a small showing of what the studio is capable of and where they intend to take the game. If you've ever played a Phoenix Wright game, expect the usual first case babysitting. You sift through testimony, press statements (called interrupt in this game), and present evidence as per usual. If you've played Dual Destinies, this game also has &quot;Revisualization&quot;, which I actually liked. There are a few different mechanics which I'll go over later.<br><br>As for standard review points: <br><br>The art is ok. There are some weird proportions and shading can use some work. Regeria and Artemis look fine. The art is decent enough that plot points which are hinged on the art can work. For Ren'Py games, it's actually one of the better ones (admittedly there is a low standard). <br><br>The voice acting is amateur-ish. That's not necessarily a knock on the studio nor the finished product, but a critique of a problem which I hope to be fixed. The original trilogy of Phoenix Wright basically had random employees do the voice work, and look how memorable those clips became. However, one can tell from listening to the very first few options available (picking the evidence-presenting voice clip, &quot;TAKE THAT!&quot; from Ace Attorney) that the voice actors need proper mics and sound engineering. I also felt as though in an attempt to differentiate itself, the studio chose not to use the same words, instead opting for &quot;Lies!&quot;, &quot;Have at you!&quot;, and &quot;Move to Strike!&quot; While trying to be different is alright, I feel as though none of these choices feel as succinct, yet always applicable as &quot;Take that!&quot;. No, they are not always lying when you present. In fact one of the very first presentations is not for a lie (not a spoiler, it's for the pre-case tutorial). And seriously? Would you say &quot;Move to Strike!&quot; while you present evidence in the courtroom? I think that the studio may want to consider using different clips based on context. This sort of already happens for the voice clips you didn't get to decide on.<br><br>The music is pretty chill. It's in synth symphonic style, quite different from Ace Attorney. If you play Ren'Py games, it will sound like those. Not really a knock on the music, it's actually good, but at the same time, a lot of Ren'Py games use &quot;synth symphonic&quot;. Lots of piano and synth string. Unfortunately, due to the way the music plays when you hit certain points, it will change frequently enough you are cycling through different tracks without getting to listen to them. In addition, due to the timed testimonies which I will touch on later, you don't get to focus on the music at all. The tracks are honestly decent if you check them out on their own, but we have no time to rest and get acquainted during gameplay, especially as we only get 30 minutes to do so (as the first episode is only that long). Finally, &quot;A Taste of Victory&quot;, the track this game uses in place of &quot;Pursuit&quot;, or the &quot;GOTCHA NOW YOU LYING \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665&quot; song for Objections in Ace Attorney is nowhere near as hype. It certainly has a feeling of coming out ahead, but it's more like something you'd hear after it's over, not when you catch somebody lying. In conclusion, for music, I'd say it's alright. It's decent on its own, but it doesn't generate as much hype when you show 'em who's boss.<br><br>Onto the game's differentiating characteristics:<br><br>The Judge's gameplay mechanic (if it can be even be called that) is questionable. It served no real purpose besides distracting the player from the case at hand and leading up to a gag achievement. I could see it serving a purpose in another case, but as it stands I feel as though it serves as a pointless distraction. Which leads me to my next topic of interest.<br><br>Timed testimonies. You have a timer which ticks down while you sift through the statements. This serves to force the player into thinking through things quicker and giving a sense of urgency, but for my play-through I felt as though it hampered my experience more than enhanced it. The player has no idea how this mechanic truly works and what its purpose is as they are playing. It seems as though presenting and progressing the story randomly decreases how much time has passed (3 minutes has passed since I presented this evidence?) You are also granted arbitrary amounts of time by the Judge for presenting correct evidence.  With no real set parameters and rules it feels as though this timer is but a gimmick. I did feel what I presume to be the studio's intended sense of urgency once my clock ran down to 3 minutes, but because I basically had the case in the bag it was a matter of clicking quickly enough to make sure I made it. This is a detriment to my experience as I do not get to read each piece of text and instead rush through because I have no idea if I'll have enough time. I understand now that I had more than enough in retrospect, but while playing, who knows how long a case could take?<br><br>It also seems as though you will have limited moves for investigation days. I have varying opinions on this. Time management can be a fun gameplay mechanic, but I feel as though in this case it may be an unnecessary limiting factor placed to artificially increase replayability. Of course, this is speculation based on the &quot;next episode&quot; preview, the final product may differ.<br><br>The studio also promises that you will be able to be corrupt and throw the trial or perform other nefarious things, but that has yet to be seen in episode 1. There is something mentioned. but it's not fleshed out and is mostly there for a quick achievement. There will need to be a damn compelling reason to do these things in further episodes however, as there does not seem to be any money system, so throwing cases for money seems pointless from a gameplay perspective. Supposedly it will change the story and how character interactions will work, but most of the time such claims amount to the tiniest of changes in dialogue. It may be interesting to see where this goes, but to say anything further is pure specualation.<br><br>At the end of it all, this is only episode 1. I can't give a recommedation or review for any future episodes or game mechanics until they are released. I can, however say that this first episode is worth a shot if you enjoy adventure games, Ace Attorney, or have 30 minutes to spare.
