compelling gameplay and artistic expression in modern games.<br><br>I've been looking forward to (and following the development of) this game since I played Sean and Joni's previous game Anodyne, which itself had a wealth of imaginative worlds to explore, each with unique puzzles and atmospheres. Suffice to say, I wasn't disappointed to find the same wealth of content, crafted with at least as much care and skill as was used in Anodyne. These two clearly know what they're doing when it comes to game design and development.<br><br>The art in this game is breathtaking. The character sprites have a 16-bit-era style to them, but the environments are outright painterly, and Joni's done a fantastic job on both. The beautiful, flowing appearance of the outside worlds contrasts with the comparitively rigid look of the power plant levels, which plays well into the theme of the imbalance between nature and machine. And fans of the SNES Final Fantasy 6 overworld aesthetic will like that of of this game's overworld as well - shoutouts to Mode7!<br><br>And just like Anodyne's, Sean has composed yet another fantastic soundtrack for Even the Ocean. The songs are very chill, and they all enhance the atmosphere of their respective levels. I'm particularly fond of the bit with the synth solo in the forest library power plant level in act 3. You'll know what I'm talking about.<br><br>The core movement &quot;feels&quot; good, which is of course crucial to platforming games. The game's horizontal\/vertical energy mechanic can be used to creatively solve or work around many of the puzzles in different ways, as well as simply providing options for how to navigate the levels, leaving the player a lot of agency to play the game in their own style. An extreme imbalance of power can be used to do things more quickly or sidestep certain obstacles, at the expense of a higher risk of getting hurt, and having to dial things back when the situation demands it.<br><br>Without spoiling anything, I will say that the gameplay, environments, and story themes complement one another well.<br><br>Both the main and minor characters shine in having a diversity of personalities, opinions, backgrounds, ethnicities, genders, sexualities, and so on - and none of it feels shoehorned, gimmicky, or token. By all rights, this game aught to be the gold standard for what inclusivity should look like in a game. The major characters are never bucketed into a na\u00efve &quot;good&quot; and &quot;evil&quot;; they're all clearly portrayed as imperfect people from different backgrounds trying to do their best in the face of disastrous circumstances, playing different parts in a flawed power structure. Even many of the minor NPCs scattered throughout the pre-powerplant areas seem to have some thought and backstory put into them, and aren't just braindead conduits for saying &quot;Hey, have you heard about the next important plot point?&quot; like the NPCs you'd find in older RPG-type games.<br><br>There's enough content that casual playthrough is likely to last even a seasoned platformer player upwards of 6 hours; the average player probably closer to 8. And this is all varied, meaningful content, mind you - nothing feels artificially drawn out or disrespectful of your valuable time as a player. Each level introduces a new element or combination of elements that has you learning how to deal with a new type of puzzle or movement technique, and each comes with a new atmosphere built by the aforementioned artwork and music.<br><br>I presently hold the record any% completion time for Anodyne, so naturally I gave this a go as a speedgame as well.
