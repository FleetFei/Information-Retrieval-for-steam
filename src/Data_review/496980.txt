class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn the year 1980, Cosmic Cavern aka the first &quot;sandbox&quot; game was released. Nowadays, we tend to associate the term &quot;sandbox&quot; with franchises such as <i>Grand Theft Auto<\/i>, <i>The Elder Scrolls<\/i>, and <i>Minecraft<\/i>. So, you're probably wondering how a game designed for use on a computer with 48KB of RAM can be compared with what's available today. To put it simply, a &quot;sandbox&quot; game allows players &quot;absolute freedom within the limitations of the game-world.&quot;<br><br>It sounds almost contradictory doesn't it? Please, just hear me out. <br><br>The concept behind the sandbox is that players are allowed to make use of any tool or technique, without resorting to cheats or modifications. In GTA, if you're being chased by the police, you can't just flap your arms and expect to fly away, but you can steal a helicopter. Cosmic Cavern is just as much a sandbox game, despite its lack of vehicles, weapons, or locations. The goal of this game is to get a high-score, but the approach is left entirely to the player.<br><br>When you start Cosmic Cavern, there are three things to note. At the bottom of the screen is your base. If the aliens reach this location, the game is over. At the top of the screen are the aliens. Before long, they're going to fall into the holes on both sides of the screen. When an alien falls, they're momentarily stunned, and you can safely dispose of them. Of course, if an alien falls on you, or you run into one while it isn't stunned, then you get devoured. Like most early arcade games, there's no &quot;completing the game&quot;. The aliens will continue to spawn until the base falls, or you get eaten.<br><br>The key to surviving this game is in digging. By creating paths, players can manipulate the aliens to slow their descent. Something as simple as a staircase can make the alien problem far more manageable. However, after scoring a few points, the aliens will become capable of digging their own paths. Keep in mind also that while you can dig as much as you want, the effects are permanent. If you accidentally dig a hole, you can't just grab a shovel and refill it.<br><br>Getting the most out of Cosmic Cavern involves a lot of experimentation as well as an understanding of its mechanics. Exploring the inner workings of this game is really satisfying. There's so much that goes into something as seemingly mundane as a path. Looking past its clearly outdated graphics, this game is perfect at being deceptively simple. All you need are the arrow keys, but there are so many ways to play the game.<br><br>The MZ-700 mode (included in this release) allow players to set bait. The intended use of this item is to distract aliens for easy disposal. However, it can also be used to block aliens from below. Unlike dirt, bait can't be dug through, so it can be very handy for harvesting adversaries. At first I thought that this technique was game-breaking, but eventually the aliens are able to dig out alternate paths, just by sheer force of numbers. All in all, the presence of bait adds dimension to the game, giving clever players an additional tool to assist in developing their strategy. Gem and heart power-ups also appear in every game. Their location is hidden (and random), but its worth digging around for them. Gems double your current point total, and hearts fill random spots with dirt. The usefulness of the heart is debateable. While it could resolve a terrible situation, it could also ruin a well-constructed path, but hey... it's worth 500 points.<br><br>Exclusive to this port is what can be considered the &quot;arcade&quot; release. It has quaint early 80s graphics and music, and there more changes like stunned aliens being worth extra points, if you collect two or more in a row. Also, unlike the previous versions of the game, walls can be created while digging. This gives the player far more space to develop paths, though again, aliens can dig through them if they have to. For the newcomer, this is the game-mode that they're likely to play first. In my opinion, all three versions of this game are worth checking out.<br><br>I've never been particularly fond of puzzle-games. All too often, there's only one solution. Cosmic Cavern appeals to me because there's no perfect method for solving the game. The aliens always win in the end. Even with this in mind, I'll keep retrying to get a few extra points or test different paths. In my view, this is what sandbox gaming is all about. Nothing is pre-determined, but players have all of the agency they need, to craft the game into their image. While it's sensible to point out that this game isn't for everyone, I believe that it succeeds in what it sets out to do. <br><br><i>If you liked this review or want to see more recommended games, be
