the developer for review purposes. This did not influence my review in any way.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n---------------------------------------OVERVIEW-------------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Note: Looterkings is currently in Early Access.<br><br>Looterkings is a three-dimensional action dungeon crawler in reverse: you play a goblin living at the bottom of the dungeon, ascending through the levels in search of more and more loot, until you eventually break through to the surface and the court of the Elven Queen.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n----------------------------------------QUALITY-------------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Graphics - Graphics are colourful and cartoon-like, with more lifelike textures than many other cel-shaded games, especially on the goblin skins. The goblins themselves are great, although with only a few skins to choose from, there's not much variation. They're well modelled and animated nicely, and they have a certain cuteness to them. Enemies are also nicely modelled and animated, and fit in well with the general theme. Unfortunately there's only a small number enemy types so far. I hope there are a lot more planned as the game moves through Early Access.<br><br>Weapon and ability effects are okay, if nothing jaw-dropping, and lava looks a bit meh, but the fire and smoke particle effects are good, as are the dynamic lighting and the water. The room graphics range from okay to excellent; I think they get better as the game progresses.<br><br>Graphics settings are limited: one single detail level setting, as well as vsync and window\/full-screen options. The game mostly performs well, but can occasionally slow to a slideshow in large rooms when there are many enemies about.<br><br>Sound - The music and sound effects in Looterkings are good, but very limited in scope.<br><br>There seems to be only one music track, which is played only on the title screen and menus. It's a jaunty sort of tune that fits well and sounds good<br><br>The goblins' sound effects are great, but there aren't enough of them, and it seems to me that they need some volume normalisation, as some are too quiet.<br><br>There's also a nice touch where sounds are dulled and lowered in pitch when you're close to death, together with a corresponding visual fade.<br><br>Overall, the few sounds and lack of music in game leave the game feeling a bit lonely and empty. I hope more will be added soon.<br><br>Gameplay - Looterkings is action oriented. You control your goblin from a third-person view with WASD, using mouse for aiming and weapons and a few other keys for jumping, running, etc. There is no controller support, but the gameplay is really asking for it.<br><br>The game is divided into multiple levels, each with a procedurally generated layout that changes every time you play. This is performed through the use of a relatively small number of set rooms connected by short hallways in a modular fashion, so although each time you play the layout is different, the rooms you see are all very similar to what you've seen before. The limited enemy types -- the bestiary lists eight -- make everything seem even more repetitive, too.<br><br>Each room allows you to enter and then locks you in, immediately spawning a number of enemies of a single type, up to a predefined maximum. As you kill them, more spawn to keep the room maximally populated, until you've fought your way through a predefined number of enemies for that room. Once they're all dead, the doors unlock, you pick up your loot and experience point glowy-things, and move to the next room. The game's difficulty stems from swamping you with enemy numbers.<br><br>For the time being, ignore the 'RPG' category tag on the store page, as this game doesn't include any of the traditional elements of RPG games: inventory, quests, character customisation and meaningful progression are all missing from Looterkings.<br><br>The 'loot' you find is simply gold. It's used to buy items from the shops (one per level), but you can only have one weapon, one mask, and one suit equipped at a time. One original aspect of the game is the way in which the items you equip change your god abilities. It's an interesting mechanic, but it can be a frustrating one: I bought a suit of armour, but didn't like the ability change it forced on me, so I ended up not wearing it at all.<br><br>Progression in the game is also very simple: kill enough enemies and collect the XP left behind, and you go up a level. But all this does is unlock a new item in the shops. You don't get better at anything, or gain any new abilities. The game features permadeath, but your level and the associated item unlocks are carried across after you die. Unfortunately, though, you lose all gold and items, so there's no real increase in ability. Some sort of level-based stat or ability increase, or better starting weapon options, or even being able to keep one item, would all add to the replayability. As it is, it feels like progress is all but lost with each play.<br><br>Looterkings is designed to be played with three friends in a cooperative mode. So far I've only been able to find one other person with a game that I could join, and that only lasted for an hour or so; the rest has been singleplayer. In either case, combat is very basic and tends to involve kiting enemies until they trap you, then using your god ability to run away and start again. The alternative is to take advantage of the poor AI and jump up higher than your enemies, in which case they tend to ignore you. Teamwork makes it more fun, but the basic combat mechanic needs improvement.<br><br>I also found the following bugs and questionable design choices that I thought were worth mentioning in passing. Bear in mind that the game is still in Early Access, though.<br>. There are three modes: normal, casual, and twitch. Only 'normal' is available at the moment.<br>. Poor grammar and spelling.<br>. There's no map.<br>. It's easy to become stuck on geometry in some levels.<br>. Some enemies can sometimes shoot through geometry sometimes.<br>. The statistics screen is funny, but some of the stats are wrong.<br>. Starting weapons get duplicated in the shop.<br>. There's no way to eat a mushroom once you've picked it up; you can only throw it.<br>. Poisoned mushrooms cause too much damage.<br>. Sometimes the 'loot' just disappears.<br>. The crossbow doesn't always aim at the crosshairs, particularly when you're trying to shoot above you.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n-------------------------------------PROS &amp; CONS------------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Pros:<br>+ Excellent graphics<br>+ A fun game idea<br>+ Steam Achievements<br><br>Cons:<br>- Procedural generation needs more components<br>- Combat is repetitive, especially with no real progression<br>- Not enough monster types<br>- Not enough items<br>- Not enough people playing yet<br>- Other minor bugs and issues (see above)<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n--------------------------------------CONCLUSION-----------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Looterkings isn't quite what I expected it to be. It has a lot less RP in it than I thought it would, and the combat needs some work. It's very pretty, though, and there's a good core game idea here.<br><br>At the moment I recommend it if you can pick it up at a discount, and especially if you can play it with friends.<br><br><div
