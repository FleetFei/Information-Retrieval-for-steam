10\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've been using gamepads since early 90s. I got my first dual-stick controller with vibration feedback when the first DualShock came out for PSOne, and I loved it so much that I bought a gamepad for my PC and have been using it in every PC game with controller support ever since. I'm basically a sad console peasant who ended up being a PC gamer.<br>Recently I've realized it's time to replace my ancient controller with something with analog triggers and more buttons, and started looking for options available on the market. I had my first-hand experience with almost every controller of current and previous console generation, including some 3rd party ones, and came to know that none of them were perfect for my taste. And so, I've decided to go with a device where innovations outweigh its downsides - the mysterious Steam Controller (hereinafter SC).<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Build Quality and Ergonomics<\/div><br>I've read a lot about SC's build quality, that it feels plastic and cheap, and was afraid to be disappointed. Imagine my surprise when I grabbed it, pressed all the buttons, moved the analog stick, and found out the build quality and feel is similar to that of an Xbox One controller, and that one is anything but cheap. I'd say SC feels more expensive even.<br>Ergonomics is great. The controller is big and feels natural in my big hands. Some may find it to be a bit heavy with batteries inside. It doesn't have rubber grips, but then again, only some 3rd party controllers do, and it's not a big deal here because your hands rest so comfortably when you're holding it that palm sweating becomes less of an issue, and the textured grips prevent it from sliding anyway.<br>I had my big doubts regarding the shifted placement of buttons and the analog stick, but all of them turned out to be within perfect reach from your normal holding position without any additional effort.<br>The analog stick is convex, which I personally prefer to concave sticks of X360 and XBOne controllers, as when I roll it with my thumb, a convex stick allows me to have a constant surface contact in every position, which is not the case with concave ones. No wonder Xbox One Elite controller comes with convex replacement sticks. The stick itself has a rubber cover with a textured rim, and its resistance is just what I want it to be. Definitely one of the better analog sticks I've tried.<br>The ABXY buttons are smaller than those in regular controllers, but this didn't cause me any kind of discomfort, I could still hit any button easily without looking at them. Of course, after decades of using standard controllers I had to adapt to new button placement at first, but one day was enough for that.<br>Right and left bumpers have the same mechanical click and resistance as the 2nd revision of XBOne controller, you can press them across the entire button surface, but heavy button mashing may eventually wear the mechanism out, so you have to be careful with those (FIFA players, I'm talking to you!).<br>Triggers are pure joy with their dual-stage mechanism, as in, they click after you fully press them. The click serves as a separate button. This is particularly useful in GameCube emulation, as the original GC controller also had dual-stage triggers, and many games made use of that functionality. Trigger travel distance is rather small, but the resistance is just right. They are placed a bit lower than bumpers, which helps keeping fingers more relaxed when you press them.<br>Right and Left Grip buttons located at the back of the controller are great additions, they're very convenient to press and hold, and work great for features like enabling gyro control.<br>Gyro is an often-overlooked feature of SC, but it's actually an asset that provides an extremely high accuracy that can exceed the one of the removed right analog stick, and compensates all the possible awkwardness standard controller users may feel from switching to a trackpad. The only downside is that currently it's only possible to use 2 gyro axes as analog inputs at the same time, while a third one will act as a threshold for activating digital buttons. It's nothing Valve can't fix in one of the future Big Picture updates, however.<br>And last but not least, the very things that make SC unique - two trackpads. They have a nice texture, both can be pressed, making a rather loud click. Left Trackpad usually works as a D-pad, while Right Trackpad replaces the 2nd analog stick, and in conjunction with gyro totally outperforms it.<br>Another little bonus feature many people fail to mention that was a pleasant surprise to me is a built-in speaker that makes funny PC-speaker-like noises when you turn the controller on or off, and it's possible to choose different sounds or melodies from the list, separately for each of those actions.<br>Rumble was another one of my concerns, as I'm used to powerful rumble motors of DualShock controllers and love the extra immersion it provides. SC features haptic feedback motors, making trackpads vibrate when you touch them or slide a finger across them. These same motors are now also used to emulate a standard rumble feature, and the result is very similar, but less shaky. Overall it feels more like heavy scratching rather than shaking, but it's actually stronger than I thought it would be, and provides a very similar experience to regular gamepads.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Configuration and Usage<\/div><br>SC requires Steam to be running, and a game has to be launched via Steam. It's not a problem for a heavy Steam user like me, but some people may be annoyed. These people may be happy to know there's a 3rd party software called GloSC that provides a system-wide X-input emulation and Big Picture overlay layer for the SC. Steam still has to be running in the background though. I thought one of the downsides of the SC would be the inability to use it in UWP (Windows Store) games, however with GloSC it's actually possible now! That's one downside less.<br>Now that Valve has added support for all the available controllers out there, you probably already know how powerful Steam's controller configuration module is, and if not, go check it out! Everything is configurable and rebindable, double-presses, long presses, turbo, mode shifts, dead zones, action sets etc. etc. So I'll only mention SC specifics.<br>Trackpads can be used as sticks, mouse, d-pad, scrollwheel, button pad, touch menu - you name it! I was afraid SC will be bad for playing old-school 8- and 16-bit 2D platformers. However, it's possible to set trackpads to work as touch d-pad and buttons with haptics. Once you do that it becomes surprisingly convenient - you can button-mash all you want without damaging the controller, the response is great, and the virtual buttons are big and familiarly placed.<br>Mouse and keyboard games work great, particularly RPGs and MMOs where a touch menu can be used to turn a trackpad into a virtual button panel with all of your skills - you just click or touch it in a place where the virtual skill button is located.<br>In racing games, you can use your SC as a wheel thanks to gyro, or use a trackpad, which is still more precise than a stick for a smooth wheel-turning (hardcore racers use racing wheels anyway).<br>For FPS and other games that require precise aiming - trackpad for direction and gyro for aim correction work wonders.<br><br>The only real downside here is the learning curve, it's very steep, and almost every game requires individual approach. To those who feel lazy to learn all the configuration nuances, community configs are there to help making it a plug-and-play experience.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Summary<\/div> <br>When you compare SC with other controllers, don't forget that it's a multifunctional device, and its new features can actually replace the old ones you're used to. What you see as downsides can actually turn into advantages once you feel how it all works together. I used to think Xbox One Elite was the best controller out there, but now if I'm given this choice, I'd rather take two Steam Controllers.\t\t\t\t\t<div Furthermore, the requirement to initially configure (and likely adjust) the controller configuration for each game will turn many away.
