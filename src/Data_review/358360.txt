class=\"postedDate\">Posted: March 24\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a classic top-scrolling shooter from 1994, with an upgrade shop system being the main selling point. That is, your score is measured in credits which you can use to buy upgrades for your ship between levels. The graphics are pretty detailed for the time, similar to an Amiga or Atari ST game, especially the scrolling terrain. Sound effects are crunchy and satisfying, and the cheerful MIDI music is simple but effective. Keyboard, joystick, and mouse control schemes are available.<br><br>The Steam version runs in DOSbox, since this is an old MS-DOS game. It was very stable on my Windows XP system. If you want to play fullscreen with the proper aspect ratio on modern widescreen monitors, you will want to disable the Steam overlay for this game, and edit one of the DOSbox initialization files to make sure &quot;aspect=true&quot; is in there.<br><br>I used the mouse for this, and it worked out OK. Left button fires all your weapons, right button cycles selectable weapons, middle button releases a Megabomb. You need to choose mouse or joystick control schemes from the setup program, and you may need to do some tweaking to get the setup program to run if you have an operating system after Windows XP. (There are workarounds for these issues in a post in this game's Steam forum if you need help with that.)<br><br>You can pick one of four face icons and a name and callsign for your pilot, although these are purely cosmetic, and are used mostly to identify the save file. There are four difficulty levels available, the highest is challenging for experienced shmup players. Then you enter a hangar where you can browse the upgrade shop, start a new mission, save your progress, or exit.<br><br>There are buttons for each of the three episodes (Bravo Sector, Tango Sector, and Outer Regions) and the &quot;Auto Pilot&quot; button starts the next mission you haven't finished yet. It's possible to play missions from each of the three episodes out of order, or replay a set you've finished to farm for more credits if you're not ready to take on the next episode yet.<br><br>The ship you start with has paired machineguns as a starting weapon, but by the end of the first level you've picked up an air-to-air missile launcher, and your arsenal builds from there. A few types of missile weapons can be found throughout the episodes as pickups, but for the most part you're eventually going to want to sell them, and use the cash to buy something more powerful. There are also non-weapon upgrades worth getting, like a boss shield meter and extra shield layers.<br><br>It's also possible to find Megabomb pickups in some levels. Middle button releases a Megabomb, which floats toward the center of the screen and detonates, cancelling enemy shots and delivering a powerful punch to all targets on the screen, usually wiping out anything short of boss ships. How long they take to go off depends on how far you are from the center, so it's best to use them before you get backed into a corner. You can carry up to five at once, and buy more in the shop.<br><br>You can also buy extra Phase Shields to protect your ship, up to five at once, effectively giving you five extra shield bars.<br><br>There are two types of weapons available, marked &quot;Always Equipped&quot; or &quot;Selectable&quot; in the shop menu. The left button fires all of your installed Always Equipped weapons together with your current Selectable weapon, which can be cycled with the right button or selected with the number keys. Your Machineguns are Always Equipped, and you can buy Micro-Missiles and a Plasma Cannon to help them out later, these function much like extra machinegun barrels. Everything else falls into the Selectable category, of which you can carry several but use only one at a time, switching between them in flight as needed. (You can lose these permanently if your shields get severely damaged, but by then you're about to get shot down anyway.)<br><br>A quirk of the combat system is that not all weapons can hit all types of targets. There are aircraft, vehicles, and structures. Some weapons can hit all three, some only flying aircraft, some only ground vehicles, and others can hit air and ground targets but bypass inanimate structures like buildings. This can work for or against you in different situations, so knowing your weapon capabilities is important. Usually you'll switch to a ground attack weapon briefly to shoot a tank or anti-aircraft turret, then switch back to your air combat weapons to hit the next formation of planes and drones, and so on.<br><br>Getting hit by bullets or missiles chips away at your shields, getting rammed by anything takes a huge chunk out of your shield bar. Some enemies and bosses in later levels have plasma or laser weapons that can strip your shields in an instant, mine-layers are also a serious threat. Getting shot down ends the game and sends you back to the main menu. There is only one save slot per pilot, which is overwritten every time you save, so be careful.<br><br>Each mission ends with a massive boss ship to destroy before you can proceed, and some missions have an extra boss ship appear in the middle of the level. These will follow you to the end if you don't destroy them, so get rid of them quickly.<br><br>There are a few gripes I have with the weapon balance. The Auto-Track Minigun and Laser Turret easily overshadow most of the other weapons in their price range, and The Bomb (not to be confused with the Megabomb item) would have been useful as an Always Equipped weapon but is basically useless as a Selectable weapon. There's a Power Disruptor which briefly jams the weapons of any ships it hits, but of course to keep it from being too useful they made most ships immune to the jamming effect. And it's priced directly between the Minigun and Turret, so you're not likely to bother with it anyway. Once you get the ultimate weapon late in the game, the Twin Laser, almost everything else you've acquired becomes obsolete except for rare situations, and enemy toughness really requires you to use this weapon by the endgame.<br><br>Ship variety and level design are impressive for a game from this period; they do a good job of keeping the pressure on. There is also a huge amount of detailed destructible terrain to tear down.<br><br>Overall, this is pretty satisfying for those of us who remember old school arcade games.
