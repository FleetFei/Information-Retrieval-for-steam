class=\"postedDate\">Posted: January 13\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHailing from the Artifex Mundi catalog, <i>Dark Arcana: The Carnival<\/i> isn't probably one of the best games brilliant HOG developer\/producer team presented us so far. You see, said publisher has spoiled us to the degree of expecting narrative quality and story consistency from ordinary hidden object games alongside artistic quality in display, so compared to some prior master pieces, this one feels a bit pallid. Yet, it is still a charming enough game to go for in a lazy evening, endowed with your casual Artifex Mundi display quality.<br><br>We are yet another lady detective, this time summoned to the place of the local carnival to investigate an unusual case. A little girl's mother has disappeared, leaving her in an anonymous crowd of a carnival, of all places. Usually, it should be the other way around, right? Little girl disappears and the mother pleads that we should find her? Nope, not this time. Presented with the photograph of her mother quite conveniently, we start our search. Through our investigation, we are quick to notice that the only current inhabitant of this colorful yet creepy place, is the master of the carnival: knife-thrower extraordinaire Jim Gibbons. He also has a spectacular talent of avoiding us, so we keep tracking Jim to the point of travelling to a mirror dimension where everything ends up as an eerie, twisted version of the original. This is one persistent detective, I tell you!<br><br>Track the victim, learn the reasons of the perpetrator, save the day. Text-book hidden object story pace, alongside the millionth Hollywood action movie. Even though the earlier parts of the game are a bit ambiguous and unrealistic - detective chasing after Jim and casually hopping down into mirror world without even going through &quot;wait, what, alien dimensions are real?&quot; phase - , the game makes up for the most with a neatly arranged plot-twist nearing the middle. The rest is your casual puzzle solving genius, wrapped around a tragic and desperate romance.<br><br>The theme of choice is a big plus, if you ask me, considering travelling carnivals have always been the melting pot of the eerie and the spectacular for the Western culture. The Ferris wheel, lion tamer, acrobats, knife-throwers, fortuneteller, freak shows... you name it. Anything extraordinary comes with it, which doesn't necessarily mean safe or reliable. All the artwork here in this game, are accordingly appropriate for the theme: half-way enwrapped in vibrant colors and innocent displays of wonder. Yet, the moment we travel to the mirror world, everything turns into an elegant creep fest. I loved every single scene of it. This package of display comes with decent character animations, believable voice-acting and an efficient soundtrack. Kudos for the display, as always.<br><br>Gameplay consists of various HOG scenes, yet you get to visit the same scenes over and over again in their doubles: the original world version and the mirror world version. It becomes a bit annoying after a while. They are fairly easy to go through though, taking just a bit of your attention span. If you are bored of HOG scenes, you are presented with an alternative Monaco gameplay to replace it, which is a nice flavor. Puzzles are not really challenging, most of it is flavor for the setting, nothing more. Which really makes the game lose some points is the excruciating amount of backtracking though. If you are playing in expert difficulty for the sake of the achievement, it becomes a chore to go through all areas that might have opened in the given time.<br><br>As the last word, <i>Dark Arcana: The Carnival<\/i> is an okay HOG with a nice little story, enchanting setting and somewhat tedious gameplay. I'd recommend going for it in a sale, or as the part of a bundle rather than jumping into it. If you are familiar with the developer team, you'll find this one likable anyhow. If not, I strongly recommend a much more well-crafted title to begin. expertise is required in solving a mysterious case. The game features a new, alternate method for solving hidden object scenes and features a fairly good story.<br><br>In the intro we see the kidnapping of a young woman right next from her daughter in a carnival. There's also a shady and disturbed looking man around, who - as we later find it out - is involved in the criminal act. The villain is probably the timidest one ever seen in a game, seriously, I can't remember seeing a bad guy running away so many times from us as this one. As we progress, we'll encounter the dark side of the carnival and of course it'll turn out that this isn't just a simple kidnapping and there are much greater things at stake. There's also a not so forseeable plot twist towards the end of them game, which makes it even more interesting.<br>After finishing the main story we can undertake the bonus chapter in which we take the role of the aforementioned shady character and basically continue from the point where we left off. It's great fun and is definitely worthwhile to give it a go.<br><br>We'll have the opportunity to travel to the twisted and bizarre version of the carnival (by stepping through mirrors, which function as portals) and these scenes are almost identical to the original ones, except they are usually very disturbing, almost nightmarish locations with the twisted and grotesque equivalents of the real world (this includes a fair bit of dead bodies as well).<br><br>The balance between hidden object scenes, minigames and puzzles including item use is fairly good, altough we get to revisit almost every HO scene sooner or later. Most of the minigames are carnival or circues themed and are fairly easy to solve. The monaco game (we have to match adjacent cards displaying the same figures) helps those stuck in hidden object scenes and have hard time finding a particular item (or we can just play solely monaco instead). There are achievements related to monaco exclusively, making the full obtainment of HO achievements impossible, therefore a minimum of 2 playthroughs are required for completionists. For some reason the map travel doesn't work, but since I rarely use it in other games, it didn't really bother me.<br>We are also introduced to a helpful companion: a monkey, who'll be staying with us most of the time throughout our adventure, assisting us acquiring otherwise unreachable items. As an additional help he also lets out a squeaking sound whenever he can aid us.<br><br>Since I mentioned 'child- or family friendliness' before (it is a HOG afterall, with the prime audience being women and children), it's fair if I make it clear: the game can be disturbing and shocking. As a fan of horror games and thrillers, personally I loved it, but it may not be ideal for small children. There are other, cute, but still exciting HOGs out there with great stories (I'll try to review them as well in the future, if I can). <br><br>Graphically the surreal imagery works well, with the greenish-yellow colour dominating (in the alternate world), the bizzare and disturbing scenes look very artistic and beautiful. The cutscenes are some of the best I've seen in this genre, there's hardly any pixelation and they look greatly fluid. Maybe some of the facial animation during certain voiceovers appear to be not that good, but these are rare.<br>The music is generally OK (with a well known carnival-tune), at least it isn't annyoing. The sound effects that co-occur during spooky moments are quite effective and gives a certain additional horror feel to the game.<br><br>Dark Arcana: The Carnival a well put together hidden object game with a good plot, interesting characters, some interesting elements (monaco + animal companion) and a surreal art design, which may attract or repulse players.
