free<\/div>\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a very iffy yes. I was a fan of the original Gunmetal Arcadia Zero, as a nut for Zelda 2-alikes, and I liked what it was doing with the worldbuilding, the factions, etc. So Gunmetal Arcadia expanding on that was definitely appealing to me. But Arcadia doesn't expand so much as re-do.<br><br>The game goes in a rogue-like direction, with new music, the ability to play as different characters, and lots of new stat-boosting upgrades. There are factions to join, which will affect how much things cost to sell\/buy in certain shops. The game is still linear-with-exploration like Zero and like Odallus the Dark Call, but the levels are randomly generated. The level design needs work, with rooms that have ladders\/platforms that lead to nowhere. Tricky jumps in the air across temporary blocks that.. lead to nowhere. At one point I encountered item shops on back to back screens, then went through a locked door to find... an item shop. This is the kind of random generation that really frustrates me. It shouldn't feel so obviously randomized. It's not organic at all. It's not always this bad but when it happens it definitely warrants a side-eye.<br><br>If you die in this game, unlike Zero, you have to start over from the beginning (in a newly-generated world). But the Legacy system will give you perks upon starting over based on what happened in your previous run; what factions you joined, if you didn't join a faction, if you fell in combat, etc. Which can confer bonus hearts, temporary hitpoints (shields), or stat boosts. It's not a grind-to-win game, thankfully. They're just a minor boost to give you a little help getting off the starting block. <br><br>Each item and faction shop carries some sort of stat boosting item, weapon, or subweapon. They can increase your movement speed, attack speed, jump height, attack damage, attack multiplier, etc. One upgrade lets you attack vertically (including the infamous pogo-attack of Zelda 2). There seems to be a double jump (which I didn't run into). Subweapons work like Castlevania (stars being your ammo), and range from straightforward dagger-style attacks, to spread shots, spiraling orbs, rising tridents. Some of these return from Zero, while others are brand new. An interesting addition to the subweapons converts your stars into a random item. Sometimes it's a heart, or bombs, or even an enemy.<br><br>Like in Zero, each level has a mid-boss and a final boss. On each run, the bosses you face are randomized with the exception of Anathema at the end. These bosses are all pulled from Zero. It's possible that there are brand new bosses that I missed, but from my playthrough of the game (getting to Anathema and everything), it was all repeats from the first game. Similarly, if you are a fan of the CRT effects of Minor Key Games' other titles, it's here as well, with the ability to create presets as well. Even the areas themselves are based on the regions from the first game, including the very cool hive world, with platforms made of organs and ladders made of intestines.<br><br>That's why it's best to think of Arcadia as a re-imagining of Zero rather than a sequel or expansion. While Zero was a flat-out old school retro game, Arcadia is the modern take on the same type of game. For me, personally, I prefer Zero, but I also can't deny that people LOVE these kinds of games so I would not want to sway people from it just because of a personal preference. If things go your way you can get a really good run. My big run got me to two rows of hearts\/temp HP and 4 stars in a lot of stats. I just prefer hand-crafted level design and that'll never change. Minor Key Games has never really failed me, though.
