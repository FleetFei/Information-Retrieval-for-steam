class=\"postedDate\">Posted: September 19, 2015\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Weird Park games tell the story of a spoopy ghost clown and his sinister little jester buddy, operating out of an abandoned amusement park. Each installment casts you as some kind of investigator looking into a part of the park's past, and having to assemble crests, collect socks, and shoot crossbows to solve the mystery. They're all hidden object games in that grand European style where the English and the logic are just a little bit off, but you can see the polish get layered thicker and thicker as the series progresses. Honestly, there's a lot of difference between the three games, so I'll talk a little about each one.<br><br>Broken Tune is the first in the series, and is about as straight-forward a hidden object game as you can get. You pick up inventory items, you use them on things, you find 12 doodads in hidden object scenes to get another inventory item. There are a few puzzles, but no gimmicks, no item combinations, and no map. However, the game itself is very well laid-out, with items arranged so that they will send you back to areas that have new points of interest. The uses of items are crystal clear, and the hidden object scenes are clean and detailed. One interesting feature of the game is that it is split evenly into two acts, with no backtracking or items shared between halves. The graphics are good, the voice acting is goofy, and it'll last you a good two hours.<br><br>Scary Tales is the middle installment and a huge step up from Broken Tune. This one adds more puzzles, cutscenes, and areas to explore. The game is also WAY longer, clocking in at nearly 4 hours. In terms of design, there's more variety to the different areas. The first third of the game is extremely open, allowing you to travel to many different rooms right from the start. This does make the opening act a bit harder, however, because you get a crapload of items at once and no real clear direction for using some of them. After that the game goes into some really creative places and plays with fairy tale concepts. It's a great game to follow the first with, especially since it's more difficult overall.<br><br>The Final Show is, unsurprisingly, the last in the series. Again it improves over the previous game, this time adding new variations of hidden object scenes, interactive items you can manipulate, an upgrade system (!), achievements (in-game, not Steam), and a map for ease of travel. Final Show is just as long as Scary Tales if not longer, and is split into convenient chapters with no backtracking between. It also goes full-on crazy with the setting, having only the tutorial set in the real world. Oh yeah, it has a tutorial, too, for some reason. The graphics are fantasic, and there are quite a few voiced NPCs in this one as well, as opposed to the mostly barren games before it. The upgrade system might just be cosmetic, but having something else to find in addition to the necessary items was a treat. There's even a bonus chapter you unlock after beating it that fills in some of the backstory, and is honestly one of the best-designed chapters in the series. My only gripe is that Final Show is harder still than Scary Tales, which pushes it into slightly unfun territory sometimes. In particular, some of the item logic is really questionable. Beyond that, though, it's easily the most polished and deep game of the three.<br><br>Taken as a whole, Weird Park Trilogy feels like a great way to get into hidden object games because each part of the series is so different. They form a spectrum from bare-bones to feature-rich, which lets you discover precisely what your taste is. There's even a complete story told across them, though it's perfectly goofy and predictable. It's also hard to beat the value, since you're getting 10+ hours of game in a genre where most will only get you 2. My one piece of advice if you do get into it, though, is to play on the lower difficulties. Whereas the &quot;Expert Modes&quot; in other hidden object games make the puzzles and searches more challenging, all it does in the Weird Park games is eliminate the highlights around hidden object scenes. It's an annoyance made worse by the amount of backtracking you have to do sometimes, because each scene has to be done multiple times at different points in the game. Remember that if you ever get stuck, too... go back and look for a scene that's reactivated. With the hints and the improvements of the later games, though, it shouldn't distract too much from the overall experience.
