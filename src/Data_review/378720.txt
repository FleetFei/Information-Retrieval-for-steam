rel=\"noopener\" >http:\/\/www.gamebits.com.br\/2017\/03\/resenha-thea-the-awakening.html<\/a><br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Review in English:<\/div>Thea: The Awakening is a hard game to define. It has elements of survival, has card based combats, elements of crafting, has a deep narrative like an RPG, uses procedural generation and a system of a roguelike, with permanent death. If you summarize all this and seek to be as concise as possible, it would be correct to say that it is a &quot;4X Roguelike&quot;, as these are the most apparent characteristics. Just like a 4X game, you will need to explore, expand, search for exploits and exterminate to win. All, of course, with the high difficulty and randomization of roguelikes.<br><br>This is a cautious recommendation though. Thea is not a game for anyone, and even the most persistent will need to be patient while learning some tricks. Despite the simple mechanics and the robust tutorial, this game has a rather high learning curve. There are several times when the player finds himself in a seemingly impossible situation, even in the easiest difficulties, and it will take a good deal of strategy and even a time out of the game to reflect and understand some of the conflicts you'll find. If you're willing to be patient and experiment enough to know how to deal with each situation, Thea will become more and more enthralling.<br><br>Based on Slavic mythology, Thea tells a tale of a cursed world, and it is up to you to rebuild it before it collapses. Even using random elements, such as more than 100 missions and differentiated quests, there are some passages that are always the same and must be completed with each match to achieve victory. These passages are special missions that give continuity to the narrative, and there are several different endings for the player to discover, which increases the replayability. <br><br>The gameplay basically has three pillars: collect, craft\/ build and battle; Everything is done in your village and also during expeditions that you send to clear dungeons and fulfill objectives. There are several classes available, and you can choose the focus of your village when you start the game. Would you rather have more gatherers than warriors? Or is it better to focus on medics? Either way, whatever your focus is, the game distributes the classes well, and soon your village will have all of them; The focus you have chosen will increase the chances of a child growing up to become a chosen class when it reaches adulthood. Well, these three pillars are equally important, although it is possible to create a slightly more inclined strategy for some of them. Food and wood must be collected to support the population, and it is necessary to craft armor, items and weapons to equip your people, so they will be able to perform well in battles.<br><br>Speaking of battles, there are nine types of battles in the game. It is not necessary to resolve conflicts only in fights, but also in discussions (social challenges), challenges of strength, cure of diseases, among others. Each character class has the most accented skills in some aspect that will be useful for each different conflict. For instance, warriors are exceptional in fights and strength challenges, but on the other hand they can't cure sickness like medics, who are also good at social challenges. All these types of battles are solved in a very strategic card game that was developed by people (from MuHa Games) who created Gwent, the card game of The Witcher 3 recently got their own title. This system is easy to learn, but I'll tell you a detail that is not mentioned in the tutorial: Determining which card will be attacked (left or right) is done randomly, with a 50% chance of being left and 50% of being right. If there is a card between two cards, it can attack any of them, but if the card is the first card on the table, it will always attack the front card, just as the last card at the table will always attack the back card. With that in mind it is much easier to plan your strategy, since we have to take into account the two closest cards instead of just leaving a card with more health points always ahead, for example.<br><br>Although it can be considered a 4X game, the expansion aspect is not taken here in the traditional way of this genre. That is, it is not possible to settle or conquer new villages, and you will fight only against monsters, not other civilizations. The expansion in Thea is done at the population level. Gradually new children will born in your village, and every turn there is a chance to this child become an adult. It is not possible to interfere in this, but it is possible to make constructions that can attract more (already adult) people to your village, in addition to being able to attract some creatures to join you too. There is also no control over when your characters will level up. Extreme randomness, incidentally, is a watershed in Thea. It significantly increases the replay value, making each match unique, but on the other hand it can generate impossible situations, such as the fact that your village (which always starts at a fixed place that you can't choose) Is located in a place that doesn't have food available, requiring restarting.<br><br>Thea seems to take the concept of roguelike to the extreme; More than Conquest of Elysium took. The game also adopts the roguelike system, in which it is not possible to reload a saved game file, and there is only one slot to save. I understand that this is a game design because it was developed with this challenge in mind, but this makes some mistakes lead a match to its end, because the player will hardly be able to recover from a whole dead expedition by a dragon, for instance. This makes the game extremely brutal for people who are not accustomed to the permanent death system, which unfortunately can take away a portion of the community that is passionate about RPGs and 4X, which are other strong genres in Thea, and can discourage some people to continue playing. In fact, Thea was made not to be beaten in the first match, even in the easiest difficulty. Each god chosen at the beginning of the game has five levels with buffs, which are unlocked as you accumulate points at the end of each match, even in a lost game. In addition to premade options, you can customize the difficulty the way you want, but notice that even though it will not guarantee victory for a long time, it's a great tool for when you get more experienced and want to add extra challenges.<br><br>Thea has received several content patches and free expansions, so you do not have to spend extra money to experience the full game.<br><br><b>Pros<\/b><br>* Addictive and fun to play<br>* Great card battle system<br>* Excellent tutorial<br>* Remarkable soundtrack<br>* High replayability<br>* Narrative very well
