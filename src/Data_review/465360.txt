I originally played the demo when I was growing up, which only had 2 maps (this version has 20), but the game was that good and innovative that I must have played those maps dozens of times each!<br><br>Major con: This game likes to crash. A lot. And if memory serves this was true in 1997 as well. I mean seriously, how did they ever release it like this? As a programmer I know that memory corruption bugs can be damn hard to track down, but jeez! Just make sure you save fairly often (at the very least after every mission) and you won't lose too much progress. Mostly I only lost a minute here or there, though I did lose an entire mission when it crashed right as I won :(<br><br>The single player campaign took me 17 hours to complete every mission, which is a pretty decent amount of content for it's time. It's possible to skip some missions or play them in a different order, so it could probably be done in less time if you rush. The difficulty curve is good - the earlier missions should ease you into the game, while some of the later missions really keep you on your toes and you really can't let your guard down. I tried doing one of the missions earlier than I should have, and was absolutely creamed as neither I nor my support units could take down any of the enemy turrets. Went back and did a few other missions to get some better tech and had a... less difficult time.<br><br>Since the game is pretty unique, I'll go over it's gameplay a little:<br><br>You directly control a one of a kind souped up Tank (The Wraith) on the battlefield, but you also call in support units as you need them. But, unlike other strategy games, most of the support units (except the Bomber) teleport directly to your location allowing you to instantly deploy a formidable army whenever you need it, and periodically reinforce it. When you've finished blasting whatever it was to smitherines, you simply recycle the units to recover their resources so you can deploy them again elsewhere (or not, if you would rather leave them to defend the area or just have plenty of resources to spare).<br><br>You capture Citadels in the game, which are huge towers with big guns that serve as your bases. They are capable of defending themselves, but you will often need to take direct control of them when they come under attack (which leavs your Wraith vaulnerable, but doing so automatically raises it's shields to maximum and it can take quite a beating). This also allows you to easily deploy more units and turrets around the Citadel to help defend it, even though you might be way over the other side of the map.<br><br>Given it's from 1997 you will have to spend some time rebinding the controls to modern best practices (and turn that damn mouse acceleration off and bump the sensitivity up to max), but that's all doable. The main changes I had to make was WASD for movement, TAB for overhead map, Q to control a Citadel, R to recycle a unit. Cycle power triangle is fine on C, but learn to use it effectively! I set my mouse wheel to generate [ and ] to switch secondary weapons, though you can use the number row for some of them as well. I left the unit deploy keys as F1-F7 even though they are a little awkward because I'm still used to them being that from 1997.<br><br>Minor con: This was one of the earliest 3D DirectX games and the draw distance sucks as a result. You can raise it in the settings a little, but even the max is still pretty low. I find this more annoying than the low resolution, especially when you know there is an enemy building just out of the draw distance from a Citadel and if only you could see where it was you could
