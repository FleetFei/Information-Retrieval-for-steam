class=\"postedDate\">Posted: March 10\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI first bought this game when it came out, since I'd heard some great things about these games. I ended up playing for a couple hours before shelving it for something else, and for the next couple years just glanced at it in my Steam library, thinking &quot;I should probably play this&quot; but never actually doing so. Finally in January I just knuckled down, got out of the prologue, and then almost didn't stop playing it until I was done both games. I wish I had done so earlier, as the two Trails in the Sky games make up one of the best RPGs on Steam, and I'd say certainly the best JRPG.<br><br>The Legend of Heroes games all take place on Zemuria, a continent in the midst of an industrial revolution using what's called Orbment technology, a fantastical power source that fuels just about everything, even empowering humans to superhuman abilities. The Sky games specifically occur in The Kingdom of Liberl, a small nation who specialize in the technology that's recovering from a costly war with the much larger Empire of Erebonia. There's a lot of political background in the two games, which is interesting.<br><br>You play as Estelle and Joshua Bright, two teenagers working to become Bracers, general do-gooders that take jobs across Liberl. Not long after they attain the Junior rank, their father, legendary bracer Cassius Bright, is called away on a job, and mysterious things start to happen that propel them across the continent. I'm sure that sounds a bit trite, but believe me when I say Sky's story is very good, and virtually all of the important characters are likeable in their own way, in part owing to the great translation XSeed gave it. Estelle and Joshua in particular are both great lead characters, and I found myself actually wanting to do side quests just because the dialogue in them was always good. There's a ton of world-building in both games, and Liberl feels fully thought out as a setting.<br><br>The combat system is also nice, showing you the exact turn order Final Fantasy X-style. You can manipulate by buffing someone's speed of course, but also by using attacks that will push an enemy back down the line, or by using Arts, the magic your orbments give you which take two turns to cast. Bonuses will pop up along the turn order, and using the right move at the right time will prevent an enemy getting a boost to their damage, for example. A character's Arts are determined by what augments you equip in their Orbment - those give bonuses but also decrease a stat too, so to some extent you can minmax characters how you like.<br><br>There are some negatives to Sky, though. Not only is this game quite long itself even if you try to rush it, it's also only half the story, since it ends right when another JRPG's plot would have gotten going. It wasn't intentional by Falcom from the start, and it owes to the fact this was originally a PSP game, but it still means you have to buy Second Chapter to see the proper ending. In that regard I suppose it's good I didn't play it originally, since SC took a bit to come out afterward. <br><br>I also said the battle system was interesting, but I found myself disliking a lot of the boss battles, which in theory should utilize it most. Practically all of them between both games rely a lot on having multiple targets or minions that are around to be annoying, and can often be resummoned by the boss. In many cases it felt like the main difficulty was just dealing with them, and then the actual boss wasn't much trouble after, although I was only playing on normal both times. I felt a couple bosses were a bit spongey too. Some characters also feel more useful than others in battle, like one who in theory is meant to be a glass cannon, with high damage and low health, except many characters can do a large amount of damage without having that problem. <br><br>Finally this is just a personal gripe but one character you encounter early on in FC, Olivier, I basically couldn't stand. He's the comic relief of the bunch (which the game doesn't exactly need a specific character for), but that amounts to him saying something useless or annoying in every conversation he's present for, and it gets old. He also has a habit of flirting with male characters in really over the top fashion, something the characters think of him as a weirdo for, which is...yeah, seems a bit needless.<br><br>Overall though these flaws only really brought the game down to a 9 from a 10 for me. The Sky games may be close to a masterpiece, and have a hugely satisfying ending when all's said and done. I fully recommend both of them to anyone looking for a good RPG, particularly on sale as they are at the time of this review. Seems like XSeed is finishing up their translation of The Third, kind of an extra epilogue chapter after the conclusion of Second Chapter, and I'm looking forward to that as well.\t\t\t\t\t<div
