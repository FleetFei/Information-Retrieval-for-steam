I feel many people could feel like even their $1 was wasted on this game, so please read the review if you might want to use your money on another game.<br><br>The concept behind the game is quite clever. Basically, the core of the gameplay revolves around making dialogue choices to uncover things about characters you can use to frame them, as well as choosing two items to use in your deed (generally, an 'evidence' item to plant, and a weapon, though you can choose two evidences or two weapons if you want).<br><br>With many conversation paths and all of the various combinations of evidence, rooms to plant evidence, and weapons to use, there are a lot of different ways the game seemingly can unfold. The main entertainment in the game is from playing repeatedly (the game is only 20 minutes long or so the first couple of times, and you can go as fast as 5 minutes on successive runs if you're not bothering to read all the text you already read before)<br><br>But this is where the first real problem with the game arises. You don't have to be perfect and make every &quot;right&quot; choice in order to frame someone, which is good, since there are tons of possible combinations of things to say and item usages. But what's very disappointing is that the solutions are so obvious and loose to success that you once you frame one person, you can pretty much follow the same 'formula' to frame anyone just as easily. There's almost no variety in the solutions, and everything is so obvious you'd have to purposely try to fail. I got an &quot;unsolved&quot; case my first game because I was just experimenting with the systems and choosing whatever, but after that I solved everyone on my first try without any effort.<br><br>Which items are connected to which character are so obvious you don't even need to talk to the characters to guess who it should be linked to. Your dad's shotgun is obviously linked to your dad. Your mom's diary is obviously about your mom. The descriptions of the items will usually just tell you almost directly who it will help frame.<br><br>The conversation paths are pretty much the same. Basically just make sure you talk to the person you're wanting to frame, and they'll just reveal something you can abuse as a motive and you're done. There are like one or two cases where you'll have to be careful about what you pick or actually talk to more than one person. But that's rare. You generally will have the option of being extremely rude to the person which will make them not talk to you, or doing literally anything else which will have them reveal a motive for you.<br><br>As for evidence dropping, it seemed to me as long as you dropped the evidence in a certain room, it would always point to the person you're wanting to frame. For every character in the game except one, I dropped the evidence in that room and it worked perfectly.<br><br>By the end of the game, I was starting to become convinced you could use any weapon you wanted and still frame whoever you wanted, too. The solutions seemed loose enough for that. I think you could even shoot her in the back with a shotgun and get it ruled a suicide.<br><br>But if you somehow don't realize the best solution right away, you could play through a few times and still be curious about how to frame someone. And I don't just mean the fact that it obviously was not proofread. But the author is definitely an amateur. Expect the story quality to be on par with something like the Jeff the Killer creepypasta.<br><br>There's a ton of awkwardness that arises from the story not being thought through well or proofread by another person, from the character knocking on the door to the house when he is expected and the butler is already escorting him, to being able to kill someone with a few whacks with a lightweight broomstick, without them causing any kind of struggle or reaction.<br><br>Most of the story revolves around getting to know the characters and the aspects of them you use to frame them. And almost none of the murder motives make any sense. And the conversations all feel a bit rushed and unnatural. Everyone's motive seems to boil down to one character trait about them. &quot;He has a temper, so obviuosly he would kill someone.&quot; That kind of thing. Sometimes the &quot;character flaw&quot; you're exposing doesn't even make sense as a murder motive. &quot;He sexually harasses women&quot; is not a murder motive. &quot;She's a lesbian&quot; is not a murder motive.<br><br>Even the main character who really is the killer -- I don't really understand why he wants to kill his sister. He hates his family and left them to start his own independent life, where he was apparently doing just fine. But now he decides he is going to go through the trouble of killing his own sister and framing someone for it so that he can get an inheritence... of a family name and property and business that he hates and wants to have no part in?<br><br>But by far the worst part about the writing is that the story attempts to cover a lot of serious themes -- mental illness, suicide, child abuse, sexual harassment, murder motive, etc. -- but the author clearly doesn't know much about these things and did not research any of these topics and handles them all completely inappropriately and unrealistically. These topics are portrayed with ignorance and shallowness. <br><br>The portrayal of a person suffering from mental illness amounts to pretty much how a small child would describe a &quot;crazy person.&quot; I realize that in the 1940s, mental health issues were not commonly understood at all, and doctors did indeed just diagnose &quot;insanity.&quot; It would make sense for the characters to talk about mental illness in an ignorant way -- but a character who is supposed to actually suffer from a mental illness should, well, actually be written like a person with a real mental health issue, even if it's not understood by the characters themselves. I feel like the author doesn't understand mental illness any more than a 1940s doctor would.<br><br>Pretty much every serious topic in the game is handled in the same manner. The game is clearly meant to be taken seriously, and it is story-driven, so the story needs to be well-researched, proofread, and developed, but it sorely fails in this area. If I hadn't been playing while talking to everyone on twitch and being able to talk and laugh about it, I may have been extremely frustrated with the poor writing. Instead, my friends and I have some new in-jokes based on some of the ridiculousness (poison turns you green, cunning pixie, etc.)<br><br>If you're wanting an interesting or intriguing story, or gameplay that requires a lot of tough decision making to solve (or rather, commit!) a murder, don't waste your dollar.
