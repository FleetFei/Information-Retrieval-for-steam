The story isn't very deep, but it's well written and gives enough background.<br><br>The gameplay isn't without flaws though. There's the odd auto leveling of prices for inns and items. class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<i>At first glance, The Princess' Heart isn't your textbook RosePortal product. The pre-made Enterbrain RTP visuals and mapping seems lazy. But is it? Is the game just a quick cash grab or is there more to it than a simple premade asset layer?<\/i><br><br>At first, when I saw the promotional screenshots for the game, I was appalled. Not only by what I saw as below average mapping (at least by RosePortal's usual high standards, compared to many other RPGs out there it doesn't stand out that much) but also horribly small characters that had barely anything redeemable to them. And the battle system - goodbye side-view custom battles, here we go, pre-made &quot;first person&quot; combat with sprites not really animated at all.<br><br>And yet, I gave this game a chance and played it through. In full.<br><br>And I don't regret one single minute.<br><br>Why? Like I said, graphics-wise the game is very much below RosePortal's usual average. It's not all that about the RTP tileset: the resource can be used to create beautiful environments that would be very much on par with Whisper of a Rose's (it just didn't happen here, instead we have a bunch of square-looking mazes), it's more about the characters, both the playable and NPC ones as well as the monsters. RosePortal made it a habit to have each monster fully animated in combat, here we have a simulation of movement by close-up effects or stretching and un-stretching the enemy pictures. The character sprites are RPG Maker VX Ace's defaults, meaning they occupy one tile (that provides little room for them to look good) instead of RosePortal's regular, whooping three. While the environments are varied, some of them just don't look authentic. Instead of posting a series of trees or a large unpenetrable layer of bushes\/trees as the level &quot;wall&quot;, RosePortal applied the one tile that I think shouldn't be used: a tree trunk on a black background... that looks grotesque when seen on the corners as the blackness between the trees would have been understandable in the middle but not at the corner. A few of the maps seem empty and barely decorated\/filled with content, I'm mainly looking at the Old Dayaz desert area and Maplefield. The only actual custom graphics I've noticed are: the title screen and the intro movie, which both are really well done.<br><br>However, I'm bringing up what I said before: I don't regret one single minute spent on playing this game, not one bit. And that is because, quite frankly, The Princess' Heart delivers much more in terms of other departments than in terms of just visuals.<br><br>Let's start with the music. It's, again, downright beautiful. RosePortal have once again delivered an alluring, orchestral-sounding set of musical pieces which all fit quite well into the game's environments and narrative. I can't say it more clearly: buy the Deluxe version, just for the soundtrack alone, the music being already in the game files be damned. They deserve support just for the quality of that soundtrack alone. My absolute favorites are Dark Heavens (the demon boss combat theme) and The Beyond, named after one of the game's areas. Epicness absolutely ensues. Not a single RTP track was used in the making of this game, a stark contrast with the visual layer.<br><br>As far as the gameplay goes, this is your standard jRPG fare with a wee little twist. Just a little. There is no crafting option nor there are any weapons to be picked up (so that option is useless), instead like in Sweet Lily Dreams, the only thing we can equip our characters with are accessories. The combat system is simplistic but works - albeit it does have two new options. First, some spells have an area of effect - so a single-target spell may actually hit some neighbouring targets if you choose the right victim. That can actually save you a lot of MP which would normally be used for all-enemy-targeting spells. Second, you can swap characters in the active party not only outside of battles but also in-between turns! This can get you out of sticky situations - if some characters get knocked out and you have no means to bring them back, simply replace them with others, fully healed and just waiting to kick the monsters' butts. I happened to use this feature just once, on the final boss, but it sure counts: when it was almost dead and I knew my current party doesn't have what it takes to kill it in one volley nor will they survive its next attack, I just replaced them and in three further turns, the battle was over. I was the one left standing.<br><br>About the battles: they aren't a chore but they can be a challenge. Areas can technically be completed outside the beaten track as besides the final three dungeons, you can reach pretty much every location quite early on in the game. Enemies do NOT respawn in-between level sections most of the time, so you have to leave to the World Map - this may be a lifesaver if you're particularly wasted after just narrowly beating That One Boss. They do however have quite a level of difficulty, also due to the scarcity of items. There isn't an abundance: there's just what you get from the chests plus what you'll buy in the shops but with one catch: the prices rise alongside your overall character level so you won't be able to afford 999 of those Elixirs or Magic Waters. Most of the time, you need to make do with what you have found from chests. Money isn't earned by just winning the battle either, nor is it rewarded by the completion of a quest - you need to sell what you've acquired from battles in order to make some money.<br><br>About quests... well, there's just eleven of them, so not that all much, but then again a game advertised as 15-hour is less than a half of what you'd spend on Whisper of a Rose, so that number would kind of fit. What irks me is their overall lack of variety. Half of the sidequests involve you bringing the questgiver a bunch of high-quality drops from the monsters. The others do make much more sense but there's more than one occasion of the trope &quot;Guide Dang It&quot; being invoked. And it's a bad trope to invoke. I mean particularly the Cambolton Fortress quest involving the king's soul. Honestly, given what the Counselor was saying, one would think that the first place one should visit is the desert city Daragit. Uh... suffice it to say that it's a waste of time. The solution is right there in Cambolton, it's just so non-intuitive and so hard to spot that I literally needed a guide to complete it and I wasn't the only one, judging from the forums. A couple quests end in a really anti-climatic way, such as the Arsonist one (why didn't I get the option to deal with the guilty party?!) or the Emily exorcism one (props for the &quot;Exorcism of Emily Rose&quot; reference by the way) - why wasn't I able to actually WHACK the possessing demon instead of just having it expelled like someone snapped a finger and said &quot;begone&quot;? Yep, the sidequests definitely lack polish. The only reward you'll ever get for them is Journey Points which are used to learn skills. And again, there's a glaring issue: at some point (as a little grinding <i>is<\/i> recommended to progress smoothly and you also get JPs after gaining character levels) there's simply TOO MUCH of these points to spend'em.<br><br>----- review continued under &quot;Comments&quot;
