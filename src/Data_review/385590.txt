class=\"postedDate\">Posted: November 14, 2015\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt seems that lately I've become more of a casual JRPG* fan than I care to admit. Just to give an example: While my <i>The Legend of Heroes: Trails in the Sky<\/i> installation gathers cobwebs and silently weeps, I've played through three <i>Compile Heart<\/i> games. Now when I use the word &quot;casual&quot;, I don't mean it in the sense of &quot;Those filthy casuals are killing my hardcore games!&quot; A casual game is something I play when I want to relax. Lethal RPG: War isn't much to look at, but it's a perfectly safe product for casual consumption. <br><br>The undead menace must be stopped! The main character is named Lethal and...oh...really? <b>Lethal?!<\/b> The only way this clown could be any edgier, is if he had spikey hair and an oversized sword. *ahem* Anyway, you're tasked with leading <b>Lethal<\/b> and his party on a world-saving adventure. On your journey you'll complete several quests, fight vicious monsters, upgrade gear, and challenge dangerous bosses. The storyline is serviceable, and the few instances of personality in the cast are welcome. However, this game can be beaten in less than 10 hours, so don't expect much in the way of extensive character arcs. The guy who tells dumb jokes? He's staying that way. Don't expect him to stop joking and then tell everyone about his troubled past as a bandit.<br><br>Part of what makes Lethal RPG a casual JRPG experience is the lack of what we're accustomed to. There is an overworld, but there aren't any random encounters. There are several fixed fights all over the map, and you can even choose to fight whenever you want. Grinding isn't particularly necessary, because you get more than enough experience and materials, from fixed-fights and solving quests. The more startling omission is the complete lack of dungeons. There are numerous locations in this game, they all have dungeonesque names, but there's nothing to explore. What usually happens is that you'll fight a battle or two, and then move on. Aside from the arena, there aren't any situations where you have to carefully manage your resources. Oddly enough, I consider this a positive element. This game is very stream-lined, which makes the idea of dungeons, sound like needless padding.<br><br>The battle-system in this game is fairly solid. There are a remarkable number of characters to use, and they're mostly well-balanced. Much of my strategy for the boss-fights revolves around status effects. Yep, this is one of those rare JRPGs, where debilitating effects actually work. Bosses afflicted by poison, burns, and bleeding can lose large chunks of HP every round. You might find it more effective to use tomes to exploit weaknesses. This can include casting spells the enemy is weak against, as well as enchanting weapons with particular elements. Every party member has their own set of skills, and a touch of variety is always nice. Unfortunately, characters - depending on when they're introduced - can fall behind in experience. On the other hand, a couple levels usually won't make a difference. I also appreciate that even the regular encounters can be dangerous. Losing a party-member during a fight with a &quot;trash-mob&quot; is not uncommon. <br><br>Upon completion of Lethal RPG: War, you're allowed to access New Game+ mode. As per genre standards, this mode lets you play through the story again while carrying over all levels, characters, and equipment upgrades. This also raises the monster's experience level, because it'd be too easy otherwise. It's a nice gesture, but it's like prestiging in a Multiplayer FPS. All you really have is an excuse to jump back on the hamster wheel, and dirty newspapers don't even get replaced. You're liable to use the same strategies for every encounter. Sure, you'll have a few more options to play around with, but all that's really changed, is that the numbers are much higher.<br><br>Another complaint that I have is the music. There really aren't enough tracks to go around. Actually, I guess I could consider this a positive. Since the soundtrack isn't all that great, it's a perfect excuse to catch up on other music or a few podcasts. Also the writing is a little too dry. Some of the NPCs will mention dramatic events, but they will be stated in such a matter-of-fact manner that your only response is &quot;Oh okay&quot;. I would have preferred a little drama, give these traumatic moments time to sink in, instead things just tend to happen. <br><br>Awhile back I reviewed another JRPG by the name of <i>Crystal Story II<\/i>. I stated that it had a certain nebulous charm. I think the same can be said for Lethal RPG: War. The protagonist is a dork, the writing is bland, and there isn't really much to do aside from fight monsters, upgrade equipment, and view story scenes. However, everything in this game is endearing in its own twisted way. It's a good palette cleanser when other games get so wrapped up in themselves, you forget which way is up. Maybe this also says something about my tastes. When it comes to reviewing 2D shooters and beatemups, I can be very strict. With JRPGs, even if they don't do much to stand out, I tend to be pretty lenient. Perhaps there is a certain joy exclusive to the genre. Adventuring, obtaining treasure, or raising stats, just feels good to me. Whatever the case, I think this game is okay. <br><br>*While not made in Japan, Lethal RPG does have the attributes of a common JRPG.<br><br><i>If you liked this review or want to see more recommended
