mankind; the lawyer all the wickedness, the theologian all the stupidity. - Arthur Schopenhauer<\/b><br><br>Before even attempting to review this game, I must say that <i>The Preposterous Awesomeness of Everything<\/i> is one of the most successful existential satires that I've ever come across, more so in a PC game. In its brilliance to take note the whole awkwardness of human nature and history, it never becomes boring. It is both a parody and a genuinely marvelous critique of the humankind itself. It is surprising, extraordinary and straight up hilarious in a bitter way.<br><br>If we are to present a vague baseline for out setting, our character is the member of a seemingly primitive society, residing on an island. We've lived here for all our lives in relative peace and prosperity, if you do count the occasional natural selection out. This idleness is bound to be lost though, the moment we discover the remains of a space rocket. Now, considering that the collective IQ of this limited society is below room temperature, they'll need some guidance for that kind of achievement. Luckily there is someone who isn't flat-out pre-neanderthal around: a mysterious gentleman named Drake. It will become your quest to aid Drake for the sake of your species, if we'd like to see some progress not based on the scale or pace of evolution. For the long run, keep in mind that humankind is supposed to be a malfunction in evolution itself though.<br><br>Without presenting spoilers - because it is pretty easy to do so by describing the narrative or ranting about the collection of topics tenderly noted in game - I may describe this short point &amp; click adventure as a philosophical experience, an invitation to observe the contemplative process of the developer himself; about nature, about politics, about social dynamics and a bit about himself. It is bold and refreshing, and completely relatable, at least for me.<br><br>The artwork consists of patchwork collages with exaggerations. It is not elegant, neither satisfying to look at. I personally think that it is a conscious choice to invoke some kind of discomfort to accompany the topics discussed in the game. Art itself alarmingly signifies the ugly nature of the creature we all know as human, dropping a clue about the whole progression of the story. You see, artistic aesthetic isn't the aim here. It aims for you to get annoyed, or at least get you to identify your already well founded annoyance about many things. The title of the game contains all that there is: The Preposterous Awesomeness of Everything. The whole journey that we've taken so far, and all of its side effect; its consequences. It all is grotesque, curious and absurd. And you know what? As a species, given a new chance, we'd do the same thing again.<br><br>About the gameplay... there is not much to say. It is a very basic point &amp; click adventure with a scrolling background and some areas within the island. You interact with people and objects - sometimes not in the most intellectual manner, I must add - and hope to make progress. Every little absurdity out there is for you to discover, and I must say that discovery has its own rewards in this game. The game autosaves when you manage to make some progress in game and reloads to that autosave when you return. It is possible to die via many unexpected and imaginative ways, all parodying some social standing points yet the autosave returns to your aid for you to keep trying uninterrupted.<br><br>At the end of the day, I think <i>The Preposterous Awesomeness of Everything<\/i> is a wonderful gem for your enjoyment, and it would worth every penny to check it out. It is a one man indie game, with some brilliance stored in it.
