>https:\/\/www.ticgn.com\/review-salmon-ninja-steam\/<\/a><br><br>Here\u2019s an indie title that took me by surprise: Salmon Ninja! The name may not say much upon first glance, but the actual game states otherwise. It\u2019s a challenging platformer that is not unlike that of Super Meat Boy. Like the aforementioned title, it\u2019s kind of a good game to say the least.<br><br>Story<br><br>The story in Salmon Ninja is a simple one. Our ninja character here is lost and wants to find his way home. There are cutscenes at the beginning and ending of each world that provide some character interactions as a means of substance for the minimal story. While I do wish a couple of the characters spoke English, the dialogue is otherwise amusing and almost makes me wish the respective speakers had more screen time.<br><br>Graphics<br><br>I never tire of the pixel art in these games. The graphical style in Salmon Ninja in particular is an enchanting use of pre-16-Bit-esque visuals. It helps that the performance is at no less than sixty frames per second! Considering how the game does thrive on being difficult and giving the player several looks at the death screen, it\u2019s a treat to realize that the transitions between everything are as instant as they could ever be. There are no loading screens; the action is front and center \u2013 as it should be! Speaking of, the animations and special effects are fluid and can be dazzling in their own right even if portions of the level get painted red with enemy or player blood.<br><br>Audio<br><br>The music is whimsical and atmospheric. The sound effects complement the pixel art. Together, this creates for a distinct kind of sound only some of the top-tier indie games seem to enjoy displaying. It gives off a bit of a Thomas Was Alone vibe to it. Although Salmon Ninja obviously doesn\u2019t play by the same rules as that game, the atmosphere does display a feel of something grand thanks to the audio complementing the scale of the level layouts.<br><br>Gameplay<br><br>Salmon Ninja, as said earlier, takes quite a lot of inspiration from Super Meat Boy. There are four worlds, and each world contains several levels that are more-or-less bite-sized platforming gauntlets. The ninja has wall-jumping abilities, a sword, and a grappling hook at his disposal to help progress through unscathed. He\u2019s also a glass cannon, however, so one hit from anything and he\u2019s done for! Thankfully, each level has a generous amount of checkpoints. That way, no level ever gets too frustrating.<br><br>I was actually pretty surprised how I managed to play through this one. Since players use the mouse to wield the sword or fire the grappling hook, I thought I was in for a danger zone I could never be able to finish playing through. In theory, it seemed like the mouse functions would only complicate the precise platforming players have to go through. But you know what? The game actually works wonders with the tools provided! Granted, the default button for jumping shouldn\u2019t have been the spacebar, but interchangeable controls make this a nonissue; I found myself being comfortable with using W for jumping and A and D for moving.<br><br>Salmon Ninja also has a solid degree of challenge. There is admittedly an emphasis on trial-and-error in a few places, but the checkpoint generosity and the magnificently swift transitioning from death to respawn drastically diminish the problems I typically have with trial-and-error gameplay. It assists in shifting the focus to what obstacles lie straight ahead and how the player should overcome them. Basically, legit challenge and not fake difficulty. Adding to the fun factor is the abundance of interesting enemies, obstacles, and clever design layouts.<br><br>Verdict<br><br>The overall result is an awesome title that is well worth your time! Salmon Ninja has all the right elements for a great Meat Boy-ish game, and it used them properly and triumphantly!
