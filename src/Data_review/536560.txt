experience with the &quot;vanilla version&quot; of Chaos Code on PS3.<br><br>One of the best fighting games out there that isn't from an established developer, and arguably a contender for the title of best non-Japanese fighting game. (FK Digital is based in Taiwan and Australia, although all the voice acting is in Japanese.) Chaos Code mixes attributes and mechanics from various Capcom, SNK and ArcSys fighters with a surprisingly appealing and memorable cast of characters, along with a well-animated visual style that's very much its own thing. While there isn't much backstory to the cast, and it's all buried within the arcade mode and website, you'll probably enjoy getting acquainted with a magical girl themed after the Cthulhu mythos, a wrestling chef who incoporates a huge variety of cooking tools and ingredients into his moveset, and a flamboyant crossdressing manga artist who has a completely different costume for almost every one of his attacks. <br><br>Despite a surface similarity to the KOF series, there really isn't another fighting game with a roster like this one, and none of the characters fall neatly into the usual archetypes of shoto, grappler, zoner, etc. Unusually for a fighting game, there is a dual emphasis on both traditional SF\/KOF-style ground game footsies, and GG\/BB-style, high-flying airdash antics. Thankfully, these elements are integrated together smoothly, and don't feel disjointed when they're used at the same time.<br><br>The combo system is quite deep, yet significantly easier to pick up than in KOF, GG or Blazblue, largely because of the intuitive &quot;magic series&quot; combos for normals and the generally forgiving input windows. (Unlike in other recent 2D fighters, there are no autocombos.) Because the original PS3 version infamously lacked any form of online play, I've almost never had the chance to play it with other human beings until now. But it seems to me that the roster is pretty balanced, aside from the super armor-heavy brawler Ray being perhaps a bit too cheesy.<br><br>The most original gameplay concept of Chaos Code is that before a match begins, every character can choose to equip two unique extra moves out of a pool of four, which includes both specials and supers. You can take both extra specials, both extra supers, or one of each into the fight if you want. A personalized move selection might help you to be able to pick up a character that you wouldn't otherwise be comfortable playing as, and allows for a lot of added variation in your playstyle. A Street Fighter III-style parry is also present, and is far easier to pull off than in that game, although it costs meter to use here.<br><br>Additions to New Sign of Catastrophe from the PS3 version include two completely new characters, a color edit mode (though you can only select from preset colors), an expanded gallery mode that unlocks with in-game currency, a KOF-style mission mode that should help to familiarize players with the different characters and mechanics, and most importantly, the long-awaited online mode.<br><br>My only real issue is the annoyingly long loading times I've been experiencing, which were almost as bad on PS3. In this case, though, it seems they're the result of my playing the game on my near-toaster level PC, and anyone with the specs to run modern AAA games should have no problem at all. I've also encountered some occasional sound glitches, though these could also be on my end. The online netcode itself is of decent quality, especially for the developer's first try. It probably can't be relied upon for opponents on the other end of the United States, or on other continents, but it should function fine when fighting anyone in your general area.
