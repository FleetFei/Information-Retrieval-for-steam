after seeing a mixed public opinion on it. TL; DR version is at the bottom.<br><br>Temple of Osiris is the sequel to the action-adventure \/ puzzle game &quot;Lara Croft and the Guardian of Light,&quot; which holds an <b>overwhelmingly<\/b> positive score on the Steam store. Both games are played from an isometric perspective and have a heavy emphasis on solving environmental puzzles with some action-shooting thrown in for good measure. Gameplay-wise, the two are very similar in controls and mechanics, such as the use of the grappling hook or rolling spheres to certain locations to advance. The big difference, however, is that Temple of Osiris can be played with up to 4 players in comparison to Guardian of Light's two-player experience, and Lara Croft now uses Osiris's object-altering staff to assist with puzzle-solving instead of Totec's spear.<br><br>Despite being very similar in its core aspects, it is very clear that the developers attempted to improve virtually every aspect of the game from the first, and one huge change from the first game to this one is the graphics and the aesthetics. Probably the most immediate and biggest contrast from the first game in terms of aesthetics is the menu screen:<br><br>In Guardian of Light, the menu screen is a still image of an open book with several comic panels serving as the &quot;buttons,&quot; while some background music played softly in the distance. <br><br>In Temple of Osiris, the player is immediately shown a dramatic, fully-animated scene of Croft and her companions in an intense back-lit part of Osiris's kingdom, with very sharp contrasts in light and color. Lara Croft is standing in a pose that suggests a state of confidence that screams &quot;My body is ready.&quot; All of this happens with incredibly dramatic, cinematic music playing in the background.<br><br>The list goes on: <br><br>The first boss of Guardian of Light is in a rigid isometric view that puts an emphasis on puzzle-solving.<br><br>In Temple of Osiris, the first boss features explosions, flames, particles, special effects, and general chaotic insanity galore. The screen shakes at every boom, at every quake of the ground. Even the camera is tilted at a lower angle to show the scene behind the objects in the foreground; a background that is blurred by the depth of field hints at the utter chaos and ancient horrors that lurk below. Unfortunately, I didn't get a good, long look at it because I was too busy dodging the missiles exploding on the ground.<br><br>The difference is as huge as Super Mario 64 and Super Mario Galaxy. I almost thought that somewhere in the development team of Temple of Osiris, people were seeking Michael Bay as a consultant.<br><br>The keyword here is &quot;cinematic.&quot; The graphics and the aesthetics of Temple of Osiris are clearly intended to be much more cinematic in comparison to Guardian of Light. Depth of field is much more emphasized and commonplace. Ambient occlusion is everywhere. Overall, the graphics have been massively touched upon compared to Guardian of Light. All of these changes to the aesthetics serve to give the player the sense of wonder and adventure, to strike home the point that they are about to explore an unknown yet exciting danger. The chaos during the action sequences and the boss fights emphasize the desperation and insanity of the moment, that the player is literally fighting against the gods with barely anything more than guns. Not a single scene in Guardian of Light gave me that same sense as Temple of Osiris did.<br><br>But games are not all about the graphics, the sound, or the first-impression, and that leads us to the biggest factor: the gameplay. <br><br>As stated before, the two games play very similarly compared to each other, with the only big difference being the manner in which puzzles are solved. However, that difference in puzzle-solving is one of the biggest factors that is causing the divide on the public opinion towards this game.<br><br>I will go ahead and admit it: <b>the puzzles in Temple of Osiris are much easier compared to Guardian of Light.<\/b> This is the deal-breaker for many people; it is the biggest gameplay-related complaint that constantly keeps being mentioned in the reviews. Personally, I believe it to be so for one big reason.<br><br>First, the nature of the staff mechanic. When Lara (or Isis \/ Horus in multiplayer) activates the staff, all objects with the symbol of Osiris are &quot;activated&quot; and react accordingly. For example, floor tiles with the symbol will lift higher in height, allowing players to reach higher locations and advance, and time-bombs will have their timer slowed to be safely pushed across longer distances or even used as portable platforms. In Guaridan of Light, the spear is used primarly to ascend or cross terrain due to its nature as a temporary player-created platform.<br><br>What ends up happening is that the staff mechanic of Temple of Osiris forces the player to immediately look for objects marked with the symbol, which usually end up giving away the answer simply by their positioning and two-way function. More often than not, these objects are floor tiles that stay in one place in the world in which the player only has a limited amount of control over; up or down. In Guardian of Light, the spear mechanic teaches the player to look at the problem as a whole. Because the means to advance is directly in the player's hand, there is no obvious focal point in the puzzle for the player to look for. This in turn encourages players to analyze the situation and create a plan, making mental guesses on the outcomes of each possible plan before actually attempting it.<br><br>Looking at the design of the puzzles and the action sequences in both games, Temple of Osiris is clearly meant to have much more emphasis on the action-adventure aspects rather than the puzzles. There are many more &quot;chase scenes&quot; in Temple of Osiris than in Guardian of Light, and the boss battles are much more intense and engaging. Combined with the addition of 4-player co op instead of the duo team in Guardian of Light, it may have been a design choice in order to make the game more accessable and appealing to a larger audience, people who may or may not be avid puzzle-solvers or simply don't want puzzles to be the bigger aspect in the action-to-puzzle ratio. It's anyone's guess.<br><br>A very big complaint I do have to address is the control. The controls in Guardian of Light are absolutely fantastic; they are responsive, quick, and fit perfectly in the game. Unfortunately for Temple of Osiris, the movment controls were much more sluggish compared to Guardian of Light, for reasons unknown. I speculate that it may have to do with the turning that Lara does when changing directions, which can easily send the player into situations where he or she is exposed to taking damage or dying immediately. The sluggish movement can be a huge source of frustration for the player.<br><br>According to other reviews of this game, Temple of Orisis also has a rather sizable amount of bugs and glitches, some of which end up ruining the gaming experience completely. However, I have not experienced any of these bugs or glitches in the game so far, leading me to believe that the game has problems on specific graphic cards and\/or computer setups.<br><br>With everything laid out on the table, it is time to answer the big question: &quot;Is this game worth buying?&quot;<br><br>Honestly, I think the game is pretty good. It may have some very noticable flaws, but the game as a whole is very playable, and more importantly, enjoyable. I still ended up having the same amount of fun with this title as I did with Guardian of Light, despite all of the reviews that say otherwise. <br><br>Players that really enjoyed the puzzles of the first game, or are avid Tomb Raider fans may end up disappointed with Temple of Orisis. is a sequel of the previously published game, The Guardian of Light. This game was released in December of 2014 on PC and both PS4 and Xbox One.<br><br>Like the first game, The Temple of Osiris is an adventure game with a isometric view of Lara. This game also includes co-op like the original, but this time offers the choice of more than two characters.  Lara returns with her classic dual pistol setup whilst her other friends have their own unique set of abilities and weapons. The game focuses heavily on puzzle solving, like the Tomb Raider series; but also with a little bit of platforming and gun-play thrown in too. All of the puzzles are easily solvable if you have any sort of experience in puzzle games however.  All of the gameplay elements which were in the first game are in The Temple of Osiris such as grappling hooks and bombs, and nothing really new is added in this respect. It's just more of the same.<br><br>Graphically, The Temple of Osiris looks really nice; it's not top of the range graphics as you can see from the screenshots, but it does have a very nice art style, and it fits the genre and setting of game very well.  It looks very much like a Diablo style game. You get to see a variety of different environments and all of them can have different weather effects in them too.  Some of the latter levels really look special, they give you the feel of something epic is about to happen as you are exploring all of these deep and gorgeous looking tombs. <br><br>The story is very linear, you can't really effect the outcome of the plot in anyway; you have to collect &quot;keys&quot; in a specific order. It would of been nice if you could of changed the order in which you have to gather them, just to give the game a little bit of an open world feel to it; however I can see why they stuck to a linear style so that you are fighting against the right difficulty of enemy according to your equipment. I really did enjoy the story line though; the ending is amazing and something which I will remember for a while since the last boss fight is so epic!<br><br>The Temple of Osiris is fully voice acted; and done very well, Keeley Hawes voices Lara Croft - as she did in the previous game, and a number of other Tomb Raider games since 2006 (apart from the reboot in 2013). The game also includes very classic Tomb Raider sound tracks, both increasing the urgency in certain events, or creating a creepy and spooky atmosphere.<br><br>I found The Temple of Osiris to be a little easier when compared to the first game, whether this was because I was used to the type of puzzles I was encountering, or just because it is plainly easier, I'm not sure. But none of the puzzles are mega hard and even I didn't have to resort to looking up the resolutions online. Boss fights can easily be conquered once you work out what is needed  to be done, granted this can take a while but the execution is easy. I personally found the platforming aspect to be the hardest aspect of the game.<br><br>Beside the abundance of puzzles in each level, you can collect gems which in turn can be used to open treasure chests at the end of a level. These chests hold loot, such as rings and pendants. I really liked this aspect; as it allowed you to customize Lara to play however you wanted her to play. I personally opted for a more speedy build, so had run speed increases and weapon firing rate - so spent a lot of time farming gems in order to find the right equipment in the chests to that end.  Each level also comes with a few option challenges that you can complete, these can vary from completing the level in a certain amount of time, or simply not dying - these too reward you with gems allowing you to open more chests at the end. There are plenty of other items and pickups to find along the way on each level, such as permanent stat boosts to health and ammo pools. <br><br>A full playthrough of The Temple of Osiris took me just under eight hours to complete. This game is a bit shorter than the original, however I see there to be more potential for replay-ability in this game. I would be more inclined to boot this game up once I have finished it since I can carry on using my same save file to complete challenges and try to find more loot and collectibles. With the addition of the co-op mode too, which result in you having a different playing experience since a number of the puzzles and events change depending on how many people are playing - The Temple of Osiris is a game which I think you can get at least eighteen hours out of if you really push it. <br><br>The only technical problems I had with this game was that sadly I wasn't able to play it on 4k resolution without frame drops; and even on 1080 I still got the frame drops but not as server, it was only really noticeable when I was in a water heavy environment. Given that I can play more recent graphically intensive games on 4k and full graphical settings, I don't think The Temple of Osiris has been optimized as well as it could. This game is also heavily reliable on you having a controller to play with. It is horrible configured for using a keyboard and mouse and is just downright uncomfortable to play if you do use them. Stick with your controller to get the best out of this game. <br><br>To sum up, I really enjoyed Lara Croft and The Temple of Osiris - whilst all of the gameplay elements are the same as the original; the game's world, story and replay-ability really make this a better title for me. Tomb Raider fans really should experience this game, and anyone wanting an introductory game to a puzzle and action genre style game will get a welcoming experience here too. The price tag of \u00a314,99 may be a little steep if you are just planning the one playthrough; but if you are wanting to have a game which you can get almost twenty hours of gameplay out of when played with friends and aiming to complete plenty of challenges, you should not hesitate to pick this up. Everyone else just looking for a one hit pony, will probably want to wait till it goes a 50% sale. <br><br><b><u> Tom's Score Card<\/u><\/b><br>1) Stay away<br>2) Only recommended when on sale<br><b><u>3) Recommended<\/u><\/b><br>4) Highly recommended<br>5) This is a must play<br><br>If you found this review helpful, please consider giving it a thumbs up. of this game, but due to the fact that it just came out, people are obviously going to want some impressions. Also, please note that earlier today I played around the first third of Guardian of Light so I would be prepared to compare the progress made on this game.<br><br>TL;DR This game improves on GoL in literally every respect, rendering its predecessor almost obsolete in the process. Breathtaking visuals, refined gameplay, larger and more diverse areas\/puzzles, more enemies supported on screen at the same time than GoL really ever managed, 4-player drop in\/drop out with public matchmaking support, all for the lowly price of twenty dollars. Avoid using SSAA, as it seems to have very little impact on visual fidelity, make sure you plug in your gamepad, and I can almost promise you will enjoy this game.<br><br>*REVIEW STARTS HERE*<br><br>First, the negative (and trust me, there isn't much).<br><br>-Bad keyboard controls return. The scheme is similar to the first game, and for many that will be a bad thing. Keyboard controls just feel unresponsive and awkward, especially since the way Lara gradually turns while running seems to be designed for an analog stick. Get a controller for this game or you will have a suboptimal experience.<br><br>-PERFORMANCE. Okay, I've been seeing some initial feedback saying the performance is not very good. I was worried about this (I have an R9 290x Gaming, i5 4670, and 16 GB Ballistix) because nothing frustrates me more than poor optimization when I have a fairly nice rig. After glancing at the video options, I immediately noticed what the issue is. This game offers two types of anti-aliasing: FXAA and SSAA. Most people will be familiar with FXAA. It is very low cost but also tends to blur the image quite a bit. SSAA (commonly confused with the much less performance heavy SMAA), however, is a little bit more uncommon and is an EXTREMELY performance heavy and ham fisted form of anti-aliasing that usually looks great. I don't see much of a difference here other than a distinct performance drop in some areas while having it on. On my rig, I pull an unwavering 60 FPS with FXAA on. SSAA on drops the framerate in only the most demanding cutscenes.<br><br>*EDIT*<br><br>I feel like I have to add another negative here, if I am to be completely honest. I gave the multiplayer co-op a shot and... let's just say they could have done better. Now, I could see this game TOTALLY being a blast if you and a friend or two get together and communicate. Right now the solo queue is an absolute mess, though. Players drop in and drop out fairly frequently, which halts the action for a few seconds, and if the host leaves then a new one is picked and progress seems to be reset to the last checkpoint, which can be quite a ways away. Adding to the issue is the fact that these puzzles were not designed with four players in mind (it would have been quite an enourmous task to make puzzles that could be completed with one person but challenged four as well). Both Egyptian gods can wield their own respective staves while both explorers have grappling hooks and can use fire (though I've seen an issue where Lara loses fire and only Carter can use it; I don't know if this is intentional). The biggest issue seems to be a lack of communication and ability spam during complex puzzles. The session I was in consisted of one person who seemed to know how to solve the puzzle, myself, who was trying to figure it out but having difficulty communicating with the leader, and two people who seemed to be actively inhibiting progress by messing with switches, dropping mines, and moving the ball of sunlight to places it did not need to go. Fighting enemies with four players is also quite a mess. Fights are chaotic, yes, but not in the good way. Dying seems almost impossible considering the sheer amound of ammunition being simultaneously fired off and mines annoyingly cause friendly fire, which leads to some serious griefing. All in all, with maybe a single friend, this could be fun. Right now solo queue is a nightmare, though.<br><br>*END EDIT*<br><br>Now, the good.<br><br>-This game is breathtakingly gorgeous. I mean, the screenshots look quite nice, but I never expect the same quality in game. This is an exception. From the minute you run out of the temple and outside into the first level, there is an obvious difference in graphical fidelity between this game and the last installment. The smoke effects are some of the best I have ever seen, textures are of above average quality, and the lighting is spot on. Character models look much better than the first game, though not quite as sharp as the environment (which isn't a problem since this game plays from the isometric perspective).<br><br>-Gameplay is smoother and more diverse than the first game (based on my limited experience with it). While the first game had the gimmick with the golden spear, which was essentially a one trick pony, this game features a staff that allows you to interact with certain normally static objects in the world so long as they have Osiris's emblem on them. Co-op features two players wielding the staff, which does cause problems (particularly with there only being a need for one staff, so the other player is left twiddling their thumbs). Dodging, jumping, and especially grappling all feel finely tuned. It should also be noted that the UI is cleaned up, and the gear equipment screen is easier to navigate.<br><br>-Level design, I feel, deserves its own category. I have only played roughly half an hour of the game and I have already encounterd larger, more open areas than were available in the first game, twice as many more enemies on screen at the same time than there ever were in Guardian of Light, and very well designed platforming sections. I won't spoil anything, but let's just say that the first chase scene in the game is far more compelling and heart pounding than anything that happened in my time with Guardian of Light (and I do enjoy that game, as well).<br><br>-The story is so corny and I absolutely love it. Clearly this isn't a narrative driven game, but the writers still seemed to have some fun with the script and nailed the &quot;so bad it is good&quot; tone perfectly. Lara, as usual, is her sassy self, but the new Egyptian additions are just a riot, particularly Set. His voice is so goofy and evil-villain-echo-tastic that one could almost not feel threatened by him, if it weren't for the very menacing and threatening demon chasing you for what seems like it will be throughout the game (a wise choice to include this to contrast Set).<br><br>-Miscellaneous: The loot early on seems to possess those classic ARPG modifiers ala &quot;Debilitating Iron Ring of Major Grappling&quot; and so on and so forth, which seems to suggest that there will possibly be LOTS of (perhaps random) loot to collect. There is a nifty matchmaking tool in the main menu for online co-op, which is always welcome. Many of the great features from GoL have returned, such as laying and detonating bombs, carrying four weapons at a time, the ammo meter for secondary weapons, and the collection of gems (though this time they can be used to open chests containing loot; a smart addition).<br><br>Overall, I'd say this game is fantastic so far and very much worth the low price tag of twenty dollars. Furthermore, you don't have to have played GoL at all to understand the story. Think of this like a 007 sequel. Sure, if you watch all the movies you will notice a broad timeline and catch on to some of the more prominent tropes, but nothing stopes you from watching them out of order and still knowing exactly what is going on. Keep in mind that co-op with strangers feels disjointed, but great with a friend. I would actually encourage you to buy this game RATHER than GoL, as this improves the gameplay in literally every area, rendering its predecessor almost completely obsolete. Anyway, if you got this far, thanks for sticking with me.
