class=\"bb_h1\">General Impression<\/div>You know what\u2019s better than a walking simulator type of adventure game? Such a title that also adds atmospheric horror elements in the mix. Don\u2019t get me wrong, since I thoroughly enjoy even regular adventure games that don\u2019t incorporate spooky elements in themselves, but playing a horror title that is all about the experience of discovery and less (if at all) about combat, is a refreshing break for me. You don\u2019t even have a sense of urgency, of terror or even overused jump scares. The Cursed Forest\u2019s been in Early Access for more than one year, yet you wouldn\u2019t be able to tell since it\u2019s already very well polished. <br><br>Even more surprising for me was to find out that this game, is in fact a Steam remaster of a DRM-free game that was pretty much the work of a single skilled developer. The remastering process though, has upped the ante by a large margin just by simply using CryEngine 3 as the visual driving force behind an already promising narrative project. So you cannot say at any given time, that Cursed Forest doesn\u2019t look as good as the seemingly disparate collection of tales which unravel before your eyes. A taxing game engine to say the least, yet I was more than happy to run it even on 2K instead of 4, simply because 60 frames per second in this title matter a lot more than in The Vanishing of Ethan Carter (which also had an in-game lock option for 30fps). I\u2019m comparing these two games because I could draw several similarities between them while for the sake of a spoiler-free review, I will describe them in general terms.<br><br>In both games, eye candy is mirrored by tense gameplay with plenty of mysteries and puzzles to solve and the connection between them is initially circumstantial at best. You also have to walk a bit from one area of interest to the next one, though Cursed Forest\u2019s maps (divided by three Sections\/chapters) are a bit smaller yet just as detailed. I do wish the game wasn\u2019t enforcing the gloomy mood by actually being so dark all the time. I didn\u2019t wish to interfere with the brightness setting since I do believe that games are meant to be played in the way their developers envisioned them. So while I strive to max out the graphical details, the brightness setting always stay at its default value. Dark and gloomy. At least the mood is maintained through a smooth transition between each game section.<br><br>One amusing aspect about The Cursed Forest that I\u2019d like to point out, is the case regarding  the achievements you can earn within the game. Even when \u201cfailing\u201d as in, dying for whatever reason and creative method, you get an achievement in the same fashion as when completing the aforementioned mystery. And since you have many static save game points throughout the maps (in the form of altars), you can experiment the different outcomes which sometimes are quite deadly encounters. Achievements for failing an objective are a nice example of thinking outside the box.<br><br>There is no combat option per se, yet you must still use your wits. So pay close attention to your surroundings as the game most certainly rewards those that explore the areas, read the notes and map indications scattered in each game section. As soon as you boot the game, you are being warned that some bugs may still be prevalent yet so far I met nothing, minor or game breaking, that would hinder my progress. You can\u2019t go wrong at all, if you jump in and purchase this game even in this Early Access stage. By the looks of it, this title has a lot more to show for it and I do hope it shall receive further attention from both the developers as well as the buyers and players of Cursed Forest. It is fully deserving of its price tag, fear not.
