game should be. It needs work, but it nails the atmosphere and style which is unique to that universe.<br><br>Your character is of no significance at all.  No world saving or god-slaying heroes here, just you and your crappy AK against hordes of mutants, deadly environmental anomalies, and fellow scavengers.  I'll run through what's good, what's bad, and what could be improved.<br><br>The Good:<br><br>-Atmosphere:  Mostly cloudy days running through abandoned towns, old factories and railway stations, with not much more than the sounds of nature to accompany you is in line with how STALKER is supposed to be.  <br><br>-PvP + PvE:  There aren't strict divisions between PvP and PvE;  rather, you can opt to turn on PvP at any time using an icon in the upper left, which will change the color of your name and render you vulnerable to other players' weapons.  PvP is automatically turned on, however, if the total worth of your inventory\/gear exceeds a certain threshold, which I think is a great way to put you in the mindset of a scavenger among other scavengers - you don't want to have your most valuable stuff with you for too long and around too many people, lest they try to take it from you.  Killing other players within sight of town guards\/NPC's will net you a negative reputation temporarily, meaning players who get too trigger happy find themselves losing out on certain conveniences for a time.  There's also a PvP arena with different modes similar to the multiplayer in the actual STALKER games, with rewards even for when you lose.<br><br>-Quests:  There are an ungodly number of quests, and while they can be difficult, I've yet to run into more than one or two which explicitly required a team of people.  Rewards are usually shown before the quest is completed, meaning you can make more effective decisions about how to use your time, and you rarely get surprised with a bad reward for a hard quest.  Some involve a LOT of traveling, though, which can wear on you a bit.<br><br>-Maps:  They're gigantic.  Lots of places to explore, with randomly-generated, respawning loot, as well as valuable artifacts if you choose to investigate anomalies.  It is not hard to make a lot of money outside of questing, if you learn where to look.<br><br>-The Community:  It's weird to say this is good when the dominant quality of the community is indifference.  People don't seem to communicate much, and when they do it's usually very short and to the point, but it's so fitting with how STALKER works that I see it as a plus for the game.  You'll get used to aiming first and asking questions second, as you never know who might decide to pull the trigger and rob you.  And  yes, you can get robbed.  That said, helping people goes a long way, and you build trust more by actually doing something rather than talking to someone.<br><br><br>The Bad<br><br>-Sounds:  Sound quality is fairly basic, and some of it doesn't work consistently.  Music plays in some locations but is apparently so specific to a location that it just cuts out when you move.  Monsters make noise, but you can only really hear them when they're on you, meaning you could have a small horde of them chasing you and not actually know it until you've been gored and left for dead.  It's highly annoying sometimes when you're exploring only to be killed without so much as the sound of a footstep for warning.<br><br>-Aggro distance:  Sometimes a monster sees you and comes after you in a way which totally makes sense.  Sometimes they show up almost out of nowhere and kill you in one hit.  Sometimes they kill you, leave, and when you respawn show up from some crazy distance away to gnaw on you again.  Combine that with the lack of audio indicating their presence, and you'll feel like you're getting cheap-shotted a LOT.  &quot;Verliokas&quot; are especially terrible about this - they make no noise, travel very quickly, and will aggro from insane distances in places where you can't see anything.<br><br>-Graphics:  The graphics are dated and have some issues here and there.  Textures flicker, sometimes whole sections of the map won't load (you have to quit and restart to remedy that, I've found).  The aesthetic is spot on, it's more that it just looks old and the performance doesn't quite match what you'd expect from what you're seeing.  It performs smoothly for the most part, but some locations lag like crazy for reasons I can't grasp.<br><br>-Stats and Mechanics:  It's not that things here are broken, more that there's little explanation as to how different things work, and the requirements for raising skills are crazy even when you specialize in something.  You spend experience points to get specialization points, which are then used to unlock skills, but the &quot;exchange rate&quot; for these can get insane quickly.  The upshot is that you receive EXP for practically everything you do (you can gain quite a lot by opening a rare loot box or by eating a large meal early on), but it doesn't feel like it means much when you can't use it for anything for a looooong time.<br><br>-Pay-to-Win:  While the paid items usually have a time limit, their being available at all means PvP in some areas is ridiculous without paid gear.  PvE doesn't suffer much from this, as even with some of the best gear you can still get completely wrecked if you're not paying attention, but if you're big on the arena, you'll be investing some real money to stand a fighting chance.  I will say though, in comparison to a lot of other pay-to-play schemes, you do get a lot for what you buy.  Things like experience and stat buffs are very cheap (a 2-hour EXP rate buff is 6 cents, for example), and almost all of the paid gear has a limited time of use.  Permanent benefits come more in how you can accumulate different items and sell them off for lots of in-game money, which while not exactly the best, could be (and is, in many p2p schemes) worse.<br><br><br>Overall:  It's worth it if you're a big fan of STALKER.  If you're not, you'll find this to be a difficult game that's really tough on you from the get-go, without much in the way of a payoff until a long ways into it.  You do NOT need to pay anything to get far - if you are adventurous and remember to mark valuable spots on your map, you can easily make money to get good gear without ever dropping a cent.  Outside of the arena, PvP is done in a way which I think suits the type of game extremely well, where your personal wealth affects others' perception of you as well as having PvP mode as a punishment for shooting people in major areas.  It fits, and that's what I think is most important - Szone FEELS like STALKER, and STALKER was all about feeling like you were in a weird place.<br><br>Feedback:  Sounds for creatures would be tremendously helpful and add to the realism a lot.  While animals and mutants do make noise, they need to be heard from a greater distance, if only because it's ridiculous that a bunch of them can gang up on you without you hearing anything.  Very, VERY annoying.  Ambient audio and music needs work too, as they're not  very consistent in, well, being present.  A better explanation of mechanics, via a more comprehensive guide than the forums would be great too, as there's a lot of numbers but not a lot of info as to what they really mean (one assumes higher
