Final Station?<\/div><br>As the developer and publisher describe it The Final Station is part \u2018train simulator and exploration game\u2019 and part \u2018hardcore sidescrolling shooter.\u2019<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Expectations<\/div><br>My expectations for The Final Station were relatively high. This is in part because I enjoyed the Reddit AMA that Alex Nicihiporchik from tinyBuild and developer Oleg Sergeev created to hype their game. As a former marketing student I am of the opinion that Reddit can be a great \u2013 and cheap \u2013 marketing activity to promote your game (or any product for that matter). I believe that you have to take every opportunity that you can to communicate with potential customers. It\u2019s not something that nearly enough developers are doing.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">It Starts Very Slowly<\/div><br>The game is approximately four or so hours in length. However, the first half an hour or so of the game builds up to the main gameplay. I personally found this period to be very slow and tedious. It doesn\u2019t do enough to generate interest and keep you emotionally connected to the game. It\u2019s not until the game is well and truly into its meaty portion where I felt in sync with the gameplay.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Linearity Reduces Replayability<\/div><br>In the AMA Igor makes the claims that the game is one that many people will want to play numerous times in order to completely understand the game. This is generally true for most media. The more you play something the more its secrets are revealed. You will discover something that you didn\u2019t previously know. However, I don\u2019t believe that most players would want to play the game through multiple times. This is because the game is extremely linear.<br><br>There may be things that you wish you knew earlier in a game to get a different outcome. For example, I\u2019ve read reports of numerous people not realizing that you could craft items on the train until late in the game.<br><br>I would suggest that those who are likely to play the game multiple times are the achievement hunters. This is because a number of the game\u2019s achievements require you to meet certain numerical requirements in order for the achievement to unlock. This isn\u2019t always going to happen on the first or even the second playthrough.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Outstanding Level Design<\/div><br>Bravo to the devs on outstanding level design. The greatest asset that the game has is its level design. Although replayability is minimal because of its linear approach the actual level design makes an initial playthrough an exciting adventure (after the initial starting period). The decision to make everything behind a closed door dark was brilliant. This provides an air of mystery for everything that you do. Finding out what is lurking in the darkness is a rewarding experience.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Combat Becomes Trivial<\/div><br>Arguably the most significant criticism that I have with the game is in a disappointing way combat becomes trivialized. Once you learn how to manage your available ammunition the risk almost feels non-existent. This is because of the overly generous checkpoint system. Whenever, if ever, you do die you don\u2019t ever get put back very far. This means that aside from ammo management players can be as reckless and as carefree with the enemies as you would like. I\u2019m not asking for a game that is as punishing as a Dark Souls game where every misstep carries a significant cost but I would like to know that I will be penalized proportionately for poor play.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Clever Train Simulation<\/div><br>I appreciate the design that went into the actual train simulation The creation of a number of mini-games is intelligent and keeps players engaged throughout the rail journey. Having to maintain and repair parts of the train through mini games was fantastic. They are all easily accessible to most gamers and are engaging. I also liked the little onboard messaging system with other characters within the game. I felt that this added to the game\u2019s narrative in a clever way.<br><br>The fact that you have to do this at the same time as tending to the needs of your passengers also gets a thumbs up from me. It mixes the realities of the customer service aspects of train travel with the hard work maintenance. Of course, the reality is that real life train services have multiple people doing these jobs but it is still interesting and well-designed nonetheless.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Pixel Graphics<\/div><br>The pixel graphics have been designed by Oleg and he has done a masterful job in their creation. I have a tendency to believe that pixel graphics have been overdone lately and that they don\u2019t suit every game style. However, I believe that they really help build the atmosphere that they were hoping to achieve. It is one of the standout features of the game. I love the contrasts that they achieve in relation to shape and size.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Is it Steamified?<\/div><br>The Final Station has Steam achievements and Steam trading cards. This makes the game Steamified.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Verdict<\/div><br>I am honestly on the fence in regards to The Final Station. It\u2019s a game where I truly wish I hadn\u2019t had my expectations and hopes raised for it. There is a high likelihood that I would have enjoyed my time with the game that much more had I not. The Final Station isn't a bad game but it's certainly not as good as it could have been. I can recommend the game to those of you seeking a relatively short survival adventure game especially to those of you not seeking significant replayability. Beaten the game + all achievements <br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> <b>Overview<\/b> <\/div><br>The Final Station (TFS) is quite a bleak and emotionally draining indie game. It's pretty much set in an apocalypse, where there are capsules falling from the sky, spewing out gas and turning people into incurable zombie-like creatures. You possess a valuable asset, a train which can transport other survivors and important cargo. Picking up survivors can yield rewards though it's not always worth the trouble. TFS is a very unique game and one which I enjoyed much more than I had anticipated.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"><b> Components<\/b> <\/div><br><b>Visuals<\/b> - TFS is a 2D pixel graphics game, as you can tell from the screenshots.   The overall visual style is dark and evokes emotions of sadness and hopelessness. While colours are used sparingly, they are used very well to provide a great contrast. When riding on the train, the parallax backgrounds can be quite stunning, and offers a nice break from the corrupted stations. The graphics are fairly well done and there's lots of nice details, but they could be improved. Overall, TFS has a really good visual style that gives the game a lot of atmosphere.<br><br><b>Audio<\/b> - The audio in TFS is very immersive and really pulls you into the game. All sound effects are clear and vivid and the ambience sets up nice atmospherics. The music is both beautiful and emotional, and goes really well with the game. My only gripe here is that music is quite a rare occurrence, but I guess it makes it more impactful when it does play. I think the audio of TFS is really well done and does a good job at providing immersion.<br><br><b>Gameplay<\/b> - This gameplay of TFS mostly involves riding your train to various stations, of which there are 25 in total. Most of these are corrupt stations, overrun with blobby, gunky zombie creatures. Ammo is very limited so you'll mostly resort to using bare fists. The shooting mechanics are solid and the punching includes a charge-up attack which is pretty neat. You'll sometimes encounter survivors, which you can exchange conversations with, and sometimes they will board your train. When you depart the station and ride the train, it's up to you to keep your passengers alive. If they survive, they'll give you rewards. On top of this, you have to manage the train to make sure it doesn't use too much power, else it will slow down and stop. The longer it takes to reach the next station, the more your passengers will starve. I found the train rides to be quite intense and anxious and a decent gameplay mechanic. Overall, TFS offers intense and enthralling gameplay, which captivates you and keeps you on the edge of your seat. <br><br><b>Story<\/b> - A little while into the game, the feared Second Visitation begins. Like the First Visitation, which occurred around a century ago, the Visitation involves capsules which are appearing around the world, emitting gas which turns nearby people effectively into zombies. You soon learn about a government led initiative, a Guardian which is supposed as a means to fight back. The Guardian gives most people hope, but through conversations with other survivors, you hear that some people are doubtful of the Guardian and whether it can even help. TFS has a very intriguing story where it is told almost exclusively through other people's unique perspectives. It gives you a lot to think about and leaves room for interpretation. Overall, I highly enjoyed the story aspect of TFS and it is probably one of the best stories I've experienced in an indie game.<br><br><b>Functionality<\/b> - There's nothing I can fault the functionality of TFS for. The game runs smoothly and never has frame drops, and I encountered no glitches. <br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"><b> Pros<\/b> <\/div><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li> Great visual style<br><\/li><li> Intriguing story, and applaudable method of storytelling<br><\/li><li> Good gameplay, but does get slightly repetitive<br><\/li><li> Immersive Audio<\/li><\/ul><div class=\"bb_h1\"><b> Cons<\/b> <\/div><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li> Feels a bit slow at times<br><\/li><li> Very short, only lasted 4 hours<br><\/li><li> No replayability<\/li><\/ul><div class=\"bb_h1\"> <b>Conclusion<\/b> <\/div><br>If you're looking for a unique sci-fi, horror or side-scrolling game, then The Final Station might appeal to you.
