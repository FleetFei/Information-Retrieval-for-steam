class=\"postedDate\">Posted: December 21, 2015\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLike the undying pilot of a battle-worn Dreadnought, Warhammer 40,000 stomps around the Steam store groaning in agony and begging to be released from its mechanical servitude. And much like any Dreadnought, we don't want it to die but we don't want it to be pained any further. Legacy of Dorn: Herald of Oblivion isn't the blessing of the Emperor that it needs to soothe its exploitation from the warp-addled minds of Games Worskhop, but it's certainly greased the intellectual property's joints.<br><br>Infusing themselves with the Terminator armour of an Imperial Fist, players join their battle-brothers to cleanse the ancient space hulk &quot;Herald of Oblivion&quot; of xeno filth, be they necron, dark eldar, ork or tyrannid, it all depends on which paths you choose and when you walk them. Because Legacy of Dorn is a choose-your-own-adventure game, your interface with the game is minimal - you'll read blocks of very descriptive text, and then choose one of multiple actions. The limited number of save states, and needing have a certain amount of purity for some actions makes things all the more interesting, and encourages replay value so you can see what you'd missed in previous playthroughs. From the start you'll be seperated from your crew, so you'll notice how differently each playthrough is from the get-go by choosing a different path. It also helps that randomness is kept only to 'wheel of fate' rounds and fights (the former particularly rare), so the more you play the better you can plan. Eventually you'll know who to rescue in which order, and what wargear will be necessary to get better results. However you can expect that to take a fair few hours and at least one playthrough where you reach one of the multiple endings.<br><br>The game's combat is as simple as it gets, bearing similarity to Enchanted Arms and Phantasy Star games in that how close\/far away from a target determines your chance to hit and be hit. Both ranged and melee combat function in similar ways, where you have a percentage to hit and can use focus to increase your chance to hit, as well as reduce an enemy's damage done to you for that turn.  It's primitive to say the least, especially when on medium difficulty you can Focus-Attack-Focus (a move that costs 3 action points, and makes you virtually invulnerable), but with some tweaks to weapons so that they *feel* different to one-another I'd find it rather enjoyable.<br><br>Legacy of Dorn's visuals are..... interesting, despite being almost nonexistent. It's a text game that has the retro-futuristic look of the Imperium technology; the game's dominated by emerald-green screens and lime text, and the battles take place on a black and green grid. And yet this is one of the best visual styles I've seen for Warhammer, because the game's writing paints a much more evocative image of what it must be like to be on a Space Hulk than.... well, even Space Hulk. As for its music, it's nothing special - bleak chimes and horns make up most of the soundtrack, and I fail to be inspired by the battle music. There's also no audio options to have the game read the text back to you, and given how good Warhammer voice acting's been in the past, it's a shame not to have it here where the lines are superb.<br><br>In conclusion, Legacy of Dorn: Herald of Oblivion's a damned fine game, but it makes the same mistakes as other Warhammer 40k games that may cause you to roll your eyes and look elsewhere. The Space Marines (albeit a different chapter) are the protagonists, and as per usual other armies are given little room in the spotlight. It's unfortunate given how well it describes the lore, and makes you feel proud to have purchased codexes entirely for the fluff material (or am I the only one who does that?).<br><br>I rate this game a Teleportarium out of a Your Adventure has come to an End.<br><br>Need a second opinion?
