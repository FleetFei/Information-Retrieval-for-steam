roll-out, the new Kanye West album is here. The Life of Pablo is the first Kanye West album that's just an album: No major statements, no reinventions, no zeitgeist wheelie-popping. But a madcap sense of humor animates all his best work, and the new record has a freewheeling energy that is infectious and unique to his discography. <br>Pablo Picasso and Kanye West share many qualities\u2014impatience with formal schooling, insatiable and complicated sexual appetites, a vampiric fascination with beautiful women as muses\u2014but Pablo Picasso was never called an \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665. Kanye, specifically, toasted them. The Life of Pablo's namesake is a provocation, a mystery, a sly acknowledgement of multitudes: Drug lord Pablo Escobar is a permanent fixture of rap culture, but the mystery of &quot;which one?&quot; set Twitter theorists down fascinating rabbit holes, drawing up convincing stand-ins for Kanye's Blue Period (808s &amp; Heartbreak), his Rose Period (My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy), and his Crystal Period (Yeezus). If Kanye is comparable to Picasso, The Life of Pablo is the moment, after a turbulent life leaving many artistic revolutions and mistreated women in his wake, that the artist finally settles down. In this formulation, Kim Kardashian is Jacqueline Roque, Picasso's final muse and the woman to whom he remained faithful (she even kinda looks like a Kardashian), and the record is the sound of a celebrated megalomaniac settling for his place in history.<br><br>The Life of Pablo is, accordingly, the first Kanye West album that's just an album: No major statements, no reinventions, no zeitgeist wheelie-popping. It's probably his first full-length that won't activate a new sleeper cell of 17-year-old would-be rappers and artists. He's changed the genre's DNA with every album, to the point where each has inspired a generation of direct offspring, and now everywhere he looks, he sees mirrors. &quot;See, I invented Kanye, it wasn't any Kanyes, and now I look and look around and there's so many Kanyes,&quot; he raps wryly on &quot;I Love Kanye.&quot; The message seems clear: He's through creating new Kanyes, at least for now. He's content to just stand among them, both those of his own creation and their various devotees.<br><br>Kanye's second child Saint was born in early December, and there's something distinctly preoccupied about this whole project\u2014it feels wry, hurried, mostly good-natured, and somewhat sloppy. Like a lot of new parents, Kanye feels laser-focused on big stuff\u2014love, serenity, forgiveness, karma\u2014and a little frazzled on the details. &quot;Ultralight Beam&quot; opens with the sound of a 4-year-old preaching gospel, some organ, and a church choir: &quot;This is a God dream,&quot; goes the refrain. But everything about the album's presentation\u2014the churning tracklist, the broken promises to premiere it here or there, the scribbled guest list\u2014feels like Kanye ran across town to deliver a half-wrapped gift to a group birthday party to which he was 10 minutes late.<br><br>Thankfully, he's bringing a Kanye album, and Kanye albums make pretty \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 good gifts. His devotion to the craft of album-making remains his greatest talent. Albums are his legacy, what he knows, deep down, will endure after the circus of attention he maintains around him subsides. His ability to package hundreds of stray threads into a whole that feels not just thrilling, but inevitable\u2014at this, he is better than everyone, and he throws all of his best tricks into The Life of Pablo to remind us. He picks the right guests and gives them idealized settings, making people you don't care about sound fantastic and people you do care about sound immortal. Chance the Rapper, a spiritual heir to backpack-and-a-Benz Kanye if there ever was one, is given the spotlight on the opener &quot;Ultralight Beam,&quot; and uses his dazed, happy verse to quote both &quot;Otis&quot; and the bonus track to Late Registration. His joy is palpable, and it's clear he has waited his entire adult life to be featured on a Kanye album. On the other hand, &quot;Fade&quot; pits Future knockoff Post Malone, of all people, against a sample of Chicago house legend Larry Heard's &quot;Mystery of Love&quot; and a flip of Motown blues rock band Rare Earth's &quot;I Know I'm Losing You&quot; and rigs the mix so that Malone, somehow, sounds more important than both of them.<br><br>This moment is also a reminder of Kanye's audacious touch with huge, immediately recognizable pieces of musical history\u2014his best work as a producer has always drawn from iconic songs so venerated most sane people wouldn't dare touch them, from &quot;Gold Digger&quot; to &quot;Blood on the Leaves&quot; and beyond. He doesn't just sample these songs, he climbs in and joyrides them like the Maybach in the &quot;Otis&quot; video. On &quot;Famous,&quot; he does it twice, first by matching up Nina Simone's &quot;Do What You Gotta Do&quot; with Rihanna, who sings the song's hook before Nina does, and then with Sister Nancy's &quot;Bam Bam,&quot; which gets flipped so it sits atop a chorale-like chord progression. It sounds like a dancehall remix of Pachelbel's Canon, and it's the most joyful two minutes of music on the album.<br><br>&quot;Waves,&quot; a song that made the tracklist at the last second at Chance the Rapper's insistence, has a similar energy. You can hear why Chance, specifically, might've wanted it back: It is a throwback to the Rainbow Road maximalism of &quot;We Major,&quot; and it is so warmly redemptive it even makes Chris Brown, who sings the hook, sound momentarily benevolent. &quot;Waves&quot; is hardly the only last-second change made: The Kendrick Lamar collaboration &quot;No More Parties In L.A.&quot; is back on here, as is an inexplicable minute-long voicemail from imprisoned rapper Max B, granting Kanye permission to use his popular slang term &quot;wavy.&quot; Such last-second fidgets seem to say something about The Life of Pablo itself. After years of agonizing over how to follow up the conceptually triumphant 808s &amp; Heartbreak, My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy, and Yeezus, he seems to have settled upon eternal flux as a resting place, and the album plays like Kanye might still be remixing it furiously in your headphones while you listen.<br><br>&quot;Father Stretch My Hands&quot; tosses a sample from Southside Chicago icon, activist, and one-time fraud convict Pastor T.L. Barrett into a gurgling trash compactor alongside some pigeon-cooed backing vocals and an entire undigested verse from another Future knockoff, Brooklyn upstart Desiigner. It's the least-finished-sounding piece of music to ever feature on a Kanye album. This is the logical endpoint to the sort of obsessive perfectionism that led West to make 75 near-identical mix downs of  &quot;Stronger,&quot; and in the song's lyrics, Kanye admits that the same workaholism that made his father a distant figure in his childhood now keeps him from his family. On &quot;FML,&quot; he name-checks the antidepressant Lexapro on record for the second time in a year, and alludes to something that sounds an awful lot like a manic episode. The life of a creative visionary has dark undercurrents (&quot;name me one genius who ain't crazy,&quot; Kanye demands on &quot;Feedback&quot;) and it's possible that The Life of Pablo title serves as much private warning as boastful declaration.<br><br>The album's most humane moments come when he reaches for his family: &quot;I just want to wake up with you in my eyes,&quot; he pleads at the end
