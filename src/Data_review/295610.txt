Another World would be a good talking point, although the visuals are less stylized. The game lacks music, but it lends a harshness to the proceedings with nothing to accompany the player except the ambience of gunshots.<br><br>What's more, though, is that the game plays well. Some have complained about the lack of a map here, but perhaps that just shows that people have become too dependent on them. The world is well designed, small but quite labyrinthine. As usual with open-world adventures, the amount of backtracking may bother some, but over the course of the game, you do find a few shortcuts. The game also adds a few twists that are less common such as severe limitations on drops early in the game and a health bar that doubles as ammo for a special weapon, though admittedly the action rarely gets heated enough to necessitate the risk.<br><br>All of this makes for a decent game, but it's the storytelling that makes it stand out. The ending, in particular, has clearly rubbed some people the wrong way, and it can't be called anything but an anticlimax, denying its audience of any reward, but by way of a quote from David Foster Wallace, the game makes some interesting metacommentary on the hero worship we often see in games and other media and how it disconnects from the reality of courageous people.<br><br>Undoubtedly, Inescapable is designed for a very specific audience, and while it's well made, it does have problems, particularly  in its challenge or lack thereof.
