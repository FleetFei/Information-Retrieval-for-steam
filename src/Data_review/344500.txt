game by developer [bracket] games, is a beautiful piece of interactive fiction. As a finalist for the IGF\u2019s Excellence in Narrative award, Three Fourths Home is a balancing act between game and story, between driving and speaking, and between truth and fiction. Available for PC and Mac, this game is a worthwhile experience for those who like interactive fiction and branching narrative, but gameplay won\u2019t inspire or challenge many players.<br>The player, twenty-something Kelly, is on a long drive home. Whatever history you craft for your version of Kelly, it becomes obvious very quickly that she has a strained relationship with her family. Her mother is frequently biting and sarcastic while her father self-medicates; her brother\u2019s mental illness casts a vague but lingering shadow over family interactions. Three Fourths Home doesn\u2019t offer you a lot of answers\u2014almost none, in fact\u2014but it does offer a realistic view of the ways families speak to one another, blending secrets with facts in ways that only make sense to the participants.<br>The wide Nebraska cornfields provide a unique backdrop for the game. They\u2019re desolate but thriving, unique but plain, a perfect complement to Kelly\u2019s family. And when a storm kicks up and grows in intensity, so do your feelings about the situation. You can\u2019t fix the relationships any more than you can stop the storm\u2014all you can do is keep moving forward, advancing the conversation and hoping you can figure it all out in the end.<br>Spoiler alert: you can\u2019t. There are some things that can\u2019t be fixed. All you can do is understand; an option the game affords you without forcing it upon you. This is one game where exploring the additional menu options and replaying multiple times will enhance your appreciation of the narrative. While you\u2019ll always come to the same end, each playthrough will help you navigate the conversation a little better, just as reliving a memory will.<br>The writing in Three Fourths Home is beautiful, although reading the dynamic gray text may strain the eyes a bit at times. The dialog is elegantly crafted and realistic, and Kelly\u2019s brother\u2019s short stories are an excellent way to flesh out the family\u2019s character without using a ham-fisted approach. It encourages the kind of understanding that comes from analyzing a situation after the fact, making it a flawless way to approach the story\u2019s holes and questions.<br>Three Fourths Home is an interesting example of the interactive fiction\/video game divide. While it absolutely has to be a game\u2014the combination of visual, audio, writing, and interactivity require this medium\u2014there\u2019s very little for the player to do.<br>To advance, you have to hold down either the right arrow or \u2018d\u2019 key, otherwise all progression stops. So prepare for some finger cramps. While this mechanic makes sense\u2014forcing you to manage your interactions along with your car progression, just like when you have a conversation while driving\u2014it feels like too little, until it\u2019s too much. As it stands, holding a key down to drive is a tedious way of getting the point across when you\u2019re much more focused on the conversation.<br>Adding more complications like having to steer or follow directions would definitely complicate the driving, but also distract from the necessarily slow pace. It\u2019s hard to say whether that would be an improvement or just a distraction, but as it is, it treads a very thin line between being too much and too little gameplay, and sometimes feels like it leans too heavily to one side or the other.<br>Three Fourths Home is a simple game\u2014 you can\u2019t lose. But you can\u2019t really win, either. All you can do is see it through to the end and fill in your own ideas about what \u201csuccess\u201d means in this context. Those who want a traditional game experience with wins, losses, and challenges aren\u2019t going to find it in this game.<br>There\u2019s a lot to like about Three Fourths Home. It\u2019s the kind of game that makes you think. It\u2019s the kind of game that makes you want to force others to play it so you can talk to them about it, and collectively tap into the moody, melancholic heart of it. It\u2019s a perfect example of using game mechanics to tell a story, while also sometimes stumbling to do it well.If story and interactive fiction interest you, and if you like musing on regret and
