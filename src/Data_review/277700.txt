radioactive goo-ball destroys your house? <\/div> <br>On Rusty Trails is a fast-paced platformer from Black Pants Studios. In ORT you play as a small red robot called Elvis. After Elvis\u2019 home is destroyed in a strange storm he decides to venture into the city to redeem his house warranty in order to get a new house. You would have thought this would be a fairly straightforward task but a combination of environmental hazards and rising tension between the two different factions that live within the city makes Elvis\u2019 journey a dangerous one.<br><br>Before I get underway I would just like to mention that there are a few things that I\u2019ve made the decision to not talk about in the review. They aren\u2019t major but they are related to the story and I don\u2019t want to spoil it for any prospective players.<br><br>The first thing that\u2019ll strike you when you look at ORT is that it\u2019s a fantastic looking game. The game looks good through and through but the in-game billboards are particularly good. They\u2019re well designed, vibrant and create a real sense of the world you find yourself in. There\u2019s also visual variation between all the different parts of the city which gives each section of the game its own flavour.<br><br>ORT\u2019s gameplay is focused around a colour changing mechanic and it plays into the lore of the world you\u2019re in. Elvis is a rusty red robot, one of the two in-game factions, the other is a furry blue monster looking faction that\u2019s reminiscent of Sully from Monsters Inc. Early on in the game you find a monster suit which allows you to switch between the two. The city is split between the two factions and some platforms can only be used by red, others by blue. Switching between the two using the suit allows you to access the platforms you wouldn\u2019t be able to use otherwise. If you\u2019re red then any blue platforms will disappear as you come close to them but switching to blue will make them pop back out. <br><br>The switching mechanic does to some extent make the game into a reaction based game. There are a lot of parts in levels where you have to switch colours in very short amounts of time to avoid dying (if you do die you just return to your last checkpoint). Your colour doesn\u2019t just decide what platforms you can use though, there are other environmental hazards. Some hazards only effect one colour, others only target one colour. These hazards are often combined with colour-specific platforms (there are some platforms in the game which can be used by either colour) which makes for particularly tricky levels. You\u2019ll probably have to restart most levels\/checkpoints a couple of times before you\u2019ll get it.<br><br>The levels themselves are fairly short though there are quite a lot of them. I had to restart from checkpoints on most levels at least two or three times and the levels generally took between twenty and forty seconds. There are a handful of levels longer than that but they\u2019re definitely in the minority. <br><br>Speaking of length, the whole game is fairly short. It took me just shy of three hours to reach the end of the game which for a game priced at \u00a39.99 is a tad on the short side. The length is right for the game, it tells the story it wants to without any filler but like I said, I do think the price tag is a bit steep. <br><br>Elvis\u2019 story is engaging and does a good job of tying all of the levels together but really it\u2019s the relationship between the two factions in the game that I found to be the most interesting story element. The tension between the two factions increases as the game goes on and the relationship between the two factions is mainly shown to the player through the billboards I mentioned earlier. The changing relationship manages to be incredibly interesting despite the fact that the player is given very little information about the game world, in part due to the fact that it mirrors our own world.<br><br>Being a fast-paced platformer, the quality of the controls are obviously very important. Most of my playtime was spent with a controller and I can\u2019t fault the controls, they were tight and very responsive. I also tried playing with my keyboard &amp; mouse. The KB&amp;M controls are also very good though I did feel as though it was \u2018more natural\u2019 to play with my Xbox One controller. It\u2019s worth noting that your controls can be re-bound for KB&amp;M or a controller.<br><br>In case it\u2019s not clear, the gameplay is fairly simplistic. Other than the switch-colour mechanic there\u2019s really not much else to do in the levels. There are tokens which can be collected but these are just used to activate checkpoints (or they can be saved but as far as I can tell there\u2019s no benefit to not using them). However, I don\u2019t think the simplistic gameplay is a bad thing, I think it\u2019s great. Keeping it simple keeps your focus on getting through the level which helps to keep all of the levels fast-paced.<br><br>I\u2019ve covered most of the major stuff but there are a couple of other things I\u2019d like to mention. The sound track and the sound effects are great, they really help to make the world \u2018come alive\u2019. ORT sort of has a leaderboard but I\u2019d like to see some improvements in this area.
