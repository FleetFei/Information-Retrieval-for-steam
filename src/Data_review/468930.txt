developer for review purposes. This did not influence my review in any way.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n---------------------------------------OVERVIEW-------------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Phantaruk is a no-combat first-person horror game set on a space ship on which a deadly infection has broken out.<br><br>The game only reveals the backstory slowly as you play, but it seems that the H+ Corporation, which owns the Purity-02 space ship, is a medical corporation that deals with human modifications, cloning, and diseases, and became very powerful after a smallpox resurgence wiped out over three billion of Earth's inhabitants.<br><br>You play an unnamed clone aboard the Purity-02, awakening in confusion and infected with some sort of disease, trying to find out what happened to the ship and its inhabitants. A repeated computer message tells you to proceed to the Biomedical Section for evacuation, and to avoid the infected.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">==========================================================<br>\n----------------------------------------QUALITY-------------------------------------<br>\n==========================================================<\/div>Graphics - Phantaruk's graphics are mostly excellent. The models and textures are great, and the static lighting is also very good. The dynamic lighting and shadows, however, leave a lot to be desired, especially for a game so heavily focused on darkness, shadows, and stealth. I have yet to see the main character cast any shadow at all, and a number of the lights are actually just bright textures, and don't cast any light into the game world at all. I was particularly disappointed with a room with sparks falling from giant piston-like devices: the sparks were just bright blue textures, and didn't brighten the scene at all.<br><br>The game mostly performs very well on my old gaming laptop, but the open areas (e.g. Biomed Hub and Administration Hub), which look beautiful, slow the game to a crawl -- sometimes less than 1 FPS. This occasional freezing occurred a few times during play, mostly when panning on levels with large open areas, even if those areas weren't visible, suggesting a potential culling issue.<br><br>Options are sufficient: a few detail drop-down menus, anti-aliasing, and the usual full screen or window selection.<br><br>Sound - The sound in Phantaruk is very good. Individual effects and music cues are nothing to be amazed at, but they're used to excellent effect to increase the atmosphere and sense of desertion you feel while exploring the Purity-02.<br><br>The ship's computer voice is good: a pleasant, but informative and commanding female reminiscent of many others in the genre. The captain's audio logs are also well read, although the subtitles don't quite match what is being said -- and they're too slow, as well.<br><br>Gameplay - Phantaruk is played from first-person perspective, using typical WASD movement controls and mouse look. Your character is quite slow and movement sometimes feels a bit cumbersome, but for an exploration game, this is okay.<br><br>The game sounds similar to the original System Shock and indeed it has a similar tone, but the complete removal of combat and the heavy use of stealth sets Phantaruk apart. So, too, does the infection mechanic, whereby your character has been infected with some sort of disease that continually increases his 'toxin level' (visible using his arm implant) and can only be decreased temporarily through the use of an antidote, which is in short supply. When the meter reaches 100%, the screen gets a pulsating green overlay, and the clone's heart rate increases until it reaches 200, at which point it dies. Inject a precious dose of antidote, and the toxin level drops to 0% and heart rate begins to decrease.<br><br>This infection mechanic probably seemed like a good idea, but what it actually does is severely limit opportunity for slow and methodical exploration of the Purity-02, instead making the best way to play running around like a nutter to work out where the various keys, codes, switches, etc, are, getting killed, and then heading for the goals directly. A similar approach has been taken with the flashlight that becomes available after a short period: batteries are limited, so you're best off running around with the flashlight on to make sure you don't miss anything, then dying and starting again and saving your precious batteries. It's a shame, too; if the infection gimmick had been left out, or based only on certain areas, and the flashlight was infinite, the whole creepy abandoned-spaceship feel of the game would have been so much more powerful.<br><br>Anyway, seeming to dog your steps is a weird-looking mutant creature (presumably Phantaruk, of the game's title) that plods along until it sees you, and then charges, hits you twice, and you're dead. You're supposed to avoid the creature by staying in the shadows (as represented by an open eye symbol in the bottom left fading away when you're hidden), and this is the core mechanic of the game, but unfortunately it doesn't work that well either. I've been spotted down a dark corridor while supposedly in full darkness, and sometimes the creature just seems to know you're there, whatever you do. The lighting and shadow problems mentioned in the graphics section, above, too, mean that you can appear to be completely hidden, but still not be able to get the eye symbol to disappear, or be in what looks like partial light and have the symbol indicate that you are hidden.
