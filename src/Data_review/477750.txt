I am also a huge fan of musket and pike games, so delighted this game has been developed.<br><br>The positives are leaders and units are accurately represented and have many detailed attributes that go to help make a complete army. Supply and reinforcement is also paramount. You need to ensure a good stretch of regional control to enable supplies to flow, else you\u2019ll risk some units falling unsupplied. If you have too many troops for the leader, a significant penalty could be applied in any battles. Also, if you move too many armies through a territory at once, the armies could slow down in pace. <br><br>Each turn represents one month and you will issue orders once per turn. After you click end turn, your orders will be completed, as well as the opponents, simultaneously.  This, in my view, is a better system and more realistic than I go you go.<br><br>For new players, the best game I can compare this to is the Total War series but without the tactical battles, which are replaced by a complex system of calculations in the background, which you can help influence using all the factors I have described and more. In this game you don\u2019t get the option to build buildings (apart from a few specialities like supply depot), but you will recruit units and manage armies in more detail.   The interface is very similar in that you drag and drop armies and units where you want them to move to.  Sieges are also simulated where you have to station an army in a territory and try to create breaches, and then, if activated, order an assault on the town. <br><br>What really makes the game for me is the use of \u2018cards\u2019 that can be played, diplomatic boxes on the board and notifications on what is happening in the World.  The number of notifications popping up is similar to Paradox titles and the scale could be seen as similar to some Paradox games, but more detailed armies in my view in this game. The addition of the cards and boxes is going back to something reminiscent of a board wargame.  I could easily imagine handling the cards while pushing around Nato style and leader card counters  The cards will give various bonuses, if you pay their \u2018price\u2019.  So you could pilfer the land to increase army cohesion (yet another important factor to manage) for the loss of some local loyalty, set a landmine up during a siege \u2013 which may or may not work, or dig some trenches around your territory. <br><br>The negatives are the game does crash on occasion, which is quite frustrating, but I save every turn just in case. The automatic \u2018resume\u2019 option generally picks up the game at the right point. I can\u2019t think of many more negatives for me personally. But, if you like flashy graphics and big tactical battles like the Total War series battles you may be disappointed to see that the battles are actually one screen of auto calculations. If you\u2019re not used to dealing with minutiae, you may miss some important aspects of this wargame simulation.  So, the game is best suited to someone who likes dealing with numbers, sorting out army structures, working out how best to spend limited resources on a variety of options \u2013 all very analytical and detailed number work involved, as well as the grand strategy view of understanding not just which army to pit against which army, but how you can wear the enemy down through clever supply cut off and use of weather to your advantage. I would also like to see a English Civil War DLC, which this game would be ideal for. into the AGEOD game library which present themselves as very historically accurate and wonderfully variable historical war games. I've had my eye on them for awhile and when the chance to pick up seven of their games on sale for under ten dollars came up, I took the opportunity. I'm quite impressed by the variance in historical period within their library: they cover periods from ancient Rome, the wars of Napoleon, the imperialism age of colonial expansion, American independence, and even the Russian civil war, with quite a few other titles covering other conflicts. All use the same, slightly altered for historicity, AI system that delivers quite the challenge.<br><br>As a strategic game, it's rather complex, though not to the point of excess. I'm still learning the basics of field supply, zones of control, and the vast array of algorithms involved in the combat resolution process, and I'm sure I've quite a road to travel before I can state with any confidence that I've proficiency in the system. The game, in its most simple description, is the building, maneuvering, and management of military units, many of them led by actual historical leaders of the conflict, and the diplomatic manipulation in order to incorporate foreign powers in your war either through aid or additional force.<br><br>In Thirty Years' War we have the political, religous, and ideological conflict that took during the period between 1618 and 1648, decimating Europe, and resulting in the complete reformation of its religious institution and political boundaries, setting the stage for the Second Hundred Years' War', the decline of the Holy Roman Empire, the unification of Germany, and the Napoleonic wars. The player is offered several scenarios that focus on smaller level engagements, highlighting specific moments in the extended conflict, and even the entire thirty year period in a monster 300+ turn scenario that will provide the player with hours of gameplay. <br><br>While I find the algorithm and the high number of variables for combat interesting, it is the combinations of this algorithm, unit manuevering, and the historical events that the designers have laced into the game to offer the player an expansive number of options each turn that really create a feeling of epic engagement. It plays out like a large-scale boardgame with the player negotiating with other nations, recruiting and moving soldiers, requesting political and religious favors, and all while moving to take and hold a series of objectives in order to either break your enemy's will (which ends the scenario in an immediate victory) or obtain victory points so that when the scenario ends you have more than the opposing army. That sudden death potential keeps the player on edge both offensively and defensively, and keeps the player's focus constant, as you'll be deciding how and where to attack, lay siege, defend, retreat and regroup. <br><br>In addition to the quality of the system there is wonderful art and sound design which really brings the period to life. The ringing of monastery bells, the firing of Arquebus, the clashing of pike, sword, and shield, it creates a wonderful atmosphere and allows the 17th century to play out before the player in one of its darkest and most horrible periods. The only thing I find lacking in terms of sound is in the game's music, which contains a few tracks and it often repeats them. While I think its very easy to just put on an audiobook, or even other music in the background to listen to, I would suggest a Baroque music mod which I found in the AGEOD forums, as well as here on Steam. It adds just that much more enjoyment to the game.<br><br>I'd recommend this game specifically to hardcore strategy gamers, and players interested in the period of the Thirty Years' War, but not many others. It's not an easy game to learn, nor is it extremely user friendly. The manual sits at almost 200 pages and it's recommended the players work their way through it in order to fully understand the complexities of the game. As for myself, I find the game to be a pretty great experience in terms of long-duration strategy games. I think the intricacies may be more than I care to learn though I'll return to it on occasion, and it's been interesting sampling the system and AGEOD library. They continue to produce high quality content of this nature which are sure to satisfy a variety of historical strategists.
