class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHegemony is a grand strategy game from indie developer Longbow Games. It's pretty similar to Total War in a lot of ways, except that everything happens on the world map. All the action is in real time, but the player can pause the game and issue orders at will. This becomes very important as your empire expands, because the map is very large and past a certain point you'll likely be fighting multiple battles at any given time. Overall, it's a much slower-paced and deliberate kind of game than AoE or Starcraft that depends more on careful planning than actions-per-minute, but still much faster than turn-based strategy. It's also a rather low-stakes game, since no particular battle field defeat is likely to be fatal to your empire, and you can pretty much always recover with some time and effort. Outright losing a campaign would essentially require intentional self-sabotage.<br><br>In terms of production values, the game clearly shows its creators' lack of resources. The unit models lack fidelity, the map is somewhat plain (aesthetically, that is, in mechanical terms there are a wide variety of environments), the sound effects are functional but get repetitive quickly, and the less said about the (thankfully rare) voice acting, the better. Still, none of these issues impact the gameplay itself.<br><br>Like Total War, units are created as groups rather than as individual guys, and fight in formation. There are only a few broad unit categories, and most of the factions (especially in Greece proper) are pretty much the same, but most factions have at least one unique unit, and some of these really do alter strategies quite a bit. Basic tactics revolve around a battle line of heavy infantry, and an unskilled player could probably get through the game eventually just by swamping the enemy under as many hoplites as possible, but a mixed army with ranged support, light infantry for flanking, and cavalry for fighting the enemy light units will perform much better if used properly. Units have a morale stat, and the goal of most battles is to break the enemy's will rather than fight to the death. Broken units drop their weapons and attempt to flee at top speed to the nearest friendly city, at which point they return to their home town and join the reassembling unit there. If they are captured, they can either be executed or enslaved, without moral judgement. Units recruited from the player's native faction gain experience with each victory, which the player can invest in a few different stats (speed, morale, rate of food consumption, and siege skill).<br><br>There is also naval combat, though the trireme (with Athenian and Persian variants) is the only ship available. it's a little bit finicky, to be honest, since how much damage you do is determined largely by the angle and speed at which the ships collide and they maneuver like, well, ships. It's usually just about who brought a larger fleet, or being lucky. Fortunately it isn't very important unless you're conducting amphibious invasions or raiding naval trade routes.<br><br>A lot of the strategy in the game revolves around supply lines. Trade routes are constructed by the player, but in most cases it's pretty clear where they're supposed to go and it's mainly just a matter of connecting the dots. These routes produce a bit of gold, but their main purpose is transfering food. All land combat units consume food, and if they run out their morale will quickly drop to zero. Since demoralized units are pretty much useless, keeping an eye on your food supplies and ensuring that the far-reaches of your empire don't starve is in many ways more important to victory than the actual fighting. Harrassing an enemy's trade routes and pillaging their farms to starve a city's defenders can be a very effective strategy that the AI practices routinely. Since food production varies by season throughout the in-game year, the timing of a campaign can be very important. Do you want to move during harvest time so your armies can forage off the land while they slowly overcome the well-fed defenders, or in the middle of winter to more easily starve the enemy but at the risk of your units collapsing from hunger? There's no one &quot;correct&quot; strategy, but your tactics will have to reflect the specific conditions you're marching into.<br><br>There's no base building in Hegemony, which ordinarily I would be disappointed by, but here it works for me. All structures have fixed, pre-placed positions on the map to reflect the actual geography of Ancient Greece. In addition to cities, there are forts, mines, watchtowers, farms, and shrines. The first three can be deconstructed, and are often found ruined, but the site can always be rebuilt. Forts and watchtowers are pretty self-explanatory, mines can be filled with either paid workers or slaves to provide gold, which acts as a population limit, and different types of shrines (heroic, logistics, and healing) provide different benefits to the units of the faction that controls them.<br><br>There are three campaigns in the game and a sandbox mode. The campaigns are a bit more structured than Total War since the player is given specific objectives throughout, and the rewards for completing them are often substantial, but there aren't any time limits and once you complete the tutorial section of the beginner's campaign the map is completely open. I spent about 60 hours conquering the entire map in my first playthrough of the Macedonian campaign, even though I had technically finished all the objectives much earlier than that. As a history student, I personally really appreciate the Civilopedia-style background information on pretty much everything in the game, and the developers make it clear that they've done their homework.<br><br>There are a number of different factions in the game, and a diplomacy system, but it's very simplistic. Other factions have a fixed hostility level to yours, based on the historical relations between them, and a variable intimidation statistic that depends on your relative strength and how close they are. These factors affect the frequency of attacks, and how much it costs the player to establish a truce or an alliance. The most important effects of the faction system are on the main gameplay. A faction's hostility determines the size of the garrison required to supress the inevitable rebellions, while allies fall under the player's control immediately and don't require a garrsion at all. Allied units are also slightly superior to the mercenaries recruited from most conquered cities, though they still have weaker morale than the player's native units, and don't gain experience. As I mentioned earlier, each faction also has unique units which can only be produced from their native cities. I often choose my targets specifically to gain access to a particular unit I like.<br><br>In conclusion, Hegemony is an extremely innovative game which makes up for low production values with simple but deep mechanics.
