It also really likes cooldown-based abilities, so at times you'll feel like you're doing MMO combat.<br><br>I found the game too easy initially on Normal, starting the game as a Cornac Berserker regular enemies were no challenge at all, only bosses took some work to kill. You unlock new characters and features through quests in the game, which I found cool.<br><br>The interface, however, is <b>REALLY AWFUL<\/b> (despite ridiculous frontpage claims of &quot;modern graphical and customisable interface, intuitive mouse control&quot;), especially the inventory. It's not really comfortable to use with the mouse: instead of displaying a tooltip for what you're hovering your cursor over, it insists on always having some object selected, obscuring your view with the information display. Movement with the mouse is also not very intuitive, as your character will move to where you point... until it finds something &quot;interesting&quot; or an enemy, and then you have to revert to moving a square at a time if you want any semblance of control.<b> It would actually seem like a good idea to learn the game solely with the keyboard at first, maybe even play another, keyboard only roguelike before this one. Only then will the mouse control layer make full sense.<\/b> There's also a problem of information overload when looking at map tiles (and other stuff): do I really need to know that I need a &quot;special breathing method&quot; to traverse walls everytime I'm looking at one? The game cruelly needs to display only crucial information at a glance, and then offer to say, press shift to show detailed info like now in the tooltip (see Civilization IV and V and its most complex mods for good examples).<br><br>The game has a fixed overworld, story elements and quite a bit of lore, which set it apart from NetHack and Crawl which only offer a giant dungeon. I found this refreshing, but I wonder how it's gonna hold up to repeated play. There are towns and shops to return to and sell your loot: I think I slightly prefer the approach of Crawl where you can't sell stuff or transfer it to other characters, so you just dump stuff you don't need without hesitation. But if you like sorting through tons of loot Diablo or Borderlands-style, you'll be happy with this game.<br><br>There's no downside to resting ('R' key) in this game. In fact I recommend resting as soon as you finish beating a group of enemies, to recharge your health and stamina and bring all your abilities off cooldown. (Annoyingly, some charge bars like &quot;equilibrium&quot; which is need for some powers are ignore by the rest function, leading you to spam the skip turn button or use a mod if you want to get back into an optimal state.) Somehow the author hacked that in with a magical item at some point and left it as-is. Also, most annoyingly the game drowns you in loot but unlike, say, Crawl, anything you don't pick up or leave on the floor will disappear after a while. EXCEPT in &quot;vaults&quot;, small enclosed areas with treasure guarded by monsters. Good luck identifying what was a vault when coming back to an area you've cleared, though, there are no indications except the room shape. Some of the gameplay choices made that set it apart from other roguelikes give a different, but not necessarily better experience. And as mentioned there are small but nasty oversights in the game design, which ends up feeling like a work in progress based on other roguelikes rather than its own though-out entity. The community also seems to be blind to most of these flaws, and over-protective of any choice the author makes.<br><br>Try it out to experience the sheer amount of content a true roguelike can offer, but do expect a flawed gem.
