formula to enhance an already engaging and well-engineered experience. <br><br>Disclaimer: This review focuses on changes to this edition. For a comprehensive review of FF6, you can find thousands on Google. :)<br><br>Justification for Opinion:<br>Final Fantasy VI is one of my top 5 favorite games, and I have played through the original SNES version and GBA port dozens of times over since their respective releases in 1994 and 2007 (the 1999 PS1 port is garbage). Being 13 when the game originally came out in 1994, I have many nostalgic memories tied to this game. The story, characters, music, and world in Final Fantasy VI helped me cope through a very rough adolescence when I needed an escape.<br><br>The Whoopla:<br>Final Fantasy VI is well remembered as the pinnacle of the classic 16-bit RPG, right alongside Chrono Trigger. The story, music, depth of gameplay, and amazing open world that presents itself after you think you've reached the end game still enthrall gamers to this day. It's no surprise that Final Fantasy VI is often ranked as the #1 best game in the franchise. And many, like myself, consistently place it in top 20 lists of the best games ever made. Like any classic piece of interactive entertainment that is remade, revised, or updated, fans new and old have their opinions on what should be left out, kept in, or remain unchanged.<br><br>From GBA to iPhone to Steam:<br>The Steam\/Smartphone ports of Final Fantasy VI are based on the updated Gameboy Advance port from 2007. Along with a host of added features to even out some rough edges and unbalanced spots, Tose (who ported the game to GBA) added 4 new summons, new dungeons &amp; bosses, retranslated the script, added some additonal dialogue to flesh out a few scenes, and added some goodies to cater to longtime fans. Having the original GBA port embedded in the source code for the Steam\/Smartphone releases ensures that the experience that the user plays in the new engine is as close to its original source material as possible. Although, some changes were made in order to cater to a more modern crowd. <br><br>Visual Changes:<br>All sprites and maps have been updated with higher resolution assets and added color. However, the developer chose to apply a muddy soft filter that dithers the sharpness of the visuals. The highlights in the color and overall saturation are also noticeably washed out. While both of these blemishes can be fixed with two very simple mods (found in the Steam Discussion board), it is still disconcerting that the mod community had to step in (as usual) to fix what should have been caught in R&amp;D. That being said, the overall presentation, post mod, is a rather pleasing update to the base sprite set. <br><br>All reused assets are about 1.5x larger than they were in the original SNES version. While some fans find this stylistic change off-putting, I find it enduring and am very much enjoying the richness in detail that went into the original sprite work I had never noticed before. The only completely redrawn assets I\u2019ve noticed are the character sprites, the towns\/castles in the world map (which are now made of polygons), and the battle backgrounds are now comprised of much higher detail and color depth. The overworld scrolls nicely while traveling on foot in the new engine. However, while riding a chocobo or flying the airships there is a noticeable amount of slowdown for some reason. While not game breaking by any means, it does disrupt the smooth presentation of the rest of the game. Other &quot;Mode 7&quot; sequences work very well and have FAR less pixelization than the SNES and GBA editions: example, traveling through Serpent Trench in Sabin's senario and the escape from the Magitek Facility. <br><br>Battle System Changes:<br>Perhaps the most polarizing change to this edition; the User Interface was left unchanged from the transition from Smartphone to Steam. This has deterred many fans from revisiting this masterpiece on PC. Here's the low-down: Instead of having a single, large window split between enemy names and a list of character names, hp\/mp, and the active time bar for each character, each party member has their own individualized window indicated by an avatar of that character (this set up somewhat reminds me of the classic Genesis RPG series Phantasy Star). The character action window will slowly creep up and reveal more of the window\/available actions the closer it gets to that character's turn. This simple mechanic works really well, eliminates some screen clutter by cleverly hiding the Active Time Bar without taking the mechanic away, and has some other bonus results. <br><br>Example: In order to access Cyan's Bushido skills In the original game, you had to wait until a progress bar filled up and reached the number of the particular Bushido attack you wished to use.  While the Bushido bar was filling up you were unable to control other characters, and the enemy could attack you during this time. With this new system you choose upfront which Bushido skill you want to use. Then, while the move charges just like it used to, you are now freed up to control the other party members. Cyan will then activate his Bushido technique when his charge is complete. This works just like magic spells that take longer to cast, and it seriously speeds up the flow of battle when Cyan is in your party.<br><br>Another welcome change is the magic menu. It is now separated by tabs that represent the 4 types of magic in the game for easy sorting: black, white, status, summon. Sabin's Blitz attack now has arrows for all directions that surround Sabin when he activates his skill. This is to ensure that you got the motions correct before you commit and end your turn. This is especailly helpful for people who are either using a keyboard or are unfamiliar with fighting game motions.<br><br>To all naysayers of the battle UI, I say just try it out. With Steam returns there is no reason not to give it a shot. It quickly won over this old jaded gamer, and hours into the game I'm still REALLY enjoying the new battle UI.<br><br>Sound Design Changes:<br>Final Fantasy VI's stunning soundtrack has never sounded better. These are the original SNES tunes, and from what I can tell they are running at a higher bit rate. This is compared to playing the game on SNES and over 5 SNES emulators on the same sound system. All sound effects seem to be in place and I have come across no differences in my recent play tests. As this port is pulling all source material, including sound cues, from the GBA rom embedded in the game's code, I doubt there is much missing. I have yet to complete the game on Steam so if I notice anything missing\/off cue I will update this review. <br><br>Overall:<br>Does this port replace the SNES original? Of course not! However, these few updates make the game a bit more approachable to a new audience that may have missed the game the first time around, or have never played a classic Final Fantasy. Many younger gamers who were weened during the PS2 era and beyond often find it hard to revisit retro classics. It's impossible for younger generations to feel the same sense of wonder and amazement as those of us who got to experience these classics when they were cutting edge. Nostalgia can be a fickle thing, and the wonderful memories older gamers have tied to a particular experience are as individual as the games themselves. If older gamers approach this port with an open mind, I think they\u2019ll find this edition an absolute joy to play. For those new to Final Fantasy VI, I say play this version first and then revisit the SNES original for reference.<br><br>No matter what edition you choose to play, Final Fantasy VI is a masterclass
