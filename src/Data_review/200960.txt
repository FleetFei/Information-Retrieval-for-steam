All of these actions have consequences within the world and how that world reacts to you. This is probably the strongest part of the game and what makes it worth playing through.<br><br>The growth and progression of your character is also well done. You can choose to play Geneforge as three quite different &quot;classes&quot;. Every level up you get a set of skill points that you can apportion as you wish. The class you choose doesn't dictate what you can do, but it does make it easier\/harder to level up certain skills. Skills range from physical combat, magic spells, the ability to &quot;shape&quot; creatures to fight for you. General skills, such as Leadership and Mechanics, are also available. Pleasantly, these are actually very useful to find non-combat methods to progress through the game.<br><br>Again all of these things are well done, and are what make the game worth playing. Where it falls apart a bit, is in the interface and to a certain degree, the combat.<br><br>Combat is turn-based, and on the surface, can be quite tactical. Timing, and awareness of the positions of both your allies and your enemies are all quite important. Particularly, when you do have allies (such as the creatures you can &quot;shape&quot;), you do have to take into account whether or not you or your creatures are blocking a path forward. You can use this both to your benefit and your detriment, by funneling enemies down a corridor into one of your stronger creatures.<br><br>In theory anyway.<br><br>The problem is that, at least in my experience, the game is rather easy. So, tactics more or less go out the window, when you can just click-click-click your way to victory. Positioning at that point becomes more of an annoyance, as you try to coax the pathing algorithms to make sure that you don't waste a turn because you clicked slightly to the left of something, so it paths all the way around and your turn is wasted. And it's even worse that the punishment for this is not death, but a test of your patience, more often than not.<br><br>Towards the end of the game, the challenge is upped, but by presenting annoyances, like poisoned areas that do damage to you and your creatures for just being in them. There is an optional area towards the end that serves as a pretty robust challenge, but again, the tactics aren't really that interesting at the end of it. It's more a battle of attrition that you just need to push through.<br><br>Overall, the combat side of this game seems like it would be great on paper, but it ends up being boring most of the time, and annoying some of the time. Very rarely is it actually much fun.<br><br>I should mention that the presentation of the game is quite old-school, and to top it off, there is no music aside from the title screen of the game. It's a good game--I can't deny this.<br><br>But in many ways it doesn't even come close when compared with some of the titles I feel to be truly great.  When I played it I compared it specifically to Final Fantasy 6 and Chrono Trigger, and the first Dungeon Siege, which were basically the only RPG's I'd played.  Like most typical RPG's your character increases in strength and abilities by killing enemies and completing quests.  But the classes differ vastly.<br><br>Your character can be one of three classes: a Shaper, who specializes in creating living creatures directly from magic; an Agent, who is far more focused on using magic spells to inflict the actual damage; and a Guardian, whose specialty is dealing direct physical damage, mostly with swords.  I tested Shaper and Agent, but in the end enjoyed Guardian much more--the other classes seemed playable, but I think Guardian is easiest from my recollection, and didn't require as much micromanagement.  The gameplay is good, and in combat your character gets action points you can spend to move, fight, and use items and abilities.<br><br>Combat makes up a lot of Geneforge, though you'll probably also spend lot of time talking to characters in the game, picking up items and selling them and trying to amass enough money to purchase the best items.<br><br>But the best part of the game is the story.  You will learn more about magic, and the Geneforge in particular, a powerful piece of magical machinery that can greatly enhance those who use it properly.  You'll learn about the different magical creations in the game, learn about the different factions in the game (four of them IIRC), and learn about the history of the land you've been stranded in.  And much of the heart of Geneforge is freedom to choose--your character build, your faction, your ending.  If you sufficiently improve your character, you will be able to roll over most enemies.  <br><br>Anyway . . . if this review sounds a little bland, perhaps it's because the game itself is kind of bland as well.  There are people who absolutely adore the whole idea of turn-based strategy, the idea of tactically micromanaging the actions of each of your party members, and carefully choosing their equipments and stats.  I used to be one of those people, when I first started playing RPG's, but less so today.  And if you are one of those people--I suspect that you will LOVE Geneforge.<br><br>But these days, I suppose I feel I need more from a game.  I think all the micromanagement has gotten to me over the years.  But also, Geneforge has very little atmosphere or enjoyable character.  It has virtually no music, though it does have atmospheric sounds.  I can't remember the name of a single character from Geneforge--whereas I can remember the names of all the main characters from Final Fantasy VI and Chrono Trigger, even though it's been even longer since I played them.  Geneforge's environments are mostly brown--deserts and dirt, though there is some variety thrown in.  And--while the ability to create life in Geneforge is quite awesome--I didn't even feel that this aspect of gameplay was very fleshed-out.  The story in Geneforge is definitely interesting, and there is a huge volume of writing in the game, so if you're a big fan of reading in your games, I can see it appealing to you.  But frankly, I still feel that both FF6 and CT have significantly more interesting stories, characters, environments, and of course excellent music.<br><br>Geneforge is a good game.  Some people love it.  You may.  Someday, I may go through and play the others in the series.  But . . .
