class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFlywrench is a cool game with a cool concept and atmosphere. I'm going to go in depth about this game, because I had fun with it up until a certain point. Flywrech is a type of puzzle game focused on movement and momentum. It achieves this by allowing you a limited degree of control over your character: you can freely move left and right, but you remain constantly falling and need to &quot;flap&quot; by pressing a button. You can press it rapidly to &quot;fly&quot; upward and by leaning your character in the other directions you can maintain movement to a certain degree. Additionally; flapping also changes your characters color, switching from white to red as you press or hold the button. This affects how fast you move directionally as well, along with a third button that allows you to turn green and move similarly to a rubber ball.  By having colored barriers that can only be passed if you're a matching color to them: Flywrech begins to shape up as a unique way to move through obstacles.<br><br>It's similar to the Super Meatboy brand of game. That makes sense as I noted that the creator of this game was originally a contributer to the Newgrounds community and the character &quot;Flywrench&quot; makes an appearence in Meatboy. (I'm unfamiliar to the degree of which the creator is involved with the creators of other Newgrounds related games; google it for info if you're interested). In each level there's a startpoint and an endpoint. Fail the level and you're sent back to the startpoint. There are no collectables, and it's a very straight-forward game. However, I feel compelled to note that Flywrech contains a difficulty curve that ruins the experience of anyone less than a hardened gamer. I know a lot of people disregard the notion that there are games that are too hard, and that most people need to &quot;git gud&quot;, but that isn't what I mean. The levels up till the last world, Mercury, felt fair and gave relative variety to design and challenge. Mercury does not. The difficulty curve spikes harshly towards the end of the game, to almost Cave Story level. Most of the Mercury levels add challenges that cause you to rush when you normally would try to balance your movements and think, and having imprecise movement centered on momentum ruins any sense of challenge as you don't have enough time to react to anything that comes near. There is an easy mode that you can activate, but it only blocks a certain color of wall and most of the challenges on Mercury do not involve the color of that particular barrier. So it won't help you very much. This kind of ruined the experience for me, as Flywrenches atmosphere felt more reserved and dreamy with a mix of urgency that never felt outright disasterous (especially the music; dreamy electric tones overlayed with the occasional voice noise).<br>Flywrench has a cool concept. You play as a spaceship or hacking tool that needs to make it to the center of the solar system. You pass through satellites as the levels and slowly increase the signal to make it to closer planets. There's indication that you're doing something more surreal as an instructor gives wisdom each time you unlock a new planet. This adds to the dream-like state that the game conveys through its visuals of clashing colors. As of now, I have not seen the end of the game because of the difficulty curve; you need to clear all the levels to access the final area of the Sun. But I felt engaged and enjoyed it enough to go back and mess around with some of the other features that unlock as you progress, including palette swaps and a Level Editor that allows you to make your own planets and zones. There is also a Time Trial mode for the exellently skilled. Not only that, but there is an option to &quot;unlock everything&quot; which I can only imagine unlocks the rest of the game defeating the purpose of struggling through the levels. However, it is nice to see that the developers thought ahead of the fact that the game gets incredibly hard towards the end.<br>Overall, I'd say Flywrench is worth your money. It's short, but fun and most of the time you aren't to mad about being stuck on a certain level.
