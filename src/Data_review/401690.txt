underground, a group of humans must survive until it is safe to return to the surface. <\/div><br>Life in Bunker is a strategy simulation game similar to the likes of Banished and Planetbase with a hint of Prison Architect thrown into the pot. LiB puts you in the role of Warden of a bunker right after the somewhat ambiguous events that have forced you underground (there is a little intro cutscene but it\u2019s not clear what it actually shows). Starting with only the bare minimum you\u2019ll need to quickly begin expansions in your bunker in order to meet the needs of your starting group of ten inhabitants. Your bunker\u2019s habitants will of course need the basics such as oxygen, food and water but they\u2019ll also need entertainment and ways of exercising in the bunker so they can stay happy and healthy. Meeting their needs is no easy feat though, you\u2019ll often find yourself dangerously close to catastrophe!<br><br>Full Disclosure: A review copy was provided by the developers.<br><br>LiB\u2019s low poly graphics look good and fit the game well, even on the game\u2019s lowest graphics settings (though I\u2019m not convinced that there is actually a difference between the different graphic presets, I certainly can\u2019t tell if there is). The UI is well designed for the game and easy to navigate, particularly the building menus.<br><br>The aim of the game is to keep your bunker going for 50 cycles (500 years) at which point the massive bunker door will unlock and you\u2019re humans can return to the surface, this is the game\u2019s win condition. However, you don\u2019t have to leave at this point and you can continue playing for as long as you like though the devs have said in the forums that they plan to add a proper endless mode to the game at some point soon. 50 cycles is the equivalent of around four generations which means that you\u2019ll need a steady flow of new humans so that once your first ten inhabitants become too old to work you\u2019re not left with a gaping hole in your work force. Luckily for you there\u2019s an incubator which you can use to \u2018grow\u2019 humans, when they\u2019re born they\u2019ll be children but one cycle later they\u2019ll be old enough to work. Every human has certain jobs that they are capable of doing and these are randomly chosen. However, you can spend research points to ensure that the babies will be capable of a specific job, more on research later.<br><br>The meat and potatoes of the game is designing and building your bunker. You\u2019ll have to get your workers to clear the dirt and rock away so that you can use the game\u2019s simple building system to set up everything your humans could ever need. All you have to do is mark what you want removed and your workers will get busy, the same applies to building, you simply decide where you want something and it\u2019ll be built as soon as your workers are available. There are upgrades to improve the speed of destroying and building things too and these are unlocked using research points. Research points are earned by having a scientist at a research station (it\u2019s worth mentioning you can have as many as you like) and they\u2019ll earn you points over time which can then be spent on unlocking new objects or upgrading current ones\/the capabilities of your workers. Sounds pretty easy so far, the real challenge comes from effectively managing your resources and planning ahead. You do start off with plenty of building materials and food but your supplies will be quickly depleted and so it\u2019s incredibly important to set up ways of getting more of these very early on. While food is obviously very important, it\u2019s actually a lot easier to run out of building materials just when you need them most. Life support objects require a huge amount of building materials and if you run out just when you need more life support you\u2019re going to be well and truly stuffed, hence planning ahead for such situations is important. Anyway, you can get more building materials by using ore converters which will take the ore you mine from deposits and convert them into building materials. This is quite a slow process but thankfully the speed of the mining machine and the converter can be upgraded.<br><br>While the first level of your bunker is quite small and has few resources (unless you set the map size to large) you can expand your bunker vertically, not just horizontally. With four levels for you to work with you actually end up with a huge amount of space and, honestly, it\u2019s rather daunting. That\u2019s not to say it\u2019s a bad thing, it most certainly isn\u2019t, but expanding into a new level is pretty challenging and requires a lot of stored resources. Not only will you need a lift to the new level (requiring a lot of building materials), you\u2019ll also need a power, oxygen and water supply for the level as you can\u2019t build cables and pipes vertically and the lift is sealed so your oxygen won\u2019t make it down either. Once you\u2019re down on the level you will have a little bit of oxygen to work with but you\u2019ll need to get those suppliers set up quickly if you want your workers to actually work and not just asphyxiate. <br><br>LiB may be focused on the building side of things but that doesn\u2019t mean there\u2019s not an element of danger in the game. As well as the obvious problems associated with running out of food or water, you\u2019re also faced with cave-ins and molemen. I haven\u2019t actually seen a human get killed or injured during a cave-in but I\u2019d guess that if debris falls on top of them it won\u2019t do them much good. Cave-ins can be prevented by building floors as when you build floors your workers also build ceilings which will prevent dirt and rocks from falling on you. Molemen appear to be mutated humans that have mole-like qualities. They\u2019ll sometimes dig up into your bunker and start attacking your inhabitants, even kill them if they become overpowered by the molemen. Thankfully these pesky little so-and-so\u2019s can also be stopped by building floors on top of the dirt floor as this will prevent them from digging up into your bunker. Your inhabitants can also get ill but as long as you have enough infirmaries to handle all of them (infirmaries are also staffed by scientists) these illnesses shouldn\u2019t be too much cause for concern. Then again, there will sometimes be outbreaks of illness and disease which will put serious pressure on your infirmaries and not dealing with these outbreaks effectively can be fatal. <br><br>My main gripe with the game is how the AI works. Even if a bunch of stuff breaks down, putting the entire bunker at risk, the engineers will just carry on with their life until it\u2019s their time to work. When there\u2019s no food the cooks won\u2019t bother cooking more until it\u2019s their time to work etc. For the most part, this isn\u2019t really an issue, but when these situations do arise it\u2019s a huge problem and I\u2019ve had this kind of thing lead to what is effectively a complete failure of the bunker, it\u2019s pretty damn frustrating. The other problem with the AI, while less pressing, is more frequent, your inhabitants will often leave a job without finishing it. Seeing a worker walk from one side of the bunker to the other just to do 20% of a job is again, rather frustrating.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Verdict <\/div><br>Life in Bunker is an enjoyable strategy simulation game that adds a new and interesting scenario to the city\/colony building genre. Whether you\u2019re incredibly good at these kinds of games or, like myself, you\u2019re not too good at keeping fictional humans alive, you\u2019re going to have fun with this game.  <br><br><i><a
