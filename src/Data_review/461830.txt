Fortunately the game does not ask for much when it comes to platforming; most of the game is instead spent fighting monsters using the array of weapons found in the game.<br><br><u>Recommended to:<\/u>Metroidvania lovers, those who can handle slightly imprecise controls in their platformers<br><br><u>Detailed information below:<\/u><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Gameplay<\/div><u> Fighting: <\/u>As mentioned most of the game will be spent fighting monsters. This is done by using different ranged weapons. While these weapons do have their specific uses outside of combat (destroying blocks, activating &quot;buttons&quot; and so forth) they do not change the fighting with the exception of some weapons dealing more damage and one that works as an AOE attack. This is unfortunate since the meat of the game is spent shooting these monsters and having the weapons feel more different would have made for more variation. For example one weapon could've caused a slow effect and another could've had a pushback effect.<br><br>The bosses are nothing special either. The feeling of a metroidvania is slightly lost in my opinion when the upgrades are used so sparingly. Most of the time the upgrades are only used in the level they were found, such as the swimming ability, and are not needed in the other levels with maybe one or two exceptions.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Conclusions<\/div>Ultimately I had fun playing Adventure Apes which is why I recommend it.
