class=\"postedDate\">Posted: March 11\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI would recommend this game overall, but the execution could have been tighter.  Starting off on a positive, the game's graphics are actually pretty unique as it has been programmed to replicate the graphic style of playing an NES game back in its hey day.  It has so much detail that if you look at the edges of the screen it's rounded off and reflects the side of the screen, just like the TVs did from that era.  It really reminds me of playing games back in the 90's, though it can hurt your eyes after a while, especially since you'll be sitting closer to your computer monitor then you would have sat next to the TV.  I didn't adjust any of the options for this myself, but there are several graphic options you can adjust if you weren't a kid of that era and don't want to strain your eyes like that.  <br><br>The game opens with a basic tutorial showing you the mechanics of the game, but otherwise it just dumps you in the middle of nowhere with no explanation as to who you are, what your mission is, or where anything is.  You actually don't even get a name in this game; you're just called Wayfarer.  I've noticed this is pretty common in many indie games, and while I don't mind it that much it doesn't build up the story very well or give the player any sense as to what it is they're trying to accomplish.  It isn't like the old days where we'd have an instruction manual to browse through.  However, this is somewhat dealt with by the fortune teller system.  Merely speak to one of them in any town and they'll tell you the next place to go, which is very helpful, as otherwise there's nothing else to guide you on your mission.<br><br>This proves especially meaningfully in certain areas of the game.  For instance, while exploring the desert area, if you pay attention to what the Arcadian in that area says, they tell you about a secret cache to go to in order to get an important item.  After getting that item and leaving the area, if you continue going East, you'll hit the edge of the sea, and you're able to walk across it and access an island (which is never explained or accounted for how you do that).  No NPCs mention this area, so it is somewhat obtuse to find, especially since nothing draws your eye to that set of islands; I feel like I was pretty lucky to have stumbled upon it.  What really aggravated me though was when I was there, I explored around the island to see if I could go further, but I must have missed the one place I could have done so.  With absolutely nothing even hinting at this secondary secret area, even when I thought it might have been there, I do think this is a flaw in the game since others might not have even found the first island like I did.  <br><br>This also happens while exploring different dungeons, as hidden areas are secreted away throughout.  Some of them have very good clues cueing you in to subvert your expectations and try something different, such as jumping through a wall of spikes because there's an arrow pointed towards them.  Other areas, however, don't seem to have any clues whatsoever.  In the very first dungeon you explore, it turns out there is quite a bit more there then you first expect.  After exploring for a while with greater power-ups at my disposal I was able to see an area I could platform through, but had no idea had to get to.  It turns out, all I had to do to access it was to walk through a solid-looking wall.  I could have done that part much earlier, but with no visual cues or hints, I had no reason to attempt that, which is a real problem in a game like this.  The player shouldn't have to be of the mindset to check every wall on the off chance it contains a secret; they should be rewarded for noticing the clues laid out around them, not for blindly jumping against every wall possible. <br><br>Another area that lacked polish was how the game distributed its hidden items and the usefulness of its power-ups.  Early on, with many of the collectible gems out of your reach because you lack power-ups, they feel very well distributed and tucked away, with some being easier to obtain then others.  However, in the late game, after you have all of the power-ups, many of them just seem to be tossed around randomly without much thought, as if they wanted to make sure they hit a certain number or just wanted to get the game finished.  Another example of this issue is how you get missions from NPCs to explore an area or get an item, making you think this might be a recurring theme through the game, but this only happens four times throughout the whole game.  It happens so infrequently that if you took it out entirely nobody would have noticed, especially since the main quest overshadows them.  <br><br>Similarly, one of the early power-ups you get in the game (if not the very first one) is a red block to help you platform in certain areas.  It's useful, but later on it becomes completely unnecessary, as you gain a double jump and the ability to slide and jump alongside walls.  If it weren't for an NPC rewarding you for getting the item, you wouldn't actually need it at all throughout the entire game.  Even if it there were areas it was necessary to use, these blocks show up in only a few, scarce areas, making it seem all the more pointless, especially since there are two versions of these same item.  In order to gain an achievement in the game you actually have to get rid of these two items.  I really think this needed a bit more thought since its existence in the game is so moot.  Another item that didn't seem to serve as robust a purpose as it could have was the mask that showed you invisible items.  It was immensely useful the few times it was used, but it could have served a greater purpose in showing you hints to find secret areas that wouldn't otherwise show up.  Such a shame they didn't do that as it would have removed one of my bigger complaints about the game.<br><br>A simple aspect in the game I found lacking was never getting an ability to engage in combat with the enemies.  I have played games like this before where you always platform and dodge around your enemies, but somehow in this game I found myself craving it more than usual.  I think it's because despite the game providing you with ample checkpoints so you can quickly retry areas if you die (very good job in that regard), the game has several enemies that are very annoying to dodge and avoid.  Namely the flying skulls and the giant eyeball; I craved a means of destroying these guys so badly by the time the game was over and was so disappointed it never came up.<br><br>The last thing I'd mention is that the story of the game is quite abstract and I would suggest fairly dark at that.  Nothing horror movie level, but I felt like I was reading the quotes of someone struggling with writer's block and depression that their life isn't going in the direction they want it to.  Not really what you'd expect with a game title like Super Win the Game.  I don't mind it being there, but I'd kind of like to play an indie game that didn't have this darker overtone laid over the gameplay.  There's a reason people love Nintendo so much; the platforming is happy and upbeat.<br><br>Overall, I would say this game probably isn't worth its full asking price.  With a few hints and pointers, I was able to beat the game 100% in just under six hours and I know others could do better then that if they had looked up stuff earlier than I did.  I picked it up while it was on sale and would recommend you do the same, as it does merit its overall favorable reviews.\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"gradient\"><\/div>\r\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div
