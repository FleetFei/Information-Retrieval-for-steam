can't say I regret it - most of the substantial gameplay changes are available in the free patch, but the additional features of the expansion itself are all worthwhile. Unfortunately, the mechanics are a substantial enough change that many players don't know how to play effectively with them, so they take to the reviews to complain about how they love to play Eastern countries, but they're ruined because it's too hard to get tech - or, how they love to play Western countries, and the game is ruined because Eastern countries aren't subhumans who easily fall to European guns.<br><br><br>The new mechanics for Prussia, the Great Powers, and most of all and what I'll be focusing on, Institutions create a much more historically accurate, diverse game, where multiple different strategies are truly viable. In the past, the optimal strategy was always 'blobbing', just conquering every piece of land you could until your enemies were so weak, and you so strong that the game ceased to be any sort of challenge after 1500. Blobbing is still the best, easiest strategy, but 'tall' (powerful, but smaller nations as opposed to larger ones with poorer land on average - how an industrialized Germany was far, far stronger than Russia in WWI, for instance) have enough advantages to be somewhat competitive.<br><br>The most important change by far is the Institution system of technological development. It used to be that everyone in the world was trying to be Western European, because if you weren't Western European you got tech penalties because slavs and brown people are just naturally inferior. Institutions model the spread of ideas, methods, innovation and technology, and they begin at one point based on various factors, usually in Western Europe and spread from there. They spread rapidly through rich, developed provinces, and slowly through poor land, meaning that while France and Britain will always be on the cutting edge, Poland, Russia and Eastern nations have a harder time keeping up, although it's entirely possible.<br><br>I have played five very different campaigns in 1.18 - Wallachia, Brandenburg, Friesland, Saxony, and Muscovy. <br>Wallachia is a three province minor, flanked by major powers on all sides. With expansion opportunities limited and bad access to Institutions, I bid my time, taking ideas and developing my land whenever tech penalties were bad, taking new tech whenever I got rid of the penalties, but only the early game was particularly painful - mid and late game Institutions, you can get quickly by having factories, universities, marketplaces, etc. - So my small, heavily industrialized nation managed to stay on the edge of modernity, outpacing my regional rivals by several tech levels and ideas at a time. Things were really slow-going early on, but as I grew stronger from what conquests I could gain, and the Ottoman Empire grew more and more backwards, the game culminated in a series of incredible wars where my small, extremely disciplined, elite army shattered Kebab armies twice their size and removed the Ottomans from Europe alone. <br><br>Brandenburg is medium sized, in a fairly good position to expand, and my strategy was a mixed one of aggressive expansion and economic development - being in central Europe, I could access most Institutions fairly quickly. Taking Economic Ideas from the start, I managed to become an industrial powerhouse as well, and I was always among the first nations to get a new Institution. My combined military\/economic strategy made me perhaps the single strongest country in Europe, once I formed Prussia. Austria defeated France, annexed Hungary and had an extremely successful game, and despite having twice my development, my smaller nation was still competitive with theirs in terms of wealth. With my status as an industrial superpower and the quality of my troops, I was able to defeat Super-Austria and Super-Poland - four times by development, combined - dismantle the Holy Roman Empire and form Germany. <br><br>Muscovy was the most interesting of them by far. The idea groups they need the most, Religious and Administrative, are both administrative idea groups, and you need administrative points to core land - and without rapid expansion, Muscovy is an unimportant regional power. Thankfully, they start the game with two vassals, and two small nations they can easily diplomatically vassalize. My personal expansion was relatively slow, from the start - I attacked Novgorod immediately, taking about half of their land for myself and coring it, and then I invaded Livonia and Riga, gave the land to Pskov and immediately began annexing it.<br><br> While I annexed Christian lands directly into my empire, I avoided severe religious unrest by giving most of Kazan to Perm so they'd core and convert the religion for me, then annexed them as soon as possible. Ignoring the vassals you start the game with is a serious error, and it's beyond stupid not to make good use of them because 'I don't like vassals.' Once you get administrative and religious ideas, it's cheap and easy to conquer and integrate lands directly, so you should only be using the vassals you start the campaign with to speed things up vs heretics and heathens.<br><br> By 1500 I had gotten to the fifth spot as a Great Power and my power was steadily rising, but Colonialism is the one Institution that Russia really has trouble getting - I dumped diplomatic and military points into a random province in the middle of Russia, saving my precious administrative points to core land. Poland and it's central European allies were more than I was eager to fight at the moment, so I invaded the hordes to my east - who had far worse tech penalties and Institution stats than me. I embraced the Institution much, much faster than the Commonwealth and got four tech levels ahead, a lead I'd maintain for the rest of the campaign starting in 1600. After a slow start in the early game, though, Russia's power just skyrockets, for various reasons. Most of their trade goods greatly increase in value, and since you dominate the Kazan and Novgorod trade nodes, you should be able to easily make an incredible amount of money off Production and Trade, enabling you to keep highly skilled advisors. The three final Institutions are spectacularly easy for Russia to get, because building marketplaces, universities and manufactories gives you free spread. Since you should be incredibly wealthy, it's an easy matter to spam buildings and surpass your neighboring great powers with ease, and it's helped that Russia has an amazing national idea to boost the spread of Institutions. The reality of Muscovy's starting position means you have to play a bit slow in the early game, but after 1550-1600, the gloves come off and Eurasia is at your mercy. Ally Austria, and it's partitioning time for Poland. <br><br>The Prussia mechanics are noteworthy in that there used to be nothing special about Prussia - Brandenburg, possessing Prussian ideas was exactly as powerful, and the change to Prussia did few substantial things. The Prussian Monarchy government type is a fair attempt at simulating the extensive militarization of Prussian society, and how this would suffer if they needed to oppress, I mean control a more culturally diverse, large nation.  Prussian Ideas are enough to ensure Prussia will always be powerful, but balancing expansion with development and maintaining Militarization of Society will make for a nation that outclasses even Brandenburg and Sweden.<br><br>Some other gameplay changes are great, too - rather than being a roadblock, forts are defensive strongpoints that are best built in rough terrain because the sieging army is always the attacker.
