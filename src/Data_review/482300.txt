But in 98% of occasions, horror games arguably live or die by the &quot;spookiness&quot; of their environments, and Investigator certainly gets off to an amazing start in this department.<br> <br>   The very first thing you'll notice about this game is, in fact, the visuals. An instant &quot;whoa&quot; from my mouth, I gotta say, and I wasn't even playing at top graphics (I tend to hold off and find out how laggy a game is gonna be 'til I commit to such heights). The sound is quite gob-smacking also, as I found when I wandered under the plastic roof of a greenhouse in an abandoned farmyard, and admired the vividly-realised raindrops pitter-pattering overhead. All around, an instantly immersive experience, which could perhaps be improved only by the presence of a VR headset.<br> <br>   But what about the STORY, I hear you ask? And the GAMEPLAY, for \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665's sake? Okay, okay, I was getting to that. Um...okay...honest truth? They're both pretty close to non-existent.<br><br>   When all is said and done, this is essentially a &quot;walking simulator&quot; in which you can't really die - well, okay, there was one fatal jump I failed to make, but this only set me back a couple of metres of gameplay - and none of the danger you appear to be in (crazy masked dudes with guns, giant spiders, various weather-based forces of nature) is particularly time or skill-dependent, as the game simply shifts to a cut-scene whenever your character needs to be nimble enough to make a desperate getaway. <br><br>   All of this might be perfectly acceptable, if only the story was involving enough to feel like a bona fide &quot;experience&quot;. As it is, there's almost bugger-all suspense once you realise you're basically strapped safely into a very linear rollercoaster ride with virtually no chance of derailing; and on the whole, there doesn't seem to be a great deal of reason why this is a video game as opposed to, say, a movie, as the interactivity is ultimately borderline inconsequential.<br><br>   Now, I recently gave a very praising review to a game called Layers of Fear, at which much the same criticisms could perhaps be leveled. But that game not only threw the odd semi-challenging puzzle at the player, but also somehow managed to make you feel like you were on a very personal and delirious &quot;journey&quot;, whereas Investigator never seems to overcome an undeniable sense of detachment from events. Perhaps this isn't helped by the fact that we frequently get to SEE our protagonist, the back of whose head we zoom in and out when it's time to have a cut-scene portrayed in third-person. At least this little technique, along with the aforementioned graphics and sound, assures us that we're not just looking at some typical, lazy knock-off job by some European hack who got their hands on a copy of the Unreal engine roughly this time last week.<br><br>   Regrettably, though, the STORY could easily have been penned by any one of those bored bedroom warriors. Suffice it to say, it takes you all of eight seconds to work out how to ESCAPE from said cage, thanks to a crowbar he's conveniently left within easy reach. Your attempts to flee from him then FORCE you to walk directly over the spilled contents of some radiation barrels (I \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 you not)! He subsequently coaxes you to come his way because he can &quot;help&quot; you now that you've been infected with radiation; and given that you have little other choice, you foolishly walk right up to him so that he can knock you unconscious and put you in a cage a SECOND time. <br><br>   He pours some more glowing radiation liquid over your legs, seemingly endowing you with powers comparable to that of The Incredible Hulk, as you don't even need a crowbar to escape this time around; you simply kick your way free. I'm sincerely hoping, however, that these devs are looking to use this game to advertise their skills as audio-visual artists, as opposed to competent storytellers. There's also some irritations to be endured in regard to settings, etc. - i.e. you have to reset them every single time you play - and there's some minor lag and &quot;getting stuck on the scenery&quot; problems as well. But on the whole, it's a long, long way from the most insulting thing Steam has thrown at us die-hard horror fans in recent years.<br><br>   I wish the devs the best of luck, and hope that they use this game as the &quot;learning experience&quot; which I'm sure they'll be sensible enough to see it as. Not a bad effort at all, just not a particularly extraordinary one, and definitely not recommended to anyone who hasn't already enjoyed the far superior likes of Outlast, Amnesia, Alien: Isolation,
