game<br><\/li><li> Smooth, responsive control<br><\/li><li> Good choice of musician <span class=\"bb_spoiler\"><span>in my opinion, anyway!<\/span><\/span><br><\/li><li> Raises awareness for a grossly available for those capable of beating it<br><\/li><li> Visual style is Atari-level retro; an understandable art direction for a shoestring-budget indie game, but some may find it charming, others unappealing<br><br><br>Edit: As of 05\/04\/2016, Steno Arcade received an update that added two songs with much lower BPM and slower vocals; it's infinitely more accessible for the average player now, which makes it much easier to recommend to non-typing experts. Obviously this does not compromise on the fact that, by design, anything above easy is likely to be incompletable for a keyboard user. It's great to see that For All To Play &amp; co. are listening. <br><br>This game is a kind of genius that not many people will understand or appreciate at first glance. Stenotype is to keyboards as bicycles are to walking: Both are many orders of magnitude more efficient than their alternatives, offering faster speeds for greatly reduced effort. On a bicycle, you can move at the speed of a full-out sprint-or faster!-- with the equivalent effort of a leisurely walk; largely the same can be said for our dear friend, stenotype. Any frequent typist can benefit from the speed and convenience of steno, just as any frequent pedestrian can benefit from the speed and convenience of a bicycle; nevermind the freedom either offers to those with injuries or disabilities. <br><br>But much like the bicycle, unfortunately, steno is a century-old advancement stifled by a world that makes little effort to accommodate it. Learning stenotype is prohibitively costly due to the absurd tuition fees that for-profit schools charge, and even used, student-grade stenotype machines run upwards of several hundred dollars; think thousands for professional equipment. Further, these machines are simply not designed with non-professional usage in mind, much less the software they operate on. <br><br>This game is a part of an ongoing effort to change that by facilitating a cheap entry barrier, compatibility with any software and ease of learning, bringing steno into the mainstream. It isn't just for middle-aged-and-up court reporters and other professionals; everyone can benefit from stenotype.<br><br>But what about the game itself? Unfortunately, there's not much to talk about at this juncture. I personally feel that For All To Play pushed this to Early Access a bit <i>too<\/i> early; it shows in the lack of content and the absence of accessibility that the average person has <i>to<\/i> that content. My understanding is that Steno Hero is designed to be the hardest of the four games planned for Steno Arcade, and in my view implementing it first was misguided- perhaps it was the easiest to code\/produce assets for? I can't be certain, but the attitude of the negative reviews here are telling. <br><br>The difficulty curve at the time of writing is less like a curve and more like a wall; I type at an average of 115wpm with an unmodified QWERTY layout and no shortcuts or other aids. The easiest of the four songs was challenging for me, but I managed 95% accuracy; my accuracy plummeted to &lt;50% on the second song and only got more abysmal from there. This readily highlights the obvious advantages of steno over traditional keyboards, but many players won't know what they're getting into when they start this game, and their first impression will be one of frustration rather than wonderment. It fails to have broad appeal to the already-niche community of typing game enthusiasts because of its uncompromising difficulty, which cheats it of its most likely audience.<br><br>This can easily kill an Early Access game before it has any chance to blossom by depriving it of positive reputation and revenue, though Steno Hero, being a passion project, is not directly reliant on Early Access success since it's, well.. free. This was a smart move, as I doubt it would get off the ground in this state otherwise. My advice to the developers would be to implement at least one or two songs that cater toward the average typist, as first impressions are everything. Additionally, the growth of an actual community is likely to hinge on the possibility of community-generated content, so I would be one to make that a focus.<br><br>Ultimately, though, there's a lot of love and polish behind this game, and undoubtedly a very competent team working on it. I'm excited to see it progress. What is steno? Look here at me playing this game on a QWERTY keyboard and a steno machine: <a class=\"bb_link\" href=\"https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=UtQzTUEuPWo\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noreferrer\"  id=\"dynamiclink_1\">https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=UtQzTUEuPWo<\/a> You can see that I don't write individual letters, rather words at a time. And writing words at a time means that my hands don't move much, much less than a QWERTY keyboard where you have to type every letter.<br><br>The steno machine is much faster at text entry than a regular keyboard, but it takes months to become proficient. Part of the reason that many haven't heard of steno is that for the longest time it wasn't public information! A major stakeholder in this game, Mirabai Knight, tried to bring steno to everyone, starting in 2010. She first had a program made that interfaced with steno machines and handled all the steno logic \u2192 that program is Plover, and I use and develop it now.<br><br>That was a great first step. Free software meant that anyone could download and play around with a steno engine. But, software is no good without hardware, so that's where the SOFT\/HRUF, Stenosaurus, and Stenoboard come in \u2192 they are all efforts to make the hardware cheaper (compared at professional steno machines that start at $1,000, these efforts are about one to two hundred dollars)<br><br>Great! Hardware, software\u2026but how do you do it? Mirabai worked with a technical writer to create &quot;Learn Plover!&quot;, a free online textbook that teaches steno theory. Many people I know, myself included, self-taught themselves steno using this book. That makes hardware, software, and learning resources. But once you are at the basic level, it's a lot of practice. In steno school, they just listen to people talk at pace and rewrite what they say. It's boring and costs lots of money.<br><br>Steno Arcade is the opposite of boring and expensive. It's supposed to be fun, to help people learn steno in an engaging way, and it tops off Mirabai's dream of free stenography for everyone. The difficulty scale is meant for a steno machine. 100 words per minute is rather &quot;easy&quot; for an intermediate steno student, but you'll see that regular keyboard users will find it impossible (and it nearly is). However, I really encourage you, if you want to write faster, to start learning steno. There's a great, active community, and it's a very fun skill. I use a steno machine instead of a keyboard full-time now, I write code with it. I'm writing this review on a steno machine.<br><br>So, by all means, try the game and enjoy it for what it is at the &quot;very easy&quot; level, and know that if you want to do the hard levels, an entire steno community is around to help you learn. It's not easy, but nothing worth your time is. I've been going for less than 2 years and I write at about 150 words per minute. Some people hit 225 (the graduation speed for steno students) in less than a year, some take up to 4 years. It all depends on how much time you put into it and how dedicated you are.<br><br>I'm excited for Steno Arcade to grow. As it is at the time of review, it's good for about 30-60 minutes of fun and I can see myself drilling and building my dictionary for the harder levels. I still replay levels when I'm feeling bored and want to practice the steno machine.
