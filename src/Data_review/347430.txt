Death? Apart from a fierce name, that is?<br><br>Frankenstein: Master of Death is one of those casual adventure games that contain some hidden object search. The story of this game is based on the famous novel by Mary Shelley: You play as the hero who needs to help Frankenstein in containing a monster that his assistand Igor created.<br><br>The game is completely controlled with the mouse. I'm not sure in how far it is a typical hidden object game, so I'd like to elaborate how it plays:<br>You go through a number of screens, always being able to return to every previous screen. Those screens contain points of interest that for the most time have a puzzle or keyhole you need to interact with. In a few cases, those points aren't shown by a &quot;navigation&quot; tool that shows the possible interactions - I think there are three points like this in the game where you have to use an object on a piece of wall to advance -, but usually they are shown. So when you interact with them, you might have to use one of your items in the inventory, you might just collect something, a puzzle comes up or a keyhole shows. If a puzzle comes, it could be a hidden object game where you have the additional task of assembling three items before you can collect them, sometimes including easy minigames. Other puzzles are visual puzzles where you for example slide pieces around, or at one point even a tangram that took me the longest of all to figure out. In the case of a keyhole, you can open it with the fitting items if you found them earlier, such as a flower key or some ornaments. In addition, solving a puzzle might be required.<br><br>And that is pretty much all that happens, essentially it is a classical adventure but with lots of casual puzzles thrown in. Now in theory you can skip puzzles after a while, but this is never necessary (except maybe at the tangram) and locks you out of one achievement if you do so. Talking about achievements, there are 20, all which can be gotten with a single playthrough on the &quot;hard&quot; difficulty mode. Since everything is still easy, the &quot;easy&quot; mode is probably a complete no-brainer. Apart from the three hidden walls you can open. There is a hint system, which to my knowledge does not invalidate any achievements. Achievement hunters still should face no challenge here, as the missable achievements only require to not skip anything - including cutscenes and dialogue - and play on the hardest difficulty.<br><br>The story is not completely uninteresting, but doesn't add much to the original. Which is a shame since the thoroughly interesting ethical issues of a Frankenstein machine are only addressed briefly. The music is good, but forgettable, the sounds are fitting.<br><br>So what remains is a game that kept me entertained for three and a half hours, I played through it in one go with losing interest at one point but not enough to stop playing since the progression is nicely paced. There is often a wonderful flow, leading you to get one item and remembering from an earlier scene that you can try it there to open a locked passage or container. Can I recommend this then? Well, the main strength of being nicely paced and short here works against it in matters of playtime, so I can recommend it if you get it on a sale or are generally interested in the
