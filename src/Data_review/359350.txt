Defunct is a fast-paced exploration-adventure game with some racing here and there. You\u2019re a small robot that drives around on one wheel and you\u2019re living on some kind of cargo ship travelling across a post-human Earth. Unfortunately for you, your motor doesn\u2019t work properly and so you can only travel at a fraction of the speed of other robots. Thankfully you\u2019re a smart little bot and you\u2019re equipped with a device that you can use to harness gravity and increase your speed, and that\u2019s good because you just fell off the cargo ship. It\u2019s been one of those days\u2026<br><br>Full Disclosure: A review copy was provided by the publisher.<br><br>Please note: While I don\u2019t have a huge amount of game-time I have in fact completed the main game mode (it took me just shy of two hours) but I\u2019ll talk more about the length of the game later on.<br><br>The first thing you\u2019ll notice when you load up Defunct is that it\u2019s a good looking game. The graphics are by no means cutting edge and a few of the textures are a bit bland but most of the game\u2019s levels take place in bright and colourful biomes which do indeed look great, particularly the desert biome levels. The actual models for the player\u2019s robot and for other robots and vehicles you come across are all great. On the subject of your robot\u2019s appearance, there are paint jobs which can be unlocked in the game though I\u2019m not certain what the unlock requirements actually are.<br><br>Once you\u2019ve stopped gawping at the game\u2019s appearance it\u2019s time to actually start playing it. You have two ways of increasing your robot\u2019s speed. First off, you\u2019ve got the broken motor which will get you moving, even up steep inclines, but it sure as hell is going to take its time about doing it. Your little motor is really just there in case you run out of momentum in the middle of a level or there\u2019s a hill you want to get up. Now the other way of getting around is a lot faster and a lot more fun. It\u2019s called the gravitation engine. The name gives an idea of what the engine actually does, it uses gravity. It\u2019s mainly useful when you\u2019re going down a hill of any length as a way to increase your speed. Going down the hill will give you a speed boost anyway, that\u2019s how forces work, but when you use the gravitation engine you\u2019re effectively \u2018pulled down\u2019 and as you can imagine that makes you pretty zippy heading down a hill.<br><br>Now accelerating is all well and good but it\u2019s pretty pointless if you can\u2019t keep that speed. Defunct\u2019s fast-paced sections are all about getting to a high speed and then staying there. For the most part this means you have to pick out a route through the level that\u2019s going to keep you downhill or at least flat-ish for most of the level. You\u2019ll also find two kinds of speed boost pick-ups, one which gives you an immediate and rather short boost of speed and one which is stored in a tank and gives you slightly more bang for your buck, especially if you get an entire tank of the stuff.<br><br>I eluded to the fact in that last paragraph that Defunct isn\u2019t the same all the way through the game. Depending on how you look at it, this is either a good thing or a bad thing. While most levels do require you to keep your speed up to some extent, possibly to reach a battery that\u2019s high up and needs to be activated, there are some parts of the game which are quite slow, usually the up-hill sections. Personally I wasn\u2019t too keen on the slower parts where you\u2019re metaphorically plodding along looking for stuff but they do offer a good opportunity to take in the world around you. <br><br>Speaking of the world, now would be a good time to talk about the fact the game has eleven \u2018main game\u2019 levels and a further five levels which are made for the time trial mode only. As I said earlier the main game took me just shy of two hours to beat and since the levels are roughly the same length that works out to about ten minutes per level. The length of the game doesn\u2019t necessarily make it a bad game and with the time trials and collectibles there are reasons to return to levels you\u2019ve previously played but when you consider the price of admission I\u2019m not convinced that it\u2019s worth it.<br><br>The time trials are where the Trials mention in my title come from. Depending on the level you\u2019ll have a certain objective to complete, such as pass through a certain number of points as fast as you can. While the time trials are pretty fun they are pretty much just bolted on to the game as the same levels with a different objective. There are medals for certain times so if you\u2019re a completionist you may be able to squeeze a few more hours out of the game. While most of the levels are reasonably well suited to the time trial mode a couple of the longer levels felt like they didn't really fit in a should have either been shortened when playing time trial mode, i.e. remove the battery objectives, or skipped over completely.<br><br>Just a few more things to mention. There are a couple more gameplay mechanics, tricks and a magnetize engine, but they don\u2019t play a huge part in the gameplay as a whole and the game does a good job of explaining them. The game runs very well, I\u2019m below the recommended spec for my GPU but I was still able to run it at 30FPS on max settings though, as others have mentioned, the game does suffer from occasional FPS drops. There have been a couple of complaints about controller support not working correctly but I didn\u2019t have any trouble using my Wireless 360 Controller. Lastly, while there is a story there to give you purpose for moving through the levels, it\u2019s pretty too basic to be at all interesting which is a shame because the robot is a pretty cool character.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Verdict <\/div><br><b>Worth Purchasing on Sale. <\/b><br><br>Defunct is a fun little adventure game with good graphics and gameplay but it\u2019s let down by its length and price.
