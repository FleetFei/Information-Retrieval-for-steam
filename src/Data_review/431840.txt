While lacking polish, this game is surprisingly fun. <i>If you just want the bullet list, scroll down to &quot;Final Say&quot;, as this will be TL;DR for many.<\/i><br><br><b>Controls<\/b><br>Control of this game is intended for controller, and controller is active by default. If you plan to use a keyboard, make sure to set controls in the pre-game setup menu, and go over them so  that you may become familiar. Once in game, navigate to &quot;options&quot; and set the control type to &quot;keyboard&quot; so that you have proper control of the game. Beyond this, still expect some odd control choices, such as a second confirm button when crafting.<br><br><b>Gameplay<\/b><br>Be warned: There is no tutorial in this game. You will have to learn everything yourself.<br><br>Gameplay is broken in to two distinct modes: map and exploration. In map mode, you travel the overworld square by square, consuming food to move or perform actions such as adventuring or mining; exploring the map can also trigger events, which can lead to treasure, events, or combat.<br><br>Exploration encompasses hunting and gathering food, adventuring for treasure, and exploring towns. When entering an explorable, the zone is randomly generated, giving constant play even in a single playthrough. Monster kills and treasure finds generate resources that can be used to craft equipment, while hunting\/gathering\/fishing provides food necessary to perform exploration actions.<br><br>As noted in the game description, all NPCs in towns are recruitable - simply talk to an NPC and, if you meet their requirement, you can add them to your party to be used in combat and crafting actions.<br><br>Character development follows two systems: level and skill. As you kill enemies, you gain experience, rising in level and gaining HP ans SP, as well as progressing &quot;killer&quot; tracks for enemy types. By using skills, everything from your attack skill to crafting skills, they rise in effectiveness over time, much in the way of adaptive level games such as the original Dungeon Siege.<br><br>Combat follows the semi-standard &quot;line formation&quot; style. Your party and the enemy are on opposite sides of the field and take turns, based on stats, performing actions. Attacking an enemy in combat uses a quicktime event, requiring input when a target passes in a reticle to perform a successful hit. All attacks take SP, which are regenerated in combat using the &quot;rest&quot; command, artificially slowing combat both on your side and on the enemy's side. After a successful battle, experience and loot is awarded, monster log entries are updated, HP\/SP are restored, and the game continues.<br><br>A playthrough consists of selecting your appearance, class, stats, and main task, with &quot;find someone to take the place of your soul&quot; and &quot;get revenge on your killer&quot; being the initial unlocked tasks, then running the game. The goal of a playthrough is to complete your main task to win the game, but gameplay is otherwise freeform.<br><br>Overall, gameplay is fun, if a grind, but the quicktime events and artificial stretching of combat requiring rest produce a dull, &quot;do I have to go through this again?&quot; break from the game.<br><br><b>Graphics<\/b><br>Let me clear the air in light of Rusinylancer's review. <i><a class=\"bb_link\" href=\"https:\/\/steamcommunity.com\/linkfilter\/?url=https:\/\/en.wikipedia.org\/wiki\/Voxel\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\" >Voxel<\/a> =\/= Buildable or destructible.<\/i> Voxel graphics are exactly as you see in this game: objects in game are comprised of &quot;3D pixels&quot; - filled cubes - rather than shaped polygons. The misconception that voxel games are solely sandbox or destructible builders can be attributed to the fact that the style is used in an abundance of such games, most notoriously Minecraft (though this itself is a misconception) or Steam-distributed games such as Trove or 7 Days to Die, while it's use in terrain and objects can be dated back to Amok on Sega Saturn in 1996 but likely goes back even further.<br><br>By using small voxels to render objects, in much the same way as PlayStation 3 game 3D Dot Game Heroes, with which this game shares MANY similarities, the game produces a &quot;neo-retro&quot; feel, being both 3D yet pixelated at the same time. While the voxel usage produces uncommon, pleasing visuals, this is weakened by a pervasive blur effect on even close objects, a camera locked to 90 degree angle turns at a non-adjustable plane, and opaque zone walls that obstruct the camera when too close to the screen-side wall of a given zone.<br><br><b>Polish<\/b><br>To an extent, the game feels a little &quot;cranked out&quot;. If you like randomly generated RPGs there are worse games you could by with your money, but definitely weigh other game options when looking at this one.
