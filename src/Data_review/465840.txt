Versus is strictly player-vs.-player and the game relies on mastering its roster to get the most for your dollar. By no means should you get this game to get into fighting-games as a beginner; the difficulty and the lack of extra modes doesn't warrant a full-purchase price. <br><br>\tHowever, if you are a seasoned fighting game player who has never played a SNK game or if you are a fan of other SNK classics like KOF, then TLB can be a treat of a by-gone era of fighting games. The game provides a Speed\/Power playstyle, which really only changes the damage and speed output. It also understands the appeal of fighting-games is that characterization matters the most not only as a visual spectacle but also as the unseen complexity behind each fighter.<br><br>\tEach fighter controls slightly differently with different types of combos, special moves, speed, offensive and defensive capabilities, and each fighter appears to have a natural counter-opponent. It's difficult to go into details without becoming too technical but the important take-away is that you can feel the changes in-battle, and it's mastering this self-imposed challenge the appeal of SNK games. The simplicity of the four button design undersells the polish that you can find in the game's core gameplay.<br><br>\tThis polish is what leads to the greatest quality of TLB; it oozes style with all its substance, and that alone may be worth the admission price. (I only wish there were subtitles or English voice-overs to let the banter add to the spectacle.) The visceral nature of the battles is emphasized when the final blow is struck, the game literally cutting the music in the final blow with a red-flash.
