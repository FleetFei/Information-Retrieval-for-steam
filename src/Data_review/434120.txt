well-written text-based experience! While the game's design lacks some fundamental elements found in other titles of its genre, <i>To Burn In Memory<\/i> overcomes this by presenting a rich and in-depth story. 8\/10.<br><br><b>Long Review:<\/b><br><i>To Burn In Memory<\/i> is an extremely well written text-based adventure. Unlike the numerous poorly written click-through adventures on the Steam Store, this title is reminiscent of both traditional MUDs as well as interactive adventure novels of old.<br><br>To simply compare this to other interactive adventures is to do it a great disservice. The writing style and overall quality is above average. The author paints a perfect picture of a city devastated by warfare: riddled with death and destruction and yet maintaining the notion that it was once great. The use of imagery through describing colors and the city's architecture make game's setting well fleshed out. Even the selection of quotes in the game's introduction are well selected. <br><br>While I don't want to give too much away, <i>To Burn In Memory<\/i> seemingly takes place in alternate re-telling telling of our own history (yet I'll let the narrative speak for itself as some things aren't clearly explained). The few technological innovations illustrated seem to be almost steampunk-ish, plausible in their &quot;old world&quot; existence but dissimilar from our own (at least my grandparents never gave me an Oroboros trinket). This story is told through &quot;memories&quot; that you'll experience along the way, learning of the city's hellish past and the lives of its denizens.<br><br>Telling the story is one of the game's strengths but there are a few glaring weaknesses. There's no Steamworks integration at all: no cloud saving (or any other kind of saving), achievements for seeing the title through to the end, or screenshot capability (if you have a favorite moment or like a well written segment of the story, you can't store it for later). <br><br>Another weakness - why I can't give this a higher rating - is that the in-game navigation is <i>terrible<\/i>. With the exception of <i>Free Roam<\/i> mode, you're supposed to collect items around the city in order to advance through locked areas. But losing your way is easy: there's no compass and the descriptions do not always match up with the directional options. What's the point in putting &quot;east&quot; or &quot;south&quot; in the choices if we don't know what's in the north or west (just an example)? Many MUDs had better directional control, many interactive stories do as well. I get that there are only a few options for movement but it probably could have been handled better.  <br><br>There's also a negative that can actually be taken as a positive: the story is <i>extremely<\/i> linear. This isn't &quot;choose your own ending.&quot; Because &quot;north or west&quot; aren't defined, the story's path is <i>usually<\/i> pretty clear. The narrative is actually tight because of this clarity. It also makes the game less &quot;game-like&quot; and more like reading a story (though the inclusion of background music probably wouldn't have hurt). Some think this is a negative, I don't. But the game should also be tagged as a text-based adventure since some gamers obviously don't read the reviews before installing. <br><br>I'm a huge fan in the way in which the story unfolds on screen. The story's elements present themselves as memories: initially you start with little paragraphs of text and to see the whole history of a thing, you need only click to &quot;experience&quot; it. Additionally, you can rush the entire game in <i>Free Mode<\/i> by not reading any of the memory entries (I'm not really sure why anyone would do this!) <br><br>Collectables needed to unlock areas of the game to advance are listed on the right-hand side of the screen. The only collectable I had diffculty with obtaining was the &quot;crescent moon key&quot; but I associated this with poor directional control rather than defect or error.<br><br>Overall this was a great experience and - if in novel form - I'd purchase it in a second.
