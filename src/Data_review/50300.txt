The gameplay is fun, but nothing revolutionary.  It's a little difficult, but it makes the combat feel more desperate.  The desperation makes good set dressing for the events of the plot.<br>The big selling point of this game is the narrative.  I don't want to spoil it too much.<br>Let's talk about the concept of choice in games for a minute, because that's one of the heavy themes of this game.  In Mass Effect, you're made to make lots of choices in dialogue.  Most of it resolves the same way no matter what you pick.  It tricks you into beleiving that the choices you make matter, but the truth is that Mass Effect 3 happens, and nothing you do even matters.  So, you make choices based on what abilities you want to use.  Maybe you make some choices so that you can fill a red bar instead of a blue one or bang a specific alien chick.  The writers of Mass Effect, and Fable, and InFamous, and any number of games that deal with morality fail to understand that choices made unconsciously during gameplay can add another layer to the story if the player is made to face the consequences.  Do you flank left or right?  Do you cast a healing spell, or press the offensive?  Do you jump on that goomba or over it?  What if you faced a penalty at the end of Super Mario Brothers for breaking all those blocks who were, at one point, ordinary citizens of the Mushroom Kingdom?<br>Spec Ops: The Line does this to great effect.  By the end of the game, you'll want to curl up into the fetal position and cry.  <br>It felt like whoever wrote this story had respect enough for the gamers to deal with mature subject matter in a way that games seldom do.<br>My one major gripe is that the bad guy you're supposed to be looking for in this game is named John Konrad.  It's a too-on-the-nose reference to author Joseph Conrad who wrote Heart of Darkness, the story from which this game takes a lot of inspiration.  It's like how that one goth kid you went to high school with wrote poetry about how they wanted Charles Baudelaire to turn them into a vampire.  It's just a little cringey.  It also lets anybody with any knowledge of literature know early that things aren't going to end well.<br><br>TL;DR: If you're tired of your average modern military shooter, then Spec Ops: The Line is the game for you.
