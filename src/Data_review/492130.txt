class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"bb_h1\"> <b> This game was reviewed using a code sent by the developer\/publisher but this did not affect my opinion on the game. <\/b> <\/div><br>Bye-Bye, Wacky Planet is a casual early access 2D shoot em up game developed by Rasul Mono and published by Efimov Max. It was released on 16th July 2016. You are Alan, a green coloured alien that wants to escape an enemy facility in order to return to his home planet. He works alongside Eve, another alien that guides his way throughout each level. This review will cover the campaign in reference to design, dialogue, sound, and so forth. It will also cover gameplay, performance and current issues. <br><br>Wacky Planet has a very solid campaign that incorporates diverse level designs into various settings. The campaign is split into three episodes with 16 episodes in the first, 15 in the second, and 10 in the third with three more episodes coming out in 2016.  All levels and episodes have disparate objectives and settings. For instance, episode one most levels introduces you to the game and tasks you with finding a key card and finding the exit. Furthermore, all levels and episodes provide different settings such as episode one in a facility to level two featuring various others. These settings have weather effects that I think enhance the atmosphere and enjoyment of levels. Wacky Planet emphasises challenge in complementing the different levels because the game becomes more difficult as it progresses, which climax with very strong bosses. To counter the rage-inducing and repeating nature of some points, the game has checkpoints that occasionally appear and successfully balance difficulty with relief. <br><br>Wacky Planet incorporates dialogue via the cute alien characters. All dialogue is simple and is meaningful. For instance, Eve helps Alan discuss how to complete a level and the objectives. In saying that, the dialogue has constant grammar issues that are visible to notice. Lastly, the soundtrack is neat, adding some intrigue into the atmosphere of each level. However, there are not many songs to listen to, but not enough to notice constant repeating of the same. My overall take on both paragraphs is that I enjoyed all three episodes and levels that features new settings, more enemies, often more difficult to beat, disparate objectives, and levels that require more strategies due to the progressively difficult nature of the game. I liked the simple dialogue and the soundtrack that complements the atmosphere. I completed the campaign in 6.5 hours and I feel it had a good length to it.<br><br>Wacky Planet has very solid gameplay. As you progress the game adds new gameplay elements into the levels. For instance, a level allows you to play as a tank while another allows you to play as a drone. These spice up and refresh the core gameplay to ensure the game is not stale. There is an upgrade system that is the strategic element of the game. There is three tiers, split into personal abilities, light and heavy weapons. The personal ability has three upgrades and the latter two have four upgrades. You upgrade by collecting in-game currency following killing enemy. I think the upgrade system creates some variety in engaging your enemy and completing levels. Lastly, Wacky Planet features some strong AI that does not feel like an afterthought, rather something crucial that increases the difficulty of the game while not feeling stale and buggy.<br><br>Technically, the game needs some polishing. There is no visual options in the game and I do surpass the recommended specs, but I only constantly achieve 50 FPS. I barely notice any stutters while at 50FPS though. However, when I engage multiple enemies my FPS drops to around 35-40 FPS and when I use a machine gun, which is an upgrade in the game, FPS drops to 27-30FPS. In other words, I think the game needs more optimisation.  <br><br>Wacky Planets store page suggests the game is &quot;absolutely honed and the bugs completely absent&quot;. I disagree with this assessment, but I have not noticed many bugs at all. For instance, I have noticed very rarely in some levels of unresponsive AI walking into walls. I noticed another very rare bug of going into steam overlay and exiting out of it and being unable to move the player, but I could shoot. These issues are very minor as stated. Two of my main issues is one through the numerous and very frequent grammar issues. Secondly, I do not like that due to the abundance of enemies you have to beat the screen constantly shakes while shooting. The developer currently seems very committed to fixing and improving the game so I will post my negatives with the game onto the forums for him to review and hopefully fix. If he fixes these little issues he has covered my negatives minus performance. I think some minor features could be of use in the game, particularly load games and a mouse cursor as both are excluded.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> <b> Conclusion <\/b> <\/div>Overall, Bye-Bye, Wacky Planet is a very good game. It features three enjoyable episodes with 41 differently designed levels. All levels progress the story of Alan attempting to escape a facility to reach his home planet and incorporate some simple dialogue to nail that point. Furthermore, each episode incorporates diverse environments coupled with increasingly difficult AI to add a wrinkle of strategy and tactics to the game. It features very solid and fluid gameplay and decent performance. At $USD$4.99 I recommend this game because you can complete the game and return regularly as new episodes release to play it before stopping it. Furthermore, the low price allows you to support a developer that has evidently presented a nearly bug-free game, which is not what you usually get in early access.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> <b> Rating <\/b> <\/div>8.5\/10<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> <b> Pros <\/b> <\/div>\t- Very nice levels that are diverse and incorporate different settings.<br>\t- Straight-forward gameplay that expands as the game progresses.<br>\t- Upgrade system.<br>\t- Fine story.<br>\t- Cute artwork.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> <b> Cons <\/b> <\/div>\t- Subpar performance. class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"bb_h1\">Bye-Bye Wacky Planet Hits with the Nostalgia<\/div><br>Although technology enables improvements in games part of me misses the era where games were simple. Simple concepts with simple gameplay that were brilliantly designed. Simple never necessarily implies easy. Some of the most simply designed games offered players enormous challenge. If you were to talk to many gamers today who were raised on the classics you will likely discover that they found the games of yesteryear to be more challenging and difficult.<br><br>Some of the classics that I was raised on were 2D side-scrolling shoot 'em ups. The original Duke Nukem games come to mind. It was these games that took hours of my childhood. I lost count how many times I played through those games. Yet this isn't some cry that they don't make them like they used to. Because that isn't true. Bye-Bye, Wacky Planet is testimony to the fact that when developers sit down and consider making one of these games that they are still able to find the magic touch.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">What is Bye, Bye Wacky-Planet?<\/div><br>Aside from being one of the most promising 2D side-scrolling shoot 'em ups to come along in recent memory, it's a charming space adventure. You play the role of Alan, a green alien who is attempting to escape an enemy facility in order to return to his home planet. So imagine if E.T. were green instead of brown and rather than being sick he had a weapon and shot at all of the murderous enemies that attempt to take him out. It's a fun concept albeit not one that is full of originality.<br><br>It is important to note that it is episodic in nature. As of writing the game has three of its six episodes published. Each episode has a varied number of levels. Each tells a different part of the story.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Presentation<\/div><br>Story<br><br>Given that the game is episodic and has a story a key component of the presentation is in fact the story. Eve's dialogue with Alan conveys much of the story. Eve is another alien who is in constant communication. Although the idea behind Eve is well thought out and she does work rather well as a guide - and tutorial - for Alan there are issues with the presentation. Unfortunately, there are a number of grammatical issues that bog down the game.<br><br>Checkpoints<br><br>The game is set at a medium to higher difficulty. Platform and shoot 'em up experts will probably breeze through most of the available content. However, if you're not an expert some of the game will induce rage. Therefore, I am rather thankful that a checkpoint system has been incorporated into the level design. However, you'll want to be quick on your toes as some of the checkpoints can thrust you immediately back into the action. This becomes a constant battle of the wills and you may die many times over before reaching the subsequent checkpoint.<br><br>Graphics<br><br>The color palette chosen for the game is largely a bright one. I believe that this should appeal to many. The graphics are relatively pleasing on the eyes but they are not exactly perfect.<br><br>I have one issue with the artistic design decisions of the game. Occasionally I find that the game is set too close and not enough of the playing field is visible to the player. I argue that this artificially, and not for the right reasons, increases the difficulty of the game. I'd like to see the game set back a little bit. The result, I believe, would highlight the level design much more than what is currently conveyed. The level design is one of the highlights of the game. Why not show it off?<br><br>Visual options also needs to be improved. Although others have reported that they have had FPS issues with the game I have had none whatsoever. However, I easily breeze past the minimum required specs so it would be interesting to see how someone closer to them reports.<br><br>Sound<br><br>The soundtrack, what there is of it, is easy to listen and has been well chosen for the game. With that said it needs to expand as the rest of the game is released.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Gameplay<\/div><br>Strong AI<br><br>The game has some very well designed AI... when playing the game properly (see my next point). There are an onslaught of different enemy types and they amp up in difficulty over time. They are incorporated at well designed intervals giving you just enough time to learn but not become comfortable. AI has always been one of the most important elements of a side scrolling game so it is nice to see the dev nail it this early in development.<br><br>Upgrade System<br><br>The highlight of the gameplay features is the upgrade system. There are three areas where you can choose to upgrade. These are light attack, heavy attack and a special feature. This is the most strategic element of the gameplay.<br><br>However, the upgrade system can be gamed. At the moment when you die and you go back to your checkpoint you can constantly get enemies to respawn from their respawn device. Enemies do not stop spawning nor do they stop dropping coins. If you keep your space from a respawn point there is nothing stopping you from killing over and over again until you have banked enough coins to visit the game shop (generally near a checkpoint) and purchase an upgrade. I hope that this is patched before release.<br><br>Intuitive<br><br>Perhaps most importantly is that gameplay - certainly with a controller - is very intuitive. I prefer to play with a controller but there are keyboard and mouse controls established. I would argue that the game would play better with a controller but that may just be personal preference. Everything plays and feels as it should control wise.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Is it Steamified?<\/div><br>Yes. Bye-Bye, Wacky Planet has a set of Steam trading cards and has achievements for the hunters out there. There are eight trading cards and currently fifteen achievements.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Verdict<\/div><br>Bye-Bye, Wacky Planet is in Steam Early Access. Half of the game's six episodes have already been released. The game is certainly playable and the average amount of gameplay is estimated to be about 6 to 8 hours at this point. With a further three episodes to be developed and subsequently released you are looking at ten hours or more of content. The game is currently at a price point that would, in my opinion, reflect a bargain price (estimated $1 per hour of quality gaming). If you're the type to provide feedback then the developer will certainly take it on board. I recommend the game to people interested in the genre.
