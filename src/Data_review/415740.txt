and making every single kind of mini game they can with that concept, set to a cheery 8 bit soundtrack, a visually striking PS1 art style and some genuinely chuckle-worthy writing.<br><br>However, the charm wears off FAST, as the later levels ramp up the challenge by adding in new gameplay quirks, new obstacles, new mechanics, and a ungodly amount of trial and error. For example, in World 4 (I do not know how many overall worlds there are), there is a stage that asks you to drive a bus that can jump first across several cliffs, then onto a draw bridge separated into the three pieces you must jump between that will sway RIDICULOUSLY fast completely upside down if you are even a milimeter off center, THEN you have to jump from the bridge, probably at a terrible angle, into a literal minefield stacked with TNT crates so densely that it's nearly impossible even with full control to avoid them, and if you somehow make past all that there is STILL MORE as there is a nest of moving bumpers and possibly even another challenge after that, I ragequited after a solid 20 minutes on that level alone, already upset at an earlier set of levels that implemented a strange gravity mechanic that I had to look up gameplay footage of to understand how to work. Challenges that could make up 3, 4 or even 5 separate levels in other indie platformers are jampacked into checkpoint-less gauntlets that feel like they were designed for rage-based YouTube gameplay videos. <br><br>The difficulty isn't helped by the physics, either. They are consistent and fully functional, but they are unrealistic and therefore often unpredictable, especially since they change without warning for certain stages. On the ground, the gimmick of never being able to stop, slow or even change the camera goes from amusing to annoying as the difficulty ramps up, as the game demands deliberate forethought while giving you a set of controls that makes such a thing literally impossible, contributing to the aforementioned overall aura of Trial and Error. On the air, the problem is way more excerbated as you have almost zero control over your bus once it's airborne, which leads to a lot of cheap deaths. In a Tony Hawk game, if you bail from a midair trick, it's because you misjudged a half pipe or went for too many flips. If you bail in midair in this game, it's because the game randomly decided to make you spin 21 times instead of 20 and therefore you landed upside down. Given that there's a physics engine, it's not actually random, I'm sure, but it certainly feels like it in certain stages.<br><br>Now, platforming challenge is platforming challenge, and you might just say I need to 'git gud', but the difficulty - the amount of obstacles on screen, the janky mechanics you have to master with little instruction, the crazy one hit kill spots - genuinely doesn't feel fair after World 2, like I said it seems to go almost entirely into trial and error, in which you can't possibly predict what's next; one hit kill bumpers and explosives are placed inside or behind buildings, completely removed from your view, so that you will literally not be able to react to them on first run unless you single frame reactions. For me, at least, waiting eagerly to see what funny dialogue or cool hat or interesting stage the game was going to throw at me turned into waiting anxiously to see what half-functioning mechanic or overpowered boss or cheap and broken segment the game was going to throw at me. I know Dark Souls comparisons are beyond cliche, but it literally starts to feel like that as one mistake - which is incredibly easy to make for all the reasons listed above - WILL kill you and force you to restart (to the game's credit, there is a dedicated restart button and there isn't any lag between death and restart). <br><br>The reason for the title of my review - Omnibus seems like it's going to be a short, sweet and charming treat but after the first few layers quickly turns into a chafing, painful ordeal that you only keep soldering through just you can say you did.<br><br><br><br>However, despite all my complaints, I still have it listed as Recommended, as like I said the first few levels still have a ton of charm, and if you're a fan of brutal, masochistic, perfectionism based platformers, (Johns, I am not) you will still love the game. If you aren't, you might still crank some fun out of the multiplayer and the sheer experience, and with the game's low price point (which saves the game honestly, at $20 this would be utter garbage),
