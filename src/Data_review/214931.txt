This journey back to Rapture is very short, and I consider myself one of the slowest Bioshock players ever because I can't help but search through every nook and cranny of the luscious and deep environments, and it bothers me because just being back in my favorite city makes me yearn for more time in its grasp. The second small thing I wish Irrational would have taken more time to change is the character models of the NPC's; not Elizabeth, but the rapturians in which you are able to see and interact with toward the beginning of the game. Most of the women look exactly the same, with the men having slightly more variation but still a lack of different characteristics. I feel Irrational has the time and resources to do so, and it would have been the delicious icing on the environmentally genius cake.<br><br>Now onto the good stuff. It is absolutely gorgeous and I was giddy with excitement the entire time. Elizabeth is back, looking better than ever, and she fits in perfectly with the artistic design of a booming Rapture. Honestly, they could have just had another 2 hour long section of exploring this setting and I would have been satisfied, but we can't get everything we want. The story (I don't want to give anything away) is very well put together and it provides reasonable mission requirements and objectives to help keep the experience fresh based on where it takes you. There are even a few new weapons and abilities in which I will allow you to find for yourself, but they certainly add new ways to experience the amazing combat of Bioshock. All the places in Burial at Sea are beautiful and it makes me long for another complete game based in Rapture because they do it so well. There are constant references to the original Bioshock, as well as Bioshock Infinite, and it certainly makes you contemplate everything you know about the Bioshock world. <br><br>In conclusion, I believe this add-on is a must-play for anyone who enjoys a good game, gameplay, and story, not to mention those who love Bioshock. I will most likely be playing this another 3 times by the end of the week and exploring it in new ways. You can expect a visit from some old friends, and even some new ones, and if there is anything this expansion will do, it will leave you wanting more Rapture in a way you have never All too often developers cut corners in an effort to deliver the same kind of profit margins that comparatively cheap multiplayer map packs yield and that's when singleplayer additions are attempted at all. Given the fact that the narrative is what has come to define the Bioshock franchise, fans have been eager to see what Irrational Games would produce for Burial at Sea. Infinite's first expansion was an arena combat focused affair that rewarded players with trinkets for enduring repetitive waves of enemies and by doing so, entirely avoided what makes Bioshock such a revered franchise. Returning to Rapture, Burial at Sea strengthens the connection that Infinite's characters have to Andrew Ryan's sunken dystopia and elaborates on some of the narrative elements in Infinite. It's a two part tale that promises to be equal parts fan service and classic Bioshock. <br>\t<br>Strait away it's apparent that Irrational games has not compromised the production values of Burial at Sea in the way that many single player add-ons have in the past. Full voice overs have been recorded and given it's new setting, assets from Columbia have not been reused. Burial at Sea feel like a proper stand alone title. Elaborately animated sequences involving iconic characters from previous games reinforce the notion that this is a no expense spared affair. It's classic noir inspired opening makes a strong impression and exploring a thriving Rapture is a real treat. It doesn't take long however for the warm color palette to abate and the familiar cool blue and green tones to take over as the plot moves to a sunken, splicer-filled department store. It's a shame the intro sequence is so brief. It could easily have been expanded upon, adding gameplay mechanics befitting Booker's vocation, further defining Burial at Seas as a unique chapter in the Bioshock franchise.<br>\t<br>Your trek through the submerged department store is made more perilous due to the fact that it has been sunk by Andrew Ryan and the pressure has destroyed some of the structure making passage difficult. The only people left in the abandoned store are splicers. Interestingly enough, Burial at Sea severely constrains how much ammo you will have at any given time and forces players to mix up plasmids, gunplay and a bit of stealth. In doing so combat has become a bit more interesting as groups of four or five Splicers present some challenge on a tight ammo and eve budget. To deal which such threats players are given access to The Old man Winter plasmid which freezes enemies so that they can be shattered and the Radar Range. The Radar Range is pretty much a microwave gun that allows you to cook enemies until they explode like hot dogs with their bodily shrapnel damaging enemies in the vicinity. It's a nifty toy but it doesn't find its way into Booker's hands until the last quarter of the episode. <br>\t<br>This first episode is brief at around three hours if you delve into every nook and cranny and unfortunately the only real plot point comes at the end. It's a bit of a doozy however and will make waiting for the next episode seem like an eternity. Burial at Sea is a beautifully constructed slice of content that is a much better representative of what players love about Bioshock than Clash in the Clouds. The new focus on resource management is an excellent gameplay tweak though it's a shame that the opening wasn't bolstered by new mechanics that capitalize on Booker's Private Eye skill set. For Bioshock fans, being reunited with classic Bioshock characters and being a part of a new chapter in Booker and Elizabeth's story will be worth the price of admission. Those who weren't exactly thrilled by Infinte however might want to wait and see how the second episode shakes out in order to decide whether this is a return to Rapture that they want to be a part It deepens the lore of Bioshock without ever disrupting or repeating what has already been said throughout the three games already, turning a city I thought I had known into a new and exciting place to explore once again.<br><br>At the same time it doesn't ignore its own plot, that of a different Booker and Elizabeth looking for a lost child who is somehow tied to both of them. It's a compelling look at Booker and Elizabeth as characters outside of the relationship they had in Infinite, still needing to reply on each other to get what they want, but the result being something far more like a mutual transaction than friendship. Their roles are essentially reversed, with Elizabeth guiding Booker who remains entirely confused yet compliant with her instructions. The ending however is what sets everything in its place, both tying Burial at Sea into Infinite, and providing a window into a side of the latter's narrative that I didn't know I was missing. It's startling and genius, and begs the question of just what else I hadn't considered about the series that presumably awaits me in episode two.<br><br>Burial at Sea also serves to rather drastically overhaul some of Infinite's combat mechanics, namely that of stealth. Stealth was never really an option or even something that existed in the series before, but here it's almost a requirement to survive, as you're given far fewer resources to combat enemies and thus must make use of melee and back stabs far more liberally.<br><br>I'd have probably grown tired of the change in a larger experience, but for a few hours it made for tense, refreshing encounters that forced a new play style on me without simplifying the combat. Being cut off from most of my weapons and vigors made me conscious of every bullet shot and chunk of salt used up, keeping me especially aware of where enemies were and how I could most efficiently take them all out.<br><br>Burial at Sea unfortunately doesn't have time to fully refine its stealth mechanics as I would have liked, but it has me eager to dig into the second half of the expansion, where I'm hoping these systems having been introduced can then be better realized and more deeply tied into the heart of combat.
