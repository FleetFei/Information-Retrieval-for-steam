game with solid gameplay which sometimes gets ridiculous with its use of physics, but it does force you out of stealth and into combat later on. Still, there's just an insane number of hilarious ways to kill things.<br><br>Dark Messiah of Might &amp; Magic is a first-person action game with some RPG elements developed by Arkane Studios and Floodgate Entertainment.<br><br>In the game, you play as Sareth, the underling of a mage, in a quest to find a powerful item. The plot is pretty bad, with the only remotely funny thing about it being that there are two women constantly stroking your ego on proportions that the Half-Life 2 devs wish they had achieved. It's so much that I actually found it funny unlike Half-Life 2 which was plain annoying.<br><br>Anyway, there are 10 levels, all mostly linear, though with some wide open spaces throughout. The game provides you with an extensive assortment of tools with which to murder you enemies (ghosting is also possible but not in the entirety of the game, as there are too many well-lit areas where you can't hide.) There's physics, weapons and magic. &quot;Wait, physics?&quot; Yes. Dark Messiah uses and abuses the Source Engine with weakly-supported structures whose weak supports can usually be brought down by sword, arrow or fireball. You also have a mighty kick which can be used against high drops and spikes to instantly kill enemies. Most objects are also grabbable and therefore throwable. You have a freeze spell and bow which when used on the ground can create a surface where enemies will slip and maybe fall to their deaths.<br><br>By now some people already realized the amount of insanely entertaining \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 you can do in this game and are playing it. Have fun!<br><br>Apart from that, there's a great melee combat system. The direction of your movement affects the type of attack you can power up, and upgrading the Close Combat skill allows for charges, jump attacks and whirlwinds, bringing your number of attacks per weapon to seven. There's also an adrenaline meter which when full will mean your next attack is slow-mo and instantly kills and dismembers\/beheads the enemy. The adrenaline meter also has special effects on each spell. You've also got bows, which can usually instantly kill with a well-placed headshot out of hiding (though the hitboxes are somewhat clunky), and even sometimes nail and enemy to the wall, which is pretty fun. There's also a rope bow like in the first two Thief games, which makes vertical exploration pretty fun. Weapons beyond the basic usually have skill requirements whch restrict you from equipping them.<br><br>The aforementioned skill system is divided into Combat, Magic and Misc skills. You get skill points for completing objectives, which means you don't have to kill everyone for an optimal character. The amount of skill points lets you max out a skill tree and then more, though it's cooler to make a jack-of-all-trades kind of character. I started with a sneaky assassin (Archery and Stealth), and then started building more into open combat (Close Combat and Strength.) I managed to get through some areas undetected with wall-nailing headshots out of darkness, which was pretty cool. As for stealth, there is a meter indicating how well you are hidden but the parameters for being seen are never explained and sometimes it seems you're invisible in pretty light spots, which is strange.<br><br>As fun as the game is, by chapter 9 I was already done with all the fun. I did finish the game but the repetitiveness of it was pretty evident, and the fact that chapter 9 does throw a lot of the same enemy at you at once, making it pretty hard to get through it without anything but straight close combat doesn't help. There's also not a huge variety of enemies in game, and by about half of the game you've seen all of them, even though that's compensated by the variety in killing methods that you have.<br><br>Overall Dark Messiah is a pretty fun and silly game, and prefectly replayable if you don't feel you got enough on the first run.\t\t\t\t\t<div
