class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you enjoy playing older-style pinball games - ones that are focused purely on scoring loads of points with a minimum of gimmicks and razzle-dazzle - you aren't going to find a better way to play than Zaccaria Pinball. While the more famous Pinball Arcade is host to all the licensed, fancy, crazy tables most people remember, Zaccaria's games are from the generation before that. Gameplay is fast, simple, and addicting. Drains are extremely frequent and sometimes seem downright unfair, due to how pinball games were designed in the 70s, but the brief nature of the games means it's very easy to get into a game and make a quick attempt at a high score, as opposed to the lengthy sessions you'd need to play a more modern machine. If you can stay in play on a Zaccaria machine for five minutes on three balls, you're doing well.<br><br>What really makes this game worth my recommendation is the work that has been put into making it replayable. Quality of Life features are everywhere - a quick and easy restart, ways to sort the tables, the ability to try any table before you buy it, constant bugfixes and updates - I've been playing for less than a month and Magic Pixel has added two new game modes and two new tables in that time. Every game has TWELVE leaderboards, and with over forty games in the collection, that means you have over 480 leaderboards to compete on. The games themselves are hard and unforgiving, yes, but Magic Pixel has done everything possible to make them presentable to the modern player without actually changing them into something they aren't.<br><br>If you play the free version (which includes Time Machine and free trials for all other tables except unlockables) and you enjoy it, I highly recommend the Gold Membership Bundle. It works out to just over a dollar per table, with the ability to unlock more tables, and is definitely the best value. The &quot;remake&quot; tables, sold separately from the bundles, attempt to bring Zaccaria into the modern era, which is good if you like both kinds of pinball and TPA and Zen aren't doing it for you, but are separate purchases. I can't comment on the VR DLC, because I don't use VR.<br><br>In short:<br>+ Free trial is generous and, as long as you don't touch the options, never gets pushy<br>+ Beautiful graphics with faithfully, lovingly reproduced tables<br>+ Fun, quick gameplay you can quickly restart if you mess up or get screwed over<br>+ Tons of modes to play and leaderboards to compete on to increase replay value<br>+ Updated frequently to fix bugs and add new content by developers who care (unlike Pinball Arcade which is ignoring months-old bug reports)<br>+ Some of that new content is added for free, such as the &quot;Story Mode&quot; that went live in March 2017.<br>+ Achievements actually mean something! You can unlock free tables by getting lots of achievements.<br>- Having to pay for options is a little silly<br>- The tables themselves tend to be &quot;drain monsters&quot; and some drains can be hard to swallow, so this game isn't for everyone<br>- Unlike PBA, no background information is given about the games besides their original release date, even in the &quot;Story Mode&quot;, which is more of an endurance challenge, not a story. This absence strongly changes the dynamic of the game, requiring the ball to traverse lanes far more times (for the average player), requiring more aiming skill and a different strategy. But overall, older tables will generally require increased skill.<br><br>One aspect of the increased skill, as well as the lack of control over lane lights, is an increased benefit to nudging. This player is required by circumstance to play one-handed, with a controller. As it is far more difficult to accomplish substantially more actions with what amounts to just two fingers, I am unable to specifically comment on the effect, need, use, or advantages to nudging in Zaccaria Pinball. Others will be more qualified, and no doubt will be anxious to help. It should be safe to say that nudging is substantially more crucial in Zaccaria Pinball than in newer tables, or tables that originated in digital form.<br><br>Sound Effects<br>The original Zaccaria Pinball tables came at a time when sound was finding a more significant place in pinball. As such, even ZP tables that might be expected to have great sound, such as circus or military themed tables, may have almost no sound at all. And the several tables that bravely use voice acting may present largely unintelligible phrases. But a few of the later tables really do have great sound effects, and considering historical context, weak sound should not be considered a negative.<br><br>The Dreaded Outlane<br>Now for Zaccaria Pinball\u2019s dark little secret. Numerous tables present a strong argument of having been specifically designed to feed balls into outlanes. Many, or most (or all) players will have games where every ball leaves through an outlane, never having been touched by flipper. And they will be on the same tables. (This reviewer had one table quickly direct nine out of nine balls to outlanes during testing. But this extreme case may certainly be an anomaly.) Seeing the ball \u201cladder\u201d up the slingshots into an outlane is one of the most distressing actions in pinball\u2026and in Zaccaria Pinball is far from rare on several tables. Experienced players will see all the design elements, such as lowered outlane barriers and slingshot strength and angles, which encourage gravity to consistently act contrary to the design of a long-play table (and the wishes of many players.) Safe to say, identifying those tables by name is neither prudent nor necessary, but they are the minority and they do not attempt to hide. Please keep in mind that these tables were designed to be coin operated, so games of extreme length were not always in the best financial interest.<br><br><br>Cameras<br>While Zaccaria Pinball has wonderful control over camera views, there may still be some issues to be considered. This reviewer has been playing computer pinball in portrait mode exclusively for well over a year, and Zaccaria Pinball makes this incredibly easy. Simply set your monitor to portrait view through Windows, Zaccaria Pinball takes care of everything else instantly and automatically. But what this player found on two i7, Windows 10 laptops is that by using any of the seven default ZP camera views, left and right edges of all table were lost to the degree that outlanes weren\u2019t even a trace on the screen, much less the outlane flippers that can be so vital. But the problem may be the solution: camera options.<br><br>It appears that Cameras 1 -3 may be fixed, but 4 \u2013 7 are easily modifiable. Since option changes appear onscreen as they\u2019re chosen, select the camera in that four camera range that shows the view you prefer. Then change the FOV (field of view) setting to something around 60%. This should allow you to see the entire table, and further tweaking may provide even better results. (This basically emulates Fly Cam, which, if one might think adequate to SHOW the table, would be adequate to PLAY the table.)<br><br><br>Options!!!\t<br>It may be safe to unequivocally state that there has NEVER been a pinball game that allowed the player such a remarkable degree of control. Changes can also be made to physics, but unless you have selected USER as game mode, they will have no effect. USER mode has its own leaderboard, but this player\u2019s single experience was that the leaderboard wasn\u2019t populated. Some aspects of the physics of the tables are being adjusted on an ongoing basis during early access, but the game is already in a state that I would say is virtually complete. <br><br>The tables themselves are authentic tables manufactured by Zaccaria, an Italian company which existed between 1974 and 1988. On first sight they don't match up visually to later tables, particularly those of U.S. manufacturers who often market their tables with tie-ins from Hollywood movies. However, the Zaccaria tables do have a beauty of their own. Their characteristic style is representative of the popular cartoon art of the era in which they were made. Being quite old, the tables have fewer toys, ramps and rails on the play surface than you would typically find on tables from the late 1980s onwards. They also lack the l.e.d. display fields and more complicated rule-sets typical of later tables.<br><br>For anyone too young to have encountered these tables for real, Zaccaria Pinball (the video game) is offering the experience of playing historically accurate and still highly enjoyable pinball tables on your computer (or other device), which is practically the only way these days.<br><br>In terms of playability, these older types of tables can be less forgiving, and due to the simpler rule-sets, the games aren't as engrossing as many you find in Farsight Studios' The Pinball Arcade. However, you have exactly the same issue with the older electro-mechanical and early solid-state tables in The Pinball Arcade.<br><br>Pixel Magic, the makers of Zaccaria Pinball (the video game) have done a superb job of rendering the game and it looks amazing. The only exception is the back glass, which looks a bit strange close up. The under-surface lighting, reflections from the table glass, and even optional fingerprints and scratches help to create the illusion of playing something well used and real. <br><br>While it might not provide quite the buzz of some other contemporary pinball video games, it achieves a higher degree of authenticity and is still still very enjoyable.
