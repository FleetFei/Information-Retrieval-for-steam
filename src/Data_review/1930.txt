It keeps you playing for its advantages regardless its flaws. Still, two worlds is a game that I start and did not abandon nor I will anytime soon. This game has something strangely addictive. For example I abandon dark souls about mid game while been an excellent game but I am now mid-game at two worlds and I hardy spent this little time to write this, right now I prefer to enter the game and continue.<br>To describe this game very briefly, it is like diablo on levelling and items, like oblivion on world environment feeling and like divinity 2 on movement and combat. The first time I play I think there were major flaws and bad designs but with a second and third closer careful looks I understand that I was wrong. Once you understand how things work it is where you really get addicted. I am sure that most negative reviews comes from hasty gamers. While levelling you get 5 attribute points and one or two skill points, usually one. Items have a special attribute called \u2018class\u2019 which is usually one. If you have a short sword for example and find another one you can drag and drop it one another and you will now have a class 2 short sword which of course is a little better, doing more damage in that case. This is a very good thing and very useful, as it allows you to use trivial equipment to avoid buying everything from the store at every couple of levels or so, or live and die by drop chance alone. For example I used a Grom Shield that happened to be a very common item groms carry and drop so I end up finding about 30 or so, effectively had a class 30 simple grom shield with a parry number high enough to keep me up to level 10 or so. Then there were better shields to buy and enemies no longer often carry that shield, so it was time to change. Spell cards also stack. Add a second and third and so on card of the same spell and the spell becomes stronger, requiring more mana however. Finally gems can be placed on a weapon for extra damage and you can put as many as you like as long as they are of the same type, e.g. rubies that add fire damage.<br>Money and trading: Surprisingly money are always needed here, and the game offers a fair amount of money. The trading is active to this game. You always earn cash and you always have reasons to spent, and not on consumables exclusively. I\u2019d rather say you may spent for making your hero more powerful. Enemies either drop inventory with a fair amount of gold and random items or a single standard automatic pickup, like the wolf drops a wolf heart which is an ingredient.  Anything may drop but specific items have better sources to be harvested and animals and beast always drop standard ingredient item instead of inventory. All except single pickups are level depended, chests, enemy inventories and shops. You are bored looking for ingredients that increase your stats permanently? No problem, pay some attention to mage shops and after a certain level you definitely come across to buy.<br>Enemies: Good variety of enemies, large size enemies are really deadly and not level depended, leveled enemies are logical like bandits; they got stronger but not as much as you.<br>Classes: The 3 archetypes work well, fighter, mage and archer\/thief that is. Hybrids also work well. There is depth on each single archetype, fighting style and weapons for fighters, magic schools for mages and lots of choices to master for an archer\/thief.<br>The story is interesting, the world is very good at least. The soundtrack is almost perfect.<br><br>Now the bad stuff: <br>The back-jump. Honestly I hated that. In my opinion it ruins melee. I do not even assigned it to a button. The game is much better without. Again, my opinion.<br>The dialogues. Not sure what English dialect that is but it is strange until you get used to it. Also some times are almost childish, some other times are very mature. Your hero speaks quite well though.<br>The NPC characters. Quite average.<br>The spells. They are lots and great and come in form of cards that stack and extra spell booster cards (more damage, less mana etch).
