class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHi!<br>Today I finished Odyssey. My overall impression is very positive. If you are a fan of Myst-like games. this is a 'must have'. Nevertheless, some remarks can be made.<br>First: the overall look. Graphics are very good. Not the best you can get, but at least 8 out of 10. For example, a few (minor) things that could be better is, that the trees and the grasses are 'shaking' in the wind instead of waving, and the way the waves break on the beaches, looks a bit unnatural, while the other water movements are done very well.  Background sounds are good, the music is nice too, although both are not spectacular. I would not buy a soundtrack of this game.<br>Although the opinions differ, movements are very smooth, and it is easilly to move everywhere while using the combination of the arrows on your keyboard (move) together with you mouse (direction of move).  Using the spacebar for opening\/closing your invertory works very nice too. So for gameplay I give 9 out of 10.<br>Secondly: the game itself. As I said it is a Myst-like game. Fans of this genre must be aware that the journal you get during play, gives a lot of background information, but also hints about the puzzles you have to solve. These hints are quite clear (at many occasions too clear), which makes them easy to solve. It would be more preferred if there sould be a hiding function for these hints, so you can read the background, without getting too much spoilers about these puzzles, already at one. But that makes this a very suitable game for beginners, who don't like (or can solve) hard puzzles.<br>Very nice is the way the physics of the universe, the gravitation and the pendules is worked out. This game must be a feast for teachers who wants to explain this in original and pleasant lessons to their students.<br>A negative point is the abrupt game end. It isn't a real end, it is only the end of a chapter. So you can say this is an unfinished game. Buying this, you will get chapter 1, 2  and 3. It seemed the creators will make chapter 4, 5 and 6 as well, to make this a whole game, with a clear, satisfactory end (finale). <br>Having said that all, this first part looks promising. When you are experienced, it will give you about 8 hours of gameplay. I was impressed by their initial video and the first puzzle was simple but inviting.  This games aims to weave a constructivist approach to learning with a myst-like puzzle-based game play.    The game traces the development of the heliocentric model from Aristotle's geocentric one by encouraging the player to follow in the foot steps of historical scientists.  This is mostly accomplished as the player retraces the discoveries a 13-year old girl named Kai has recorded in her journal.  You acquire new pages from the journal as you solve puzzles in the game.  The journal is a somewhat lengthy treatise as seen through the eyes of a young adult first discovering these historical theories.  The journal is both the games greatest strength and it's weakness.  It's very well written explanations of some complex ideas will be informative to adults as well.  The journal is reasoned and rigorous.  The puzzles in the game also serve as visualizations of the concepts described in the journal.  I was extremely impressed by the game--but I did sometimes find it difficult to follow and a bit tedious.  It would be neat if the creators could figure out a way to communicate more of the concepts and ideas  about the science through the puzzles and in the game itself rather than relying so heavily on the journal.  But again the journal is very well written and truly conveys a the sense of discovery and exploration that a good teacher might also be able to invoke.  <br><br>I've been playing this with my 5 year old son.  Many of the ideas are beyond his ability to process right now--but he's surprisinging willing to sit through my reading to him lenghty, rather dense passages from the Journal.  We periodically share some special time together where he gets to choose what we do--and the last 2 or 3 times, this has been his choice.<br><br>This game invites the player to engage in the PROCESS of discovering the science--rather than just trying to teach the end result.  This constructivst approach is both compelling and, I believe, more meaningful.
