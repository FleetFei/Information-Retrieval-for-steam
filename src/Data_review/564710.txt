numbers grow bigger, and want to see just how far you can push a team, then you might enjoy Soda Dungeon.<\/i><br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">General Gameplay:<\/div>You'll start off with only the ability to hire the most basic of adventurers, and as you send parties into the dungeon and defeat monsters, you'll gather loot and gold which you can spend on obtaining the ability to hire better adventurers and outfit them with better gear. This will enable you to push further into the dungeon, where you'll be able to acquire more gold and better items, allowing you to purchase the ability to hire even better adventurers and equip them with even better gear so you can push further into the dungeon, etc, etc...<br><br>Soda Dungeon is extremely repetitive, and you may end up getting bored of the combat itself. Fortunately, the game has an 'Auto-fight' feature where, once activated, the AI will take over control of all party members for you. At this point, the game could be described as a 'turn-based idle game', where you simply wait for the party to reach the limit of their endurance before collecting the loot after all party members have been defeated.<br><br>The AI doesn't do a bad job at controlling the party either; in fact, it's quite good at what it does. Although not perfect, the AI will use abilities and items in a generally intelligent and efficient manner, and for the most part, can be left to its own devices while you watch or minimise the game and do something else. After the first five or so hours of gameplay, I ended up hiring, equipping, and organising my party, sending them to the dungeon, then letting the AI take over from there. I imagine this is what you might end up doing as well, unless you really, really insist upon the idea of grinding out hundreds of levels of dungeon manually.<br><br>On another note, the aforementioned 'Auto-fight' feature can be toggled on and off at any time, so if you see a situation where human intervention is required, you can simply turn this off and control your party members for as long as you need to, then set them back to auto once you've done whatever you intended to do.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Level Design:<\/div>Excluding the boss floor, which occurs every 100 floors, there are 5 areas. Each of these areas lasts for 20 floors and come with their own unique backgrounds, music, and monsters. Changes in levels are announced through text, while changes in areas are announced both via text and changes in background and music.<br><br>Once the boss on the hundredth floor is defeated, the dungeon will reset and the enemies will become tougher and deal more damage.<br><br>At random points throughout the dungeon, you'll come across a 'fork in the road', giving you a choice between entering one of two doors, where one door will lead to a trap that will harm your party, and another door will lead to a room with some kind of reward or benefit. Alternatively, you can choose to ignore these doors and continue on your present path. Dungeon levels are otherwise cleared in a rather linear fashion - after clearing one floor, you'll automatically move on up to the next. This leads us to our next point. Just how far does this dungeon go? At what point do you 'finish'?<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Replayability:<\/div>The point of the game is to fight far enough into the dungeon that you can meet and fight the boss. After the boss is defeated, you can then advance the game and proceed to the next dimension, where the next boss fight is 100 levels higher than the boss you fought in the previous dimension. Whenever you advance to a new dimension, you lose all gold and equipment in your inventory, as well as the ability to hire all the adventurers you previously unlocked. However, you'll be given two relics: one affects and benefits all party members, and the other specifically enhances one type of adventurer. These relics are permanent and continue to provide boosts to party members' statistics even if you advance to another dimension.<br><br>Some relics can only be upgraded a limited number of times, but most of them do not seem to have a limit, so it's possible to keep increasing your party members' attributes as long as you can afford it.<br><br>There are a limited number of dungeon bosses and dimensions to advance to, so you'll eventually end up in the final dimension. From here, you can simply keep on pushing higher and higher into the dungeon until you're satisfied that you no longer want to continue.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Loot:<\/div>While there's a decent variety of gear to find, I feel the loot system is one of the game's weaknesses. You'll start off finding level 1 gear, and as you reach higher levels, eventually, level 2 gear will drop. An item of the next level is only marginally better than its lower-level counterpart, but requires that the player has reached much further into the dungeon in order to have a chance to drop (this is even more so the case with rare and legendary items). Additionally, some items have far worse statistics than other items that are of much lower level. A level 10 iron shield has worse statistics than a level 5 spike shield, but is far more difficult to obtain. I found the tiered weapon system uninteresting, and the power differences between weapon tiers was underwhelming.<br><br>There are also rare, legendary, and mythical items, all with their own special attributes and effects. They gave me some satisfaction in finding\/farming them, and didn't render all the non-rare items completely obsolete, though I still would have preferred to see more (new) items with their own characteristics, instead of simply slightly buffed versions of the usual junk you found when you were first starting out.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Microtransactions:<\/div>There are microtransactions in this game, but they're not at all required. Soda Dungeon seems to operate on a pay-to-save-time model, where it's possible to obtain things simply by playing the game, but would take a lengthy investment of time to do so without spending any money.<br><br>Some of the things that can be unlocked\/obtained through payment are:<br>- Special characters<br>- Relic upgrade levels<br>- Various upgrade\/ability levels<br><br><u><b>The Good:<\/b><\/u><br>+ Competent AI allows you to play the game manually or treat it as an idle game<br>+ Good entertainment value for the price of admission<br>+ The story\/dialogue is mildly interesting\/amusing<br>+ Lots of quality-of-life features (warp to specific dungeon levels already passed, automatically sell excess items, save outfits for specific classes, etc)<br><br><u><b>The Bad:<\/b><\/u><br>- Tiered loot system is uninteresting\/unrewarding<br>- Repetitive - you'll be fighting the same monsters and visiting the same areas over and over for (mostly) the same loot, only of (slightly) higher quality\/statistics<br><br><u><b>Conclusion:<\/b><\/u><br>For me, the enjoyment and satisfaction in playing this game came from watching my party of adventurers grow in power, from barely being able to clear the first 50-100 floors, to eventually powering through hundreds of floors in one run. Seeing a rare or legendary item drop from a monster or chest also gave a sense of reward, as well as doing other silly things like equipping my party all with gold-boosting gear, then watching as enemies and chests burst into ridiculous fountains of gold.<br><br>I'd recommend this game to people who like hunting\/farming loot and grinding dungeon levels, but not to those who are looking for deep or engaging battles. There is some variety, experimentation, and depth to be found in gear setups, party compositions, and skill use, but the fixed order in which party members have to move, as well as the lack of synergy or team abilities among the different types of adventurers means you'll probably outfit and arrange your party, pick a floor to warp them to, then leave them to fend for themselves.\t\t\t\t\t<div
