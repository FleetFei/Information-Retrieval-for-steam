Physical progression of the main character (MC) through his mysterious world filled with puzzles is dictated through interactions and manipulations of the environments themselves, providing only indirect control of the MC (you can not directly move him). This describes the game's ambitious &quot;fourth-person puzzling adventure&quot;, and although Pavilion created a novel experience, it is encumbered with weak execution, ultimately denying potential for true greatness.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">A Little Too Puzzling<\/div><br>As a game whose goal was to tell a story using only visual cues and relying on puzzles as its only form of gameplay, Pavilion does not manage to perform exceedingly well with either aspect. The environment and progression is so abstract and surrealistic that it can be very hard to discern what is going on. Throw in an unresolved ending to be revealed in Chapter 2, as a separate DLC to be released in 2017, and ambivalence towards continuation is what you\u2019ll get. Even though I was able to see the the developers' train of thought at the end of Chapter 1, all I can really remember about the game are the baffling puzzle mechanics.<br><br>Extremely aggravating character pathing and speed puzzles that rely on precise timing ruin the novelty that Pavilion was trying to portray with its fourth-person concept. When you know what you have to do, but the character won't path the way you want, adding that subtle difficulty layer of figuring how to control the path doesn't provide any sort of challenge, just frustration. For example, most of the puzzles will have 2 bells in order to attract the character's attention. The idea behind this is that the player will have to ring certain bells in a timely, or specific sequence in order to attract the MC towards one of the ringing bells; however, there are moments where the MC makes a weird turn or moves away from the ringing bell. Add in step pads (with a timer) that require the MC to move in a certain path and the product is a fist-clenching experience. There are definitely times when the fourth-person puzzle solving manages to succeed in an innovative &quot;ah-hah&quot; sort of way, but those moments are very far and few between. A rather ordinary man at that. He has no super-powers, is scared of the dark and is also relatively unadventurous. The whole world is a mystery and even the gameplay can prove to be a bit of a conundrum. There is no text tutorial or beginning explanation. All you are given is a man, a gorgeously drawn environment and a cursor that is controlled via the keyboard. The cursor is there to help this man navigate his away around puzzling labyrinths.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">The Puzzles<\/div><br>Puzzles generally require you to create a pathway to an end goal such as a door, moving platform, archway or other such method of progression. To do this successfully you\u2019ll need to prompt the man to move by ringing bells. Each bell is connected with a symbol on the floor that the man will run to when the bell is chimed. Sometimes there will be obstacles between the man and the symbol on the floor that will require moving, other times there may be lights that need to be turned on. As I mentioned previously, this man is scared of the dark, so he won\u2019t run through areas that have no light.<br><br>The puzzles in Pavilion are really well thought out and are rarely too hard that you start to get frustrated. This is a good yet bad point depending on how you like your puzzles. Some people might find there is a lack of challenge that is instead replaced by long-winded requirements. Other people (like yours truly) will find the puzzles are quite satisfying to complete, especially when lateral thinking isn\u2019t your cup of tea.<br><br>A lot of the mechanics involved in solving the puzzles are very imaginative and well planned. One such example of this is when you come to a point where your cursor is \u201cupgraded\u201d and can start to reflect light off reflective surfaces like crystal formations. The light provided will allow the man who is afraid of the dark to proceed. Other small nuances like being able to smash your way through wooden planks with an elevator are quite entertaining.<br><br>Along with moving the environment to allow the man to proceed are smaller puzzles inside chests of drawers that activate doors etc. Also you\u2019ll need to make sure the man accesses some drawers in order to retrieve a key to progress.<br><br>So the gameplay keeps you hooked but does the story? To be honest, I had no idea why I was doing what I was doing. At one point near the beginning of the game you see a woman in white running around in an area you can\u2019t access. Who this is, is a mystery. But to be honest I didn\u2019t really care, I just wanted to get to the next puzzle so I could continue to test my brain! Of course there is a lot of mystery surrounding the story of the game, but that\u2019s completely intentional. Curiosity is a powerful thing.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">The Controls<\/div><br>Stepping away from mouse and keyboard controls, Pavilion takes us back to the days of only being able to control a game using the keyboard. Here\u2019s where a few people might kick up a stink. The cursor feels too slow when moving it around with your arrow keys, and sometimes when moving blocks around the fourth person plane, you can easily get confused as to which keys you should be pressing to get it to move in the direction you want.<br><br>However, personally I can see exactly why the game is controlled primarily via the keyboard. Often you will come across puzzles that give you a short time limit to activate the next required object. If you were able to move the cursor quickly with your mouse, the impact of needing to hurry would be lost. Controlling the cursor with only your keyboard adds that much more of a challenge to an already brain-bending game.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Aesthetic Charm<\/div><br>Each environment and it\u2019s accompanying objects are lovingly handcrafted and provide the bulk of the pleasure to be derived from the game. Of course the puzzles are the main hook, but when a game has graphics this beautiful, it certainly boosts the entire experience.<br><br>Sound-wise, I really didn\u2019t enjoy the background music or sound effects. I preferred to keep the game\u2019s volume down to a minimum whilst playing. Some puzzles will have you scratching your head for a while, so you can imagine that the music would become quite repetitive and frustrating.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Final Thoughts<\/div><br>It\u2019s always great to see unique indie games being released, and when they involve challenging puzzles and handsome graphics, it\u2019s definitely a plus. The current version of Pavilion contains the first of two chapters, the second chapter to be released in 2017. And for the price you pay, the length of the first chapter is actually quite satisfactory. Depending on how good you are at solving puzzles, the game could take anywhere from two and a half to five or more hours to finish.<br><br>Pavilion is a nice little game to add to your library if you\u2019re a puzzle fan, but if you\u2019re looking for an absorbing story, sadly you won\u2019t find it here.
