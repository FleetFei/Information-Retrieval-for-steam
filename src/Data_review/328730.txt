which included the original version as well.  Since the remastered version would be bigger and better, I decided to play this version first lest I'd not want to go backwards and ever play it.  After finishing all of the achievements, I can say that I do recommend playing this game.  The fact that they upgraded it and allow you to have both versions without any extra fees says a lot about the developers, who did a solid job making this game.  <br><br>The gameplay is basically an RPG version of a game like Boggle, where you have a field of letters to work with to spell out various words.  Unlike Boggle the letters don't have to be connected to each other at all.  Each letter has either a single dot, two dots, or three dots on it, which represents the difficulty in using that letter in a word, as well as how much damage it will deal to the enemy.  You go back and forth as they attack you and you hack away at them with what words you spell, with a basic rule of thumb being, the longer the word the more damage you deal.  As you play through the game you will buy upgrades for your character, equipable books that grant various benefits, and selectable weapons that grant a bonus to one aspect of your character.  <br><br>It's kind of an odd idea on face value, but it gives a bit more purpose and development to what can otherwise be a perpetual routine.  Developing your character and making them stronger gives you more of a reason to keep playing as opposed to an arcade style system where you'd just start all over again each time to earn the high score.  After a bit of a learning curve where you feel like you have to grind for a while, you figure out the right pace for replaying earlier levels at higher difficulties for more gems (currency) without having to keep replaying stages to make any progress.  <br><br>I think what I enjoyed most about the game as it is designed was the enemies who took damage with multipliers based on certain conditions, such as using letters from a specific part of the board or using certain letters.  It could be a bit annoying sometimes, especially if you weren't given the letter you needed to get the damage multiplier, but there was never a challenge that was too hard to overcome given a bit of grinding or a second attempt with better letter RNG.  For me it just made it so I had to think my moves out a bit more while still trying to get maximal damage, which is far more interesting then only scouring the board for the longest word possible each and every time.<br><br>One troubling area for me was how the game replenishes your letters as you spell out different words.  I wouldn't know whether this is true or not, as it would probably require looking at the programming, but it seemed like I got certain combinations of letters more frequently then not.  I'm no Scrabble pro, but my vocabulary isn't half bad and there were several times that the best words I could spell out were the same.  It took away a degree of replayability when the same combinations of letters and words I could spell kept repeating themselves, despite having 15 letters to work with.<br><br>A similar issue applies to the final achievement.  This would only apply to those who bother with achievements, but this game's final achievement was quite a grind to earn.  All you have to do is spell out 3,000 words, but by the time you've finished clearing all of the stages you still have 1,000+ words to work on.  There is certainly some range in that aspect as some people might spell out smaller or larger words or have to grind more if they don't get upgrades that dole out more gems, but I doubt my experience was abnormal.  Before I had finished I had fully upgraded every single upgrade possible and was merely going through the motions to get the last achievement.  I don't know if the number of words would carry over if I started a new game on expert mode or not and I didn't want to find out the hard way.<br><br>After doing the last achievement it will be some time before I have an interest in going on to the remastered version of this game, but I'm sure I'll enjoy it when my interest returns. is a casual, turn-based RPG word game with light RPG elements. It\u2019s very similar to the Bookworm games from PopCap Games, but is harder and also has more content in it. That doesn\u2019t necessarily mean it\u2019s the better game between the two, but let\u2019s dig deeper and find out, shall we ?<\/i><br><br>You start the game by \u2018controlling\u2019 a grim reaper named Grimm. You don\u2019t actually control him directly; the game automatically moves the character forward if necessary. Later on you can unlock and control another grim reaper, a girl named Rose. Both characters use separate stats, so it\u2019s not just another design. They actually impact gameplay.<br>The game works with levels, and each level has additional, separate\/optional challenges that can be completed. There are 40 levels in total; technically 160 if you count all of the challenges, of course.<br><br>Small tutorials (tips &amp; hints might be a better description, though) are gradually introduced during gameplay. Most things speak for themselves, but there are a few things that are important. There are levels where you have to use a certain amount of letters (or avoid using a specific amount), use letters in corners, start words with a vowel, et cetera. These challenges are usually used in challenge levels and in boss levels. Normally you can just create the words that you want, as long as you have the letters for it. But these have to be in English, which might be a bit tricky for international users who hardly speak the language. Don\u2019t worry though, you can play the game just fine by creating smaller and easier words; it\u2019s just that it might take you a little longer. That\u2019s right, there\u2019s no time limit. Since it\u2019s turn-based there\u2019s no need to rush (unless it\u2019s a challenge). You can take your time, but once you submit your word you\u2019ll be immediately attacked by your opponent.<br>Word tiles will also change from time to time; there are poison tiles that will poison you (really !) if you use it in a word, and there are duplicator tiles that you duplicate certain letters (really !). There are even more of them, but I can\u2019t remember them all, especially with my terrible memory.<br><br>There are books that can be unlocked that might give you more health, or do more damage, but these have to be bought from the store with crystals. Crystals can be found by defeating enemies, opening chests and getting to the end of a level. Earning these takes a lot of time and you\u2019ll be frequently doing challenges and replaying levels in order to earn more of them. But you can also complete challenges. There are 70 in total and each one that you complete grants you 200 crystals.<br><br>More stuff can be bought from the store; there are weapons with different stats (one that heals you a little for each word that you submit or one that does more damage), and these can usually also be upgraded. You can also buy potions, better gear and books to improve your skills in combat, which is almost a must, because the game gets incredibly hard fast. But even failing levels will upgrade your equipped books so, again, you will still be able to beat them, it will just take a tad longer. <br>Still, it could have used a little more fine-tuning in this area, because I can\u2019t count the amount of times that I had to replay a level, or had to do a(n earlier) challenge in order to be able to buy something better from the store.<br>It\u2019s always wise to take a potion (or two) with you but it\u2019s even wiser to not use it if you\u2019re playing a new level for the first time.  I\u2019ve also spent a ton of crystals on potions that were eventually just wasted because the difficulty ramped up halfway through a level. It\u2019s unfortunately very unbalanced sometimes and that will no doubt impact people\u2019s experiences. The game relies heavily on using items and upgrading your gear, which makes sense, but for a casual game it doesn\u2019t seem to be all that casual on that front.<br><br>For a two-man company the game is extremely well polished. But it isn\u2019t hard to see that some aspects didn\u2019t get the same treatment as some of the other parts of the game. I\u2019m talking about the animations and the difficulty spikes here (which I already shortly addressed in the previous paragraph and won\u2019t get into any further).<br>The graphics all look sharp and detailed, and everything is nicely animated, except for the enemies. The enemies just stand still and when they attack you might see a flash or something but except for that nothing else is moving. I understand it would\u2019ve taken more time to animate all of the characters but it\u2019s slightly disappointing and certainly noticeable, especially when the rest of the game feels so polished.<br>Thankfully the user interface is clean and user friendly. There\u2019s also keyboard support so that you don\u2019t have to click on of the letters you want to use. A simple idea, but really clever and I\u2019m sure many people will appreciate it.<br>The music itself is retro themed, and up-tempo. I didn\u2019t really enjoy it just because of that, but that\u2019s personal and I wouldn\u2019t ever say that it\u2019s bad. It\u2019s just not my style. I did find the sounds good with selected tiles making noises, and a sound that can be heard whenever a word can be submitted.<br><br>The description of the game states that it will take at least 6 hours to finish the game and I personally think you can at least add several hours extra to that number. I think 10 hours, give or take, would be a good estimate to do most of the things. For a word game, that\u2019s pretty lengthy and, even more important, worth the money.<br><br><i>Letter Quest Grimm\u2019s Journey is a game that I very much enjoyed. There are plenty of word games out there, but nothing like this one or Bookworm Adventures. This is also one of the reasons I enjoyed it so much. This genre hasn\u2019t been oversaturated yet. Sure, it isn\u2019t as popular as RPGs or FPSs, but that doesn\u2019t make it any less fun. Who knew playing with words could be fun ? (well,\u2026 I did)<\/i><br><br><b>[Rating: 76\/100]<\/b><br><br>Small note: if you enjoy word games give Typing Of The Dead a try, too (that is, if you\u2019ve never heard of it).
