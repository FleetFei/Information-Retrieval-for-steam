review:<br><br>Edit: Thumbs up because it looks promising. (I'd hold off getting it until counting method issues are resolved, hopefully.)<br><br>Project seems promising. It includes basic tutorials for rules and scoring, a VS. AI mode and an Online multiplayer mode.<br><br>The AI is not particularly strong on any setting (Hard is when it starts playing slightly better moves, maximum tends to take a bit more time to play moves, but not a LOT of time.) So (at least at this stage) the AI is really meant for beginners. It would be nice to get a stronger one later down the line, but I'd rather see good multiplayer added than the focus be on AI.<br><br>The UI is polished and pretty. It seems simplistic, but in the same way that Go is &quot;simple&quot;, giving it a nice &quot;relaxed&quot;, almost elegant look.<br><br>A few notes:<br><br>At the current stage, it does NOT appear as though you can do the following:<br>*You cannot Select custom Komi values (7.5 is way too much on small boards! I usually play with 6.5 komi on 19x19 anyway...)<br>*You cannot Choose to play Black or White (It always made me black? Maybe the Host is always black?)<br>*You cannot Choose different scoring methods (Japanese and Chinese (area counting) being the most important of these. Are points in mutual life counted as points? I haven't encountered this yet so I'm not sure...)<br>*You cannot Choose different timing options for the game (e.g., Canadian Byo-yomi or Japanese Byo-yomi)<br><br>All of these will be vital things to add, in my opinion, to make the title complete and robust. The lack of scoring methods really stuck out to me, as requiring people to capture &quot;dead&quot; stones in their territory may teach them the value of filling in dame, or &quot;neutral&quot; points first, but not all scoring methods will allow you to play stones within your territory without penalty.<br><br>As a side note, I am not a fan of including &quot;two eyes&quot; in the tutorial. I believe needing &quot;two eyes&quot; is an emergent property of groups, which are not made until they are needed, so they should not be taught as required... Teaching the concept of &quot;two eyes&quot; being required for life has often made beginners I'm teaching\/facing over-concentrate, allowing me to gain the upper hand everywhere else on the board. This is a bad habit I have to try to knock out of them. &quot;Two eyes&quot; isn't a rule, it's just something you learn a group needs space to 'eventually' make in order to live 'if' required.<br><br><br>I look forward to seeing what this project becomes in the future!
