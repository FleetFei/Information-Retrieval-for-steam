class=\"postedDate\">Posted: October 3, 2015\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlaying Hektor is like walking through an M.C. Escher print if M.C. Escher was a sadistic psychopath. I wake up in the bowels of what is either an abandoned medical facility, prison, insane asylum, or some disturbing amalgamation of the three. The trick is that, as I wander through the dark, claustrophobic halls with nothing to light my way but the world's longest-lasting lighter and least battery efficient flashlight, Hektor folds the environment in on itself and opens it back up according to some mysterious logic.<br><br>It's subtle at first. The facility is pretty bare, with only a few boxes, desks, hospital beds, and maybe a sheet covering a human shape. It's easy to get lost, as it often is in first-person horror games, but it didn't take long before I walked around a corner only to find myself in the same room I just left behind me. Other times, I'd walk around in circles, not sure how to proceed, only to suddenly find a door that was previously locked wide open, or a familiar hallway leading me to a completely new location.<br><br>The best thing about Hektor's spatial trick is that it does it seamlessly. I could never tell when it opened doors, closed them, led me in circles, or allowed me to move on. At some point, I learned that, unlike every other first-person game I play, there's no point in trying to keep a mental map of my environment. Instead, it was best to just keep moving forward, quickly, and have faith that the rooms and hallways would align when Hektor was ready to let me proceed.<br><br>It's not the first time I've seen the geometry of a level rearrange itself radically when I turned my head in a first-person game. I remember it was quite a novelty in the first FEAR, and The Stanley Parable used similar tricks more recently in really effective ways. The problem with Hektor is that its tricky level design is the only interesting thing about it.<br><br>Cool as it is, the mind games wore thin long before I finished Hektor. There is nothing to do but move forward, choosing to go right or left arbitrarily. It's dark, so it was easier to keep moving if I had batteries for my flashlight, but Hektor is generous with them, so I rarely ran out. I suppose that the impossible, confusing level-design is meant to reflect the main character's mind, which through a vaguely told story, I understand is a screw or two loose. It's an interesting idea, but madness here is just relentless confusion. All Hektor knows how to do is rearrange hallways, so that's all it does.<br><br>There are creepy monsters, too, of course, but they don't add much. The first and more common, a zombie of sorts, did nothing but appear at random to make scary noises. With the caveat that I am a scaredy-cat, this did actually make my heart skip a beat almost every time. It's late at night, it's dark, there's scary music, and something came out at me out of nowhere and yelled &quot;boo!&quot; You got me Hektor, I jumped, congratulations at meeting the minimum requirements for being a horror game.<br><br>Startling as it is, this monster isn't interesting to interact with. It doesn't force me to rethink how I move through the environment as, say, Alien: Isolation's alien. Hektor morphed my vision at times, making the image sway, wobble, and twist, and it seems like this monster may have made that effect even more severe. At the beginning, I was told never to forget to take my pills, which are also scattered around the environment, but these didn't seem to have an effect on the wobbly vision, so it's not like I had to manage my pills supply in relation to monsters. Even after finishing Hektor, I have no idea what the pills did, and all the wobbliness did is make me a little queasy.<br><br>The other type of monster, a zombie with big sharp appendages jutting out of its back, just walked up to me clumsily and hit me three or four times before I died. I could run, but why should I? When I died, I just respawned at a random location, and since the environment is abstract, I didn\u2019t lose a measurable amount of progress. Actually, dying seemed to move me forward faster, and it also reset the wobble-vision, so I learned to embrace death, hugging monsters as soon as I saw them.<br><br>These monsters animate horribly, by the way. It was a comic relief to watch this supposedly nightmarish creature come to take my life in awkward, stiff little half steps. He was ugly too, and not in a disturbing way. He just seemed like a 3D model you'd get for free from the &quot;spooky&quot; section of the Unity 3D asset store.<br><br>Hektor doesn't look horrible overall. Most of the time I was looking at very dark, bare rooms, which is not a tall order technically or artistically. Some assets, and in particular one out-of-character poster that jokes about tweets, repeat way to often, but I don't mind that too much. Have you ever been in a hospital or a prison? They recycle art assets in real life too.<br><br>I'd tell you more about Hektor but there's nothing else to it. You're in a dark facility, you keep moving forward, and scary things happen. draws you in. Straight away from the title screen to the excellent intro sequence and the very first dead end you realise things in this world are not always what they seem.<br><br>I have played a lot of FPS horror adventures recently and Hektor definitely stands out as something a little different. The game is marketed as a psychological horror game and the debut title developed by the five-man team Rubycone. Set in an \u201cabandoned\u201d military black site, the player must escape this nightmarish facility while being pursued by monsters and battling their own psychosis brought about by apparent years of testing and abuse.<br><br>This brings about one of the game's key mechanics and that is the psychosis level. As you progress you must take pills you find to keep everything on the straight and narrow. Failure to find these pills sees the world shape and warp around you, a mechanic that reminded me slightly of the excellent Eternal Darkness on the GameCube but without the fourth wall breaking that came with it. The mechanic works well and adds to the atmosphere although I must admit it did make me feel a little bit nauseous when playing so those of you with motion sickness may have to be wary.<br><br>You start the game with a lighter progressing onto a torch which makes things a little easier as you navigate the corridors. The visuals in the game are great and while I totally appreciate the lack of lighting makes things scarier I can\u2019t help but feel I would have liked to have seen a little further than a few feet in front of my face.<br><br>Progression in Hektor is unusual. As you progress the game seems to always want you to advance at the same speed. At times I felt like the game was deliberately slowing me down by moving things around or making things less obvious. Equally though at other times the game would whisper to me how to progress in amongst the voices in my head or move the scenery around to the point where I seemed to automatically arrive at the new area. I guess it\u2019s actually kind of neat that I am starting to question my own sanity and if this was the case or if I was just imagining it but sometimes I did not feel the exploration side of the game was truly down to me (although there is an under 1 hour achievement so it must be doable).<br><br>Sections of the game are split up with dream like sequences. These add some contrast to the game and provide a fun distraction from the dank corridors of the facility. As you reach the later parts of the game you encounter the Predator, a creature not dissimilar to the Nemesis in Resident Evil whose sole purpose seems to be to hunt you down. Interacting with the Predator is frantic and tended to make my heart race much like a rollercoaster would rather than being creepy scary. I enjoyed this part of the game and feel this could have been included earlier than it was. There are other creatures that call out to the predator as you navigate the corridors but these provide simply the forewarning that the main beast is coming and I feel the inclusion of these was possibly unneeded.<br><br>The game\u2019s story is told through audio logs and much like the rest of the sound in the game these are excellent. While I am not usually a fan of this type of storytelling they are brilliantly voice acted and really make you care about the story and feel for the characters behind these notes. I would even go so far as to say these are the best audio logs I have come across.<br><br>The sound design is also wonderful and creepy and the mutterings and noises that accompany the main score are a key element of the games atmospheric success.<br><br>I finished the game in about 4 hours and while I enjoyed it I felt it could have been longer. The predator could have arrived earlier and been fleshed out a little more and I equally had a few unanswered questions related to the story.<br><br>While not perfect for a debut indie title Hektor is a fantastic horror game that I would easily recommend. At \u00a314.99 the game is a little on the expensive side and I could imagine a slight price drop helping to persuade fence sitters. Generally I don't expect much from those but play them for the laughs. But there were no laughs here, only chills. With generally decent visuals and a slow pace that builds creep factor well, it stands above the usual standard of &quot;it's dark and there are sounds&quot; games.  <br><br>Hektor keeps things simple, and at times comes close to walking simulator horror as you progress from one hallway to the next. Under the hood, the levels are randomly generated, and can change themselves around on the fly. There are puzzles to solve along the way, so it's not just a matter of walking forward. Sometimes you'll find yourself looping through the same few rooms, which means there's something for you to find in one of them. <br><br>Unlike other similar horror games though, there are actual threats in the twisting corridors. There's nothing to be done about them but avoid them, and they don't feel especially necessary, but they're there and they have some creepy sound design to go with them. There are no game overs, only setbacks, so they're not a huge obstacle. <br><br>The story isn't exactly easy to follow, but it drops enough hints to build a good sense of mystery and spookiness. There's some exposition through voiced notes, which feature surprisingly good voice acting for a cheaper title. You get plenty of pills to stave it off, but when I ignored it for a while I actually got motion sickness, which isn't common for me and games. Might wanna check out that option before you start. <br><br>By now, Steam has hundreds of games like this, to be sure. It's dark, your light is limited, you can only run and hide from the monsters. And so on. But where many of them are half-finished railroads from one jump scare to the next, Hektor isn't afraid to let you explore and take it at your own pace.
