class=\"bb_h1\">Gameplay<\/div><br>Kona puts you in the shoes of private investigator Carl Faubert in the backdrop of 70\u2019s northern Canada. He comes to Atamipek Lake to meet a local wealthy industrialist, William Hamilton, to be recruited as his personal bodyguard. But things go wrong right from the get go when he reaches the deserted town and starts seeing things he know shouldn\u2019t be there.<br><br>Kona is essentially a survival game with detective elements mixed in it. To survive, you need to build campfires and iron stoves while fending off wolves and moving from building to building and unlocking new areas. Kona maintains a creepy atmosphere throughout the length of the game while not being nerve wracking or going fully survival horror.<br><br> The narration is solid and is told with a pinch of dark humour which provides a breath of fresh air from the many other survival games with essentially no story whatsoever. The way the narrator tells the story is also right on the money and it weaves a strong narrative grip on you during the game.<br><br>Kona has you going from one place to another in pursuit of more clues, and more often than not these places will have places for you to warm up and supplies to take. The truck is essentially the only of way of travelling aside from foot and it acts as both as a container and as a protection from hungry packs of wolves. The game also has a slight tendency to lead you off roads and into the woods where you might spend a good lot of time trying to find your way through them. <br><br>You also keep a journal which helps you in keeping record of the investigations and making you feel like a proper detective. What differentiates Kona from the bunch of other survival games is the excellent atmosphere, strong narrative structure and the detective elements inserted and mixed brilliantly with the survival elements.<br><br>Not everything is good with Kona with the huge load times really breaking the immersion at times. The game also reuses a lot of its assets and you might start to feel a little bored from seeing the same environments time and time again. Even though it is out of early access, the game still has its fair share of bugs and while they\u2019re not game breaking, they can certainly distract you from your adventure. The enemy variety is nothing more than wolves and the combat leaves a lot to be desired. The game also has supplies thrown at every single place you are and that makes you feel like you don\u2019t need to scavenge for the materials. <br><br>And while these are minor nitpicks, they do impact your time with Kona and you might not see the brilliant atmospheric game which is hidden behind these issues. <br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Visuals<\/div><br>Reusing of assets aside, Kona is a brilliant looking game which provides an excellent backdrop for your adventure. The environments are lovingly crafted and the sense of isolation is present in every frame of the game. The animations of Carl himself are also very well done with subtle little touches to his regular movement and those really pull you in the game.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Sound<\/div><br>While the soundtrack is subtle considering the nature of the game, it compliments perfectly the atmosphere of the game and the sense of isolation through its sound. There are a few audio issues but they don\u2019t really distract you away from the game.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Overall<\/div><br>Kona is a superb survival game which is different from the stream of other games of the genre in a very unique way. It has a strong atmosphere, gripping narrative and wonderfully executed detective work. There are a few nagging issues which might pull you away from the game but if you\u2019re looking for something which provides you with a really tense atmosphere then Kona can be right up your alley.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Rating: 75\/100<\/div><br>This review was brought to you by <a class=\"bb_link\" href=\"https:\/\/steamcommunity.com\/groups\/SilasReviews\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noreferrer\" >Silas Reviews<\/a>.
