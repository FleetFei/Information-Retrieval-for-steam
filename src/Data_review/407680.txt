school, and wondering what to do with their lives. Put together, it is not the most successful or interesting visual novel, but it's heartwarming and carries some basic wisdom for anyone just starting their lives.<br><br>Our protagonist is Yuka Kishimoto. She is a high school student in Japan, who attends a school where her father works as a history teacher. She is an ordinary, well mannered, bright young woman who is at a total loss about her future. It is the last week of high school. These are the days that you won't be having ever again, right? The traditional Sunset Festival - which marks the end of high school for senior students - approaches, and it's up to you to figure out the ending of this chapter in life for Yuka. <br><br>As I mentioned before, <i>Last Days of Spring<\/i> isn't a great visual novel. It has some lazily snapped photograph backgrounds, no animation or voice acting; plus, the narrative has casual grammar errors. Sadly, there is no option for you to play this full screen either. The art style might be considered charming, but nope, nothing captivating. Gameplaywise, I wouldn't count any winnings either, considering it is a mostly kinetic visual novel. There are some choice opportunities, but I doubt that they'd change anything, since you can try each and every ending if you save properly at the last scene. Oh, and the save system gets whacky and fails to save from time to time.<br><br>So why am I giving this one an up vote? I find the approach of the game towards life, growing up and taking a place in society surprisingly realistic. I was surprised to see that there is more than one nonromantic - and quite logical - endings for you to choose at the end, and also a female option, which is quite rare in any of these games by itself.
