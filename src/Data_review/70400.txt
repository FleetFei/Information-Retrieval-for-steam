gist of it is that this is an extremely well polished game whose core mechanics--which is managing your item shop--are splendidly executed and introduced to the player smoothly, but this is all rounded out by the charming and endearing--optional--story, as you meet and interact with the townsfolk around the area. The characters are likeable and you learn more about them as you take more time out of the day to explore the city. It's almost like Final Fantasy 6 in the second half of the game.<br><br>All I can say is--this game is amazing. Honestly--the best way I can describe this game is a item shop tycoon\/dungeon crawler\/visual novel, all jumbled into one game. And yes, I said visual novel, which I will get into later.<br><br>So the primary premise of this game is that you run an item shop on a day to day basis, kind of like a Diner Dash-esque sort of game. From start to finish, the primary goal of the game is to make enough money to pay off your debt in weekly installments. That might seem boring, but in actuality the plethora of options that are given to you at every given moment make the game so much more than an item shop tycoon game, and it makes for such a fun and unique experience.<br><br>Regarding the item shop tycoon aspect of the game--it is spectacularly done. There is just so much that goes into managing your shop, that it just feels fresh while you progress through the game. What do you decide to put in front of your window, which will affect what kind and how many customers will come in? Should you decide to invest in expanding your shop? Should you buy vending machines for your shop, which allow for increased and automated sales at the expense of customer interaction?<br><br>But I think the absolute best thing they did with this game is the PROGRESSION that you learn all these things. Sooner or later, as you start to buy and sell more things, and decide what to do with your shop, you start to distinguish between the different kinds of customers that want to buy things. There is simply a consistent pattern--this kind of customer just won't pay that much for an item (and you will learn to hate those customers), and maybe this other kind of customer has a bit of coin left in their pocket, so you can maybe offer him a higher price. It's all very subtle and unstated, but learned by the player as you sell more items. It's honestly magical.<br><br>And what will be your main strategy to earn your keep? Should you go buy from the market and take advantage of item booms and lows? Or go dungeoneering into the depths to procure items and sell for 100% profit? Should you choose to go dungeoneering, you will learn that it's a very difficult balancing act of opportunity cost. Should I keep this item, or toss it to pick up this one? Should I use this healing item, or risk killing mobs to wait until I level up, which will heal me fully? Should I progress through the dungeon to use the day more efficiently, or not take the risk and use the checkpoint to go back home?<br><br>The day is divided into four slices. For each slice, you can choose to open your shop, go to the market, explore the city, or go dungeoneering, all consuming a certain number of slices. However, should you choose to go to the market, exploring the city will not take up any additional time. It's an ingenious way to make the player able to enjoy the story-side of the game. When you explore the city, you will interact with the townsfolk, which will bring up a short scene with your characters and their friends. As you take the time to explore the city more and more, you realize that each character has a backstory to them, and they develop more and more. It's one of the things I enjoyed most about this game.<br><br>The only gripe that I may have about this game is that the dungeoneering is actually very difficult. The controls are tight and fair, but some of the bosses spike in difficulty and you may easily die if you are not prepared for them.
