fallen sloppy dead<br>And the White Knight is talking backwards<br>And the Red Queen's off with her head...*<\/b><br><br>There comes another marvelous gem for which it is tremendously hard to write a review and do justice completely without presenting spoilers. <i>The Bottom of the Well<\/i> is the narrative of a series of staggering experiences concerning a girl named Alice - or many Alices in their different incarnations nonetheless - and her dream of an apocalyptic end. Here, we will participate in her dream many, many times to reach countless endings to try and discover a meaning to it.<br><br>The narrative opens with a basic chat screen where Alice talks to her friend M. Hatter and tells him of a disturbingly lucid dream that she has seen the other day. In this unsettling dream, the city is under the threat of getting hit by a series of nuclear missiles due to unknown reasons. The estimation indicates that she'll have a bit more than half an hour to find some kind of protective measure. Here, we become a witness to the before, present and after of a possible nuclear attack and try to lead Alice to some kind of safety while making choices of survival, logic, ethics to reach an ending. We fight against environment, we fight against ourselves; but mostly we fight against time.<br><br>Before each playthrough, we are presented the opportunity to choose Alice's stats enrolled into different abilities - consisting of survival, resources, fitness, social abilities, career and dating - to arrange a different incarnation, an alternate dream version of the actual Alice. The combination of different stats change the storyline and starting position for Alice, making different story paths to follow available. In each playthrough different choices and Alice's abilities - and also the exact timing that we chose to take the action during narrative - present countless different combinations, thus different story progressions for Alice. Mostly she dies, sometimes she survives. Yet there is always the reluctant feeling that something's missing is present. As if, that wasn't the way the dream was intended to end... There we go back, start a new game to craft a new Alice and try a new path to follow in hopes of maybe discovering what that was about this time.<br><br>We participate in Alice's narrative to make her choices and affect the happenings of her dream past in a frame tale fashion. Alice's narrative of her experience is dreadfully realistic, actualized through an elaborate vocabulary and shockingly pinpoint descriptions. It is confusingly easy to immerse into the narrative experience of Alice, finding yourself trying different combinations of stats and choices endlessly, nearly always discovering some new outcome. The main display consists of nothing much aside some beautiful and appropriately forlorn artwork and the ominous soundtrack lingering in the background to enhance the narrative towards its triumph.<br><br>At this point, it should be no secret that the game is inspired from Lewis Carroll's <i>Alice in Wonderland<\/i> yet it manages to craft the symbolic personifications into a genuine setting of its own; way more realistic and disturbing that Lewis Carroll ever intended them to be so. Aside all of its narrative glory and ever changing gameplay; it is an unbelievable fact that the game is a freeware. Can you even believe it? I'll go ahead and urge you to try this uncanny experience.
