As you progress through the game with the starting deck, you get to unlock more cards that build up equipment, gold, stats, blessings (buffs and other bonuses), curses (debuffs and other penalites), and different story-driven encounters. This helps as you grow more powerful through the game as you play randomly generated matches from your deck (with cards added by the Dealer) and each culminating in a mini-boss fight.<br><br>A mini-boss is a stronger version of an ordinary mob with a unique and deadly uninterruptible attack that will be spammed with gusto. If you see them preparing to launch one, your only defense is to get away.<br><br>During a match you will have any number of encounters that throw you into a succeed\/fail situation, a choice-making situation, a pit stop to buy\/sell stuff, or a combat situation. Combat has all the basic Arkham moves: Strike, Counter, Stun, and Dodge. There's also a combo meter that improves your damage if you can keep chaining hits. If you've equipped a weapon that has a magical power it will have unlimited uses + cooldown. You can also have an artifact equipped with limited uses + cooldown (artifact vanishes after last charge is spent).<br><br>if you run out of health for any reason (damage from combat, starvation or a failure on a random event) you have to start the entire match over again, but any new unlocked cards are always added to your permanent deck. A match contains 3-5 levels made up of 6-12 randomly drawn encounter cards per level. The random layout of the cards at any level determine if you have to proceed through all of them or get to pick your path while searching for the exit to the next level. Also, you start every match with basic equipment. You <i>only<\/i> get your upgraded equipment via card draw awards or having enough money to buy an item from a shop. This means it's entirely possible to reach the end of a match and face off against the mini-boss with only starting armor\/shield\/weapon. There are no guarantees. You can even lose your starting items along the way from failed encounters and be forced to use light armour, a rusty axe, and\/or no shield.<br><br>Despite the set-up sounding a bit tilted against you, it actually tends to balance out pretty well.<br><br>Well, most of the time. Then you get into the clunky stuff:<br><br>Unlike the Batman games, you have no camera control. Therefore in a 3D space certain maps will have blocked lines of sight due to poor camera placement -- either you won't be able to see enemies or you won't be able to see your own character. While most combat scenarios don't suffer too much from this, it can cause serious health loss when fighting large numbers, especially if a mini-boss is thrown into the mix. Imagine playing an Arkham game where a building suddenly blocks your view of the combat and only resolves if you blindly roll some direction until the camera clears the building. The same thing can happen with a ton of mobs in the foreground, so being near the back of the map is almost never a good idea. Healing in combat is tied to a handful of items and even then it's all passive (except for one weapon's magic ability) and almost never enough to make a difference. So there is no failsafe against taking hits due to camera issues -- the only rule of combat is &quot;don't get hit&quot;.<br><br>This alone wouldn't cause me to dislike the game. In fact I was having a blast despite this. Usefulness depends on what you chose to put in your play deck.<\/li><\/ul><br>The disadvantages are:<br><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li> You start with two curses. One removes any blessings and prevents you from acquiring others. The other adds a curse card every time you move to the next level in the match.<br><\/li><li> The Dealer's pain cards are significantly shifted toward removing health permanently, lowering your survivability.<br><\/li><li> Most combat encounters no longer drop loot, meaning fewer chances to snag equipment upgrades or money to buy them.<br><\/li><li> Increased chance to fail in succeed\/fail encounters. Some choice scenarios also get worse.  For instance, there is an encounter card where a wounded knight lies on the side of the road with the option to help him or ignore him. Under normal circumstances you could ignore him and nothing will happen, but in this match if you ignore him you'll draw a curse card and sometimes you won't get the option to help, he'll just attack.<br><\/li><li> More pain cards are drawn for failure and huge failures.<\/li><\/ul><br>The entire match leads up to the final combat against the Dealer. But you don't fight him. Instead he summons four waves of enemies each containing <b>THREE<\/b> mini-bosses you fought before and sometimes a few adds. You'll have at least 5 curses unless you spent money to remove some, no blessings, most likely a 10-25% health penalty, very possibly not at full health when combat starts, no healing, mostly the starting armor\/shield\/weapon, and possibly a useful magic weapon or artifact. And that's assuming you actually make it to the fight. If you die at any point, you have to replay the entire match.<br><br>So while normal story mode keeps raising the challenge gradually from match-to-match, in the last one the difficulty spikes drastically. The imbalance has caused the average player to either give up or spend more time on the last match than the rest of the game combined to beat it.
