The game's backgrounds don't immediately jump out as being anything special, but as I played I found myself drawn to watching them to see what changes were wrought by my actions. Each world has a different feel to it, but they're all very nicely crafted.<br><br>The character graphics, too, are excellent. Your chosen man or woman is shown in silhouette, in what looks like a fairly low-polygon model, but which looks very nice. Animation is fantastic -- motion capture, I assume -- and your character moves very fluidly about the platforms.<br><br>The platforms themselves are sufficient, if nothing spectacular. They do their job well enough.<br><br>The interface is minimal, with a few very occasional and discreet panels introducing new controls as they become available, and menus with simple symbols rather than any text. In fact, I don't think the game features any text at all, apart from percentages and fractions in game that make up part of the platform puzzles. I found it reminiscent of Monument Valley on Android in this manner, although they're very different types of games.<br><br>Sound - The first thing I noticed about the sound in Metrico+ was the music, which was excellent. It begins with a sort of minimal oriental theme, and works its way through a number of ambient themes as the game progresses. It's very pleasing to the ear, doesn't distract from the game, and I didn't find it becoming boring either.<br><br>Sound effects are appropriate and work well with the game. I've not encountered any voices, but there are some sampled sounds for certain character actions and events, such as jumping.<br><br>Gameplay - Metrico+ is a group of connected series of geometric puzzles with themes based on metrics, most of which boil down to lowering, raising, or otherwise moving platforms based on following certain movement patterns.<br><br>For example, on one screen, moving to the right might move one platform to the left and fill a column moving to the right, jumping might raise one platform, and landing might raise another by a different amount. To solve each puzzle you first have to work out how each component responds to your various actions, and then work out how to make your way across the screen to the other side, using those actions to move the platforms where you need them.<br><br>The game starts out easy, with only left and right movement, jumping, and falling available to you. After completing the first world you gain another available action, and the platform level puzzles become more complex. This continues with each world that you complete.<br><br>Although there seems to be no story to speak of, the weird map that progresses as you finish each world, and the progressive changes in your character, hint that there may be more to the world of Metrico+ than is obvious. After the third world you're given a choice of two different ways to proceed, too, and I'm not sure what effects this branching has on your progress through the world.<br><br>At this point I've only played up to the fifth world, I think, and I've hit a level that I don't yet know how to complete. I hope it will come to me, as I don't want to stop my exploration of Metrico+'s strange world yet.<br><br>Metrico+ features Steam Achievements, too, but they're all hidden. class=\"bb_h1\"> Metrico+ is a fun, info graphic inspired platformer, that unfortunately suffers from lack of replayability <\/div><br><br>Metrico+ is a game for those who just couldn\u2019t get enough out of their statistics class! Welcome to Metrico+! Where platforming and puzzles meets Info graphics and pie charts. In Metrico+, you\u2019ll explore a multitude of worlds, solve puzzles, listen to some pretty sweet music, and think outside the box! But the question remains, is Metrico+ worth your time?<br><br>The basics of the gameplay are fairly simple. You play as random generic guy and or girl. As \u2018random generic guy and or girl\u2019, it\u2019s your job to platform and puzzle-solve your way to victory throughout each of the six worlds. With every world, you gain a new ability to change the way the world and it\u2019s puzzles react and as such, you gain more difficult and more challenging puzzles to solve. Each world is based on the set of skills you gain and with that in mind, each world\u2019s puzzles become more challenging with more steps involved as the level progresses, as well as adding in skills you previously learned. In the beginning of each world, the puzzles are simple and are introductions to the main character\u2019s new skills. As the puzzles progress, the puzzles involving those skills become more challenging.<br><br>There\u2019s a nice sense of progression in each world, and furthermore, the entire game. Individual puzzles don\u2019t have the cohesion that I feel they need in order to master any one particular style of puzzle, but they all play out in interesting ways that make up for their lack of core cohesion with each puzzle by making each world\u2019s difficulty ramp better. With that being said, the \u2018featured\u2019 ability you gain in each world often takes precedence over previously learned skills, and a few skills the player ends up learning aren\u2019t used nearly as often as others.<br><br>There\u2019s also the ability to quit and return to any individual checkpoint or puzzle in the map area. It\u2019s a nice touch that means people who aren\u2019t into puzzles (or for those who don\u2019t play 1 game until it\u2019s finished) can go back at any time to any puzzle of each world, rather than just the worlds. It\u2019s a nice touch to include for more casual puzzle-game lovers into the universe of Metrico+.<br><br>The only thing that will kill the game is it\u2019s replay factors. While it\u2019s a wonderful 2-4 hour experience, there\u2019s little reward to replay the game, making it a bit pricey compared to other platformers in a similar price point.<br><br>Visually, the game is very beautiful to look at, and the idea of basing a puzzle platformer on graphs and info graphics is a neat concept. The vibrant colors combined with the simple visuals make a nice and appealing game. The background takes full advantage of it\u2019s 3D and adds infographics and pie charts galore. That being said, it\u2019s visual style is purely that, and it doesn\u2019t take any true advantage of the math portion that it might have if it focused the style more on game integration, rather than it simply being a reactive world. With that, it is interesting how the background reacts to the player and his or her decisions. In fact, we didn\u2019t actually notice it until the second or so world in. A nice, stylized touch.<br><br>As for audio and sound, I love it. The musical soundtrack is simple, in a \u2018what you probably think your math teacher likes\u2019 sorta way. The world\u2019s reactive style also lends itself to the audio effects. Everything the player does makes a sound, and it\u2019s hard to describe because it plays well into the musical background pieces. It\u2019s interesting hearing the sounds you make while solving the puzzles blend into the music so well.<br><br>Control wise, the game is fairly straightforward. I played with  my Lunar Xbox One Controller, but it also worked fine with a 360 Controller, and we also played a world with keyboard and mouse. The controls seemed to work fine with their transition from PlayStation Vita to PC. Settings wise, there are some basic graphical and resolution settings, as well as options for things like Depth of Field and Vsync. The frame rate also appears to be locked at 60fps. The UI also appears to have been unchanged from the Vita version of the game it rather suffers for it if I\u2019m frank. It doesn\u2019t look terrible, but it could have used a new menu layout to accompany it\u2019s PC release.<br><br>For Metrico+, it\u2019s a fun game that suffers from replayability and the passage of time. Once you play through the game once or twice to master the puzzles, there\u2019s little to really keep players coming back. It\u2019s a short, fun 2-4 hour experience, but it suffers from the fact that it leans on it\u2019s puzzle elements too much compared to the platforming elements, making replayability rather difficult for a game like this. For me, the game is great at what it does, but it\u2019s lack of replayability makes it rather difficult to suggest at $13.99. If you\u2019re not fully sold on the game by now, wait on a sale. As is, Metrico+ is a great game with a great visual style and great soundtrack that suffers from lack of replayability.
