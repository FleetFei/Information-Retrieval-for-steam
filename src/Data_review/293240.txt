class=\"bb_h1\">OVERVIEW<\/div>Cosmochoria is a science-fiction rogue-like game of exploration from indie developer Nate Schmold.<br><br>In Cosmochoria, you play a pants-less little pink guy in a space helmet, spawning on an unnamed planet in the middle of space. Your task is to plant flags to claim planets and grow plants on them, thus restoring them to vitality. As you claim planets, fight off aggressive aliens, meet NPCs, and find weird items, the game slowly reveals its story and your goals.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Graphics<\/div>Graphics are simple, but elegant. The game is two-dimensional, played from a side-on view, with colourful pixel graphics used throughout, and a unique stylised look.<br><br>Your character is a chubby little naked person in a space helmet, and he (?) is almost as big as some planets. Planet appearance is variable, with planets ranging from those that look like the leftovers over some sort of nuclear war to some that bear ancient ruins reminiscent of Mayan civilisation. Even the larger planets are only an order of magnitude larger than the main character.<br><br>The camera keeps the character in the centre of the screen and, by default, rotates loosely around as you orbit planets (by running around their surface). It works well, although it takes some getting used to as you reorient yourself, especially when jetting off into space.<br><br>Special effects are simple, but nicely done.<br><br>There are only very limited graphics options related to window mode and screen resolution.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Sound<\/div>Sound effects are good: well-normalised and used appropriately. The jetpack sound has a nice feel to it, and the pea shooter weapon sounds suitably weak.<br><br>Music is good, only occurring in certain situations, such as when a boss draws near. I haven't worked out what causes little bursts of background music in other situations, but the sporadic nature of them adds to the overall weirdness of the game.<br><br>There are no voices.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Gameplay<\/div>The game is very easy to play, but hard to master. You have only five controls: move\/rotate left and right, jump\/jetpack, crouch\/pick up\/use, and shoot.<br><br>When on a planet, gravity keeps you on the ground, so your most common actions become running around the planet either clockwise or anticlockwise, shooting enemies, and crouching to plant or build. Once you've restored a planet, you can leap in the air and use your jetpack to escape orbit, then navigate your way to another planet to start all over again.<br><br>Control in space is fun, with your escape vector governing your path across the galaxy, and only limited fuel in your jetpack to correct it. Planets all have localised gravity fields, though, which helps to bring you back to ground when you find another one.<br><br>Making your job harder are a horde of nasty aliens of various types, who chase and shoot at you, and a 'boss' enemy that gets activated after every few planets that you restore. These become difficult fast, but keep at it and you'll soon learn some tricks to make defeating them easier.<br><br>The galaxy is procedurally generated each game, including planet placement and size, and NPC and artefact placement. The game includes three resources: seeds, bricks, and some sort of currency. Seeds are only collected and used in the current game. Bricks and currency are carried over to your next game when you die. Bricks can be collected and used to build towers and other structures on planets. Currency can be used to buy permanent upgrades in the shop before you start a new game. It's a good system. As you play not only do you start to understand and get better at the game, but your character also becomes more powerful and able to do what's required of him.<br><br>The game also has a strange collection of unique artefacts that can be collected. Deliver these to the correct NPCs and you end up with another artefact that allows you to change the universe in some way. Again, this is activated or deactivated before each game. Some are beneficial, like a NPC and boss detector, whereas others are downright dangerous, such as one that makes enemies more aggressive.<br><br>Unfortunately, though, Cosmochoria either doesn't like new hardware and software (i7, GTX1070, 32 GB RAM, Windows 10), or it's become more buggy since its early days. I first played it about a year ago, then put it away until it was 'finished'. It's in full release now, and is much more fleshed out, but it also seems less stable. A few times an hour the game will just freeze for about 20 seconds, then resume again as if nothing happened; there's no stuttering or other performance-related issues, it just seems to decide to have a rest. I've also had occasional crashes to desktop, with the game giving no warning and no error, and just dropping back to Windows. This has invariably happened while I've been doing very well, too, so it's immensely frustrating.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">PROS AND CONS<\/div>Pros:<br>+ Creative universe and plot, with unique interactions and a good sense of humour<br>+ Rogue-like aspects are well thought out<br>+ Addictive and very replayable<br>+ Pleasant pixel graphics used to great effect<br>+ Nice music and sound<br>+ Creative Steam Achievements<br><br>Cons:<br>- Buggy: in-game freezing and occasional CTDs<br>- No save system (which would be almost forgivable if it weren't for the CTDs)<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">CONCLUSION<\/div>Cosmochoria is a unique rogue-like game of space exploration. story of a lone astronaut, equipped curiously with nothing but his trusty laser pistol and space helmet. No, seriously, that's all, this dude is completely naked. Not much is known about our naked astro-protagonist other than his mission to bring color and life to a dying galaxy randomly sprinkled with lifeless planets. You'll jetpack around in classic arcade fashion pew-pew-pewing all that stand in your way as you rocket between the rocky spheres in an effort to rescue these planets from bleakness.<br><br>Your early career as a cosmic space horticulturist will be painstakingly difficult and a bit of a slow start. The game starts you off with the core essentials and not much else, including a basic jetpack for floating between planets, a puny peashooter and just enough seed to get you started with your first plant of each run. The planets start off barren and empty but as you fertilize them with your psychedelic plants become more and more cosmic and groovy. With each anomaly you plant a new wave of enemies approaches and you're faced with defending yourself while managing to plant more in the process. The further you get and the more you plant, the more intense and difficult the enemy waves get forcing you to skip and jump over shots and fight back the small alien creatures dropped by UFOs.<br><br>Luckily, filling a planet with enough funky space plants to fill the empty heart at the core means you've accomplished reviving it, and the planet gives back its lifeforce to your missing health as some strange act of gratitude. This lengthens your lifespan giving you a chance to collect the dropped gems from enemies used for buying new equipment or items between plays. The equipment you accumulate through your many playthroughs are what take Cosmochoria from being just a fun arcade romp to a fully addictive and almost roguelike experience with persistent elements that expand on the length of each trip. You'll find permanent upgrades for everything from maximum health, speed of your character, to the amount of starting seeds you bring with you at the beginning and all will help push you on your journey to discovering the secrets of Cosmochorias world.<br><br>The world <b>is<\/b> chock full of secrets to discover, and given the randomized nature of the game will appear at the least expected of times and runs. These secrets are obviously.. a bit secret so I don't want to spoil them but can come in the form of anything from strange and mysterious turds to ancient space eggs. Their usage is generally as cryptic as the item itself, and usually involves finding another oddball character in the galaxy to bring the item to. The rewards you gain from these strange, cryptic, and secret side quests randomly sprinkled across this colorful cosmos are extremely worth the trip and usually give you permanent upgrades that will carry over and speed up your future endeavors into this trippy realm.<br><br>Cosmochoria makes me feel like a kid on saturday morning again, the oddly nostalgic twinklings of the opening song evoke a melancholy feeling in me that is both very happy and at the same time a bit sad.
