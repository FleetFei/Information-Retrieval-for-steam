getting noticed is to have a different approach. <i>Koihime Enbu's<\/i> angle is &quot;Romance of the Three Kingdoms&quot;, but almost every character is a girl. Guan Yu? Cao Cao? Sun Shang Xiang!? All girls. Furthermore, this game is a spin-off of <i>Koihime Musou<\/i>, an adult visual-novel\/strategy game about a young man who travels back in time to this Three Kingdoms era. To be honest, I'm a little surprised that <i>Koihime Enbu<\/i> is an awesome fighting game. <br><br>In terms of game-design, <i>Koihime Enbu<\/i> is unlike other contemporary 2D fighters. Instead of having its combatants soar into the air and devastate each other with dizzying combos, this game keeps it very grounded. For the most part, combos rarely go beyond two-in-ones, or jump-in &gt; follow-up &gt; special. A lot of matches are determined by fighting-game basics. By that I mean spacing, footsies, pokes, anti-airs, and so on. It's not that these elements aren't present in other fighters, but players mistakenly tend to ignore them as the genre becomes increasingly complicated. If I were to draw comparisons, <i>Koihime Enbu<\/i> could be considered a faster-paced <i>Samurai Showdown<\/i>. <br><br>Going further, the moves you perform all have wildly-varying degrees of safetyness. Light attacks are generally a good idea for applying pressure, but they probably won't decide matches. Medium and heavy attacks have their uses, and with proper timing and spacing, you can control the flow of the match. However, when you hold forward, or down\/forward and perform a medium or heavy attack, everything changes. Your heroine performs a high-risk attack. It's slow, can be countered in many ways, but it can also dramatically effect the outcome of the fight.<br><br>Now I'm not sure what to call it. A critical-counter? A fatal-counter? A romantic-counter? Well, it doesn't really matter. Basically, when the opponent is hit with a certain move, they crumple to the floor. This gives the player still standing an opportunity to land a massive combo. We're talking wall-splats, ground-bounces, the works. Off of a single one of these counters, players can expect to lose anywhere from 30 to 60 or even 100%(!!) of their life. It sounds crazy, but keep in mind that the larger combos will require some rather precise positioning, execution, and a lot of meter. To add to this, you can't get hit by these devastating counters if you aren't caught pressing buttons. Obviously the answer to avoiding these situations isn't &quot;don't try to attack&quot;, but you have to be wary.  <br><br>While I've just discussed what can be considered the x-factor of a Koihime Enbu fight, these occurences are few and far between. If your opponent is well-aware that you're fishing for a crucial-counter, they're going to have no trouble picking you apart. In this game, the simplest combos or even single-attacks can do serious damage. A lot of the time, you'll find that the best strategy is to stick to the basics. Aside from those mythical 75-100% combos, the execution barrier for this game is very low. This allows players to devote their energies towards understanding match-ups, looking for openings, and hopefully, winning matches. While I think that the roster could stand to have a few more entrants, it does strike the right balance between depth and accessibility. Every character is easy to use, but they can also offer some unique moves or interesting tactics. Aside from everyone having good and bad match-ups, there doesn't seem to be any truly ineffective characters. <br><br>As far as content goes, it's fair to say that this game is a better buy for competitive players. While this tends to be the expectation when it comes to fighters, a sizable number of these games still include extensive single-player modes. Unfortunately, this isn't the case for <i>Koihime Enbu<\/i>. I'm going to start this portion of the review off by admitting that... well... I got a little curious and did some research. I browsed the Koihime Muso wikia, looked up some other related materials, and now I'm interested in seeing more of the storyline. Unfortunately, the scenario mode in Koihime Enbu is a little disappointing. Each character has their own story, and dialogues with their opponents in-between matches, but the plot isn't there. <br><br>I suppose I shouldn't be asking for a storyline that affects the <i>Koihime Muso<\/i> canon, especially with the omission of a few key characters (such as the male lead and Ry\u016bbi Gentoku aka Liu Bei). Still, as a huge fan of the <i>Three Kingdoms<\/i> novel, I was hoping for something with a little more depth, rather than a handful of gags and sorta-intimate moments. Still, the scenario mode might be worth checking out, since at least it gives some insight on everyone, and will help newcomers get used to how the game works and the franchise that it spawned from.<br><br>The training mode in this game is very well-designed. It covers all of the functionality that fighting game fans are accustomed to, such as a multitude of options, the ability to practice against the CPU, and even allowing for two players to spar with each other. Alongside the training mode, there is also a challenge mode where players can attempt several combos, each more convoluted than the last. Training mode can also be accessed while waiting for online matches, which is always welcome.<br><br>As far as the online-play is concerned, I believe that it's satisfactory. There have been some instances of lag, most notably when I (an American) was playing against someone from Japan. Speaking of, expect the competition to be extremely stiff and punishing. <i>Koihime Enbu<\/i> has been in Japanese arcades since 2014, and on consoles since the beginning of this year. I've already had a few matches, where I put up as much of a fight as a training dummy. This is partly because some of the strategies I've run into, simply won't occur in the single-player modes.<br><br>To give an example, one of my opponents was a Sonsh\u014dk\u014d (Sun Shang Xiang) player. The first round was a close win for me, my opponent won the second. After the second round, I noticed that my opponent had a full bar of meter. So what happened in the third round? On my end, everything went to Hell. Each faction in the game has a couple selectable strategists. Summoning help costs meter, but they offer some useful techniques, if you know how to employ them. Anyway, Ryom\u014d (one of the strategists), throws three projectiles at the opponent. While this is happening, Sonsh\u014dk\u014d has some freedom to attack or defend at will. I wasn't prepared for this. So needless to say, all of my advances were thwarted, I fell victim to some nasty mix-ups, and I couldn't even land chip-damage on my opponent. <br><br>That's not to say that all of your fights will be decided by unfamiliar tech. In every match, there is a measurable level of chaos. To explain it better, I'll post a link to a video: <a class=\"bb_link\" href=\"https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=VwaDqSMs5iE\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noreferrer\"  id=\"dynamiclink_0\">https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=VwaDqSMs5iE<\/a> . Notice how several seconds pass by in the first round without a single hit being registered. As the match progresses, both players start taking risks, and the rounds are decided far more quickly. High-level play can be extremely methodical, so if you don't have the patience for it, you'll get hurt. <br><br>Although <i>Koihime Enbu<\/i> is lacking in terms of single-player content, I have every reason to believe that this is one of the better fighting games on Steam.
