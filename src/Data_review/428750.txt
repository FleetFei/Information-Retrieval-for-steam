seems like a bit of a jerk. We should do something about that. <\/div><br>Neon Chrome is a cyberpunk, top-down shooter from the developer of Crimsonland, 10tons. In Neon Chrome you play as assets, used by a strange character (who you also play as, sort of) in order to try and take down the mysterious Overseer. The Overseer has full control over Neon Chrome\u2019s human and robotic guards and he\u2019s more than happy to use brutal force to stop anyone in his way. That includes you by the way. As you battle through thirty-one levels, taking place over six levels of the building, you\u2019ll face increasingly difficult enemies and even a few mini-bosses and bosses. It won\u2019t be easy but you\u2019re not exactly in short supply of assets, you\u2019ll get there eventually.<br><br>Full Disclosure: A review copy was provided by the developer.<br><br>Before every run you can move around a level of Neon Chrome which is halfway between an apartment and a human storage complex. I\u2019m not sure how to label the character but they have a strange chair\/headset device that they use to control the assets, the assets are who you mainly play as. In this stage of the game you can also buy upgrades (such as improved health or damage) as well as purchase weapons, abilities and cybernetic enhancements that your next asset will use when you use them. In this area you can also track your stats, such as how many assets you\u2019ve used, as well as see what achievements you\u2019ve unlocked and what rewards they have earned you.<br><br>The assets themselves vary a fair bit, the main variation coming from what class they are. There are a few different classes in the game, ranging from Assassin (my personal favourite) to Cyber Psycho, each with their own class-specific ability. The Assassin\u2019s ability, for example, is that they become completely invisible when they are standing or moving around in shadows while the Cyber Psycho has +20% Hit Points and +2 Enhancement slots. Each class also has its own stats, i.e. health, energy (used for the asset\u2019s other ability), speed, damage and critical hit chance. The classes are very different to each other and require slightly to drastically different strategies to be successful with them, adding a nice level of depth to the game.<br><br>The assets also have a weapon, a non-class-specific ability and a name (well duh). The weapon can be any of the weapons you have unlocked (more can be unlocked by finding special box-type things in the levels, which also applies to enhancements and the second kind of abilities.) You can also unlock the same weapon but with a different projectile, for example you can have a regular shotgun as well as an Ion Shotgun or a Plasma Shotgun. The second type of ability is effectively a secondary attack, this could be lock-on missiles or blast grenades, there are quite a few to unlock. These abilities require energy which is collected from loot boxes. Each ability can be used a certain number of times before you run out and need more energy, for example you can use your grenades three times. Enhancements can be equipped from a CyberHance Express station (found on most levels). You have a certain amount of slots for these enhancements (upgradeable) so if you run out of slots you\u2019ll have to choose one to replace with the new enhancement. There\u2019s a wide variety of enhancements available from the basic stuff that increases health or speed to enhancements that cause your bullets to split and cause 30% more damage.<br><br>As I said earlier, Neon Chrome consists of thirty-one levels across six floors. When you reach a new floor (the level after a boss fight) you can then start from that new floor and skip the levels leading up to that. It\u2019s a great way to help you progress through the game though it does mean you miss out on all the loot from the previous floors when starting from that floor which is a problem seeing as there\u2019s a spike in difficulty from one floor to the next. There is of course a difference in difficulty between the levels too but it\u2019s more noticeable when you reach a new floor. For the most part, you just have to reach the green (or purple for a special level) elevator at the end of the level to go to the next one but some levels will have objectives for you to complete first, e.g. destroy five specific targets. The levels are also randomly generated (apart from the boss fight levels) so you\u2019ll never see the same run twice.<br><br>An increase in difficulty doesn\u2019t just mean more enemies though, it means harder enemies and new kinds of enemies. From what I\u2019ve played, enemies don\u2019t get phased out, they just get phased in meaning you\u2019ll be introduced to new enemy types every few levels. You\u2019ll also face off against \u2018special\u2019 enemies. These consist of normal enemies with some kind of buff\/special ability, for example an enemy marked as SWIFT as a much higher movement speed than a regular enemy. Some levels have a mini-boss, again this is a variation of a regular enemy with a special ability and in this case better stats too and at the end of every floor you come across a full boss. The boss fights are incredibly challenging, it took me several attempts to get through the first one for example. Thankfully (if you\u2019ve collected a lot of loot) you\u2019ll be able to buy new upgrades which will help you next time you take on the boss. Furthermore, once you beat the Overseer you reach a new OS level which significantly increases the difficulty across all levels. As far as I\u2019m aware there\u2019s no limit on OS levels but it does take a lot longer to get through them anyway.<br><br>So, let\u2019s talk about loot. There\u2019s quite a lot of it. Most of what you\u2019ll find are small boxes which, when opened, will net you a couple of hundred bucks and a little bit of energy, you generally find one or two in each room when playing. Then you\u2019ve got slightly bigger boxes in which you\u2019ll find more money and energy as well as (sometimes) health kits and even weapons. Then you\u2019ve got healing stations, unlock boxes (the things I mentioned earlier which contain new abilities, weapons or enhancements), weapon crates and weapon upgrade machines (levels up your gun which improves its stats). Also, enemies drop loot in the form of money and energy, they drop about the same as a small box but special enemies drop more. While energy, weapons etc. only matter for the asset you collect them with, your money and unlocks are persistent.<br><br>Just a couple more things to mention. The sound track is absolutely fantastic, it really fits the game well but there didn\u2019t seem to be many tracks so it would\u2019ve been nice to see a few more. The controls are also really good, they felt very responsive and the default configuration works well (though it can be changed). While I\u2019m talking about controls, it\u2019s worth knowing that your asset\u2019s speed has an impact on how quickly they turn, it\u2019s not a case of the controls being unresponsive, this also applies to enemies which can be used to your advantage.<br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\">Verdict <\/div><br>Neon Chrome is a thoroughly enjoyable top-down shooter with a lot of gameplay variation, unlockables and quite a bit of replay value. All of this wrapped up in an awesome cyberpunk setting. The perfect loop?<\/div><br>There comes a time in every AAA addicts' library when everything starts to feel samey and me-too. When hunting variably nonsensical objects around a gorgeously produced, big-budget-world starts losing its' flair. A time when the easy infinite death-&gt;rebirth of most modern games begs the question: if nothing happens when i die, then why try to stay alive? <br><br>All of these issues are solved with one simple, indie, twin stick shooter. This game will make you remember why skill matters in a single player game (though it's got co-op here too, if that's your speed). Though you'll quickly find satisfaction in the simple design, the geniusly paced levels and excellent boss encounters; it's the inclusion of the roguelike feedback loop of progression that sells it for me.  That loop involves levelling aspects of your randomly rolled, cheekily named and classed character that are permanent to the game regardless of how far you made it last game, the cash you had upon death and the unlocks you discovered during your play will carry onto the next avatar.  All of this happens automatically while blasting fools and leveling your weapons (which happens per run), and while the levels seem to be 'seeded' in such a way, they still manage to feel designed by hand rather than tossed together by a ruleset.  All of this makes each play feel fresh and accomplished and satisfying, and every tense moment worthwhile.  Reaching another checkpoint between &quot;chapters&quot; automatically unlocks access to warp at the beginning of your next run from that level or any other previous level, these chapters at first last first 5 levels, then get longer as you get further into the game.  Just a few days in and i can already feel myself becoming more strategic in my approaches.  If you have played roguelike games then you know what i'm talking about, but if not,  the important takeaway is that you never feel like you start from the same spot essentially, in addition to the levels rerolling the sense of progress carried from one playthrough to the next is tweaked so that you can always have another leap in levels within reach, all the while managing to communicate via simple skill improvements that you're always getting better and more capable.<br><br>Being one of those AAA addicts that i mentioned in the first paragraph, it's so refreshing to find an honest, skill-based shooter that leans on the generous side of rewarding, keeping things lively from the first moment until you realize you've spent half a day playing one game.  <br><br>Bought it on sale, had i known it was this well designed and downright FUN, i would've gladly paid double. I know it's a small game, I know it looks a little silly even in some of the screenshots, but believe me none of these things matter once you get into the game, it harkens back to a standard you probably completely forgot, tasting overall a bit like the cyberpunk offspring of 'Gauntlet' and 'Smash TV'.  After a handful of unexpectedly engaging marathon sessions, you'll still be asking yourself: &quot;why is it that some great games are barely on the radar, while other bloatedly shiny yet mediocre ones hog the limelight so often?&quot; <br><br>Check this game out if you're a fan of rewarding arcade-style twinstick shooters that keep you hooked.  Best budget purchase in my library in YEARS.
