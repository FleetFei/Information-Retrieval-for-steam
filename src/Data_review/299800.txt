TL;DR at the bottom)<br><br>The Land of Eyas is a unique and mentally challenging puzzle-platformer developed by Happy Square Productions as a result of a successful Kickstarter campaign. You play as a boy, Eyas, who wakes up in a cat suit in a strange world with no memory and a talking tree who may be able to help him. The game acts as a support group for people like me because I finally feel like someone understands me. Until this game, I thought I was the only person who constantly wakes up in a cat suit, talks to a tree, has difficulty telling which way is up and which way is down, and forgets about what happened the night before. But that all changed with The Land of Eyas. <br><br>As I briefly alluded to before, the unique game mechanic you'll see in The Land of Eyas is the two dimensions that exist in every level. There's this wavy horizontal line that represents the center of gravity, and your character is always pulled to it. So when you fall below it, it pulls you up, and when you're above it, it pulls you down. You'll have to find ways to use this mechanic to your advantage in every level if you hope to make progress in your adventure. This, alongside other game mechanics like building blocks, makes the game require you to take a moment and plot your way to the end of the level. It's not the kind of platformer where you simply make your way onwards - no. The Land of Eyas is a platformer where you'll have to think hard in order to complete the level as you would to solve a math problem. Just as soon as you think that you've mastered the use of the gravity horizon the game will throw more mechanics at you like saw blades and building blocks. You'll constantly have to adapt to these new mechanics in order to continue achieving success.<br><br>As if the game wasn't hard enough there are challenges you'll have to complete if you wish to experience everything the game has to offer. Remember that talking tree I mentioned earlier? Well, some levels have seer stones within them that you have to return to the tree in order for him to discover the truth behind his lost memories. These seer stones are often challenging to get and require you to really go out of your way and think even harder than you would to simply complete the level. The levels also display &quot;perfect&quot; times in the top right corner that you may attempt to match or beat, but I don't see any indication that the completion of these challenges in particular will unlock any kind of content.<br><br>The soundtrack and sounds in general are also very nice. The music is often fast paced, rushing you onwards and driving you to make mistakes, and sometimes it is mysterious, serving as a reminder of the strange world you've found yourself in. As for the sounds, there's something oddly satisfying about the &quot;thump&quot; you hear when your character lands. Similarly, there's something oddly disturbing about the sound you hear when your character perishes. The music and sounds within are fitting and add to the game wonderfully.<br><br>The art style is rather unique and it feels like there is more than one art style throughout the game. You can tell by looking through the screenshots on the Store Page that there are a plethora of different color palletes and environmental themes used throughout the game. There are bright levels, dark levels, cloudy levels, earthy levels, industrial levels...you get the point. Eyas's design is memorable yet it's also a direct copy and paste job of my cat suit. Shameful (\/s).<br><br>Weaknesses...I suppose it's a good thing that I have to think hard in order to come up with weaknesses. The story is <i>alright<\/i> but ultimately I feel like it serves as a backdrop to the gameplay, rather than what urges you to push forward. I haven't completed the story, but at the same time I don't feel motivated whatsoever by it. I wish there were more NPCs to run into and speak with, but they are far and few between. I also wish the game could make me care more about the story in the first place. I really don't care how Eyas got to where he is to be honest, just as I don't really care how and why I wake up in a cat suit occasionally. The only thing motivating me is the challenge of completing all of the levels. I also think you should be able to zoom out whenever you'd like, instead of it doing it automatically at certain points within the level. Furthermore, I wish checkpoints were slightly more plentiful (or better positioned) in some levels, but that's probably just me.<br><br>I'm very, very picky when it comes to platformers. I hate the ones that provide an artifical amount of difficulty. I hate the ones where you constantly die and get teleported back to the beginning. That \u2665\u2665\u2665\u2665 gives me motion sickness. But I like The Land of Eyas - I really do. It has a charm and uniqueness about it that I'm drawn to. I like the music in it and I like the freshness each level presents. I never feel like it's getting repetitive as some platformers tend to. I haven't completed the game yet but I certainly plan to, just because I want to see what other game mechanics will be thrown at me. I recommend this game to anyone who enjoys platformers. I see and appreciate the hard work that was put into this game and I wish the developer the best of luck.<br><br><u><b> TL;DR<\/b><\/u> The Land of Eyas is a charming, challenging game with a touch of mystery. It's unique gameplay and unique world come together to create a memorable experience. I\u2019m comparing this to the rather fussy issues I had when playing Rogue Legacy. I did find the jumping to be a bit \u201cfloaty\u201d feeling. As opposed to the \u201chard\u201d jump of the classic NES Super Mario Bros\u2014if anyone has even played that in the last decade. The sprinting and jumping had the typical response time challenges that I see common in many platformers though, regardless of who is playing them, so I\u2019d say it\u2019s as it\u2019s supposed to be. The type of platforming you\u2019ll do in this game necessitates the type of jump physics that are in the game. Prepare for multiple retries. Thankfully, longer levels have check points.<br><br>How\u2019s the performance? I had no frame rate drops on my mid-tier gaming PC. That said, I didn\u2019t get to test on any of the levels that had spinning saw blades, etc. But even so, I\u2019ve seen FPS drops in games on my PC that were less action oriented. So I call this a success as well.<br><br><a class=\"bb_link\" href=\"https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=Y9WjrKsTCZI\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noreferrer\"  id=\"dynamiclink_1\">https:\/\/www.youtube.com\/watch?v=Y9WjrKsTCZI<\/a><br><br>Now that we\u2019ve got the non-starters cleared, how is the game itself? Well, you\u2019ll not find anything revolutionary here. It\u2019s an action puzzle platform game. That\u2019s what you\u2019re going to get. You\u2019re also going to get really beautiful artwork. And that\u2019s what drew me to this game. The boy in the cat suit (Eyas) is cleverly reminiscent of the boy in the white pajamas from Where the Wild Things Are. It\u2019s a savvy decision on the part of the art director. I instantly gravitate to it. The rest of the artwork is crisp, ornate, evocative, and disarming\u2014which is good when your game is a puzzle game and likely to provoke frustration (that\u2019s the nature of the beast). Anything to keep me wanting to look at the game is unequivocally good. I\u2019d almost like to say that artwork is the third rail of platform games these days. Without artwork, what is your platformer bringing to the table that the platformers before it have not already? That Land of Eyas is unapologetically beautiful.<br><br>This leads me to the next important aspect of the game: its gimmick. The gimmick is that the force of gravity reverses at the midline of each level. Later levels shake this up further, but this is the core conceit. It looks like a water line. You cross it. Suddenly you find yourself falling up instead of down. It\u2019s not bad as far as gimmicks go, but it\u2019s nothing we haven\u2019t seen before. Does it need to be? What is important is that it adds just enough flexibility to the game that it can present you with some unusual puzzles. So really, I shouldn\u2019t call it a gimmick. That implies that it brings nothing to the table. But it does. It makes you have to look carefully at what\u2019s on your screen to decipher the solution to each level. So really, it\u2019s a game mechanic and it works in favor of the game. Hopefully enough that you find the puzzles in this game to be ones you haven\u2019t seen before. For a casual gamer, I think it succeeds very well. <br><br>The story? Well, there\u2019s not a lot of story to interest me initially. You\u2019re a lost boy talking to a talking tree (yes, I meant to write it that way). You\u2019re looking for \u201cMother\u201d (Isaac anyone?). I won\u2019t say that I was drawn in instantly or anything. Perhaps as you progress you\u2019ll be more invested in the character(s). I wasn\u2019t. But frankly, I don\u2019t play games any more to read a story, certainly not platformers. Play this game for the puzzles. You won\u2019t be disappointed.<br><br>Am I going to get 100\u2019s of hours out of this game? No. It\u2019s not a grand strategy game, is it? But what I will get is enjoyment for my time and a sense of accomplishment in nice bite-size brain packets. I need a nice low-stakes diversion from time to time and Eyas provides that. It even opens multiple levels to choose from so if you get stuck on one, you can solve another and give your brain a rest. This allows you to progress the game until you\u2019re ready for a fresh look at that one level you couldn\u2019t solve yesterday. Again, a very savvy development decision. It circumvents the banging-my-head-against-a-wall feeling many other linear platformers have.<br><br>Do I have any criticisms of the game? Is there anything I did not like? Yes. I did get frustrated at times by the deadliness of spikes. Rubbing the side of a crystal doesn\u2019t strike me as an offense that should be punishable by death. I\u2019m not play 1001 Spikes, after all. Yes, that\u2019s a real game. The little \u201chop\u201d you make at the apex of a moving block was inconsistently implemented and therefore annoying. When you\u2019re riding the elevator block, you want to jump at its highest point. This is not possible though because you \u201chop\u201d when it gets to that highest point\u2026 except in cases when you don\u2019t. Which is it? I want consistency here (you can witness this in my gameplay videos if it isn\u2019t clear). The blocks that you can pick up and carry were a great introduction, but there was a 1 pixel exploit I found where a block was essentially hanging in the air. Not sure I want to complain about that one though considering how hard it was to get that block up there in the first place. None of these things stopped me from enjoying the game, though. I\u2019m a rather fickle gamer, also. So it\u2019s a solid testimony to the game\u2019s strengths that I didn\u2019t give up on its weaknesses.<br><br>In the end, I easily recommend this game to someone who wants something fun to do with
