class=\"postedDate\">Posted: November 17, 2016\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"bb_h1\"> Overview <\/div> <br>Equin: The Lantern is a retro style roguelike game that puts you on a quest to obtain a mysterious lantern of unknown origin and design. You\u2019ll be tasked with conquering 50 levels of a seemingly never-ending dungeon, delving deeper and deeper, amassing fortune and cheating death the entire time in turn-based combat. With its pixelated graphics that are feel reminiscent of old Dragon Warrior titles, Equin: The Lantern provides an interesting experience into the unforgiving realm of old-styled games and with a healthy dose of modern day twists and turns. Solve the mystery of the lantern phases and discover what it all means, or die horribly trying.<br><br>This review will cover a range of emotions, as they changed drastically the more I played. In the beginning, Equin: The Lantern didn\u2019t strike me as something I found all that interesting. I\u2019m not sure what drew me to it initially, but on my first few playthroughs I didn\u2019t know what I was doing and found myself frustrated with the difficulty of the game. With no tutorial and no real help outside of tips you buy at taverns [pending you have the gold, of course], the game largely leaves you dazed and confused, wandering aimlessly about fighting monsters, collecting treasure and trying to figure out the mechanics.<br><br>For a while, if I\u2019m honest, I didn\u2019t find it fun. I found the lack of help annoying, the monsters overpowered and the gameplay mediocre. I\u2019m not sure when that changed, but my perception of the game has transformed tremendously. <br><br>Perhaps I\u2019ve just become too complacent with tutorials, games that guide you through what to do, when to do it and explanations on how to win. There\u2019s no gimmicks here, no QTEs, no monster \u201cweak points\u201d or anything of the sort. You're thrown into a dangerous world with a randomly generated assortment of items and basically left to figure it out for yourself. There is no handholding here and quite honestly, the game is there to make you fail, but also there to help you win. I found myself writing down my own hints, memorizing the tips in the beginning and finding my own ways to make each run more successful than the last. That mixture is a fine craft that makes this game so much more than it appears on the surface; let me explain.<br><br>You\u2019re given the option between three classes, a warrior [melee], a wizard [magic] and a thief. Each game starts you with three random items and you start your adventure. There\u2019s a lot of thought here and it is easily overlooked. You could start with a pickax to mine gold and gems for cash, a campfire to heal, potions to sustain hard-fought battles, weapons, armor, a fishing pole to find fish to replenish health, a membership card to reduce pricing at shops and so much more. When you see the vast amount of items that you can earn, the possibilities really start to spider out and the uniqueness of each playthough becomes more evident.<br><br>With a limited inventory, you\u2019re forced to determine what is worth keeping and what is expendable. Do you use your healing potion now, or try to level up to replenish your health and stamina? Do you wield a two-handed sword for the damage, or stick to defense with a sturdy shield? Do you spend your gold to sleep, or learn a helpful tip, or save it for what could be? There\u2019s so many ways each playthrough could play out, it starts to feel a little bit like a \u201cchoose your own adventure\u201d book.<br><br>Couple all this with randomly generated levels, enemies, loot and specialized floors [darkness, rain, infestations, exploding floors and optional boss levels] make each playthrough truly interesting and the amount of work put in by the developer shows. It doesn\u2019t hurt to have a developer that cares so much for their game and those that play it. <br><br><br><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Pros <\/div> <br><ul class=\"bb_ul\"><li> Unpredictability \u2013 The game keeps you on your toes and makes every situation important.<br><\/li><li> Inexpensive cost for how much the game offers.<br><\/li><li> The pacing of the game can either be quick or slow and methodical. There are no penalties for taking your time through each level.<\/li><\/ul><br><div class=\"bb_h1\"> Cons <\/div> <br><ul class=\"bb_ul\"> \n<li> Text Style \u2013 Sometimes I find the pixelated text difficult to read.<br><\/li><li> Anti-Magic Zones are often difficult to see what\u2019s going on. The concept is fine, but the density and slightly opaque color scheme makes me have to really focus my eyes.<br><\/li><li>No full screen or ability to zoom out due to the game\u2019s design.<\/li><\/ul><br>Overall, I feel like Equin: The Lantern might not be for everyone. For those that have played old RPGs like Dragon Warrior\/Dragon Quest, you\u2019ll find some solid inspiration in this game, however I feel like some people will glance over it without hesitation and I find that unfair. It really deserves a chance and for those that try it, I think you\u2019ll enjoy it.
